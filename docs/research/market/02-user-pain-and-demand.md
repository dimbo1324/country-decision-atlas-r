SECTION 1. THE CORE PROBLEM
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  When someone chooses a country to relocate to, they have to
  manually gather information from a scattering of sources:
  Google, YouTube, Telegram, Reddit, Facebook groups, immigration
  agency websites, legal blogs, ranking sites, expat forums,
  personal stories, government law databases.

  This information, as a rule, is:
    ✗  Stale
    ✗  Contradictory
    ✗  Subjective
    ✗  Poorly structured
    ✗  Not tied to the user's specific scenario
    ✗  Unsourced
    ✗  Not tied to trend/direction (where is the country heading)

  THE UNMET NEED:
  "What fits ME specifically — with my passport, income,
  profession, family, savings, goals, and risk tolerance — and
  what has changed recently that could make my decision wrong?"

SECTION 2. THE MOST FRAGMENTED INFORMATION CATEGORIES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  1. VISA ELIGIBILITY AND ACTUAL CONSULAR PRACTICE
     Official pages ≠ a specific consulate's local practice.
     Real problems: appointment delays, document formats,
     insurance interpretation, apostilles, rejections.

  2. RESIDENCY → PERMANENT RESIDENCY → CITIZENSHIP (the whole path)
     Each stage is described separately, in different laws.
     The links between stages are often invisible.

  3. TAX RESIDENCY AND DOUBLE TAXATION
     The 183-day rule is a simplification, not the full picture.
     Employer risk, permanent establishment, home-country
     obligations — every one of these topics triggers
     contradictory advice.

  4. THE REAL COST OF LIVING
     Tools give averages, but a newcomer pays:
     • A deposit (1–3 months)
     • A "foreigner" markup
     • Insurance
     • An agent's fee
     • An initial period with no local credit history

  5. ACCESS TO HOUSING
     A country can be cheap on paper but effectively inaccessible
     to a newcomer: income requirements, guarantors, a local
     credit history.

  6. ACCESS TO HEALTHCARE AND INSURANCE
     "Exactly when after arrival can I access care?"
     Official health-system websites don't answer this.

  7. BANKING AND PAYMENTS
     A circular dependency: you need an address → you need an
     account → you need a tax ID → you need residency status.

  8. WORK RIGHTS AND THE LABOR MARKET
     A visa may grant the right to reside but not the right to
     work. The labor market requires the language, qualification
     recognition, a local sponsor.

  9. FAMILY AND DEPENDENTS
     Rules for spouses, children, and education — spread across
     different documents.

  10. POLITICS AND LEGAL DIRECTION
      "Is the country becoming more open or more closed?"
      No existing tool answers this question.

SECTION 3. THE HARDEST QUESTIONS TO ANSWER ALONE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  THE TOP-10 QUESTIONS NO ONE CAN GET A RELIABLE ANSWER TO
  WITHOUT A STRUCTURED PLATFORM:

  1.  "Can I legally relocate there?" — depends on passport,
      income, employment type, savings, education, family.

  2.  "Can I legally work there?" — residency ≠ the right to work.

  3.  "Will I become a tax resident?" — the 183-day rule isn't
      the only criterion.

  4.  "What will my real monthly budget be?" — requires
      accounting for taxes, rent, insurance, transport, deposits.

  5.  "Can my partner work?" — depends heavily on the visa type
      and the country.

  6.  "Can I get permanent residency or citizenship?" — requires
      understanding years of residence, days present, language,
      taxes.

  7.  "Can I rent housing as a newcomer?" — requires knowing the
      real market.

  8.  "Will my profession be recognized?" — doctors, engineers,
      teachers, electricians all face different requirements.

  9.  "Which city inside the country is realistic for me?" —
      national averages hide the real differences.

  10. "What has changed recently?" — the most painful point:
      stale rules everywhere.

SECTION 4. COMMON USER MISCONCEPTIONS AND MISTAKES
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  BEFORE RELOCATING:
  • Choosing a country "by vibe" instead of checking feasibility
  • Using average cost-of-living figures as a personal budget
  • Ignoring taxes and employer obligations
  • Confusing tourist experience with resident experience
  • Underestimating the language barrier
  • Choosing a dead-end visa with no path to permanent residency/citizenship
  • Ignoring restrictions on family members
  • Overestimating the "expat bubble"
  • Ignoring the country's legal trend
  • Relying on a single type of source

  AFTER RELOCATING:
  • Missing registration deadlines
  • No evidence base for renewals (rent, receipts, travel records)
  • Using the wrong visa for what they're actually doing
  • Putting off the tax question "for later"
  • Signing bad rental contracts
  • Not adapting documents in time (driver's licenses, diplomas)

SECTION 5. WHAT USERS ARE WILLING TO PAY FOR
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  HIGH WILLINGNESS TO PAY:

  1.  A personal eligibility check
  2.  A shortlist of countries matched to personal goals and constraints
  3.  A document checklist for a specific country
  4.  A tax/employer-compliance risk map
  5.  A realistic first-month/quarter budget
  6.  Alerts on rule changes (visas, taxes, laws)
  7.  A personal timeline: pre-departure, on arrival, deadlines
  8.  A verified marketplace of specialists
  9.  Source-backed reports
  10. Watching countries on a watchlist

  LOW WILLINGNESS TO PAY:
  • Generic PDF guides with no updates
  • "Top-10 countries" lists with no personalization
  • AI summaries with no sources
  • Paid access to content that duplicates free official pages
  • Generic "how to relocate" courses with no scenario tie-in

SECTION 6. WHAT USERS WANT IN ONE PLACE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  Per the pain-point research, an ideal platform should bring
  together:

  THE LEGAL BLOCK:
  Right to enter • Right to reside • Right to work
  The path to permanent residency/citizenship • Dependent rights

  THE FINANCIAL BLOCK:
  Tax residency • A real budget by household type
  Rental cost for a newcomer • A net-income estimate

  THE PRACTICAL BLOCK:
  Housing accessibility • Healthcare by status • Banking
  Professional recognition • Document requirements

  THE INFORMATION BLOCK:
  Sources with a verification date • A confidence level
  Links to primary sources • A change history
  Legal signals (what's changing) • Alerts

  THE COMPARATIVE BLOCK:
  A scenario decision engine • Ranking by the user's own criteria
  Structured stories from other people
