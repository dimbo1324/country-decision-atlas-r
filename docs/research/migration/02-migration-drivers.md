# Migration Drivers

## Key takeaways

- Migration motives are multi-factor: economics, politics, safety, family, education, healthcare, climate, lifestyle, and legal status all intersect.
- Among stated reasons, quality of life, lifestyle, and career opportunity lead [S13].
- Real decisions are constrained by visas, money, employability, housing, language, family, uncertainty, fear, and bureaucracy.
- Communities reveal a gap between the "pre-relocation dream" and the "post-relocation reality": housing, loneliness, language, taxes, healthcare access, and bureaucracy dominate after arrival [S41–S46].

## The main motives

The most common desires: a better quality of life, higher income, better job opportunities, safety, political freedom, education for children, healthcare, personal freedom, family reunification, climate/lifestyle, and long-term legal security. In real communities, motives combine: "safer streets, better schools, less political pressure, a labor market that values my skills."

## The weight of individual factors

- **Economics (salary, jobs, taxes, cost of living)** — very important, usually the first filter: can I legally work, will I earn enough, is rent accessible, is the salary/expense ratio better than at home. For high earners, taxes and investment rules matter more; for middle/lower earners, rent, healthcare, transport, and access to jobs matter more [S08, S41, S44].
- **Politics (instability, restrictions, authoritarianism, war)** — very important for specific groups and increasingly salient: war, mobilization risk, repression, corruption, censorship, no independent courts, discrimination, polarization. The extreme end is 123.2 million forcibly displaced [S06]. For non-forced migrants, politics often works as a "risk hedge" — leaving before things become unbearable.
- **Safety and crime** — a key criterion, especially for families, women, and retirees. Safety is subjective: a statistically safe city can feel unsafe due to a neighborhood, a language barrier, or discrimination. Safety correlates with housing: cheaper areas are often weaker on safety and infrastructure.
- **Healthcare** — critically important for families, older people, people with chronic conditions, and wealthy retirees: quality, access, wait times, insurance cost, language, entitlement to services, the public/private mix, medication availability. Confusion about healthcare after relocating (registration, insurance, doctors, prescriptions, emergencies) is a major pain point [S26–S27].
- **Education and opportunities for children** — critically important for families and the middle class: school quality, international schools, language load, the path to university, safety, integration, childcare cost, long-term citizenship. This can outweigh taxes and climate.
- **Climate, environment, and urban livability** — growing in importance for remote workers, retirees, families: health, energy costs, transport, outdoor lifestyle, long-term risk; air and water pollution, heat, flooding, wildfires, green space, walkability. Climate risk is becoming a structural driver — up to 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 in a pessimistic scenario [S24].
- **Speed of access to residency/permanent residency/citizenship** — important for those who need legal stability, not just a temporary stay. "Fast" is deceptive: some places have easy temporary residency but a slow path to permanent residency; some grant citizenship "on paper" but with difficult integration or unstable rules.
- **Taxes and financial freedom** — critical for entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, high earners, retirees. Low-tax countries can be attractive but come with weaker public services, expensive private services, limited healthcare. A serious comparison covers income tax, social contributions, corporate tax, VAT, capital gains, wealth/exit taxes, pension rights, and tax-residency rules.
- **Business and investment** — important for entrepreneurs: company registration, banking, accounting burden, a visa tied to the business, the tax regime, currency controls, corruption, market size, hiring rules, IP protection. Business migration needs its own risk model.
- **Language, culture, and ease of adaptation** — critical. Language affects work, bureaucracy, healthcare, schools, friendships, legal safety, and psychological comfort. Communities warn: relocating with no language and no support creates isolation, even if the country looks "good on paper" [S45].

## What makes people abandon relocation plans

Common blockers: insufficient money, no visa pathway, weak employability, family obligations, fear, uncertainty, pets, health issues, no language, a partner's disagreement, children's schooling, elderly parents, ties to property/business, tax complexity, difficulty verifying a qualification, an inability to find housing. Communities also highlight underrated barriers: the mental cost, loneliness, guilt, and a loss of identity and status [S41, S45].
