# Migration Dynamics and the Intent to Relocate

## Global migration dynamics

- The number of international migrants keeps rising in absolute terms: around **304 million in 2024**, versus 275 million in 2020 and ~154 million in 1990 [S01–S03]. But migrants' share of world population is growing slowly — around **3.7%** in 2024, versus 2.9% in 1990 [S01–S03]. The world isn't "migrating en masse": migration is outpacing population growth, but it still affects a minority.
- Permanent migration into OECD countries cooled in 2024 after the post-pandemic surge: **6.2 million** new permanent immigrants, -4% versus 2023, but still +15% versus 2019 [S08]. By 2024, OECD countries were home to more than 160 million foreign-born residents [S08].
- Forced displacement is one of the fastest-growing categories: **123.2 million** forcibly displaced at the end of 2024 [S06].
- The main receiving regions are Europe and North America (about half the global total), plus North Africa and Western Asia, including the Gulf states [S03]. The largest receiving countries: the US, Germany, Saudi Arabia, the UK, France [S03].
- The main origin countries: India, China, Mexico, Ukraine, Russia, Syria, Bangladesh, Pakistan, the Philippines, Afghanistan [S01–S03]. Forced displacement is driven by conflict: Syria, Ukraine, Afghanistan, Sudan, Venezuela, Myanmar [S06–S07].

### COVID-19 and the phase shift

COVID-19 first suppressed cross-border movement (border closures), then a rebound followed in 2021–2023 (labor shortages, a backlog in family reunification and asylum queues). By 2024–2026, the rebound became politically contentious: many countries shifted from "open up and recruit" to "select and control" — higher salary thresholds, student quotas, stricter asylum procedures, more returns, targeted pathways for shortage occupations [S08, S16, S18–S21].

### What's grown more popular in the past 5 years

- Canada and Australia during the post-pandemic rebound (later tightening student/temporary pathways) [S08, S16].
- Germany — reforms for skilled workers and the Opportunity Card [S20].
- Portugal, Spain, Greece, Italy, Malta — lifestyle, nomads, investment migration (golden-visa rules have shifted) [S15, S17, S26].
- The UAE, Saudi Arabia, and the Gulf states — for high-income professionals and HNWIs [S26–S27].
- Mexico, Costa Rica, Colombia, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam, Georgia, Albania — nomads and budget relocators (growing housing strain and local pushback) [S32, S43, S46].

### What's losing appeal

It depends on the segment. The UK tightened pathways, and net migration dropped sharply from its 2023 peak [S18, S47]. Canada remains attractive but less "unlimited," under housing pressure and student/temporary-resident quotas [S16, S48]. The US remains a desired destination, but Gallup recorded declining desire to move there in 2025 [S11]. For the wealthy, Henley projected a large HNWI outflow from the UK and China [S26].

### Which migration types are growing fastest

The strongest structural growth is in forced displacement, targeted skilled labor migration, and remote-worker/nomad mobility. Student migration grew post-pandemic but dropped sharply in 2024 due to tightening [S08, S16]. Investment migration is smaller in volume but high-value and growing among HNWIs [S26–S28]. Digital nomad visas spanned more than 40 jurisdictions by July 2024 [S15].

## The intent to migrate

- The desire to migrate is far larger than actual migration. Gallup repeatedly warns: "wanting to move" ≠ "having a plan and a legal path" [S10–S11].
- Global desire to migrate: around **16% in 2021–2024**, then **15% in 2025** — still hundreds of millions of adults (Gallup estimated ~900 million) [S11].
- The highest regional desire is in Sub-Saharan Africa (~33% in 2025); Latin America and the Caribbean declined to ~25% [S11]. Spikes occur in countries facing economic crisis, instability, weak institutions, repression, or conflict.
- Desired destinations: the US (declining appeal), Canada, Germany, Spain, Switzerland, Japan, Saudi Arabia [S10–S12]. Lifestyle-focused data often adds Canada, Australia, the UK, the US, Spain, Portugal, New Zealand, Germany, Switzerland, Japan. For the wealthy — the UAE, the US, Italy, Switzerland, Portugal, Greece [S26–S27].

### Who's interested

- **Age**: young people and those early/mid-career are the most mobile. A striking signal: 40% of US women aged 15–44 said in 2025 they'd move abroad permanently if they could (versus 10% in 2014) [S11].
- **Occupations**: IT, engineers, medical workers, researchers, skilled tradespeople, finance professionals, startup founders, remote workers, freelancers, teachers. OECD countries increasingly build pathways around shortages, especially healthcare and tech [S08, S20–S21, S48].
- **Skilled professionals** — structural growth driven by aging economies and labor shortages [S25]. The direction is not "open migration for everyone" but selective migration for shortage occupations.
- **Entrepreneurs and investors** — growth among HNWIs and politically concerned wealthy families: tax planning, hedging political risk, children's education, banking diversification [S26–S27].
- **Students** — growing long-term, constrained short-term: -13% new international students in OECD countries in 2024 [S08].
- **Families** — growing demand for safety, education, healthcare, stability; family migration is the leading category of permanent migration in OECD countries [S08].
- **A second citizenship or residency** is sought by HNWIs/investors, mobile professionals, families from unstable countries, holders of "weak" passports, retirees, tax-sensitive individuals, and nomads. Among HNWIs this increasingly means a "sovereign portfolio" — several residency/citizenship options to reduce dependence on one jurisdiction [S26–S28].
