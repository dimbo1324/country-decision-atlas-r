8. Countries and Competition for Migrants

Main answer
Countries are competing selectively. They want high-value migrants: skilled professionals, STEM workers, healthcare workers, founders, investors, international students in priority fields, and remote workers with foreign income. They are less open to low-wage migration, passive investors, irregular migration and students seen as using education mainly as a labour-market entry route.

1) Countries actively competing for talent
- Canada: historically strong PR system, points-based skilled migration, but now reducing overall admissions and temporary residents.
- Australia: skilled migration tradition, but stricter student/graduate settings.
- Germany: EU Blue Card, skilled-worker reforms, labour shortage strategy.
- Spain and Portugal: lifestyle + remote-worker routes + EU access; strong interest among nomads and families.
- UAE: tax/lifestyle/business hub, remote-work options, investor/founder appeal.
- Singapore: business, finance, high-skill employment, but selective and expensive.
- Japan and Korea: ageing populations and gradual opening for skilled and selected labour migration.
- New Zealand: attractive for youth/skilled migrants, but high cost and distance.
- Ireland/Denmark/Netherlands/Switzerland/Austria: high-skill labour markets, strong wages, but housing and bureaucracy constraints.

2) Countries competing for students
Traditional Big Four: United States, United Kingdom, Canada and Australia. Together they still host a very large share of mobile students. However, policy restrictions are pushing interest toward:
- Germany;
- France;
- Spain;
- Italy;
- Netherlands, though it is also stabilising numbers;
- Malaysia;
- Singapore;
- Thailand;
- South Korea;
- Japan;
- UAE.

3) Countries competing for entrepreneurs and startups
Strong examples:
- UAE: company formation, tax environment, regional hub.
- Singapore: regional HQ, finance, rule of law, startup ecosystem.
- Estonia: e-residency and digital governance, although e-residency is not immigration.
- Portugal/Spain: startup visas, EU access, lifestyle.
- UK: still strong ecosystem, but immigration costs/thresholds are higher.
- Canada: startup and skilled routes, but processing and caps matter.
- Netherlands/Germany/France/Ireland: strong EU startup ecosystems, but housing, taxation and bureaucracy differ.

4) Countries chosen by people with limited budgets
Often considered:
- Georgia;
- Armenia;
- Serbia;
- Albania;
- Turkey;
- Mexico outside premium areas;
- Colombia;
- Argentina;
- Paraguay;
- Thailand;
- Vietnam;
- Malaysia;
- Indonesia/Bali, subject to visa/tax realities;
- Portugal/Spain outside Lisbon, Porto, Barcelona and Madrid.
Budget users need realistic warnings: low cost does not equal easy legal status, safe banking, work rights, good schools or path to citizenship.

5) Countries chosen for second passport or long-term citizenship
Common naturalisation / citizenship planning destinations include:
- Portugal and Spain: EU residence/citizenship appeal, but requirements and timelines vary and can change.
- Italy/Ireland/Poland/Hungary/Romania and others: ancestry citizenship where eligible.
- Canada/Australia/New Zealand: attractive but selective and slow.
- Latin America: Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Colombia, Mexico and Panama are often discussed for residence/citizenship, but real timelines and requirements must be verified.
- Caribbean CBI countries: still relevant for investment citizenship, but due diligence and global acceptance risk matter.
- Turkey: citizenship by investment exists but geopolitical/passport-quality trade-offs matter.

6) Countries losing attractiveness
- United States: still #1 desired destination, but Gallup shows relative desire is at a 20-year low.
- United Kingdom: high costs, tighter skilled/care/student/family rules, housing issues.
- Canada: housing affordability, student caps, temporary resident reduction and processing pressure.
- Australia: student/graduate tightening, high fees, housing cost.
- Portugal: still popular, but housing costs, visa delays and golden visa property-route changes reduce the “easy” narrative.
- Germany: strong economy but bureaucracy, housing, language and digital-administration complaints weaken lifestyle appeal.
- New Zealand: attractive but expensive, distant and facing citizen outflow to Australia.

7) New popular / emerging destinations
- Spain: digital nomad visa, lifestyle, EU access, large cities and secondary cities.
- Portugal: still strong despite constraints.
- Mexico: U.S. remote workers, retirees, lifestyle migration.
- Colombia: affordability, climate, digital nomads, improved urban perception.
- UAE: tax, business, safety, global hub.
- Georgia/Armenia/Serbia: easier access and lower cost for some nationalities.
- Thailand/Malaysia/Vietnam: affordability, lifestyle, regional base.
- Uruguay/Paraguay/Panama: Latin America residency alternatives.
- Japan/Korea: rising interest for students, culture and skilled routes, but language and work culture are barriers.

8) Overrated vs underrated
Overrated often means online hype exceeds legal/economic reality:
- Portugal: excellent for many, but rents, delays and changing rules are underestimated.
- Bali/Indonesia: lifestyle is strong, but legal work/tax/residence is often misunderstood.
- Dubai/UAE: tax/business appeal is real, but cost, residency dependence and heat/lifestyle trade-offs matter.
- Canada/Australia: strong long-term countries, but high cost and tighter routes reduce easy-access myths.

Underrated often means good fit for specific profiles but lower online hype:
- secondary Spanish cities;
- Malaysia;
- Uruguay for stability-oriented Latin America users;
- Paraguay for low-cost residence planning, with trade-offs;
- Poland/Czechia for EU work/study, depending on profile;
- Japan/Korea for selected skilled/student profiles;
- smaller German/French/Italian cities for students/families with language tolerance.

9) Platform implication
Country recommendations should not be generic rankings. The same country can be:
- excellent for a remote worker;
- poor for a local job seeker;
- strong for a family;
- weak for tax optimisation;
- impossible for a passport-holder with no visa-free entry;
- great short-term but weak for citizenship.
The product should rank countries by user profile and route, not by popularity.
