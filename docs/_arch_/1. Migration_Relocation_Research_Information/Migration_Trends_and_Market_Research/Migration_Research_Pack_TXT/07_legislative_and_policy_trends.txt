7. Legislative and Political Trends

Main answer
The global pattern is selective openness. Countries want skilled workers, students in shortage-aligned fields, founders, investors, nurses, engineers and remote workers with foreign income. At the same time, they are tightening asylum, low-wage labour, family dependants, international students, passive investment and irregular migration.

1) Countries simplifying or expanding migration rules
Examples:
- New Zealand: from November 2025, eligible international students can work up to 25 hours per week during semester, up from 20. New Zealand also announced new skilled residence pathways from 2026.
- Spain: OECD notes Spain increased maximum weekly work hours for students to 30 hours in 2025.
- Japan: introduced a one-year post-graduation job-search opportunity for tertiary-educated graduates of Japanese language institutes in National Strategic Special Zones.
- Greece: introduced tech/talent visa reforms and eased EU Blue Card rules in 2026.
- APAC countries: Fragomen reports growing use of highly skilled visas and digital nomad options.
- Digital nomad visas: spread rapidly since 2020, often used to attract remote workers with foreign income and revive tourism/local spending.

2) Countries tightening rules
- Canada: 2025-2027 plan reduced permanent resident admissions to 395,000 in 2025, 380,000 in 2026 and 365,000 in 2027; Canada also capped international student permits and tightened PGWP eligibility. The 2026-2028 plan continues reducing temporary resident arrival targets.
- United Kingdom: after the 2025 White Paper, the UK reduced eligible Skilled Worker occupations, ended overseas recruitment of social care workers from July 2025, increased salary/skill thresholds and tightened settlement rules. ONS reports long-term net migration fell to 171,000 in year ending December 2025 from 331,000 in 2024 and 944,000 at the 2023 peak.
- Australia: policy changes reduced “visa hopping”, shortened post-study work rights, reduced graduate visa age limits from 50 to 35 and increased English requirements; student visa fees and scrutiny have increased.
- EU: the Pact on Migration and Asylum introduces stricter border screening, faster asylum procedures, stronger external border controls and more deportation mechanisms.
- United States: 2025-2026 policy direction is enforcement-heavy and volatile, with changes affecting asylum, travel restrictions and benefit processing for some nationalities.

3) Golden visa / citizenship-by-investment restrictions
- Spain ended its golden visa in 2025.
- Portugal ended/changed property-based golden visa routes earlier and shifted away from passive real estate.
- Ireland closed its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023.
- Cyprus and Bulgaria ended citizenship-by-investment schemes earlier.
- Malta's golden passport scheme was ruled illegal by the EU's top court in 2025.
The direction is clear: passive investment-for-residence/citizenship, especially in the EU, faces growing legal, political, housing-affordability and security pressure.

4) Countries becoming more attractive to remote workers
Strong remote-worker / digital-nomad destinations include Spain, Portugal, Croatia, Greece, Estonia, Malta, Hungary, Italy, Cyprus, UAE/Dubai, Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, Thailand, Malaysia, Georgia and some Caribbean countries. Attractiveness depends on income thresholds, tax treatment, duration, family inclusion, health insurance, local cost, internet, time zone and path to residence/citizenship.

5) Countries becoming less migrant-friendly
This cannot be reduced to one list, because a country can be hostile to asylum seekers but open to engineers, or restrictive for students but open to investors. Less-friendly signals include:
- higher salary thresholds;
- fewer eligible occupations;
- caps on students/temporary residents;
- limits on family dependants;
- longer settlement timelines;
- reduced asylum rights;
- faster deportation procedures;
- rising anti-immigration rhetoric;
- housing restrictions targeting foreigners;
- banking restrictions for non-residents;
- tax crackdowns on remote workers.

6) Legal changes with the strongest impact on country choice
- visa eligibility and income thresholds;
- right to work;
- spouse/children rights;
- post-study work rights;
- path to PR/citizenship;
- physical presence requirements;
- tax-residency rules;
- foreign-source income tax treatment;
- housing/rental laws;
- bank account/KYC rules;
- health insurance requirements;
- school access rules;
- language/integration tests;
- dual-citizenship rules.

7) Can you determine a country’s direction from its laws?
Yes, with a structured law-tracking index. The platform can classify direction across dimensions:
- migration openness: easier/harder entry, residence, work, family, PR, citizenship;
- talent openness: skilled visas, shortage lists, Blue Card/startup/talent routes;
- student openness: caps, work hours, post-study rights;
- investor openness: passive vs active investment routes;
- remote-worker openness: digital nomad visas and tax clarity;
- rule-of-law direction: predictability, court independence, administrative transparency;
- tax direction: incentives vs crackdowns;
- housing direction: foreign buyer/rental restrictions;
- banking direction: non-resident access and compliance burden;
- social/political direction: anti-immigration rhetoric and policy volatility.

8) Product implication
A simple “country is good/bad” label is not enough. A serious platform should say: “This country is opening for skilled workers, closing for students, tightening family reunification, attractive for remote workers only if foreign income remains outside the country, and politically volatile on housing/tax.”
