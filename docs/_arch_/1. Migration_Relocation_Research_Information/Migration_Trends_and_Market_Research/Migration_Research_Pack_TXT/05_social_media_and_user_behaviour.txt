5. Social Media and User Behaviour

Main answer
Migration discussion communities are large and active across Reddit, Facebook, Telegram, YouTube, TikTok, WhatsApp and local forums. The most valuable behavioural insight is that people do not only search for facts; they search for reassurance, lived experience, recent rule changes and someone with a similar profile.

Important evidence caveat
Public counts for Telegram, Facebook and WhatsApp communities are hard to verify consistently. Reddit is more visible, but community size still changes over time and cannot be treated as a stable official statistic. Therefore, this report treats community growth qualitatively unless a platform API or scrape is used.

1) Are migration channels, chats and communities growing?
Yes, qualitatively. Evidence includes:
- large Reddit communities such as r/IWantOut, r/expats, r/expat and country-specific expat subreddits;
- many Facebook groups for “Expats in [country/city]”, “Americans in Portugal”, “Digital Nomads in Spain”, etc.;
- Telegram channels used heavily by Russian, Ukrainian, Belarusian and other relocation waves;
- YouTube relocation channels focused on Portugal, Spain, Mexico, UAE, Thailand, Canada, Australia and Latin America;
- digital nomad communities attached to Nomads.com/Nomad List, coworking spaces, Slack/Discord groups and paid communities.

2) Common topics in migration communities
The most repeated themes are:
- visa/residence documents;
- proof of income;
- appointment availability;
- lawyer/agent recommendations;
- rent and neighbourhoods;
- bank accounts;
- taxes and tax residency;
- healthcare and insurance;
- schools and children’s adaptation;
- work permits and job search;
- remote-work legality;
- driving licence and car import;
- pets;
- apostille/translation;
- citizenship timelines;
- integration and language;
- loneliness, culture shock and return migration.

3) Most frequent pre-move questions
- Which visa am I eligible for with my passport/income/job?
- How much money do I need monthly?
- Can I bring spouse/children/parents?
- Can I work remotely legally?
- Do I need to pay tax if my income comes from abroad?
- Is [city] safe for women/families/children?
- Where should I live first?
- How do I open a bank account?
- Is private health insurance enough?
- Do I need a lawyer?
- How long does the residence card take?
- What documents must be apostilled?
- Can I get citizenship later?
- What are the real downsides no one mentions?

4) Most frequent post-move problems
- underestimated bureaucracy;
- rental scams, deposits, guarantor problems;
- bank account rejections;
- tax-residency surprises;
- healthcare navigation;
- school enrolment issues;
- language isolation;
- loneliness and loss of status;
- poor fit between online hype and daily life;
- delays in residence cards;
- document mistakes;
- unrealistic budgets;
- misunderstanding local labour markets;
- conflict between tourist experience and resident reality;
- return-to-office or employer compliance problems for remote workers.

5) Whom do people trust?
Trust hierarchy varies by segment:
- Official government websites: trusted for legal requirements, but often hard to understand, incomplete or not user-friendly.
- Immigration lawyers: trusted for legal interpretation, but expensive and sometimes country-specific only.
- Friends/diaspora: trusted because they have lived experience, but often anecdotal and outdated.
- Reddit/Telegram/Facebook: trusted for recency and practical detail, but noisy and contradictory.
- YouTube/bloggers: trusted for emotional proof and lifestyle, but often biased by monetisation or personal experience.
- Relocation agents: useful for execution, but incentives may be unclear.
- Employers/universities: useful for sponsored routes but not neutral for overall life decisions.

6) Where information becomes outdated or contradictory
- visa law changes;
- tax rules;
- appointment systems;
- document requirements;
- minimum income thresholds;
- rental market conditions;
- banking access;
- school enrolment rules;
- health insurance requirements;
- citizenship processing times;
- digital nomad visa tax treatment;
- golden visa route availability.
The contradiction comes from timing: one person’s successful 2022 experience may be invalid in 2025.

7) Misinformation and risk
Migration information is vulnerable to misinformation because rules are complex, emotional stakes are high, and communities are segmented by language/nationality. EU research on migration disinformation focuses mostly on public narratives about migrants, but the same structural issue exists in relocation advice: rumours spread quickly, official corrections spread slowly.

8) Product opportunity from behaviour
A platform should not copy social media. It should extract structure from social media:
- cluster repeated questions;
- convert them into decision trees;
- show official-law answers next to lived-experience notes;
- time-stamp every claim;
- distinguish official requirement, lawyer interpretation, user case and rumour;
- show “last verified” and “changed since” warnings;
- rank answers by profile similarity.

9) Data design for user-generated evidence
User cases should be structured:
- nationality/passport;
- age/family status;
- income type;
- visa route;
- city;
- timeline;
- total cost;
- documents requested;
- rejection/delay reasons;
- rent paid;
- healthcare setup;
- school experience;
- tax setup;
- mistakes and lessons.
This transforms subjective stories into comparable evidence.
