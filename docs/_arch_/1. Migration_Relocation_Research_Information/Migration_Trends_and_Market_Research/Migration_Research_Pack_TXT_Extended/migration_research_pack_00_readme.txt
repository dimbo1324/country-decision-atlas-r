MIGRATION RESEARCH PACK
Language: English
Prepared: 2026-06-16
Scope: Global migration dynamics, migration demand, online interest, social behaviour, willingness to pay, policy trends, country competition, and platform opportunities.

IMPORTANT LIMITATIONS
1. Hard official data is strong for international migrant stock, OECD migration flows, forced displacement, remittances, international students, and many policy changes.
2. Publicly available data is weaker for Google Trends query rankings, Telegram/Facebook community counts, private visa-tech revenue, and exact willingness-to-pay by user segment. In those areas, this pack uses proxies, published examples, public platform evidence, and recommended measurement methods.
3. Migration "interest" and actual migration are different. Survey desire, Google searches, social media posts, visa applications, and actual arrivals should be treated as separate layers of demand.
4. Digital nomad visa counts vary because sources define "digital nomad visa", "remote worker visa", "freelancer visa", and "long-stay self-employment visa" differently. Public 2025-2026 sources commonly report more than 50 and sometimes 60-70+ relevant programmes.
5. This research is market/research analysis, not legal advice. Any platform based on this analysis should cite official legal sources and route users to licensed lawyers for legal interpretation.

CORE FINDINGS
- Global international migrant stock is still rising. UN DESA estimates 304 million international migrants in mid-2024, almost twice the 1990 level of 154 million.
- Europe and Northern America remain the largest destination macro-region, but Northern Africa and Western Asia has shown especially strong growth because of labour migration to Gulf states and forced displacement across the region.
- The largest destination country remains the United States, followed by Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, France, Spain, Canada, the UAE, Australia, Russia, Turkiye, and Italy.
- The largest origin countries in 2024 include India, China, Mexico, Ukraine, and Russia.
- Public desire to migrate is high by historical standards. Gallup reported that 16% of adults worldwide, more than 900 million people, said in 2023 that they would like to move permanently to another country if they could.
- Migration demand is fragmenting. People no longer search only for classic immigration. They search for remote-work visas, tax residency, second passports, ancestry citizenship, startup visas, safe countries, housing affordability, schooling, and political risk.
- Policy is becoming more selective. Many countries are open to high-skilled workers, founders, investors, and remote workers while becoming stricter toward students, low-wage workers, asylum seekers, dependants, or passive residence-by-investment routes.
- The opportunity for a platform is not simply to collect generic country information. The stronger opportunity is profile-based decision intelligence: eligibility, risk, costs, legal change tracking, country direction, migration path, and evidence-backed recommendations.

SOURCE QUALITY TIERS USED IN THIS PACK
Tier 1: Official datasets and institutions: UN DESA, IOM, UNHCR, OECD, World Bank, IDMC, national immigration departments, national statistics agencies, European Commission.
Tier 2: Established research/policy organisations: Migration Policy Institute, Gallup, Pew, Ipsos, Mixed Migration Centre, InterNations, INSEAD/GTCI.
Tier 3: Market/industry evidence: Henley & Partners, relocation companies, law firms, visa-tech platforms, digital-nomad reports, SEO/search-analysis articles.
Tier 4: Qualitative user evidence: Reddit, Telegram/Facebook community studies, YouTube/blog ecosystems, forum discussions.

RECOMMENDED NEXT RESEARCH STEPS
1. Pull a Google Trends CSV dataset for 30-50 keyword clusters by country and language.
2. Build a sample of 200-500 migration community posts from Reddit, Telegram, Facebook, and YouTube comments, then tag each post by topic, country, stage, and pain point.
3. Track official immigration law pages monthly and calculate a "law-change velocity" score.
4. Collect current visa application prices, processing times, rejection rates where available, tax rules, housing costs, and route-to-citizenship timelines into a structured database.
5. Interview 20-30 migrants from different segments: remote workers, families, students, high-skilled workers, entrepreneurs, investors, and forced migrants.
