5. SOCIAL MEDIA AND USER BEHAVIOUR

5.1 Is the number of migration channels, chats, and communities growing?

Answer: The role of migration communities is clearly growing, but exact global counts are difficult to verify publicly.

Evidence types:
- Reddit has large active migration communities such as r/IWantOut and r/expats.
- Facebook groups are actively used by migrants and people on the move. Mixed Migration Centre studied posts from eight public Facebook groups used by Spanish-speaking Latin American and Caribbean populations moving toward North America.
- Telegram has become important for migration and emergency relocation communities, especially in Russian/Ukrainian-speaking and crisis contexts.
- YouTube and TikTok creators increasingly function as informal country scouts, explaining visas, costs, apartments, schools, taxes, and lifestyle.

Data limitation:
Telegram and Facebook do not provide clean public global datasets for all migration communities. Group counts, member counts, and growth rates require platform-specific scraping, analytics tools, or manual sampling. A serious platform should create its own monitored panel of communities and measure post volume, topic share, sentiment, and country mentions monthly.

2. Document preparation.
3. Proof of funds.
4. Apostille, translation, notarisation.
5. Employer sponsorship.
6. Job search.
7. Housing and rent.

9. Taxes and tax residency.
10. Banking and payments.

12. Healthcare and insurance.
13. Language learning.

17. Permanent residence and citizenship timelines.
18. Bureaucracy and processing delays.

5.3 What questions do people ask most often before moving?

Repeated pre-move questions:
- Am I eligible for this visa?
- Which visa route is realistic for my profile?
- How much money do I need?
- Can I bring my spouse, partner, children, or parents?
- Can my spouse work?
- How long does processing really take?
- What documents should I prepare before leaving?
- Do I need apostilles/translations?
- Can I work remotely legally?
- Where will I pay tax?
- Can I open a bank account?
- Which city is better for my budget?
- How hard is it to find housing?
- Is the country safe for women / children / LGBTQ+ people / foreigners?
- What is the path to permanent residence or citizenship?
- What happens if my job ends?
- Can I switch visa types later?
- What are common mistakes?

5.4 What problems do people experience after moving?

Common post-move problems:
1. Housing shock
Rent is higher than expected, deposits are difficult, landlords reject foreigners, or housing scams appear.

2. Bureaucracy shock
Appointments, local registration, residence cards, tax numbers, health insurance, and banking take longer than online guides suggested.

3. Job-market mismatch
Users underestimate local language requirements, credential recognition, sponsorship difficulty, or salary-to-cost ratio.

4. Visa dependency risk
Employer-sponsored migrants may become vulnerable if a job ends. Students and temporary workers may discover the route to permanent residence is narrower than expected.

5. Tax surprises
Remote workers often misunderstand tax residency, double taxation, social-security contributions, and home-country obligations.

6. Healthcare access
Users confuse universal healthcare with immediate free access. Waiting times, insurance rules, and registration matter.

7. Social isolation
Language, culture, loneliness, and loss of status can become bigger problems than visas.

8. Family adaptation
Children's schooling, spouse employment, and childcare costs can determine whether a relocation succeeds.

9. Disappointment versus online hype
Reddit discussions often show a gap between country image and daily life. Example themes include: "what looked good on paper did not feel right after visiting", difficulty getting a job after many applications, and legal confusion around visas and self-employment.

5.5 Which sources do people trust when choosing a country?

Trust hierarchy by use case:

1. Official government sources
Best for eligibility, fees, legal requirements, processing steps, status rights, and policy changes. Weakness: hard to understand, fragmented, sometimes poorly translated.

2. Licensed lawyers and tax advisors
Best for interpretation, edge cases, family complexity, business/tax structures. Weakness: expensive and country-specific.

3. People who already moved
Best for lived experience, housing, local bureaucracy, schools, adaptation, scams, and informal tips. Weakness: anecdotal, biased, may be outdated.

4. Reddit/Telegram/Facebook groups
Best for fast answers and real pain points. Weakness: conflicting advice, old information, anonymous confidence.

5. YouTube/bloggers
Best for discovery and emotional confidence. Weakness: incentive to exaggerate, simplify, or sell services.

6. SEO visa websites
Best for overview. Weakness: often outdated, generic, and conversion-oriented.

5.6 Where do people most often get outdated or contradictory information?

High-risk sources:
- Old Reddit threads.
- Telegram messages copied from other chats.
- Facebook posts with no date or source.
- YouTube videos after a law change.
- Blog posts written before visa reforms.
- SEO pages that do not update legal thresholds.
- Influencer content based on personal experience, not current law.
- Visa agents selling one route to everyone.
- Automated translations of legal pages.

Common contradiction types:
- Income threshold changed.
- Dependant rights changed.
- Processing time changed.
- Real-estate golden visa closed.
- Physical-stay requirement changed.
- Tax interpretation is wrong.
- Remote work is allowed for a foreign employer but not local clients.
- Visitor visa allows staying but not working.

Platform opportunity:
Build a "source freshness" and "law verified" layer. Every answer should show:
- Source type.
- Date checked.
- Last law change.
- Confidence level.
- Whether a licensed professional should verify.

5.7 Social data that a platform should collect

Community monitoring dataset:
- Platform: Reddit, Telegram, Facebook, YouTube, TikTok, X, forums.
- Country mentioned.
- Origin country.
- User segment: student, worker, family, remote worker, founder, investor, refugee, retiree.
- Migration stage: dreaming, comparing, preparing, applying, moved, failed/returned.
- Topic: visa, tax, housing, work, school, safety, healthcare, banking, adaptation.
- Sentiment: positive, neutral, negative, anxious, urgent.
- Pain point.
- Source reliability.
- Date.

Useful metrics:
- Country mention growth.
- Visa-route mention growth.
- Negative-post share.
- Bureaucracy complaint share.
- Housing complaint share.
- Law-change confusion rate.
- Frequently repeated questions.
- Emerging destination signals.
