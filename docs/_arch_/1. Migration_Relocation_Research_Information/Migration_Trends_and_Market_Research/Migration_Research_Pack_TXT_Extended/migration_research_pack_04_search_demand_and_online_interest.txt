4. SEARCH DEMAND AND ONLINE INTEREST

4.1 Is the number of searches about moving abroad increasing?

Public evidence suggests that online interest in specific relocation categories has grown strongly, especially digital nomad visas, remote work abroad, second passports, tax residency, and country-comparison queries. However, a precise global answer requires exporting Google Trends data for a fixed keyword set by country and language.

Strong public proxy:
A 2024 search-analysis article reported that global searches for "digital nomad visa" reached an all-time high and grew by more than 1,100% relative to earlier benchmark periods. Italy's new digital nomad visa launch reportedly produced a very large search spike. This is not an official Google report, but it is useful as market-proxy evidence.

Recommended measurement:
Create keyword clusters and track them monthly in Google Trends:
- Generic move abroad: "move abroad", "move overseas", "immigrate to", "emigrate to", "best countries to live".
- Visa/status: "residence permit", "permanent residence", "citizenship", "second passport", "citizenship by descent".
- Remote work: "digital nomad visa", "remote work visa", "freelance visa".
- Tax: "low tax countries", "tax residency", "non dom", "territorial tax".
- Family: "best country for families", "schools in [country]", "healthcare in [country]".
- Safety: "safest countries", "crime in [country]", "is [country] safe".
- Cost: "cost of living in [country]", "rent in [city]".

4.2 Which migration-related searches appear to be growing fastest?

Fast-growing query clusters:
1. Digital nomad visa / remote work visa.
2. Residence permit.
3. Fast residency.
4. Easy citizenship.
5. Citizenship by descent / ancestry citizenship.
6. Second passport.
7. Low-tax countries / tax residency.
8. Golden visa, although some European programmes are closing or restricted.
9. Startup visa / founder visa.
10. Safe countries to move to.
11. Best countries for families.
12. Cheapest countries to live.
13. Move abroad after election / political crisis queries.

The fastest-growing search topics are not always the largest migration flows. Digital nomad searches may grow much faster than classic family migration, while actual family migration remains much larger.

Platform implication:
Fear-based searches are excellent entry points for a risk report. Users often do not need inspiration at that stage; they need de-risking.

4.7 Is interest in digital nomad visas growing?

Answer: Yes, clearly in online visibility and policy supply.

Evidence:
- Public search-analysis sources report very large increases in "digital nomad visa" search interest.
- Multiple 2025-2026 market guides report that more than 50 countries now offer digital nomad or remote-worker-style programmes; some lists count 60-70+ depending on definition.
- Newer or more visible destinations include Italy, Japan, South Korea, New Zealand's remote-work visitor rule, Spain, Portugal, Greece, UAE, Costa Rica, Panama, Brazil, Ecuador, Thailand, and others.

Important nuance:
Digital nomad migration is highly visible online but not necessarily the largest by volume. It is a valuable platform segment because users are digital, search-heavy, comparison-driven, and often willing to pay for tax/visa clarity.

4.8 Is interest rising in fast residence, permanent residence, and citizenship?

Answer: Yes, especially among users from politically unstable countries, weak-passport countries, entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, and families.

High-interest search clusters:
- Fast residency.
- Easy residency.
- Permanent residence in [country].
- Citizenship by descent.
- Second passport.
- Dual citizenship.
- Naturalisation timeline.
- Golden visa.
- Investment visa.
- Startup visa.
- Digital nomad visa to permanent residence.

Market reality:
Fast does not always mean stable. Some fast routes are politically fragile. A platform should score each route by:
- Time to temporary residence.
- Time to permanent residence.
- Time to citizenship.
- Legal stability.
- Family inclusion.
- Physical-stay requirement.
- Tax implications.
- Rejection risk.
- Policy-change risk.
