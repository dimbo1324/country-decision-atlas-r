2. INTEREST IN MOVING ABROAD

2.1 Is the number of people who want to move abroad rising?

Answer: Yes, compared with the 2010s, global desire to migrate is high.

Gallup reported that in 2023, 16% of adults worldwide said they would like to move permanently to another country if they could. Gallup translated this into more than 900 million adults. The 16% level was unchanged from 2021 and 2022, but higher than the 12-15% range Gallup observed from 2011 to 2018.

Important distinction:
A desire to migrate is not the same as an actual plan, visa application, or move. The migration funnel usually looks like this:
1. General dissatisfaction or curiosity.
2. Search behaviour: "move to Canada", "digital nomad visa", "best country to live".
3. Community participation: Reddit, Telegram, Facebook, YouTube, expat forums.
4. Eligibility checking: visa, work, money, documents, family.
5. Active preparation: documents, language tests, job applications, lawyer consultation.
6. Application.
7. Actual move.

A platform should measure all seven layers separately.

2.2 In which countries do the most people want to emigrate?

Public global sources often provide regional rather than fully open country-level rankings. Gallup's public Potential Net Migration Index shows the strongest net outflow pressure in:
- Sub-Saharan Africa.
- Latin America and the Caribbean.
- South Asia.
- Former Soviet states.
- Parts of the Middle East and North Africa.

High emigration desire is especially common where several factors overlap:
- Low salaries and weak job markets.
- Political instability or authoritarian drift.
- War, insecurity, or fear of conscription.
- Corruption and weak rule of law.
- Currency/inflation pressure.
- Youth unemployment.
- Low confidence in the future.
- Existing diaspora networks abroad.

Likely high-interest country clusters:
- War/conflict countries: Ukraine, Syria, Sudan, Afghanistan, Yemen, parts of DRC and Myanmar.
- Economic crisis countries: Venezuela, Lebanon, Argentina in crisis periods, Nigeria, Pakistan, Sri Lanka after crisis shocks.
- Authoritarian or politically unstable environments: Russia, Belarus, Iran, parts of Central Asia, and countries under increasing political repression.
- High-youth-unemployment economies: many countries in Sub-Saharan Africa, North Africa, South Asia, and Latin America.

Confidence level: Medium. Exact latest country-by-country percentage rankings often require access to paid survey tables or custom analysis. The regional direction is strong; exact country ordering should be validated with Gallup microdata, Afrobarometer, Arab Barometer, Latinobarometro, Eurobarometer, and national surveys.

2.3 Which destination countries do people most often name as desired places to move?

The most desired destinations are usually countries that combine income, perceived safety, strong passports, education, healthcare, and existing migrant networks.

Most common destination clusters:
1. United States
Still the largest migrant destination in the world and historically the most desired destination in Gallup migration surveys. However, Gallup reported that desire to move permanently to the U.S. reached a new low in recent measurement, which suggests the U.S. remains huge but less universally dominant.

2. Canada
High global brand for immigration, safety, education, healthcare, and permanent-residence pathways. Recent housing and policy tightening have reduced some of the "easy Canada" perception.

3. Western and Northern Europe
Germany, the UK, France, Spain, Netherlands, Sweden, Denmark, Ireland, Switzerland, and Norway are common desired destinations, depending on language, income, social benefits, jobs, and family strategy.

4. Australia and New Zealand
Highly attractive for quality of life, English language, education, safety, and skilled migration, but access is points-based and increasingly competitive.

5. Gulf states and UAE
Attractive for income, tax, safety, business, and lifestyle among professionals and entrepreneurs. Long-term citizenship is generally much less accessible than in classic settlement countries.

6. Southern Europe and Latin America for lifestyle/remote work
Portugal, Spain, Italy, Greece, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Uruguay, Colombia, and Argentina appear often in digital-nomad, family, and lifestyle-migration discussions.

2.4 Which age groups are most interested in migration?

Highest interest groups:
1. Youth and young adults, especially 18-34.
2. Students and recent graduates.
3. Early-career professionals.
4. People before major family commitments.
5. Young families looking for schooling, safety, healthcare, and long-term citizenship.

Lower but still important groups:
1. Mid-career specialists moving for salary, taxes, safety, or children's education.
2. Entrepreneurs moving for business environment, taxation, banking, capital access, and legal certainty.
3. Retirees and lifestyle migrants seeking healthcare, climate, lower cost of living, and safety.

Why youth are usually more mobile:
- Lower switching costs.
- Greater interest in education and early-career opportunities.
- Less property/family lock-in.
- Stronger use of social media and online communities.
- Higher tolerance for uncertainty.

2.5 Which professions and social groups most often consider relocation?

High-interest professional groups:
1. IT and software professionals
Reasons: remote work, global salary arbitrage, startup ecosystems, English-language labour market, skilled visas.

2. Engineers
Reasons: shortage lists, infrastructure, energy, manufacturing, advanced industry, EU Blue Card-type pathways.

3. Healthcare workers
Doctors, nurses, caregivers, and medical specialists are in demand in ageing countries, although credential recognition can be difficult.

4. Entrepreneurs and startup founders
Reasons: company formation, capital access, tax, visas, talent access, payments/banking, rule of law.

5. Students and researchers
Reasons: degree mobility, post-study work, research funding, PhD/postdoc routes.

6. Skilled trades
Construction, welding, mechanics, electricians, logistics, agriculture, and care work appear often in labour-shortage policy, but routes can be restrictive and employer-dependent.

7. High-net-worth individuals and investors
Reasons: second residence/citizenship, tax planning, capital mobility, family security, geopolitical hedging.

8. Politically exposed or vulnerable groups
Journalists, activists, LGBTQ+ people in unsafe countries, people at risk of conscription, and persecuted minorities.

2.6 Is interest rising among highly skilled specialists?

Answer: Structurally yes, but short-term flows can decline when countries tighten rules or labour markets cool.

Evidence:
- OECD reported that permanent labour migration to OECD countries fell by 21% in 2024, but it was still 32% above 2019 and 93% above 2015.
- Many countries are expanding or refining talent routes: EU Blue Card, global talent visas, startup visas, shortage occupation lists, health-worker routes, and points-based systems.
- INSEAD's Global Talent Competitiveness Index shows that countries actively compete to attract, grow, and retain talent.

Interpretation:
High-skilled migration is not simply "open" or "closed". It is becoming more selective. Countries want people who solve labour shortages, pay taxes, create firms, study in strategic fields, or bring capital. At the same time, they are limiting routes perceived as low-control, low-wage, or politically unpopular.

2.7 Is interest rising among entrepreneurs and investors?

Answer: Yes, but the market is bifurcating.

Growth drivers:
- Political uncertainty.
- Tax changes.
- Capital controls and sanctions risk.
- Banking access.
- Family safety and children's education.
- Mobility optionality through residence or citizenship.
- Startup ecosystem access.

Supply changes:
- Startup visas and founder visas remain politically acceptable in many countries because they promise innovation and jobs.
- Passive investment and real-estate golden visas are under pressure in Europe because of housing, inequality, and EU legal/political concerns.
- Citizenship-by-investment is under intense scrutiny, especially in the EU context.

Practical conclusion:
Entrepreneur/investor migration is one of the most monetisable user segments because users have budget and high downside risk. They do not only need "which country is good"; they need tax, corporate, banking, family, legal, and exit-plan analysis.
