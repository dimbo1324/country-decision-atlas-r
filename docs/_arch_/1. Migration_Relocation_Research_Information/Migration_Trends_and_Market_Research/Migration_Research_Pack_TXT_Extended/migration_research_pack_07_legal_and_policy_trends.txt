7. LEGAL AND POLICY TRENDS

7.1 Which countries are simplifying migration rules?

The global pattern is selective simplification. Countries are not opening all routes equally. They simplify routes for users they want: high-skilled workers, founders, investors, remote workers, researchers, and shortage occupations.

Examples:

1. European Union - EU Blue Card
The EU Blue Card framework is designed to attract highly qualified non-EU workers. Recent implementation has improved conditions in several countries, including more flexible mobility and family-related rules.

2. New Zealand - remote work on visitor visas
New Zealand announced that visitor visas applied for on or after 27 January 2025 allow remote work for foreign employers/clients while the visitor is in New Zealand. Local work for New Zealand employers remains restricted.

3. Digital nomad and remote-worker programmes
More than 50 countries now offer digital-nomad or remote-worker-style visas, depending on definition. Examples include Spain, Portugal, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Estonia, UAE, Costa Rica, Panama, Brazil, Ecuador, Japan, South Korea, Thailand, and others.

4. Startup and founder visas
Many countries continue to compete for founders through startup visas, innovation visas, entrepreneur visas, and special talent routes. These routes are politically easier to defend because they promise job creation, innovation, and investment.

5. Gulf states and UAE
The UAE and some Gulf economies are increasingly attractive to entrepreneurs, professionals, and wealthy families because of tax, safety, infrastructure, and residence options. However, long-term citizenship access remains limited compared with traditional immigrant-settlement countries.

7.2 Which countries are tightening migration rules?

1. Canada
Canada reduced permanent-resident admission targets and temporary-resident arrival targets in the 2025-2027 and 2026-2028 planning cycles. The government framed the change as restoring balance and controlling population growth after rapid post-pandemic increases.

2. United Kingdom
The UK tightened several legal migration routes in 2024-2025, including salary thresholds, student dependant rules, care-worker routes, and family-related requirements. UK net migration fell sharply after the peak.

3. Australia
Australia tightened student and graduate-visa settings, including English-language requirements and other migration-strategy reforms. Australian Bureau of Statistics data showed net overseas migration down in 2024-25 compared with the previous year.

4. Europe asylum and family policy
Several European countries have moved toward tighter asylum procedures, faster returns, more restrictive family rules, or stricter citizenship/residence conditions amid political pressure and housing constraints.

5. International student routes
Canada, the UK, and Australia reduced or restricted student flows, dependants, or post-study options. OECD data shows a sharp 2024 decline in international tertiary student permits in the largest OECD destination countries.

7.3 Which countries are closing or restricting golden visa / citizenship-by-investment programmes?

Clear trend: Europe is becoming less friendly to passive investment migration, especially real-estate golden visas and citizenship-by-investment.

Examples:
- Malta: the Court of Justice of the European Union ruled Malta's golden-passport citizenship-by-investment scheme incompatible with EU law.
- Vanuatu: the EU revoked visa-free travel because of concerns over Vanuatu's golden passport programme.
- Spain: Spain closed its real-estate golden visa route in 2025.
- Ireland: Ireland closed its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023.
- United Kingdom: the Tier 1 Investor visa was closed in 2022.
- Netherlands: the Dutch investor visa route was closed.
- Portugal: Portugal removed real estate from its golden visa qualifying investments, although other investment routes remained.
- Greece: Greece increased investment thresholds for parts of its golden visa programme.

Interpretation:
Investment migration is not disappearing. It is moving away from simple passive real-estate purchase and toward more regulated, higher-threshold, or contribution-based models.

7.4 Which countries are becoming more attractive for remote workers?

Attractive remote-worker clusters:

Southern Europe:
- Portugal, Spain, Italy, Greece, Croatia.

Central/Northern Europe niches:
- Estonia and other digitally advanced EU countries.

Americas:
- Costa Rica, Panama, Mexico, Brazil, Ecuador, Colombia, Uruguay.

Asia-Pacific:
- Thailand, Japan, South Korea, New Zealand, Malaysia.

Middle East:
- UAE/Dubai.

Selection criteria for remote workers:
- Legal right to work remotely.
- Tax treatment.
- Minimum income threshold.
- Family inclusion.
- Length of stay.
- Path to permanent residence.
- Internet quality.
- Housing affordability.
- Safety.
- Time-zone fit.
- Community.

7.5 Which countries are becoming less friendly to migrants?

Less friendly does not always mean closed. It often means more selective.

Countries/routes under pressure:
- Canada: temporary residents and student routes.
- UK: students, dependants, care routes, salary thresholds, settlement rules.
- Australia: student and graduate routes.
- Several EU countries: asylum, family reunification, and low-wage migration.
- Southern Europe: housing pressure and political backlash against golden visas, short-term rentals, and over-tourism.
- Countries with rising anti-immigration rhetoric: policy risk increases even before laws change.

A platform should separate:
1. Policy toward high-skilled migrants.
2. Policy toward students.
3. Policy toward families.
4. Policy toward asylum seekers.
5. Policy toward investors.
6. Policy toward remote workers.
7. Social friendliness and discrimination risk.

7.6 Which legal changes most affect country choice?

Most important legal variables:
1. Visa eligibility.
2. Income or salary thresholds.
3. Proof-of-funds requirements.
4. Employer sponsorship rules.
5. Dependant rights.
6. Spouse work rights.
7. Time to permanent residence.
8. Time to citizenship.
9. Physical-stay requirements.
10. Tax residency rules.
11. Double-tax treaties.
12. Social-security obligations.
13. Housing and rental laws.
14. Banking/KYC rules.
15. School access and fees.
16. Healthcare eligibility.
17. Recognition of qualifications.
18. Renewal rules.
19. Rejection/appeal rights.
20. Deportation/overstay consequences.

7.7 Can we determine a country's direction from its laws?

Yes, partially. Laws reveal direction earlier than reputation changes.

Build a Country Direction Index with these dimensions:

Immigration openness:
- Number of new visa routes.
- Expansion vs restriction of eligibility.
- Family inclusion.
- Processing times.
- Rejection rates.
- Renewal stability.
- Path to permanent residence/citizenship.

Legal stability:
- Frequency of changes.
- Retroactive changes.
- Court independence.
- Appeal rights.
- Clarity of official guidance.

Political direction:
- Election outcomes.
- Anti-immigration rhetoric.
- Civil liberties.
- Media freedom.
- Treatment of minorities.
- Protest/repression indicators.

Business direction:
- Company formation ease.
- Tax predictability.
- Banking access.
- Capital controls.
- Labour law.
- Startup policy.

Family/lifestyle direction:
- Housing affordability and regulation.
- Healthcare access.
- Education access.
- Safety.
- Climate resilience.

Score types:
- Open vs closed.
- Stable vs volatile.
- Pro-business vs anti-business.
- Family-friendly vs individual-only.
- Talent-attracting vs talent-losing.
- Remote-work friendly vs remote-work risky.
