8.2 Which countries are losing attractiveness?

A country can lose attractiveness for one segment while gaining for another.

Common causes of declining attractiveness:
- Housing crisis and high rents.
- More difficult visa rules.
- Longer processing times.
- Higher salary thresholds.
- Tax benefits reduced or ended.
- More bureaucracy.
- Political instability.
- Anti-immigration rhetoric.
- Weak job market.
- Low salaries relative to rent.
- Banking difficulties.
- Safety concerns.
- Overcrowding and local backlash.

Examples by issue:
- Canada: still attractive, but less attractive for some students and temporary residents because of caps, housing pressure, and policy tightening.
- United Kingdom: still strong for education and high-skilled work, but less attractive for lower-wage, care-sector, family, and settlement-sensitive migrants because of tightening.
- Portugal: still attractive, but its “easy golden visa / easy tax paradise” image is outdated after golden visa and tax-benefit reforms.
- Spain: still attractive for lifestyle and remote workers, but the golden visa path ended in 2025 and housing affordability is politically sensitive.
- Netherlands: attractive labour market and quality of life, but housing scarcity, bureaucracy, and investor-route closure reduce appeal for some users.
- Traditional nomad hotspots: can become less attractive after rent spikes, overtourism, or tax/legal enforcement.

8.3 Which countries are becoming new popular destinations?

Emerging or increasingly discussed destinations:
- UAE/Dubai: tax, safety, business, global connectivity.
- Spain and Italy: lifestyle plus digital-nomad/remote-worker routes.
- Greece: residence, lifestyle, tax regimes, and investor-route interest.
- Uruguay: stability, safety reputation, residence interest in Latin America.
- Paraguay: affordability and residence/citizenship interest, though quality and legal reality vary.
- Panama and Costa Rica: retiree, tax, and lifestyle interest.
- Colombia and Argentina: affordability and lifestyle interest, but safety/economic risks vary.
- Georgia and Albania: affordability, entry flexibility, and remote-worker interest.
- Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia/Bali: lifestyle and remote-work pull, but legal/tax details matter.
- New Zealand: investor and skilled routes, political safety appeal, and remote-worker visitor changes.
- Japan and South Korea: increasing interest for remote workers and skilled professionals, though language and work culture can be barriers.

8.4 Which countries are overrated or underrated by migrants?

Overrated often means online hype is stronger than practical fit.

Often overrated for some users:
- Portugal: excellent for lifestyle, but local salaries, housing, bureaucracy, and changing tax/visa rules are underestimated.
- Canada: strong long-term destination, but housing cost, credential recognition, healthcare waits, and climate are often underestimated.
- Dubai/UAE: excellent for tax/business/safety for many users, but naturalisation path, schooling costs, healthcare costs, and heat are often underestimated.
- Bali/Indonesia: strong lifestyle brand, but legal work rights, tax, healthcare, infrastructure, and long-term residence can be complex.
- Spain: strong lifestyle, but labour market, bureaucracy, taxes, and housing are often underestimated.

Potentially underrated:
- Uruguay: stable and relatively safe by regional standards, but small market and higher costs than some expect.
- Paraguay: low cost and residence interest, but institutions, infrastructure, and service quality require careful evaluation.
- Georgia: easy entry and low costs for some users, but small job market and geopolitical risk.
- Estonia: strong digital state and startup ecosystem, but climate, size, and language can be barriers.
- Poland and Czechia: improving labour markets and EU access, but language and bureaucracy matter.
- Malaysia: strong cost/quality balance for some retirees and remote workers, but legal route fit varies.

8.6 Which countries do entrepreneurs and startup founders choose?

Common founder-friendly destinations:
- United States: scale, capital, customers, talent, but difficult immigration and high cost.
- Canada: startup visa, talent, quality of life, but processing and housing issues.
- United Kingdom: finance, language, startup ecosystem, but high cost and policy tightening.
- France: strong startup policy and public support, especially Paris.
- Ireland: English-speaking EU base, tech and tax ecosystem.
- Netherlands: strong business environment, English use, but housing and bureaucracy.
- Estonia: e-residency, digital state, startup culture.
- Portugal and Spain: lifestyle plus startup/nomad interest, but local market/salary limits.
- Germany: large market and talent, but bureaucracy and language.
- Switzerland: stability, capital, high quality, high cost.
- Singapore: Asia hub, tax/business strength, high cost.
- UAE: tax, speed, international connectivity, free zones, and safety.

8.7 Which countries do people choose for a second passport?

Routes differ by user profile.

Citizenship by descent/ancestry:
- Italy.
- Ireland.
- Poland.
- Portugal for Sephardic-descendant route historically, though rules changed.
- Spain for some historical/ancestry categories.
- Hungary, Romania, Lithuania, Latvia, Greece, and other European countries depending on documented ancestry.

Naturalisation after residence:
- Portugal, Spain, Italy, France, Germany, Netherlands, Belgium, Sweden, Finland, Austria, Ireland, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, UK, Uruguay, Paraguay, Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, and many others. Timelines and requirements vary widely.

Citizenship by investment:
- Malta is one of the most scrutinised European options.
- Caribbean programmes such as St Kitts and Nevis, Antigua and Barbuda, Dominica, Grenada, and St Lucia are prominent.
- Türkiye has a citizenship-by-investment route.
- Vanuatu faced EU visa-free revocation concerns.

Important product rule:
Never rank second-passport countries by timeline alone. Include physical presence, language, tax residency, dual-citizenship permission, proof requirements, government fees, lawyer fees, geopolitical risk, EU/Schengen impact, and policy-change risk.

Conclusion
Countries are no longer competing for “migrants” in general. They compete for very specific profiles: tech workers, nurses, founders, students, wealthy families, retirees, remote workers, and crisis-driven migrants. A platform should personalise country attractiveness by segment rather than publishing one universal ranking.
