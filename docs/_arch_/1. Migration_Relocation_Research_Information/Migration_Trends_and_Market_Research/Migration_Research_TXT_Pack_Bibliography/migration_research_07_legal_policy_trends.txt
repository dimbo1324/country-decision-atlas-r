7. LEGISLATIVE AND POLITICAL TRENDS
Research date: 16 June 2026

7.1 Which countries are simplifying migration rules?

Simplification is usually targeted, not universal. Countries open routes for desired migrants while tightening other channels.

Examples:

Germany
- Introduced the Opportunity Card in 2024 under the Skilled Immigration Act.
- Allows qualified third-country nationals to enter Germany to look for work under a points/qualification framework.
- Goal: reduce skilled labour shortages.

New Zealand
- Loosened parts of its Active Investor Plus visa in 2025, including investment thresholds and presence requirements.
- Announced new skilled residence pathways from mid-2026 for some skilled roles and trades/technicians.
- Changed visitor rules to attract remote workers/digital nomads.

UAE / Dubai
- Continues to attract entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, and high earners with tax, business, safety, and residence options.

Spain, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Estonia, Malta, Portugal and others
- Added or promoted digital nomad, remote-worker, startup, or talent channels, though details and tax consequences vary.

Canada and Australia
- Still prioritise skilled migration, but are tightening temporary-resident/student volumes and some entry channels.

7.2 Which countries are tightening migration rules?

Examples:

Canada
- The 2025-2027 plan reduced permanent-resident targets from earlier levels and aimed to reduce temporary residents as a share of population.
- Policy focus shifted toward lowering pressure on housing, services, and infrastructure while preserving economic migration.

United Kingdom
- 2025 policy direction proposed or implemented tougher rules for settlement, family migration, care-sector recruitment, skill thresholds, and English-language requirements.
- The UK is trying to reduce net migration while retaining high-skilled routes.

Australia
- Tightened student and temporary graduate visa requirements, including English-language requirements and broader migration-strategy reforms.

Netherlands
- Abolished its foreign-investor residence scheme in 2024.
- Also has wider political pressure around asylum, housing, and migration management.

Portugal
- Moved toward tougher rules on family reunification, job-seeker routes, CPLP-related residence, and citizenship timelines according to 2025-2026 reform reporting. Users should verify current law because reforms can be challenged, amended, or delayed.

EU and Schengen area
- Increased scrutiny of asylum, border management, golden passports, security screening, and visa-free access linked to citizenship-by-investment programmes.

7.3 Which countries are closing or restricting golden visa / citizenship-by-investment programmes?

Examples:

Spain
- Spain formally ended its golden visa programme in April 2025, after criticism that real-estate investment migration worsened housing affordability and provided limited economic benefit.

Netherlands
- The Netherlands abolished its foreign-investor residence scheme effective 17 April 2024. The government said the scheme had not been popular.

Portugal
- Portugal restricted real-estate investment as a golden visa path before 2025 and shifted the programme away from simple property investment.

Ireland
- Ireland closed its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023, before this research period but still important for trend context.

Vanuatu
- The EU revoked Vanuatu's visa-free travel access because of concerns over its citizenship-by-investment scheme, due diligence, security, and money-laundering risk.

Trend:
Investment migration is not ending, but it is becoming more regulated, less real-estate-driven, and more scrutinised by the EU, OECD, and national governments.

7.4 Which countries are becoming more attractive for remote workers?

Remote-worker attractiveness depends on visa access, tax treatment, cost, internet, safety, healthcare, rental supply, time zone, banking, and community.

Commonly attractive destinations:
- Portugal.
- Spain.
- Italy.
- Greece.
- Croatia.
- Estonia.
- Malta.
- UAE/Dubai.
- Mexico.
- Costa Rica.
- Panama.
- Colombia.
- Brazil.
- Argentina.
- Uruguay.
- Thailand.
- Indonesia/Bali.
- Malaysia.
- Japan and South Korea for specific remote-worker/tourism-linked policies.
- Georgia.
- Albania.

Caution:
Digital nomad visas are often marketed as simple, but tax residency, health insurance, employment law, permanent-residence eligibility, and local registration may be complicated.

7.5 Which countries are becoming less migrant-friendly?

A country may be less friendly to some categories while still inviting others. “Less friendly” should be measured by segment.

Signals of lower friendliness:
- Higher salary thresholds.
- Reduced student caps.
- Longer citizenship or permanent-residence timelines.
- Higher family reunification requirements.
- Asylum restrictions.
- More deportations or faster removals.
- Strong anti-immigration political rhetoric.
- Local housing backlash against foreigners or nomads.
- Banking restrictions for certain nationalities.
- Stricter physical-presence requirements.
- Reduced visa-free access or security screening.

Examples of tightening direction:
- UK: settlement and labour/family migration tightening.
- Canada: temporary-resident and student-volume tightening.
- Australia: student/graduate visa tightening.
- Spain: golden visa closure.
- Netherlands: investor route closure and political pressure.
- Parts of the EU: asylum and border tightening, while skilled migration remains open.

7.6 Which legal changes most affect country choice?

Highest-impact legal changes:
- Visa eligibility and salary thresholds.
- Right to work and employer sponsorship.
- Student-to-work transition rules.
- Family reunification rules.
- Permanent residence timeline.
- Citizenship timeline and dual-citizenship rules.
- Physical-presence requirements.
- Tax residency and special tax regimes.
- Wealth, exit, inheritance, and capital-gains taxes.
- Banking/KYC rules.
- Housing purchase/rental restrictions for foreigners.
- Recognition of qualifications.
- Healthcare access.
- School access and language rules.
- Military/conscription laws.
- Sanctions and nationality restrictions.

7.7 Can we determine a country's direction from its laws?

Answer: Yes, partially. A “country direction index” can be built, but it must separate categories.

Useful direction dimensions:

Migration openness
- More visas or fewer visas?
- Easier or harder renewals?
- Shorter or longer permanent-residence/citizenship path?
- More or fewer family rights?
- Higher or lower salary thresholds?

Rule-of-law direction
- Judicial independence.
- Predictability of administration.
- Appeal rights.
- Corruption control.
- Arbitrary enforcement risk.

Political direction
- Liberalising or authoritarian drift.
- Civil liberties.
- Media freedom.
- Anti-foreigner rhetoric.
- National-security restrictions.

Business direction
- Company formation ease.
- Tax stability.
- Banking access.
- Capital controls.
- Labour regulation.

Lifestyle direction
- Housing policy.
- Healthcare capacity.
- Education access.
- Infrastructure.
- Safety.
- Climate resilience.

Important warning:
A country can be “open” for highly skilled migrants but “closed” for asylum seekers, low-wage workers, students, or family members. The platform should score policy direction by user segment.
