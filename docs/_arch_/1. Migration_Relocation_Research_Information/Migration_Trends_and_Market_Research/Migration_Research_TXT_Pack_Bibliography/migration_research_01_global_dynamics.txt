1. GLOBAL MIGRATION DYNAMICS
Research date: 16 June 2026

1.1 Is the total number of international migrants growing or declining?

Answer: Growing in the long term.

UN DESA estimates that the world had about 304 million international migrants in 2024. This is nearly double the 154 million estimated for 1990. The international migrant share of world population also increased, from about 2.9% in 1990 to about 3.7% in 2024.

Approximate global stock trend:
- 1990: 154 million international migrants.
- 2000: about 173 million.
- 2010: about 221 million.
- 2020: about 275 million.
- 2024: 304 million.

Interpretation:
The long-term trend is upward. The increase is not linear; it is shaped by demographic gaps, income gaps, labour demand, conflict, forced displacement, education mobility, family reunification, and regional crises.

1.2 Which regions show the strongest growth?

By absolute migrant stock, Europe, Northern America, and Northern Africa/Western Asia are the largest receiving regions.

UN DESA 2024 regional host stock:
- Europe: about 94 million international migrants.
- Northern America: about 61 million.
- Northern Africa and Western Asia: about 54 million.

Growth pattern:
- Europe recorded the largest absolute increase from 1990 to 2024, adding about 43 million migrants. Since 2020 the pace increased because of forced displacement from Ukraine.
- Northern America added about 34 million from 1990 to 2024, but growth slowed in 2020-2024.
- Northern Africa and Western Asia is structurally important because of Gulf labour migration, Syrian displacement, Afghan displacement, Sudan-related displacement, and regional corridors from South Asia and North Africa.
- Latin America and the Caribbean shifted from being mainly an origin region to also being a major receiving and transit region, especially because of Venezuelan migration and regional labour mobility.
- Asia contains both major origin countries and major destination hubs, especially the Gulf, Singapore, Malaysia, Japan, Korea, and high-income Asian economies.
- Africa shows high migration desire and significant intra-African mobility, but much African migration remains regional rather than intercontinental.

1.3 From which countries do people most often leave?

Top origin countries by emigrant stock in 2024, based on Migration Policy Institute analysis of UN DESA data:
- India: about 18.5 million emigrants.
- China: about 11.7 million.
- Mexico: about 11.6 million.
- Ukraine: about 9.8 million.
- Russia: about 9.1 million.

Other major origin countries commonly include Bangladesh, Pakistan, Syria, Afghanistan, Philippines, Venezuela, and several high-population countries with strong labour-export, conflict, or diaspora dynamics.

Interpretation:
Large origin numbers can reflect population size, labour-export systems, conflict, historical diaspora networks, geographic proximity, or economic inequality. A large origin stock does not always mean recent crisis; it may also reflect decades of cumulative migration.

1.4 To which countries do people most often move?

Top destination countries by international migrant stock in 2024, according to UN DESA:
- United States: 52.4 million.
- Germany: 16.8 million.
- Saudi Arabia: 13.7 million.
- United Kingdom: 11.8 million.
- France: 9.2 million.
- Spain: 8.9 million.
- Canada: 8.8 million.
- United Arab Emirates: 8.2 million.
- Australia: 8.1 million.
- Russia: 7.6 million.
- Türkiye: 7.1 million.
- Italy: 6.6 million.

Interpretation:
Destination-country attractiveness has different causes: labour markets, language, wages, family networks, education, asylum protection, historical ties, geographic proximity, tax regimes, and immigration rules.

1.5 Which migration routes became more popular in the last five years?

The strongest emerging or growing route categories are:

A. Ukraine to Europe
Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine triggered one of the largest forced-displacement movements in Europe since World War II. This increased the migrant stock in many European countries after 2022.

B. Venezuela to Latin America and North America
Venezuelan displacement and economic migration reshaped migration across Colombia, Peru, Ecuador, Chile, Brazil, the United States, Spain, and other destinations.

C. South Asia to the Gulf and OECD countries
India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, and Sri Lanka remain major labour-origin countries for the Gulf. India is also a major source of students, skilled workers, and tech migration to the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, and Europe.

D. India to the United Kingdom, Canada, United States, Australia, and Germany
India became especially important in student and skilled migration channels.

E. Remote-worker and digital-nomad routes
Portugal, Spain, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Estonia, Malta, Dubai/UAE, Thailand, Indonesia/Bali, Mexico, Colombia, Brazil, Costa Rica, Panama, Georgia, and Albania appear frequently in remote-worker and nomad discussions.

F. US outbound interest after political shocks
After the 2024 US election, online searches for moving to Canada, New Zealand, and Australia spiked sharply, indicating a strong intent signal even if only a fraction converts into actual migration.

G. Wealth and investor migration to Europe, the Gulf, New Zealand, and alternative citizenship/residence destinations
High-net-worth users increasingly diversify residence and citizenship options for mobility, tax, healthcare, political risk, and family planning.

1.6 Which routes or destinations are losing popularity?

This depends on the metric.

Likely declining or cooling areas:
- Canada for some student and temporary-resident segments, due to caps, housing pressure, and policy tightening.
- United Kingdom for some lower-wage and family-migration segments, due to higher salary thresholds, care-sector restrictions, and tougher settlement proposals.
- Spain's real-estate-based golden visa route, because Spain ended the scheme in 2025.
- Netherlands foreign-investor route, because the scheme was abolished in 2024.
- Portugal golden visa via real estate had already been restricted before 2025; Portugal remains attractive, but its easy-residency reputation is less accurate than in the past.
- Traditional digital nomad hotspots can lose attractiveness when rents rise, visa rules tighten, local backlash grows, or tax compliance becomes clearer.
- Jobseeker interest in foreign remote roles declined in some 2024-2025 labour-market data, likely because of return-to-office policies, compliance costs, and tighter hiring.

1.7 Which migration type is growing fastest?

There is no single answer; it depends on absolute volume versus relative growth.

By relative growth from a small base:
- Digital nomad and remote-worker migration is one of the fastest-growing categories. MBO Partners estimated 18.5 million US digital nomads in 2025, up about 153% from 2019. More than 40 jurisdictions offered digital nomad or remote-worker visas by mid-2024, and some trackers identify 60+ schemes by 2025.

By humanitarian urgency:
- Forced displacement grew sharply through the early 2020s. UNHCR estimated 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at the end of 2024. This includes refugees, asylum-seekers, internally displaced people, and others needing protection. Cross-border forced displacement is a major part of international migration growth.

By long-term education trend:
- International student mobility grew strongly. UNESCO reported nearly 7.3 million students studying abroad by 2024, compared with a much smaller base two decades earlier. Migration Data Portal estimated 6.9 million international students in 2022, up 176% over two decades.

By permanent-type OECD inflows:
- Family migration is the largest category. In 2024, family migration represented about 44% of new permanent migrants to OECD countries.
- Labour migration represented about 17% of new permanent migrants to OECD countries in 2024 and declined 21% from 2023, but remained much higher than pre-2015 levels.
- Humanitarian migration represented about 13% of 2024 new permanent OECD inflows and increased strongly.

By policy innovation:
- Talent visas, startup visas, digital nomad visas, and selective skilled-worker routes are growing as countries compete for high-productivity migrants.

Conclusion for platform use:
Segment migration by user intent, not just by legal category. A platform should distinguish: skilled worker, student, family, founder, investor, digital nomad, retiree, humanitarian, tax-residency seeker, passport seeker, and safety-driven mover.
