9. OPPORTUNITIES FOR A MIGRATION DECISION PLATFORM
Research date: 16 June 2026

9.1 What relocation questions cannot be quickly solved with existing platforms?

Unresolved user questions:
- Which country is best for my exact profile, not for the average expat?
- Which visa route is realistic for me, not just available in theory?
- What is the fastest legal path to residence, permanent residence, or citizenship?
- What changed in the law recently?
- Which country is becoming more open or more restrictive?
- What are the hidden tax risks if I move there?
- Can I work remotely legally from that country?
- How much cash do I need for the first 6-12 months?
- How likely is visa rejection or delay for my nationality/profile?
- Which documents should I prepare before leaving my current country?
- Can my spouse work and can my children access school/healthcare?
- Which countries look good online but are bad for my situation?
- Which countries are cheap but legally unstable or risky?
- What are the top mistakes people like me make after moving?
- When should I pay a lawyer, tax adviser, relocation agent, or school consultant?

9.2 Which data is scattered across different websites and can be collected in one place?

High-value scattered data:
- Visa eligibility rules.
- Residence permit requirements.
- Permanent-residence timelines.
- Citizenship timelines and dual-citizenship rules.
- Physical-presence requirements.
- Government fees.
- Lawyer/consultant price ranges.
- Tax residency rules.
- Special tax regimes.
- Social security and healthcare eligibility.
- Work rights for applicants and spouses.
- Labour-market demand and shortage occupations.
- Salary benchmarks.
- Cost of living.
- Rent and housing availability.
- School options and costs.
- Safety/crime data by city.
- Corruption/rule-of-law data.
- Banking access and restrictions.
- Language requirements.
- Document requirements: apostille, translation, police certificate, medicals.
- Processing times and appointment availability.
- Law-change history.
- Proposed bills and policy direction.
- User reviews and verified cases.
- Search trends and social media signals.

9.3 Which functions are missing from existing platforms?

Missing or weak functions:
- Personal country matching based on profile and motives.
- Visa route scoring by probability, cost, and timeline.
- Legal-change tracker with before/after explanations.
- Tax-risk warning system.
- Country direction index.
- Migration interest index.
- Verified user case database.
- “Reality check” against online hype.
- Contradiction resolver for conflicting advice.
- Family-specific decision model.
- Remote-work legality checker.
- Second-passport route checker.
- Document readiness tracker.
- Budget and runway calculator.
- City/neighbourhood comparison, not only country comparison.
- Risk report by nationality.
- Expert handoff marketplace.
- Alerts: “this route may close”, “threshold increased”, “appointments opened”, “tax benefit changed”.

9.4 Can we create an index of interest in a country?

Answer: Yes. Build a Country Interest Index.

Possible components:
- Google Trends for “move to [country]”, “immigrate to [country]”, “digital nomad visa [country]”, “residence permit [country]”, “citizenship [country]”.
- Country comparison searches: “[country] vs [country]”.
- Search velocity after shocks.
- Reddit post/comment frequency by destination.
- Telegram/YouTube/TikTok mentions and growth.
- Immigration lawyer/agency inquiry data if available.
- Official visa application volumes.
- Visa approval/denial volumes.
- International student applications.
- Work-permit applications.
- Flight-search or relocation-shipping interest.
- Long-stay accommodation searches.
- Migration stock and inflow data.
- News volume around migration and policy changes.

Suggested score design:
- 40% search interest.
- 20% official application/inflow data.
- 15% social/community activity.
- 10% media/news momentum.
- 10% law/policy events.
- 5% commercial inquiry data.

Use separate indices by origin country and user segment. Example: “Portugal interest among US remote workers” is different from “Portugal interest among Indian students”.

9.5 Can we create an index of country direction?

Answer: Yes. Build a Country Direction Index.

Dimensions:
- Migration openness: visa rules, thresholds, renewals, family rights, PR/citizenship path.
- Legal stability: frequency of rule changes, retroactivity, court challenges.
- Tax direction: special regimes, wealth/capital taxes, tax-residency enforcement.
- Banking openness: KYC restrictions, account access, sanctions sensitivity.
- Labour-market direction: shortage lists, unemployment, salary thresholds.
- Housing pressure: rent growth, foreign-buyer restrictions, local backlash.
- Political direction: rule of law, civil liberties, anti-immigration rhetoric.
- Business direction: company formation, regulation, startup incentives.
- Social direction: integration, language pressure, discrimination, safety.
- Climate direction: heat, flood, wildfire, water stress, insurance.

Output:
- Opening.
- Stable.
- Selectively opening.
- Selectively tightening.
- Broadly tightening.
- High uncertainty.

9.6 Can we predict rising interest before it becomes obvious?

Answer: Partially, yes.

Early signals:
- Search spikes after elections, wars, mobilisation, tax reforms, currency crises, or banking restrictions.
- New digital nomad, startup, talent, investor, or tax-residency visa launches.
- Sudden growth in YouTube/TikTok videos about a country.
- Rapid growth of Telegram/WhatsApp/Reddit communities.
- Rising Google searches for “visa [country]”, “move to [country]”, “citizenship [country]”.
- Law proposals before implementation.
- Embassy appointment backlogs.
- Rising rent and long-stay bookings in expat areas.
- Increase in international school inquiries.
- Consultant/lawyer inquiry spikes.
- News about tax benefits or golden visa closures.
- Job-board searches for foreign roles.

Prediction model idea:
Use a 3-stage signal model:
1. Awareness stage: search/social/news spike.
2. Feasibility stage: visa/tax/document queries rise.
3. Execution stage: application volumes, relocation bookings, rent demand, school inquiries rise.

9.7 What data helps users decide faster and more confidently?

Minimum decision dataset:
- User nationality and current residence.
- Family status.
- Age.
- Education and profession.
- Income source and amount.
- Savings and relocation budget.
- Remote, employed, self-employed, founder, student, investor, retiree.
- Desired timeline.
- Risk motives: safety, politics, tax, passport, education, healthcare, climate, job.
- Language ability.
- Passport/citizenship goals.
- Tax complexity.
- Health and school needs.
- Preferred regions/climate.

Country dataset:
- Legal route availability.
- Eligibility fit.
- Cost and proof-of-funds.
- Processing time.
- PR/citizenship path.
- Work/spouse rights.
- Tax risk.
- Cost of living and rent.
- Job market or remote-work legality.
- Safety.
- Healthcare.
- Schools.
- Banking.
- Law-change risk.
- User-case evidence.

9.8 What results should users receive after using the platform?

User deliverables:
- Top 3-5 country shortlist.
- “Why these countries” explanation.
- “Why not these countries” exclusions.
- Best legal route for each country.
- Estimated timeline.
- Estimated first-year cost.
- Monthly cost estimate.
- Tax and banking warnings.
- Safety and healthcare summary.
- Family/school fit if relevant.
- Citizenship/passport path.
- Document checklist.
- Risk score.
- Law-change summary.
- Confidence score.
- Next-step checklist.
- Recommended expert type: lawyer, tax adviser, relocation agent, school consultant.

Example output format:
Country A: Best fit, 82/100.
Country B: Good backup, 74/100.
Country C: High potential but high tax/legal risk, 68/100.
Country D: Popular online but poor fit for your profile, 49/100.

9.9 Which information must be evidence-based rather than subjective?

Must be evidence-based:
- Visa eligibility.
- Legal requirements.
- Government fees.
- Processing times if official data exists.
- Work rights.
- Family rights.
- PR/citizenship rules.
- Physical-presence requirements.
- Tax residency and tax regime basics.
- Healthcare eligibility.
- School access.
- Banking restrictions.
- Crime and safety statistics.
- Cost-of-living data sources.
- Rent and housing trends.
- Law changes and effective dates.
- Official application/approval volumes.

Can include subjective user experience but should be labelled:
- Bureaucracy difficulty.
- Friendliness to foreigners.
- Cultural fit.
- Racism/discrimination experiences.
- Neighbourhood feel.
- Dating/social life.
- Work culture.
- Customer service.
- Loneliness/integration.

Best practice:
Every factual claim should have source type and freshness label:
- Official law / government page.
- Official statistics.
- Research organisation.
- Market estimate.
- Verified user case.
- Community anecdote.
- Media report.

9.10 What features would make the platform unique?

Unique feature set:

1. Personal migration decision engine
Instead of “best countries”, produce “best countries for you”.

2. Legal route graph
Show all possible routes from tourist/status A to residence, work, PR, citizenship, and family reunification.

3. Law-change tracker
Track proposed, passed, and effective changes; show before/after comparison.

4. Country Direction Index
Score whether a country is opening, tightening, or becoming unstable by user segment.

5. Country Interest Index
Measure rising demand by search/social/application signals.

6. Reality-check module
Compare online hype with legal, tax, housing, and salary reality.

7. Risk simulation
“What can go wrong?” visa denial, tax surprise, housing problem, school issue, renewal risk.

8. Verified cases
Real user paths filtered by nationality, profession, income, family, and timeline.

9. Contradiction resolver
When official sources, lawyers, blogs, and users disagree, explain why.

10. Personal action checklist
A document and task plan based on route and deadline.

11. Expert handoff marketplace
Send the user to a verified lawyer/tax adviser only after the platform identifies the correct route and risk.

12. Alerts
Notify users when a route becomes easier/harder, when fees change, when a programme closes, or when a new route launches.

13. Family mode
A dedicated scoring system for schools, healthcare, spouse rights, childcare, safety, and citizenship.

14. Founder mode
Company formation, tax, talent hiring, banking, investor access, and visa route in one workflow.

15. Remote-worker legality checker
Separate “can enter”, “can live”, “can work remotely”, “becomes tax resident”, and “can renew”.

4. Visa route match.
