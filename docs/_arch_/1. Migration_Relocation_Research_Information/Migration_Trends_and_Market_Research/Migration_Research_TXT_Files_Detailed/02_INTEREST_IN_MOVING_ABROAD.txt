02. INTEREST IN MOVING ABROAD
Research package date: 2026-06-16
Language: English
Scope: international migration, relocation demand, digital nomads, skilled migration, investment migration, policy trends, and product opportunity for a migration decision platform.

Important interpretation rules:
- "International migrant stock" is not the same thing as annual migration flow. Stock counts people residing outside their country of birth or citizenship at a point in time; flows measure entries/exits during a period.
- Forced displacement counts and international migrant stock overlap only partly, because some displaced people remain internally displaced inside their own country.
- Online search interest is often measured on relative scales. Exact Google Trends exports were not available in this environment, so this package uses published Google Trends analyses where available and recommends a measurement design for direct collection.
- Social-media community counts are incomplete because Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Discord groups often hide membership/activity or are private.
- Commercial market-size estimates are directional; exact market sizing for relocation, immigration law, and visa-tech varies by vendor definition.


1. Is the number of people who want to move abroad rising?

Answer: globally, the desire to migrate rose over the 2010s and reached historically high levels around 2021-2023, then softened in some regions by 2025.

Gallup’s global data are the strongest repeated evidence:
- Around 16% of adults worldwide said in 2023 that they would like to move permanently to another country if they had the opportunity.
- Gallup estimated this represented more than 900 million adults globally.
- The comparable range in earlier years was lower, roughly 12-15% in many 2011-2018 periods.
- By 2025, Gallup reported some regional declines in desire to migrate, especially in Latin America and the Caribbean and Sub-Saharan Africa, although levels remained high.

Interpretation:
- Interest is structurally high, but not a straight upward line every year.
- Pandemic reopening, conflict, inflation, housing stress, political instability, and remote work all changed relocation thinking.
- Desire to migrate does not equal ability to migrate. Many people want to move but lack money, legal pathways, language skills, job offers, documents, or family support.

2. In which countries or regions do the most people want to emigrate?

Gallup reports the highest regional desire-to-migrate levels in:
- Sub-Saharan Africa: about one-third of adults in recent polling periods, though down from a 2023 high.
- Latin America and the Caribbean: about one-quarter of adults in recent polling periods, down from a 2021-2022 peak.
- Parts of the Middle East and North Africa: often high because of conflict, unemployment, governance issues, and economic instability.
- Some small island, conflict-affected, or economically constrained states can have very high potential emigration rates.

Typical high-emigration-interest contexts:
- High youth unemployment.
- Currency devaluation and inflation.
- Political repression or fear of future instability.
- Weak passport mobility.
- Corruption or lack of rule of law.
- Security risk or conflict.
- Lack of professional growth opportunities.

3. Which countries are most often named as desired destinations?

Gallup’s preferred-destination data:
- The United States remains the most desired destination globally among people who want to migrate, but its share has fallen.
- In 2025, 15% of adults worldwide who wanted to move named the United States as their preferred destination.
- This is down from about 24% in 2007-2009.
- Canada rose to about 9%.
- Germany, Australia, Spain, France, Japan, Italy, and Saudi Arabia appear in the top destination set with smaller but stable shares.
- The UK declined to about 4% in Gallup’s recent data.

Interpretation:
- The U.S. is still the largest dream destination, but its relative brand advantage has weakened.
- Canada is attractive because of official immigration pathways, safety image, high quality of life, English/French language opportunity, and perceived openness.
- Southern Europe gained lifestyle appeal, but housing and tax complexity reduce practical attractiveness.
- Gulf countries remain attractive for income and tax reasons, especially for labour migrants and entrepreneurs, but citizenship pathways are limited.

4. Which age groups are most interested in migration?

Strong pattern:
- Younger people are more migration-oriented than older adults.
- Gallup/IOM research shows that age and education are strongly associated with migration desire.
- People with at least some secondary education are generally more likely to say they want to migrate.
- Young adults are often more mobile because they have fewer fixed assets, no children or younger children, more career flexibility, and higher expected lifetime return from moving.

Age-group interpretation:
- 18-34: highest interest; students, early-career specialists, digital nomads, asylum seekers, and job-seekers.
- 35-49: strong but more practical; family, schooling, taxes, housing, healthcare, and path to permanent residence matter.
- 50-64: selective interest; lifestyle, safety, healthcare, retirement planning, second residence, tax residence.
- 65+: lower migration rate, but meaningful in retirement/lifestyle migration, family reunification, and healthcare-motivated relocation.

5. Which professions and social groups most often consider moving?

High-interest groups:
- IT specialists and software engineers.
- Engineers and technical specialists.
- Doctors, nurses, and healthcare workers.
- Scientists, researchers, and academics.
- Entrepreneurs and startup founders.
- Students and recent graduates.
- Skilled trades workers in shortage occupations.
- Remote workers and freelancers.
- High-net-worth individuals and investors.
- Politically exposed or politically concerned professionals.
- Families with children seeking safety, education, and long-term citizenship options.

Why these groups are attractive to destination countries:
- They can fill labour shortages.
- They generate tax revenue.
- They raise productivity and innovation.
- They integrate more easily if skilled, educated, and language-capable.
- Entrepreneurs can create companies and jobs.
- Students can become long-term skilled migrants.

6. Is interest among highly skilled specialists increasing?

Answer: yes, both demand from destination countries and supply-side interest among skilled workers are high.

Evidence:
- OECD indicators show that countries increasingly compete to attract highly skilled migrants, foreign entrepreneurs, university students, and startup founders.
- EMN/OECD research notes that many EU and OECD countries introduced talent-attraction policies after 2021.
- Target sectors include healthcare, life sciences, social work, pharmaceuticals, ICT, high technology, manufacturing, chemicals, and other shortage areas.
- Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Germany, the UK, the Netherlands, Sweden, Switzerland, and several Gulf countries actively compete for skilled migrants.

User-demand interpretation:
- Skilled migrants are not only looking for jobs. They compare taxes, path to PR/citizenship, spouse work rights, school quality, healthcare, housing, political stability, and long-term legal risk.
- A skilled worker may prefer a less famous country if the visa path is clearer, taxes are lower, and settlement is faster.

7. Is interest among entrepreneurs and investors increasing?

Answer: yes, but the market is changing.

Evidence:
- OECD’s talent-attractiveness framework includes foreign entrepreneurs and startup founders as explicit competition categories.
- Canada, France, the UK, Portugal, the Netherlands, Estonia, Singapore, UAE, and others have promoted startup or innovation visas at different times.
- Henley and similar firms report strong interest in residence/citizenship planning among wealthy individuals.
- Investment migration is moving away from simple real-estate golden visas and toward business, funds, innovation, job creation, and stronger compliance.
- Spain ended its real-estate-oriented golden visa in 2025; Europe has broadly discouraged citizenship-by-investment inside the EU.

Interpretation:
- Entrepreneurs and investors increasingly seek "sovereign optionality": multiple residencies, second citizenship, tax planning, banking diversification, and geopolitical risk hedging.
- But governments are raising compliance standards. Source of funds, sanctions exposure, anti-money-laundering rules, tax residency, physical presence, and reputational risk matter more than before.

Evidence strength:
- Desire-to-migrate data: B.
- Skill/talent policy competition: A.
- Investor migration demand: C, supported by commercial providers and policy changes.
