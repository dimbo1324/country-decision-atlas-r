08. COUNTRIES AND COMPETITION BETWEEN DESTINATIONS
Research package date: 2026-06-16
Language: English
Scope: international migration, relocation demand, digital nomads, skilled migration, investment migration, policy trends, and product opportunity for a migration decision platform.

Important interpretation rules:
- "International migrant stock" is not the same thing as annual migration flow. Stock counts people residing outside their country of birth or citizenship at a point in time; flows measure entries/exits during a period.
- Forced displacement counts and international migrant stock overlap only partly, because some displaced people remain internally displaced inside their own country.
- Online search interest is often measured on relative scales. Exact Google Trends exports were not available in this environment, so this package uses published Google Trends analyses where available and recommends a measurement design for direct collection.
- Social-media community counts are incomplete because Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Discord groups often hide membership/activity or are private.
- Commercial market-size estimates are directional; exact market sizing for relocation, immigration law, and visa-tech varies by vendor definition.


1. Which countries actively compete for migrants, specialists, and investors?

Talent competition:
- Canada: skilled immigration, provincial programs, settlement pathway, but currently managing temporary-resident pressure.
- Australia: skilled migration, regional pathways, but tighter student and migration-quality controls.
- New Zealand: high attractiveness for skilled workers in OECD talent indicators.
- Germany: skilled-worker reforms and labour-shortage pathways.
- United Kingdom: skilled worker, Global Talent, High Potential Individual, but stricter broader rules.
- France: startup/talent pathways and innovation ecosystem.
- Netherlands: highly skilled migrant and startup ecosystem, but housing/tax pressures.
- Sweden/Switzerland/Norway: strong quality of life and skilled-worker appeal.
- UAE: tax, business, long-term residence, investor/entrepreneur positioning.
- Saudi Arabia: labour-market transformation and high-income professional recruitment.
- Singapore: high-skilled Asian hub, strict but attractive for business/talent.
- Portugal/Spain/Italy/Greece: lifestyle, residence, remote-worker, and in some cases tax/investor appeal, but housing and policy risk are material.

Investor competition:
- UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Italy, Portugal, Greece, Malta, Caribbean programs, and selected Middle Eastern/APAC jurisdictions compete for high-net-worth individuals.
- Investment migration is increasingly about compliance, tax, residence planning, and family security, not only a passport.

2. Which countries are losing attractiveness?

Countries can lose attractiveness despite still being popular.

Reasons for losing attractiveness:
- Housing unaffordability.
- Stricter visas.
- Tax-regime changes.
- Long processing delays.
- Anti-immigration politics.
- Bureaucracy.
- Weak path to permanent residence.
- Student/dependant restrictions.
- High healthcare or childcare costs.
- Safety concerns.
- Local backlash against foreigners.
- Overcrowded expat hubs.
- Currency instability.
- Poor banking access.

Examples:
- Canada: still attractive but less frictionless because of temporary-resident caps, student restrictions, housing pressure, and affordability concerns.
- UK: still attractive but less accessible for students with dependants, care workers, lower-paid skilled workers, and people seeking quick settlement.
- Australia: still attractive but more controlled in student and temporary migration.
- Portugal: still attractive but less cheap/easy than its online reputation; golden visa changes and housing costs reduced some appeal.
- Spain: attractive lifestyle but real-estate golden visa ended; bureaucracy and taxes matter.
- Some Caribbean citizenship programs: still relevant but under stronger international due-diligence pressure.

3. Which countries are becoming new popular destinations?

Emerging or rising-interest groups:
- Southern Europe: Portugal, Spain, Italy, Greece, Malta, Croatia.
- Gulf: UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar in high-income professional/business segments.
- Latin America: Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia, Brazil, Uruguay, Argentina.
- Eastern/South-Eastern Europe and Caucasus/Balkans: Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Albania, Bulgaria, Romania, Montenegro, Moldova, Slovenia.
- Asia: Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia/Bali, Japan in selective remote-work/high-skill contexts.
- Africa/Middle East niches: Mauritius, Cape Verde, Morocco, and selected African tech hubs for remote/lifestyle/business migration.

Important caveat:
"Popular online" is not equal to "legally or financially optimal." Some destinations become popular because of YouTube/TikTok, low short-term cost, or easy tourist entry, but may lack long-term residence, tax clarity, healthcare, or citizenship prospects.

4. Which countries are overrated and underrated?

Overrated often means:
- online hype is high,
- legal path is weak,
- taxes are misunderstood,
- housing is expensive,
- bureaucracy is hard,
- long-term citizenship is slow,
- local salaries are poor,
- or infrastructure does not match expectations.

Potentially overrated for many users:
- Portugal for users expecting very low costs and easy bureaucracy.
- Bali/Indonesia for users needing long-term legal/tax clarity.
- Dubai/UAE for users needing citizenship or low-cost family life.
- Canada for users expecting cheap housing and easy settlement without a strong pathway.
- UK for users expecting easy post-study or lower-income migration.
- Spain/Italy for users expecting fast bureaucracy and low tax complexity.

Underrated often means:
- less hype,
- better legal route for a specific profile,
- lower costs,
- safer lifestyle,
- clearer residence path,
- or better family practicality.

Potentially underrated depending on profile:
- Uruguay for stability and regional quality of life.
- Paraguay for low cost and residence simplicity in some cases.
- Slovenia/Croatia for EU lifestyle with smaller markets.
- Romania/Bulgaria for lower EU costs and emerging opportunity.
- Malaysia for cost/comfort in Asian lifestyle segments.
- Georgia/Armenia/Serbia for budget, business, or bridge relocation, though long-term passport strength and geopolitical risk vary.
- Greece for lifestyle/residence if taxes and housing are managed carefully.
- Germany for skilled workers who prioritise stability over lifestyle hype.

5. Which countries do people with limited budgets choose?

Budget-sensitive migration usually prioritises:
- low rent,
- low everyday costs,
- accessible visa,
- low proof-of-funds,
- affordable healthcare,
- existing diaspora/community,
- remote income compatibility,
- low bureaucracy,
- safety.

Common budget destinations:
- Mexico.
- Colombia.
- Argentina.
- Brazil.
- Paraguay.
- Georgia.
- Armenia.
- Serbia.
- Albania.
- Bulgaria.
- Romania.
- Türkiye, depending on current rules and economy.
- Thailand.
- Malaysia.
- Vietnam, usually more as lifestyle/temporary stay than long-term immigration.
- Indonesia/Bali, with legal/tax caveats.

Product warning:
Budget users are high-volume but lower willingness to pay. They need affordable tools, not expensive consulting.

6. Which countries do entrepreneurs and startup founders choose?

Entrepreneur criteria:
- company formation speed,
- banking access,
- taxes,
- startup visa,
- ecosystem,
- investor access,
- English use,
- talent pool,
- legal certainty,
- residence/citizenship timeline,
- cost of hiring,
- IP protection,
- access to target markets.

Frequent startup/business destinations:
- United States, for market and capital, but visa complexity is high.
- Canada, especially for startup founder attractiveness in OECD indicators.
- United Kingdom, for market, language, and finance, though rules have changed over time.
- France, for startup visa and ecosystem.
- Netherlands, for business hub and skilled migration, but housing/tax issues matter.
- Germany, for EU market and engineering talent.
- Estonia, for digital administration and startup reputation.
- Portugal/Spain, for lifestyle plus EU access, but tax and bureaucracy must be assessed.
- UAE/Dubai, for tax/business positioning and international connectivity.
- Singapore, for Asian business hub.
- Ireland, for EU/English/corporate environment, but housing and costs are constraints.

7. Which countries do people choose for a second passport?

Main second-passport strategies:
- Fastest legal naturalisation after residence.
- Citizenship by descent.
- Citizenship by marriage/family.
- Investment citizenship where still available.
- Long-term EU residence followed by naturalisation.
- Refugee/statelessness routes in special cases.

Common countries considered for citizenship by descent:
- Italy.
- Ireland.
- Poland.
- Hungary.
- Portugal.
- Spain/Sephardic-related legacy routes, mostly now limited/changed.
- Germany/Austria in restitution contexts.
- Romania/Bulgaria in ancestry contexts.
- Armenia, Israel, and others depending on heritage.

Common residence-to-citizenship considerations:
- Portugal: historically attractive but changing rules and backlogs matter.
- Spain: favourable timelines for some Latin American nationalities, but general naturalisation can be long.
- Italy: long bureaucracy but strong passport.
- Canada: relatively clear pathway but stricter temporary-resident management.
- Australia/New Zealand: strong passports but selective pathways.
- Germany: reforms improved dual citizenship and timelines for some, but requirements still matter.
- Uruguay/Argentina/Paraguay: often discussed for Latin American residence/citizenship, but legal practice and timelines need verification.

Evidence strength:
- Hard rankings of "overrated/underrated" are subjective. Treat as product hypotheses to validate with user outcomes.
- Talent competitiveness: A from OECD/EMN.
- Investor competition: C with policy evidence.
- Budget/lifestyle destinations: C/D and should be scored by live cost, visa, safety, and tax data.
