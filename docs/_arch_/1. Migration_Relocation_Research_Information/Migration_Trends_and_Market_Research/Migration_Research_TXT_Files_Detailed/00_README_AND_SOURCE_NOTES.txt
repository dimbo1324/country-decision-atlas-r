README AND SOURCE NOTES
Research package date: 2026-06-16
Language: English
Scope: international migration, relocation demand, digital nomads, skilled migration, investment migration, policy trends, and product opportunity for a migration decision platform.

Important interpretation rules:
- "International migrant stock" is not the same thing as annual migration flow. Stock counts people residing outside their country of birth or citizenship at a point in time; flows measure entries/exits during a period.
- Forced displacement counts and international migrant stock overlap only partly, because some displaced people remain internally displaced inside their own country.
- Online search interest is often measured on relative scales. Exact Google Trends exports were not available in this environment, so this package uses published Google Trends analyses where available and recommends a measurement design for direct collection.
- Social-media community counts are incomplete because Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Discord groups often hide membership/activity or are private.
- Commercial market-size estimates are directional; exact market sizing for relocation, immigration law, and visa-tech varies by vendor definition.

Key headline findings:
- Global international migrant stock is rising. UN DESA estimates 304.0 million international migrants in 2024, almost double the 154 million in 1990.
- Migrants remain a small share of humanity: 3.7% in 2024, compared with 2.9% in 1990.
- Europe and Northern America together hosted about 155.3 million international migrants in 2024, around half the global total.
- The United States remains the largest destination by stock, but Gallup data show that its share of global preferred-destination demand among people who want to migrate has fallen.
- OECD permanent-type migration reached a record in 2023, then declined in 2024, while remaining above 2019 levels.
- Forced displacement reached historic levels at end-2024, although UNHCR reported a decline in 2025.
- Digital nomadism and remote-work migration remain a high-growth niche, but the sector is increasingly constrained by tax, housing, infrastructure, and visa-compliance issues.
- Student migration grew strongly over two decades, but major Anglophone destinations tightened rules and saw declines in new international student numbers in 2024.
- The market opportunity is not another generic country-ranking site. The opportunity is evidence-backed personal decision intelligence: country shortlist, migration path, risk scoring, law tracking, cost model, documentation plan, and verified user evidence.
