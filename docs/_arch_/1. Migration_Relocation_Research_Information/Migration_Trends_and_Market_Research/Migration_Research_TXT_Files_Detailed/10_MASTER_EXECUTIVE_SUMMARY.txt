MASTER EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Research package date: 2026-06-16
Language: English
Scope: international migration, relocation demand, digital nomads, skilled migration, investment migration, policy trends, and product opportunity for a migration decision platform.

Important interpretation rules:
- "International migrant stock" is not the same thing as annual migration flow. Stock counts people residing outside their country of birth or citizenship at a point in time; flows measure entries/exits during a period.
- Forced displacement counts and international migrant stock overlap only partly, because some displaced people remain internally displaced inside their own country.
- Online search interest is often measured on relative scales. Exact Google Trends exports were not available in this environment, so this package uses published Google Trends analyses where available and recommends a measurement design for direct collection.
- Social-media community counts are incomplete because Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Discord groups often hide membership/activity or are private.
- Commercial market-size estimates are directional; exact market sizing for relocation, immigration law, and visa-tech varies by vendor definition.


This executive summary condenses the nine research files.

1. Global migration is rising long-term.
UN DESA estimates 304.0 million international migrants in 2024, almost twice the 1990 level. The migrant share of world population increased from 2.9% in 1990 to 3.7% in 2024. Europe and Northern America host about half of all international migrants.

2. Migration demand is high but uneven.
Gallup estimated that more than 900 million adults, around 16% of the world adult population, wanted to move permanently to another country in 2023. Demand softened in some regions by 2025, but remains structurally high. The United States is still the top preferred destination, but its relative pull has declined, while Canada and parts of Europe remain very attractive.

3. The fastest-growing demand niches are not the same as the biggest migration categories.
Family migration remains a large stable category. Forced displacement is historically high. Student migration grew strongly over two decades but declined in several major OECD destinations in 2024. Digital nomad migration is a smaller but very fast-growing, monetisable niche.

4. Search and community behaviour show fragmented, high-intent demand.
Users search for visas, tax, safety, cost of living, schools, healthcare, banking, and citizenship. Digital nomad visa searches have spiked, but exact keyword volumes require a direct Google Trends/SEO pipeline. Reddit, Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, YouTube, and specialised nomad communities are major sources of advice, but also create outdated or contradictory information.

5. Policy is bifurcating.
Countries are opening selective pathways for skilled workers, entrepreneurs, healthcare workers, and remote workers, while tightening student, asylum, low-wage labour, family, and golden visa routes. Canada, the UK, Australia, the EU, Spain, and several European countries show clear tightening in important channels.

6. The monetisation opportunity is personal decision intelligence.
Raw information is free. Users pay for risk reduction, personal analysis, legal clarity, document planning, and expert-reviewed decisions. B2C users can pay for country shortlists, feasibility reports, and action plans. B2B customers pay for employee mobility compliance and dashboards.

7. Platform opportunity.
A differentiated platform should provide:
- personal country matching,
- visa/tax/family/citizenship scoring,
- law-change tracking,
- country interest and direction indices,
- verified user cases,
- cost model,
- risk report,
- official source pack,
- expert marketplace,
- B2B global mobility extension.

Best one-sentence positioning:
"An evidence-backed migration decision platform that tells each user which countries are legally realistic, financially viable, safe, and strategically smart for their specific profile."
