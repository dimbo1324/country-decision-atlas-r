07. LEGAL AND POLICY TRENDS
Research package date: 2026-06-16
Language: English
Scope: international migration, relocation demand, digital nomads, skilled migration, investment migration, policy trends, and product opportunity for a migration decision platform.

Important interpretation rules:
- "International migrant stock" is not the same thing as annual migration flow. Stock counts people residing outside their country of birth or citizenship at a point in time; flows measure entries/exits during a period.
- Forced displacement counts and international migrant stock overlap only partly, because some displaced people remain internally displaced inside their own country.
- Online search interest is often measured on relative scales. Exact Google Trends exports were not available in this environment, so this package uses published Google Trends analyses where available and recommends a measurement design for direct collection.
- Social-media community counts are incomplete because Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, and Discord groups often hide membership/activity or are private.
- Commercial market-size estimates are directional; exact market sizing for relocation, immigration law, and visa-tech varies by vendor definition.


1. Which countries are simplifying migration rules?

Simplification is targeted, not universal. Countries usually simplify for people they want and tighten for people they do not.

Common liberalisation targets:
- Highly skilled workers.
- Healthcare workers.
- Engineers and tech specialists.
- Researchers.
- Startup founders.
- Investors with strong compliance.
- International students in shortage fields.
- Remote workers with foreign income.
- Families of high-skilled migrants in some cases.

Examples and patterns:
- EU/OECD countries introduced new talent-attraction approaches after 2021.
- Many countries have launched or updated digital nomad and remote-work visas.
- Germany introduced or promoted talent pathways such as the Opportunity Card and skilled-worker reforms.
- The UAE continues to position itself around long-term residence, business, and high-skill/investor attraction.
- Some Southern European countries created remote-work visas while tightening golden visas or housing-related investment.
- Canada and Australia still compete for skilled migrants but are tightening parts of temporary/student migration.

2. Which countries are tightening migration rules?

Major tightening examples:
- Canada: reduced temporary resident admissions and stabilised/lowered permanent resident targets compared with earlier growth plans.
- Australia: stronger management of international student numbers, education integrity, and migration quality.
- United Kingdom: tougher rules on student dependants, care-worker recruitment, salary thresholds, English requirements, and settlement.
- Ireland: tighter rules around some asylum support, family income thresholds, and refugee citizenship residence.
- EU: the Migration and Asylum Pact introduces faster asylum/border procedures, stricter management, digital tools, more return emphasis, and a solidarity mechanism.
- Spain: ended real-estate golden visas in 2025.
- Several European countries are tightening family reunification, asylum, long-term residence, and citizenship conditions under domestic political pressure.

3. Which countries are closing or restricting golden visa / citizenship-by-investment programs?

Trend:
- EU citizenship-by-investment is effectively politically unacceptable.
- Residence-by-investment remains, but with tighter rules, higher scrutiny, and less tolerance for passive real estate.
- Caribbean citizenship programs face pressure from the EU, UK, and U.S. to improve due diligence, pricing, and compliance.
- Golden visa programs are moving from real estate to funds, business, innovation, or strategic investment.
- Spain ended its golden visa route linked to real estate in 2025.
- Portugal previously changed its golden visa away from residential real estate investment.
- Ireland ended its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023.
- The UK closed its Tier 1 Investor route in 2022.

Interpretation:
Investment migration is not dead. It is becoming more compliance-heavy and politically sensitive.

4. Which countries are becoming more attractive for remote workers?

Attractive remote-work destinations typically combine:
- A remote-work or digital-nomad visa.
- Reasonable income requirement.
- Tax clarity or special regime.
- Safety.
- Good internet.
- Rental availability.
- Healthcare access.
- International community.
- Easy banking.
- Good flight connections.
- No requirement to work for a local employer.

Commonly discussed destinations:
- Portugal.
- Spain.
- Italy.
- Greece.
- Croatia.
- Estonia.
- Malta.
- UAE/Dubai.
- Mexico.
- Colombia.
- Brazil.
- Costa Rica.
- Panama.
- Uruguay.
- Argentina.
- Thailand.
- Malaysia.
- Indonesia/Bali.
- Japan/Korea in selected remote-work or highly skilled contexts, depending on rules.

Important warning:
Remote-work attractiveness can be destroyed by:
- tax ambiguity,
- housing shortage,
- anti-nomad sentiment,
- banking difficulty,
- weak healthcare access,
- inability to bring family,
- no PR/citizenship pathway,
- infrastructure issues.

5. Which countries are becoming less migrant-friendly?

Less friendly can mean several things:
- Legal tightening.
- Higher rejection rates.
- Higher income thresholds.
- Longer settlement periods.
- Fewer rights for dependants.
- Anti-immigration rhetoric.
- Social tension.
- Housing backlash.
- Restrictions on asylum or family migration.
- Less tolerance for temporary residents.

Current risk patterns:
- The UK, Canada, and Australia remain attractive but have tightened key pathways after very high post-pandemic inflows.
- Many EU countries are politically divided on asylum and irregular migration.
- Housing-constrained countries may become less friendly toward students, nomads, and investors if locals perceive them as increasing rents.
- Digital nomad destinations can become less welcoming if tax, rents, or local backlash become politically salient.

6. Which legislative changes most affect country choice?

High-impact legal variables:
- Visa eligibility criteria.
- Income thresholds.
- Proof-of-funds requirements.
- Work rights.
- Remote-work legality.
- Dependants and spouse work rights.
- Path to permanent residence.
- Path to citizenship.
- Physical-presence requirements.
- Language/civic tests.
- Tax-residence rules.
- Special tax regimes.
- Social security obligations.
- Banking and AML restrictions.
- Housing/rental laws.
- School access.
- Health insurance requirements.
- Employment-law restrictions.
- Company substance rules.
- Exit tax and foreign-asset reporting.
- Military/conscription laws.
- Sanctions and nationality restrictions.

7. Can a platform determine the direction of a country from its laws?

Yes, but it must be a scored trend model, not a simple label.

Possible "Country Direction Index" dimensions:
- Migration openness: new visas, caps, requirements, processing time.
- Talent friendliness: shortage lists, degree recognition, salary thresholds, spouse rights.
- Family friendliness: school access, healthcare, dependants, housing.
- Business friendliness: company formation, tax, banking, startup visas, contract enforcement.
- Fiscal direction: tax increases/decreases, special regimes, enforcement intensity.
- Civil freedom direction: media freedom, rule of law, judiciary independence, protest rights.
- Security direction: war risk, crime, terrorism, civil unrest.
- Housing direction: rent pressure, foreign-buyer restrictions, vacancy, building policy.
- Digital-nomad direction: visa, tax clarity, infrastructure, community, political sentiment.
- Citizenship direction: residence years, language rules, dual citizenship, ancestry routes.
- Compliance direction: AML, sanctions, banking, source-of-funds requirements.

Scoring method:
- Track laws, regulations, official policy papers, court decisions, parliamentary bills, ministry announcements, processing statistics, visa rejection data, tax changes, and credible media reports.
- Apply direction: liberalising, stable, tightening, contradictory, or unknown.
- Timestamp every data point.
- Separate "announced" from "implemented."
- Separate "national law" from "local practice."
- Show confidence level.

Evidence strength:
- Policy changes: A where government/EU/OECD sources exist.
- Country friendliness: mixed A/B/C/D and requires careful operationalisation.
