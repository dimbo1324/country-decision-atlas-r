2. INTEREST IN MOVING ABROAD

Research date: 16 June 2026

2.1 Is the number of people who want to move abroad growing?

The long-term answer is yes, but the most recent Gallup data shows a slight cooling from the 2021-2024 peak.

Gallup’s global migration desire data:
- Around 15% of adults worldwide wanted to move permanently to another country in 2025.
- This equals roughly 900 million potential migrants.
- The level fell slightly from a record 16% in 2021-2024.
- Even after the slight decline, the absolute market of people interested in relocation remains enormous.

Interpretation:
- Migration intention is highly sensitive to conflict, political instability, inflation, unemployment, repression, crime and perceived future risk.
- Intent does not equal actual migration. Money, visas, language, family obligations, credentials and risk tolerance strongly limit real movement.

Main evidence: Gallup global migration desire polling and Potential Net Migration Index.

2.2 Countries with the largest share of people wanting to emigrate

Gallup consistently finds the highest desire to leave in regions with weaker economic opportunity, insecurity, political instability or low institutional trust.

High-intent regions:
- Sub-Saharan Africa: around one-third of adults say they would like to move permanently if they could.
- Latin America and the Caribbean: still high, around one-quarter of adults in 2025, though below the 2021-2022 peak.
- Parts of the Middle East and North Africa: high in countries affected by conflict, unemployment or state fragility.
- Some post-Soviet and Eastern European countries: elevated interest after war, sanctions, political pressure and economic stagnation.

Countries often appearing in high-emigration-intent or negative net-migration-potential groups include parts of Central America, the Caribbean, Sub-Saharan Africa, Lebanon, Bangladesh, Morocco, Nigeria, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia, Honduras and other states with economic or political stress.

2.3 Most desired countries for relocation

According to Gallup, the top desired destinations among people who want to move include:
- United States: still #1, but its share fell to about 15% in 2025, the lowest in nearly 20 years.
- Canada: #2 in Gallup’s recent ranking, about 9%.
- United Kingdom.
- Germany.
- Australia.
- Spain.
- France.
- Japan.
- Italy.
- Saudi Arabia.

For work-related relocation, BCG/Stepstone research also shows strong preference for:
- Australia.
- United States.
- Canada.
- United Kingdom.
- Germany.

Interpretation:
- The “dream destination” market remains dominated by English-speaking countries and major European economies.
- But digital nomads, retirees and tax-sensitive users often prefer different destinations: Spain, Portugal, UAE, Mexico, Panama, Costa Rica, Thailand, Greece, Italy, Croatia and similar lifestyle/tax/residency markets.

2.4 Age groups most interested in migration

The highest interest usually comes from:
- Young adults aged roughly 15-34.
- Students and early-career professionals.
- People without children or with fewer family constraints.
- Young skilled workers in countries with weak job markets.

Important Gallup signal:
- Gallup reported a sharp rise in U.S. women aged 15-44 who say they would move permanently if they could, from around 10% in 2014 to around 40% in 2025. This is a strong example of political/social conditions influencing migration desire even in a wealthy destination country.

Other groups:
- Families move when education, safety and long-term citizenship are strong enough to justify cost and disruption.
- Retirees move for cost of living, healthcare, climate, safety and lifestyle.
- Entrepreneurs move for taxes, banking, market access, business freedom and legal stability.

2.5 Professions and social groups most likely to consider relocation

High-interest professional groups:
- IT specialists and software engineers.
- AI, data, cybersecurity and cloud professionals.
- Engineers.
- Doctors, nurses and care workers.
- Researchers and academics.
- Students and recent graduates.
- Entrepreneurs, founders and startup employees.
- Finance, consulting and global corporate professionals.
- Skilled trades and construction workers.
- Hospitality, logistics and seasonal workers.

Why these groups move:
- Their skills are internationally transferable.
- They can benefit from salary arbitrage.
- Many countries have explicit shortage lists or talent routes.
- Employers often sponsor visas for these roles.
- Remote work makes geography less fixed for some knowledge workers.

2.6 Is skilled migration interest growing?

Yes, structurally. Countries increasingly compete for talent, but 2024-2025 data shows a more selective environment.

Evidence:
- OECD countries have built or expanded talent routes, shortage occupation lists, points systems, graduate routes and startup/founder visas.
- Germany introduced the Opportunity Card and expanded skilled-worker routes.
- The UK keeps Global Talent and High Potential Individual routes even while tightening general migration.
- Canada, Australia and New Zealand use points and skills-selection systems, although Canada and Australia are currently tightening some temporary and student routes.
- BCG’s 2025 talent mobility data suggests a slowdown in actual high-skilled cross-border moves in 2025, especially STEM/AI/research, but still millions of skilled workers move or show intent.

Interpretation:
- Interest remains high, but policy filters are becoming stricter.
- High-skilled migrants are favoured; low-wage, student-to-work and family routes face more scrutiny.

2.7 Is interest growing among entrepreneurs and investors?

Yes, but the geography is changing.

Growth signals:
- Startup visas and founder visas are now common policy tools.
- Wealth migration reports show high-net-worth individuals increasingly seek residence or citizenship options as geopolitical insurance.
- Americans, Chinese, Indians, Russians and other globally mobile groups show growing interest in second residence/passports.
- UAE, Singapore, Italy, Switzerland, Portugal, Greece and selected Caribbean/Latin American states compete for mobile capital.

Countertrend:
- The EU is hostile to citizenship-by-investment and increasingly sceptical of property-based golden visas.
- Spain closed its golden visa in 2025.
- Portugal removed real-estate investment from its golden visa routes.
- Malta’s citizenship-by-investment model was ruled incompatible with EU law.

Conclusion:
- Entrepreneur/investor demand is growing, but the “easy real-estate golden visa in Europe” era is ending.
- The future market is more likely to favour active investment, entrepreneurship, tax residency, substance requirements and verified economic contribution.
