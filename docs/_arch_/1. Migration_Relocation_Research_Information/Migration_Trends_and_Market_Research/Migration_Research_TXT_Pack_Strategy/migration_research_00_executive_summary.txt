GLOBAL MIGRATION AND RELOCATION INTELLIGENCE PACK
Executive Summary
Research date: 16 June 2026
Language: English

1. Bottom line
International migration is still structurally increasing over the long term, but the pattern is becoming more selective, politicised and segmented. The total international migrant stock roughly doubled from about 154 million in 1990 to about 304 million in mid-2024. However, the post-2023 picture is not simply “more migration everywhere”: some major destinations are tightening student, family, low-wage and temporary routes, while competing more aggressively for highly skilled workers, founders, investors, medical workers, AI talent and remote workers.

2. Main statistical picture
- Global international migrants: about 304 million in mid-2024, around 3.7%-4% of the world population.
- Long-run change: about 154 million in 1990; about 281 million in 2020; about 302-304 million in 2023-2024.
- OECD permanent-type migration hit a record 6.5 million in 2023, then moderated in many OECD countries in 2024.
- The OECD foreign-born population exceeded 160 million in 2024, with the foreign-born share rising from 9.1% in 2014 to 11.5%.
- Labour migration remains huge: ILO estimates 167.7 million international migrant workers in the labour force in 2022.
- Forced displacement remains historically high: UNHCR reported 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at end-2024; IDMC reported 83.4 million internally displaced people.

3. Regions with strongest growth and pressure
- Europe and Northern America host the majority of international migrants, more than 50% of the global stock.
- Northern Africa and Western Asia are major migration hubs because of labour migration to Gulf countries, refugee flows, and regional conflicts.
- Latin America has seen major changes due to Venezuelan displacement and regional labour mobility.
- Sub-Saharan Africa has the highest share of people who say they would like to emigrate, according to Gallup, but much movement is regional or constrained by cost and visa access.
- OECD countries remain the central arena for family, labour, student, humanitarian and asylum migration.

4. Top origin and destination countries
Top origin countries in recent UN DESA/MPI summaries include India, China, Mexico, Ukraine, Russia, Syria, Bangladesh and Venezuela. Top destination countries include the United States, Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, France, Canada, the United Arab Emirates, Australia, Russia, Türkiye, Spain and Italy.

5. Migration intention is high but uneven
Gallup estimates that roughly 15% of adults worldwide wanted to move permanently to another country in 2025, equal to about 900 million people. This is down slightly from the 2021-2024 record level of 16%, but still extremely large. The U.S. remains the most desired destination, but its share of potential migrants fell to its lowest level in nearly two decades. Canada, the UK, Germany and Australia remain major “dream destinations”.

6. Fastest-growing migration categories
The fastest visible growth areas are:
- Humanitarian and asylum migration, driven by conflicts, instability and weak state capacity.
- Temporary labour migration and sector-specific recruitment, especially health, care, construction, logistics, agriculture and services.
- International student migration, though Canada, Australia and the UK are now tightening or rebalancing student pathways.
- Digital nomad and remote-work migration, which grew sharply after 2020 but is becoming more regulated and less “constant travel” oriented.
- Investment and wealth migration, especially among high-net-worth individuals, though European golden visas are being restricted.

7. Main reasons people want to move
The strongest drivers are economic opportunity, safety, political stability, legal security, education, healthcare, quality of life, lower taxes, family prospects, second passports, and escape from conflict or authoritarian pressure. For skilled workers, career opportunity and salary are central. For families, schools, healthcare, safety and long-term residence/citizenship routes are critical. For founders and investors, taxes, banking, company formation, regulatory stability and residence rights matter most.

8. Search and online demand
Online interest is growing around digital nomad visas, second passports, fast residence permits, tax residency, low-tax countries, remote-work destinations, citizenship by descent, and “move to [country]” searches after elections, wars or major political events. However, search data must be treated as a leading indicator rather than a direct measure of migration.

9. Social community behaviour
Migration communities on Reddit, Telegram, Facebook, YouTube and WhatsApp are active because official information is fragmented, slow, legalistic and difficult to personalise. Recurring topics include visas, documents, housing, jobs, taxes, banking, schools, healthcare, local bureaucracy, safety, loneliness, scams, and mistakes after arrival.

10. Willingness to pay
Users already pay for immigration lawyers, visa agents, relocation consultants, premium reports, checklists, document reviews, employer-sponsored relocation support, tax residency consultations, and investment migration advisors. B2B buyers pay for global mobility SaaS, immigration compliance, relocation dashboards, payroll/EOR platforms and employee relocation vendors. The strongest monetisation opportunity is not raw information but personalised analysis, eligibility scoring, risk reduction and implementation support.

11. Legal direction
The world is splitting into two simultaneous trends:
- Selective openness: talent visas, startup visas, remote-work visas, health/care worker recruitment, founder programs, AI/tech talent pathways.
- Broad tightening: student caps, higher salary thresholds, tougher family reunification, longer settlement timelines, anti-abuse rules, closure of property golden visas, and stricter asylum/irregular migration controls.

12. Platform opportunity
The strongest opportunity is a country-selection and migration-risk intelligence platform that combines official law, cost of living, safety, tax, housing, jobs, healthcare, schools, citizenship timelines, user cases, search interest, social signals and policy direction. The unique value should be: personalised shortlist, migration pathway, budget estimate, risk report, legal trend summary and evidence-backed recommendations.

13. Recommended product positioning
Do not build “another Nomad List” or “another expat forum”. Build a decision engine:
- “Which countries fit my profile?”
- “What route is realistic?”
- “What will it cost?”
- “What can go wrong?”
- “Is this country becoming more open or more closed?”
- “What evidence supports this recommendation?”

14. Minimum viable evidence stack
For each country, collect: visa routes, eligibility, fees, timelines, renewal rules, permanent residence rules, citizenship rules, tax residency, healthcare, schools, housing/rent, salary/jobs, safety/crime, banking, sanctions/AML friction, business formation, cost of living, language requirements, political/legal direction, user reviews and official source links.
