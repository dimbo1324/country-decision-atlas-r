8. COUNTRIES AND COMPETITION BETWEEN DESTINATIONS

Research date: 16 June 2026

8.1 Countries competing for migrants, talent and investors

Major talent competitors:
- United States: still the largest destination by migrant stock and the top desired destination, but visa access is complex.
- Canada: strong skilled migration brand, but currently tightening temporary, student and overall targets.
- Germany: ageing population and labour shortages; Opportunity Card and skilled-worker reforms.
- Australia: points-based skilled migration, but student route tightening.
- United Kingdom: elite/talent routes remain, but broad tightening is underway.
- Netherlands, Ireland, Switzerland, Sweden, Denmark and Finland: strong skilled labour markets but housing/tax/language constraints vary.
- UAE and Saudi Arabia: tax, business and diversification-driven competition for professionals and investors.
- Singapore: high-skill, finance, technology and business hub.
- Japan and South Korea: selectively opening to high-skilled workers and remote workers, while demographic pressure grows.

Startup/founder competitors:
- Canada, UK, France, Germany, Netherlands, Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Ireland, Singapore, UAE, Chile and others.

Remote-worker/lifestyle competitors:
- Spain, Portugal, Italy, Greece, Croatia, UAE, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia, Thailand, Indonesia, Japan and New Zealand.

Investor/wealth competitors:
- UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Italy, Portugal, Greece, Malta, Caribbean CBI states, Panama and selected Latin American destinations.

8.2 Countries losing attractiveness

Countries can lose attractiveness due to legal tightening, housing costs, bureaucracy, taxes, safety, politics or online overhype.

Examples:
- Canada: still attractive but affected by housing crisis, student caps, temporary-resident reductions and longer uncertainty.
- United Kingdom: stricter migration rules, high cost of living, housing pressure and longer settlement proposals.
- Australia: student route tightening and housing pressure.
- Portugal: still popular but affected by housing costs, golden visa property-route removal, tax-regime changes and local backlash.
- Spain/Portugal/Italy/Greece hotspots: some cities face rent pressure and local resentment toward tourists/nomads.
- Some Gulf destinations: attractive for tax and business, but users may worry about long-term citizenship, rights, heat and family lifestyle.
- United States: remains #1 desired destination, but Gallup shows reduced preference share and visa/legal uncertainty.

8.3 New popular or emerging destinations

Emerging or re-emerging destinations:
- UAE/Dubai: tax, business, safety, connectivity and wealth migration.
- Saudi Arabia: professional opportunities linked to large-scale development.
- Spain: digital nomads, families, retirees and quality-of-life migrants.
- Italy: ancestry citizenship, lifestyle, tax regimes and digital nomad visa.
- Greece: residence, lifestyle and investor/retiree appeal.
- Panama: strong expat satisfaction and retirement/remote-worker appeal.
- Colombia and Mexico: affordability and lifestyle, especially for North Americans.
- Uruguay: political stability and safety within Latin America, but smaller job market.
- Paraguay: affordability and simple residence appeal for some users, but lower institutional/service quality.
- Japan: digital nomad visa visibility and lifestyle appeal, though strict rules.
- New Zealand: remote-work permission on visitor visas increased attention.

8.4 Overrated vs underrated countries

This is not purely objective, but a platform can compare online hype against real conditions.

Often overhyped risks:
- Portugal: still excellent for many, but housing and bureaucracy are often underestimated.
- Bali/Indonesia: lifestyle appeal, but visa, tax, traffic, infrastructure and legal work issues can be misunderstood.
- Dubai/UAE: strong for business/tax/safety, but long-term citizenship and summer climate are drawbacks.
- Canada: strong brand, but housing affordability and credential recognition are serious issues.
- Australia: strong salaries/lifestyle, but housing, distance and visa competition are high.

Potentially underrated depending on profile:
- Spain: strong quality of life and family appeal, but taxes and bureaucracy matter.
- Uruguay: stability and safety in Latin America, but small market and cost relative to neighbours.
- Panama: practical expat infrastructure and satisfaction, but climate and inequality vary.
- Germany: strong labour demand and PR potential, but language/bureaucracy are hard.
- Estonia: digital governance and business formation, but small market and climate.
- Czechia/Poland: affordable EU options for some workers, but language and route details matter.
- Malaysia: lifestyle and cost, but long-term rights depend heavily on route.

Common budget-conscious destinations:

- Colombia.
- Argentina.
- Paraguay.
- Ecuador.
- Albania.
- Georgia.
- Türkiye.
- Thailand.
- Vietnam.
- Malaysia.
- Indonesia.
- Portugal interior/smaller cities, though major cities are no longer cheap.
- Spain smaller cities.
- Eastern Europe / Balkans depending on visa route.

Budget users need special warnings:
- Cheap countries may not offer easy legal work rights.
- Cheap rent may be unavailable in capital/tourist cities.
- Healthcare quality and insurance rules vary.
- Currency volatility can help or hurt.
- Permanent residence/citizenship may be much harder than initial entry.

8.7 Countries chosen by people needing a second passport

Main second-passport routes:

A. Naturalisation after residence
Examples to compare: Portugal, Spain, Italy, Germany, France, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, UK, Ireland, Uruguay, Argentina, Chile, Mexico, Panama and others.

B. Citizenship by descent
Common interest: Italy, Ireland, Poland, Portugal, Spain, Hungary, Romania, Germany, Lithuania and others, depending on ancestry laws.

C. Citizenship by investment
Remaining options are mostly outside the EU, especially Caribbean programs and selected non-European states. EU CBI is under severe pressure.

Key comparison variables:
- Years to citizenship.
- Physical presence required.
- Language test.
- Integration/culture test.
- Dual citizenship allowed.
- Family inclusion.
- Tax consequences.
- Passport strength.
- Political stability of the program.
- Risk of future rule change.

8.8 Practical country-ranking framework

A useful platform should not rank countries universally. It should rank them by user profile.

Example profile-based rankings:
- Best for remote worker with USD 3,000/month.
- Best for family with school-age children and EU goal.
- Best for software engineer needing employer sponsorship.
- Best for founder with online business.
- Best for retiree needing healthcare and safety.
- Best for second passport within 5-7 years.
- Best for low-tax legal residence.
- Best for Ukrainian/Russian/Indian/Brazilian/South African/US citizen specifically.

The right country is profile-dependent, not universal.
