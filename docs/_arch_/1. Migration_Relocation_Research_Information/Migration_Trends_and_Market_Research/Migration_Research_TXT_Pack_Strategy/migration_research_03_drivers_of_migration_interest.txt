3. DRIVERS OF MIGRATION INTEREST

Research date: 16 June 2026

3.1 Why do people want to move abroad?

The most common reasons are:
- Better income and employment.
- Safety and lower crime.
- Political stability and personal freedom.
- Education for children or self.
- Healthcare and quality of life.
- Family reunification or relationship reasons.
- Escape from war, persecution, repression or discrimination.
- Better passport, citizenship or long-term legal security.
- Lower taxes or financial freedom.
- Climate, lifestyle and environment.
- Business opportunity, banking access and regulatory predictability.

Different user segments prioritise different things:
- Skilled workers: salary, sponsorship, career growth, recognition of credentials, permanent residence path.
- Students: university quality, post-study work rights, fees, scholarship access, job market after graduation.
- Families: safety, schools, healthcare, housing, language, citizenship path.
- Entrepreneurs: taxes, company formation, market access, banking, regulatory stability, residence rights.
- Retirees: healthcare, safety, cost of living, climate, residence rules.
- Digital nomads: visa simplicity, tax treatment, internet, rent, safety, time zone, community, lifestyle.
- Forced migrants: safety, asylum access, family networks, route feasibility.

3.2 Political instability and migration interest

Political instability is one of the strongest triggers of migration interest, even before people physically move.

Mechanisms:
- Elections can trigger search spikes for “move abroad”, “move to Canada”, “citizenship by descent” or “second passport”.
- Authoritarianisation increases interest among journalists, activists, entrepreneurs, minorities, academics and high-skilled people.
- War and mobilisation risk can create sudden emigration waves.
- Legal uncertainty motivates people to seek second passports or residence permits as insurance.

Evidence examples:
- Ukraine after 2022: massive forced displacement and temporary protection in Europe.
- Russia after 2022: emigration among skilled workers, entrepreneurs, activists and mobilisation-averse groups.
- U.S. election cycles: search spikes around leaving the country and moving to Canada; Gallup shows increased interest among U.S. women aged 15-44.
- Latin America: political instability and crime correlate with outward interest in many countries.

3.3 Economic crises and migration

Economic crises influence migration through:
- Inflation and falling real incomes.
- Currency depreciation.
- Unemployment and youth unemployment.
- Housing affordability collapse.
- Cuts to public services.
- Banking restrictions or capital controls.
- Lower confidence in future opportunity.

The strongest migration pressure appears when economic crisis combines with political instability, insecurity or weak institutions. A bad economy alone does not always produce migration if people cannot afford to leave or cannot get visas.

3.4 Safety and crime

Safety is central for families, women, retirees and high-income migrants.

Safety-driven decision factors:
- Violent crime rate.
- Rule of law and police effectiveness.
- Corruption and extortion risk.
- Discrimination and hate crime.
- War and terrorism risk.
- Domestic political violence.
- Neighbourhood-level safety.

For a migration platform, safety should not be represented by one generic score only. Users need:
- National safety indicators.
- City/neighbourhood differences.
- Gender and minority-specific risk.
- Scam/corruption risk.
- War/escalation risk.
- User reports with verification.

3.5 Taxes and financial freedom

Taxes matter strongly for entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, crypto/finance professionals and high earners.

Common tax-driven searches and decisions:
- Low-tax countries.
- Territorial tax systems.
- Non-dom regimes.
- Tax residency rules.
- Exit tax risk.
- Social security contributions.
- Double-tax treaties.
- CFC rules.
- Crypto tax.
- Wealth tax and inheritance tax.
- Offshore company formation.

Important product point:
Most users do not understand the difference between immigration residence and tax residence. A platform should separate:
- Visa/residence eligibility.
- Physical presence rules.
- Tax residency rules.
- Worldwide income taxation.
- Reporting obligations.
- Banking and AML friction.

3.6 Second passport and citizenship

Second passports are increasingly viewed as:
- Geopolitical insurance.
- Mobility insurance.
- A way to access education, healthcare and work rights.
- A hedge against authoritarianism, war or sanctions.
- A family legacy asset.
- A business and banking tool.

Main routes:
- Naturalisation after residence.
- Citizenship by descent / ancestry.
- Citizenship by marriage.
- Citizenship by investment, where still legal.
- Special talent or contribution routes.

Trend:
- Interest is rising, but “easy” routes are closing or tightening.
- EU citizenship-by-investment is under heavy pressure.
- Residence-to-citizenship timelines, language requirements and physical-presence rules are becoming decisive comparison variables.

3.7 Healthcare, education and quality of life for families

For families, migration decisions are multi-variable and risk-averse.

Key factors:
- School quality and language of instruction.
- Public vs private school costs.
- Healthcare access and insurance rules.
- Safety and child independence.
- Housing affordability and space.
- Spouse work rights.
- Childcare costs.
- Citizenship for children.
- Long-term residence security.
- Recognition of professional credentials.

Families are less likely to make decisions based only on online hype. They need structured risk reduction.

3.8 Climate and ecology

Climate influences migration in two different ways:

A. Forced or constrained climate mobility
- Drought, floods, heat, storms, crop failure and sea-level rise can push people to move internally or across borders.
- World Bank Groundswell scenarios estimate up to 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 across six regions without major climate/development action.
- Most climate migration is expected to be internal or regional, not long-distance lifestyle migration.

B. Lifestyle and quality-of-life climate migration
- Retirees, remote workers and families choose countries or cities based on weather, air quality, green space, water security, wildfire risk, heat risk and pollution.
- Climate risk is increasingly relevant for property buyers and long-term settlement.

For a platform:
Include both present comfort and future climate risk, including heat stress, flood risk, water scarcity, wildfire risk, air quality and insurance/housing effects.
