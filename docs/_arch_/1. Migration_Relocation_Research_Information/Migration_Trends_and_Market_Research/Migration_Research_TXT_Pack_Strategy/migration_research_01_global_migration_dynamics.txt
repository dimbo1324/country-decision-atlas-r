1. GENERAL MIGRATION DYNAMICS

Research date: 16 June 2026

1.1 Is the total number of international migrants rising or falling?

The long-term trend is rising. International migrant stock has roughly doubled since 1990.

Key data points:
- 1990: about 153-154 million international migrants worldwide.
- 2020: about 281 million international migrants worldwide.
- 2023: about 302 million international migrants worldwide, according to World Bank estimates based on UN DESA data.
- Mid-2024: about 304 million international migrants worldwide, about 3.7%-4% of world population.

Interpretation:
- The absolute number of migrants is clearly growing.
- The share of the world population living outside their country of birth has grown more slowly, from roughly 3% in 1990 to around 4% in 2024.
- Migration is not “mass movement by most people”; it remains a minority behaviour globally, but the absolute market is enormous.

Main evidence: UN DESA International Migrant Stock 2024; IOM World Migration Report; World Bank Migration and Development Brief 40; Migration Policy Institute Data Hub.

1.2 Regional distribution and strongest growth regions

Approximate distribution of international migrants by major UN region in 2024:
- Europe and Northern America: about 155.3 million, around 51% of the global migrant stock.
- Northern Africa and Western Asia: about 54.1 million, around 18%.
- Sub-Saharan Africa: about 24.3 million, around 8%.
- Eastern and South-Eastern Asia: about 22.6 million, around 7%.
- Central and Southern Asia: about 20.4 million, around 7%.
- Latin America and the Caribbean: about 17.5 million, around 6%.
- Australia and New Zealand: about 9.6 million, around 3%.
- Oceania excluding Australia/New Zealand: very small in global terms.

Regions showing strong growth or high migration pressure:
- Europe: large inflows from Ukraine, the Middle East, Africa, South Asia and intra-European mobility.
- Northern America: persistent attraction for workers, students, family migrants and asylum seekers.
- Gulf / Western Asia: labour migration to Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait and neighbouring economies remains central.
- Latin America: Venezuelan displacement and regional mobility changed migration patterns dramatically.
- Sub-Saharan Africa: high migration aspiration and high forced/internal displacement, though limited by money and visas.
- Asia: large origin region for workers and students; also destination region for Gulf, East Asian and regional labour markets.

1.3 Top countries people leave from

Top origin countries in recent UN DESA/MPI summaries include:
1. India: about 18.5 million emigrants.
2. China: about 11.7 million.
3. Mexico: about 11.6 million.
4. Ukraine: about 9.8 million.
5. Russia: about 9.1 million.
6. Syria: about 8.9 million.
7. Bangladesh: about 8.7 million.
8. Venezuela: about 8.3 million.

Interpretation:
- India, China, Mexico and Bangladesh are large population countries with long-standing labour, student and family migration systems.
- Ukraine, Syria and Venezuela show the importance of war, political instability and forced displacement.
- Russia appears both as origin and destination because of post-Soviet mobility, labour markets and recent geopolitical disruption.

1.4 Top countries people move to

Top destination countries in recent UN DESA/MPI summaries include:
1. United States: about 52.4 million international migrants.
2. Germany: about 16.8 million.
3. Saudi Arabia: about 13.7 million.
4. United Kingdom: about 11.8 million.
5. France: about 9.2 million.
6. Canada: about 8.8 million.
7. United Arab Emirates: about 8.2 million.
8. Australia: about 8.1 million.
9. Russia: about 7.6 million.
10. Türkiye: about 7.1 million.
11. Spain: about 6.9 million.
12. Italy: about 6.8 million.

The largest single bilateral corridor remains Mexico to the United States, with roughly 11 million people. Other major corridors include Syria-Türkiye, Ukraine-Russia, and India-UAE.

1.5 Directions that became more popular in the last five years

The strongest “newer” or accelerated routes include:
- Ukraine to EU countries, after Russia’s full-scale invasion in 2022.
- Venezuela to Colombia, Peru, Chile, Ecuador, Spain and the United States, though some flows have slowed or changed.
- India to Canada, the UK, Australia, Germany, the Gulf and the United States, especially through students, skilled work and family channels.
- Remote workers to Spain, Portugal, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Dubai/UAE, Thailand, Indonesia/Bali, Japan and New Zealand, depending on visa availability and lifestyle positioning.
- Skilled workers to Germany, Canada, Australia, the UK, the Netherlands, UAE and Singapore, though policy tightening now affects some routes.
- Wealth migration to UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Italy, Portugal, Greece, Malta, Caribbean CBI countries and selected Latin American countries.

1.6 Directions losing popularity or facing reduced inflow

Routes losing popularity or facing tightening:
- Canada student and temporary-resident routes: major caps and reduced targets after housing and infrastructure pressure.
- UK care-worker and lower-paid skilled-worker routes: new restrictions and higher settlement requirements.
- Australia student routes: stricter English, finance and visa integrity requirements.
- European property golden visas: Spain closed its golden visa; Portugal removed real-estate investment as a route; the Netherlands, UK and Ireland closed investor routes earlier; Malta’s CBI model was challenged by the EU court.
- Some post-pandemic “nomad hotspots” face backlash due to housing inflation, over-tourism and local resentment.

Important distinction:
- A route can lose legal accessibility while the country remains attractive.
- A country can remain popular online even as its real legal route becomes much harder.

1.7 Which migration type is growing fastest?

No single global category is perfectly comparable, but the strongest growth signals are:

A. Forced / humanitarian migration
- Very high and still historically elevated.
- UNHCR reported 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at end-2024.
- IDMC reported 83.4 million internally displaced people at end-2024.
- Conflicts in Ukraine, Sudan, Syria, Afghanistan, Gaza and other regions keep forced displacement central.

B. Temporary labour migration
- OECD reported more than 2.4 million work permits in 2023, up 16%.
- Health, care, construction, agriculture, logistics and hospitality are major demand sectors.

C. International student migration
- International tertiary students have tripled since 2000 to roughly 7 million-plus.
- Growth is now being capped or rebalanced in Canada, Australia and the UK.

D. Digital nomad / remote-work migration
- Very fast from a low base after 2020.
- The U.S. digital nomad population reached about 18.1 million in 2024 according to MBO Partners.
- More than 40 jurisdictions had some form of digital nomad or remote-work visa by mid-2024; some indexes track 60+ countries depending on definition.

E. Investment migration
- Growing among high-net-worth people, especially Americans, Chinese, Indians, Russians and politically exposed wealthy groups.
- But European golden visa / citizenship routes are being restricted, making this segment more complex.

F. Family migration
- Still the largest or one of the largest legal permanent categories in many OECD countries.
- OECD 2023 permanent-type flows were strongly driven by family and humanitarian migration.

Conclusion:
- Fastest by percentage growth: digital nomad/remote-work migration and some investment/second-passport demand.
- Fastest by humanitarian urgency: forced displacement and asylum.
- Largest structural categories: family, labour, students and regional migration.
