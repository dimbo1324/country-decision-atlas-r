7. LEGAL AND POLITICAL TRENDS

Research date: 16 June 2026

7.1 Countries simplifying or opening migration rules

Many countries are simplifying selectively, not universally.

Examples:
- Germany: expanded skilled-worker law and Opportunity Card for qualified jobseekers.
- New Zealand: from 27 January 2025, visitor visas allow remote work for foreign employers/clients, subject to conditions.
- Japan: introduced a digital nomad visa with high income and insurance requirements.
- Spain, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Portugal and others: use digital nomad/freelancer/residence routes to attract remote workers, though tax and housing politics differ.
- UAE: continues to attract investors, founders, skilled workers and remote workers through residence products and low-tax positioning.
- Saudi Arabia: competing for professionals and investors as part of economic diversification.
- Canada, Australia and the UK: still have skilled-worker/talent routes, even while tightening student, family or lower-wage routes.

7.2 Countries tightening migration rules

Major tightening examples:

Canada:
- Permanent resident admissions targets reduced in the 2025-2027 plan: 395,000 in 2025; 380,000 in 2026; 365,000 in 2027.
- Temporary resident targets introduced, with a goal to reduce temporary residents as a share of population.
- Study permit caps reduced and extended.
- 2024 saw a sharp fall in new study permits compared with 2023.

United Kingdom:
- 2025 White Paper proposed stricter settlement/citizenship path, including extending many settlement timelines from 5 to 10 years, with exceptions for high-skilled contributors.
- Skilled Worker restrictions moved toward graduate-level roles.
- Care-sector international recruitment was targeted for restriction.
- Family/dependent rules and English requirements became tougher.

Australia:
- Stricter student-visa requirements, including English, financial capacity and visa integrity rules.
- Higher fees and more scrutiny of low-quality education-to-work pathways.

European Union / Schengen:
- Increased scrutiny of golden visas, AML risk, citizenship-by-investment and security concerns.

7.3 Golden visa and citizenship-by-investment restrictions

Strong trend: Europe is closing or restricting passive investment routes.

Examples:
- Spain closed its golden visa in 2025.
- Portugal removed real-estate investment as an eligible golden visa route; other non-property routes remained.
- Netherlands ended its investor residence program.
- UK and Ireland closed investor routes earlier.
- Malta’s citizenship-by-investment model was ruled incompatible with EU law by the Court of Justice of the EU.

Interpretation:
- Passive real-estate golden visas are politically weak because they are linked to housing affordability, money laundering and inequality.
- Future investor migration will likely require more substance: business creation, jobs, innovation, donations, funds or tax residence.

7.4 Countries becoming more attractive to remote workers

Attractive remote-worker destinations combine visa access, tax clarity, lifestyle and infrastructure.

Examples:
- Spain: digital nomad visa, strong lifestyle appeal, high quality-of-life reputation.
- Portugal: still attractive, though housing and golden visa changes reduced some appeal.
- Italy: digital nomad visa and favourable tax regimes for some categories.
- Greece and Croatia: digital nomad/lifestyle positioning.
- UAE/Dubai: remote-work and business-friendly environment, low personal tax appeal.
- Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia: affordability, climate, regional appeal.
- Japan: high-income digital nomad visa; attractive but short and restrictive.
- New Zealand: remote work allowed on visitor visas applied for after 27 January 2025, increasing flexibility.

7.5 Countries becoming less migrant-friendly

Signals of declining friendliness:
- Higher salary thresholds.
- Lower study permit caps.
- Longer settlement timelines.
- Restrictions on dependants.
- Closure of investor routes.
- Anti-immigration rhetoric.
- Poor housing affordability.
- Stricter asylum or irregular migration policies.
- Lower public tolerance due to housing, healthcare or infrastructure strain.

Countries with visible tightening or social pressure include Canada, the UK, Australia, parts of the EU, and some over-tourism/nomad hotspots.

7.6 Legislative changes that most affect country choice

Most important legal variables:
- Visa eligibility and processing time.
- Work rights.
- Spouse/dependent rights.
- Student-to-work transition.
- Permanent residence timeline.
- Citizenship timeline.
- Language requirements.
- Physical-presence requirements.
- Tax residency rules.
- Social-security contributions.
- Banking/AML rules.
- Housing/rent regulation.
- Labour-market regulation.
- Business formation rules.
- Capital controls and sanctions.
- Dual citizenship policy.

For users, a country can become unattractive because of a small rule change if that rule blocks the exact pathway they need.

7.7 Can we determine a country’s direction from laws?

Yes, with a structured “country direction index”.

Open/pro-migrant signals:
- New skilled-worker/talent routes.
- Lower administrative friction.
- Faster processing.
- Clear family reunification.
- Work rights for spouses.
- Transparent permanent residence rules.
- Dual citizenship acceptance.
- Tax clarity for newcomers.
- Startup/founder incentives.
- Remote-work visa or freelancer route.

Closed/restrictive signals:
- Caps and quotas.
- Higher salary thresholds.
- Longer settlement/citizenship timelines.
- More language tests.
- Reduced dependants.
- Closure of student-to-work routes.
- Restrictions on property/investor visas.
- Anti-immigration legislation.
- More deportation/overstay penalties.
- Harder bank account opening.

Authoritarian/liberal direction signals:
- Judicial independence.
- Media freedom.
- NGO/civil-society restrictions.
- Emergency powers.
- Restrictions on protest.
- Discrimination laws.
- Internet censorship.
- Exit controls or military mobilisation risk.

Pro-business/anti-business signals:
- Company formation time/cost.
- Tax stability.
- Labour-market flexibility.
- Capital controls.
- Banking openness.
- Crypto/fintech policy.
- Founder visa availability.
- Investor protection.
- Regulatory predictability.

Platform implication:
Law tracking should not only list rules. It should detect direction: opening, closing, stable, unstable, or contradictory.
