5. SOCIAL MEDIA AND USER BEHAVIOUR
Research date: 16 June 2026

5.1 Are migration channels, chats, and communities growing on Telegram, Reddit, Facebook, and YouTube?

Answer: Migration communities are clearly active and numerous, but there is no single reliable public global counter across Telegram, Reddit, Facebook, and YouTube.

Observed pattern:
- Reddit has active communities around expats, Americans moving abroad, country-specific relocation, visas, and digital nomadism.
- Facebook groups remain important for local expat help, housing leads, schools, and city-specific questions.
- Telegram is especially important for Russian-speaking, Ukrainian, Belarusian, regional, crypto, IT, and war/politics-driven relocation communities, but public data is fragmented.
- YouTube and TikTok strongly shape country perception through “how I moved,” cost-of-living, and lifestyle content.

Measurement recommendation:
Track this with:
1. Number of communities per destination and language.
2. Member/subscriber counts.
3. Posts per day/week.
4. Comment volume.
5. New-post growth.
6. Top repeated questions.
7. Sentiment and complaint frequency.
8. Law-change mentions.
9. Referral links to lawyers/relocation agencies.

5.2 What topics are discussed most often in migration communities?

Common topics:
- Visa documents and appointments.
- Residence permits and renewals.
- Work permits and employer sponsorship.
- Housing, rent, deposits, scams.
- Jobs and salary expectations.
- Taxes and tax residency.
- Banks, cards, money transfers, proof of address.
- Schools, childcare, universities.
- Healthcare and insurance.
- Driving licence, car registration, pets.
- Language learning and integration.
- Local bureaucracy.
- Safety and neighbourhoods.
- Cost of living.
- Mistakes, refusals, delays, and bad lawyers.

5.3 What questions do people ask most often before moving?

Repeated pre-move questions:
- Can I legally move with my passport, job, income, and family situation?
- Which visa is best for me?
- How much money do I really need before arrival?
- Can my spouse work?
- Can my child go to public school?
- How long does the application take?
- What documents need apostille/translation?
- What happens if the law changes during my application?
- Which city is safe and affordable?
- How difficult is it to find housing?
- How do I open a bank account?
- Do I need a lawyer?
- What are the hidden costs?
- Can I get permanent residence or citizenship later?

5.4 What problems do people most often experience after moving?

Common post-move problems:
- Housing shortage and high rents.
- Underestimated cost of living.
- Visa delays and appointment shortages.
- Confusing bureaucracy.
- Bank-account or tax-number problems.
- Job-search difficulty despite a valid visa.
- Language barrier.
- Non-recognition of qualifications.
- School/childcare shortage.
- Healthcare waiting times.
- Loneliness and loss of social network.
- Cultural mismatch.
- Tax surprises.
- Scams by landlords, recruiters, or “visa helpers.”
- Realising that online hype did not match lived reality.

5.5 Which sources do people trust most when choosing a country?

Trust hierarchy:
1. Official government websites for legal eligibility, fees, forms, and deadlines.
2. Immigration lawyers or regulated advisers for complex cases.
3. Tax professionals for tax-residency and company-structure questions.
4. People who recently completed the same pathway.
5. Reddit/Facebook/Telegram for practical lived experience.
6. YouTube/TikTok/blogs for lifestyle perception.
7. Relocation agencies for execution, housing, schools, and local setup.

Platform implication:
A platform should not replace official sources. It should translate them into structured decisions and keep official links visible.

5.6 Where do people receive outdated or contradictory information?

Weak information channels:
- Old blog posts that do not update law changes.
- YouTube videos made before visa/tax changes.
- Telegram and Facebook messages without dates or sources.
- Lawyer/agency landing pages that simplify eligibility to sell calls.
- SEO articles that target “easy visa” keywords but omit rejection risks.
- Crowd-sourced cost data that is not city-, date-, or lifestyle-specific.
- Forum answers based on another person's passport, income, or family situation.

Product opportunity:
Add “source age,” “last law check,” “official link,” “applies to your case / does not apply,” and “confidence level” to every answer.
