8. COUNTRIES AND COMPETITION BETWEEN DESTINATIONS
Research date: 16 June 2026

8.1 Which countries actively compete for migrants, specialists, and investors?

Talent and skilled workers:
- Germany: skilled immigration reforms and Opportunity Card.
- EU countries using Blue Card and shortage-occupation routes.
- Canada: still talent-focused, despite temporary-resident and student tightening.
- Australia and New Zealand: selective skilled migration, though rules are stricter than before.
- United Kingdom: skilled work remains important, but thresholds and categories tightened.
- Japan: selective opening because of ageing and labour shortages.
- Gulf states: UAE and Saudi Arabia compete for professionals, founders, and capital.

Entrepreneurs and startups:
- Canada, UK, France, Netherlands, Germany, Estonia, Portugal, Singapore, UAE, and other startup-visa jurisdictions.

Investors and high-net-worth individuals:
- UAE, United States, Italy, Switzerland, Singapore, Monaco, Malta, Portugal/Greece alternatives, and selected Caribbean jurisdictions.

Sources: OECD; Germany/EU official pages; StartupBlink; Henley & Partners. [S5][S20][S21][S31][S30]

8.2 Which countries are losing attractiveness for migrants?

Examples and reasons:
- Canada: housing affordability, student caps, temporary-resident reductions, lower PR targets.
- United Kingdom: stricter immigration rules, high cost of living, tax/political uncertainty for wealthy residents.
- Australia: student visa tightening and high housing costs.
- Portugal: housing pressure, tax regime changes, golden-visa restrictions, processing backlogs.
- Spain: housing protests/pressure in some areas and golden-visa closure, although still highly attractive for lifestyle.
- Netherlands: housing shortage, healthcare waiting concerns, and bureaucracy despite high quality of life.
- Germany: attractive jobs but language, bureaucracy, housing, and credential recognition can reduce user satisfaction.

Interpretation:
A country can remain objectively attractive while becoming less attractive at the margin because expectations rise faster than practical capacity.

Sources: Canada/UK policy sources; InterNations/expat reports; Spain/Portugal legal changes; OECD. [S17][S18][S22][S5]

8.3 Which countries are becoming new popular destinations?

Emerging or rising destinations by segment:
- Digital nomads: Thailand, Japan, Italy, Spain, Portugal, Croatia, Estonia, Greece, Colombia, Mexico, UAE.
- Skilled workers: Germany, Czechia, Ireland, Japan, selected Nordic countries, Canada, Australia, New Zealand.
- Founders: UAE, Singapore, Estonia, Portugal, UK, Canada, France, Netherlands, Germany.
- High-net-worth individuals: UAE, United States, Italy, Singapore, Switzerland, Costa Rica, Malta, Monaco.
- Budget/lifestyle migrants: Mexico, Colombia, Thailand, Malaysia, Georgia, Albania, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay; each requires careful visa, tax, safety, and healthcare analysis.

Caution:
“Popular online” does not always mean “good legal fit.” Emerging destinations often lack clear long-term residence and citizenship routes.

Sources: EY; MBO; StartupBlink; Henley & Partners; Google/search-demand reports. [S14][S13][S31][S30][S29]

8.4 Which countries are overrated or underrated by migrants?

Overrated online, not necessarily bad:
- Portugal: strong lifestyle brand, but housing, processing backlogs, and tax/golden-visa changes reduce simplicity.
- Spain: excellent lifestyle, but housing pressure, taxation, and bureaucracy can surprise users.
- Canada: strong brand and citizenship path, but housing costs and policy tightening are major frictions.
- Netherlands: strong economy and English use, but housing and healthcare access complaints are frequent.
- Dubai/UAE: strong tax/business appeal, but long-term citizenship integration is limited and lifestyle costs can be high.

Potentially underrated, depending on user profile:
- Germany for skilled workers willing to learn German and handle bureaucracy.
- Japan for selected remote workers and professionals, though language and integration are hard.
- Uruguay for stability and quality of life in Latin America, though job market size is limited.
- Paraguay/Georgia/Albania for budget/legal simplicity in some cases, but lower institutional and market depth.
- Malaysia/Thailand for lifestyle and cost, but permanent residence/citizenship may be difficult.

Important:
These are positioning hypotheses. A platform should score each country for specific user profiles rather than label a country universally good or bad.

8.5 Which countries do people with limited budgets choose?

Common affordable destination clusters:
- Latin America: Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Ecuador.
- Southeast Asia: Thailand, Malaysia, Vietnam, Indonesia/Bali, Philippines.
- Eastern/Southeastern Europe: Georgia, Albania, Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania, Montenegro.
- North Africa / Middle East niches: Türkiye, Morocco, Armenia, depending on passport and risk tolerance.

Decision factors:
- Low rent is not enough. Users need visa stability, healthcare access, safety, banking, tax clarity, and realistic income.
- A cheap destination can become expensive if the user must do visa runs, pay private school fees, or cannot access healthcare.

8.6 Which countries do entrepreneurs and startup founders choose?

Common founder-friendly destinations:
- UAE/Dubai: tax/business positioning and international networks.
- Singapore: strong business environment, expensive and selective.
- Estonia: digital infrastructure and startup brand.
- UK: strong startup ecosystem, but immigration and costs matter.
- Canada: startup and skilled routes, but processing and housing concerns.
- Portugal/Spain: lifestyle plus founder/remote work appeal, with tax and bureaucracy complexity.
- Germany/France/Netherlands: large EU market, funding, talent, but bureaucracy and language can matter.
- United States: strongest venture ecosystem, but immigration is difficult and uncertain for many founders.

Sources: StartupBlink; OECD; national startup/talent visa pages. [S31][S5]

8.7 Which countries do people choose for a second passport?

Common route categories:
- Ancestry citizenship: Italy, Ireland, Poland, Hungary, and other countries depending on family history.
- Residence-to-citizenship: Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Portugal, Spain, Germany, Ireland, France, Latin American countries; timelines and requirements differ substantially.
- Citizenship by investment: selected Caribbean states and other non-EU programs; EU models are under pressure.
- Marriage/family routes: country-specific.
- Special talent/contribution routes: rare but valuable.

Key comparison variables:
- Time to citizenship.
- Physical presence requirement.
- Language/culture test.
- Dual-citizenship permission.
- Tax consequences.
- Stability of law.
- Family inclusion.
- Cost and rejection risk.

Sources: Henley & Partners; official citizenship laws should be used country by country; Spain/Malta examples show policy risk. [S30][S22][S23]
