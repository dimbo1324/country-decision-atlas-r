1. GENERAL MIGRATION DYNAMICS
Research date: 16 June 2026

1.1 Is the total number of international migrants rising or falling?

Answer: Rising in absolute terms.

Key data:
- 1990: approximately 154 million international migrants.
- 2000: approximately 173 million.
- 2010: approximately 220-221 million.
- 2015: approximately 249 million.
- 2020: approximately 281 million.
- 2024: approximately 304 million.

Interpretation:
The global migrant stock has almost doubled since 1990. The increase over the last 10-20 years is substantial in absolute numbers, but the migrant share of the world population remains relatively small: around 3.7% in 2024. This means migration is a large market, but most people still live in their country of birth.

Sources: UN DESA International Migrant Stock 2024; Migration Data Portal; IOM World Migration Report. [S1][S2][S4]

1.2 Which world regions show the strongest migration growth?

Answer: By migrant stock, Europe, Northern America, and Northern Africa/Western Asia dominate. By migrant share of population, Oceania, Northern America, and Europe stand out.

2024 host-region stock:
- Europe: about 94 million migrants.
- Northern America: about 61 million.
- Northern Africa and Western Asia: about 54 million.

Growth since 1990:
- Europe recorded the largest absolute increase, around +43 million migrants.
- Northern America added around +34 million migrants since 1990, although its growth rate slowed after 2010 and especially after 2020.
- Northern Africa and Western Asia grew strongly because of Gulf labour migration and displacement-related flows.

Migrant share of population:
- Oceania: around 21%.
- Northern America: around 16%.
- Europe: around 13%.
- Central/Southern Asia and East/Southeast Asia: below 1%.

Sources: UN DESA International Migrant Stock 2024. [S1]

1.3 From which countries do people most often leave?

Answer: India is the largest origin country by diaspora stock.

Top origin countries by migrant stock include:
1. India: about 18.5 million people living abroad.
2. China: about 11.7 million.
3. Mexico: about 11.6 million.
4. Ukraine: about 9.8 million.
5. Russian Federation: about 9.1 million.

Other major origin countries often include Syria, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Afghanistan, and the Philippines, depending on exact year and definition.

Interpretation:
Large origin countries can be large because of population size, labour corridors, conflict, colonial/language ties, education pathways, and established diaspora networks. Ukraine's position rose sharply after the 2022 full-scale Russian invasion.

Sources: Migration Policy Institute; UN DESA. [S3][S1]

1.4 To which countries do people most often move?

Answer: The United States is by far the largest destination country by migrant stock.

Top destination countries in 2024:
1. United States: 52.4 million.
2. Germany: 16.8 million.
3. Saudi Arabia: 13.7 million.
4. United Kingdom: 11.8 million.
5. France: 9.2 million.
6. Spain: 8.9 million.
7. Canada: 8.8 million.
8. United Arab Emirates: 8.2 million.
9. Australia: 8.1 million.
10. Russian Federation: 7.6 million.
11. Türkiye: 7.1 million.
12. Italy: 6.6 million.

Sources: UN DESA International Migrant Stock 2024. [S1]

1.5 Which migration routes became more popular in the last five years?

Answer: The most important newer or accelerating routes include conflict-driven, labour-driven, student-driven, and remote-work routes.

Major examples:
- Ukraine to Europe: especially Poland, Germany, Czechia, and other EU countries after 2022.
- Venezuela and other Latin American origins to the United States, Spain, Colombia, Chile, and other destinations.
- Central America and Caribbean origins to the United States and Mexico, including asylum-related flows.
- Central/Southern Asia to the Gulf, Europe, Canada, the UK, and Australia through work and study channels.
- Skilled workers to Germany and other EU countries through Blue Card-style and shortage-occupation routes.
- Remote workers and digital nomads to Portugal, Spain, Italy, Croatia, Estonia, Greece, Thailand, Japan, the UAE, Colombia, Mexico, and similar destinations.
- High-net-worth individuals to the UAE, the United States, Italy, Switzerland, Singapore, and selected tax/residence-friendly jurisdictions.

Evidence:
UN DESA identifies major interregional corridors such as Latin America/Caribbean to Northern America, Central/Southern Asia to Northern Africa/Western Asia, and Northern Africa/Western Asia to Europe. OECD data shows historically high permanent-type immigration in 2023 and still-high flows in 2024. Digital-nomad visa programs spread rapidly after 2020. [S1][S5][S14]

1.6 Which directions are losing popularity?

Answer: Some destinations remain large but are losing marginal attractiveness because of policy tightening, housing costs, backlogs, or reduced public perception.

Examples:
- United States: still the top desired destination, but Gallup reports its share among preferred destinations fell to a trend low of 15% in 2025.
- Canada: still highly attractive, but student and temporary-resident caps, housing pressure, and reduced permanent-resident targets may reduce inflows or interest among some groups.
- United Kingdom: tightening after the 2024-2025 rule changes affected students' dependants, salary thresholds, social-care recruitment, and settlement expectations.
- Australia: tighter student-visa conditions and higher English/financial requirements reduce low-confidence student pathways.
- EU golden-visa routes: Spain ended its real-estate golden visa; Malta's citizenship-by-investment scheme was ruled illegal by the EU Court of Justice; Portugal and Greece have also tightened or redirected investment routes.
- Some classic “cheap nomad” destinations may lose appeal when rents, visa runs, or tax enforcement become stricter.

Sources: Gallup; Government of Canada; UK Government; UK House of Commons Library; Australian policy updates; Spain/Malta reports. [S11][S17][S18][S19][S22][S23]

1.7 Which type of migration is growing fastest: labour, study, family, investment, forced, or digital nomad?

Answer: It depends on the measurement. Forced migration and digital-nomad migration are the fastest-growing in recent salience; labour migration remains the largest; family migration is the largest permanent-admission category in the OECD.

By category:
- Labour migration: ILO estimated 167.7 million international migrant workers in 2022. It is the largest functional category.
- Family migration: OECD reports family migration as the leading permanent migration channel in the OECD, representing roughly 44% of permanent flows in 2024.
- Humanitarian/forced migration: this category rose sharply because of Ukraine, Syria, Afghanistan, Sudan, Venezuela, and other crises. UN DESA estimates people in international protection categories reached around 51.7 million by mid-2024.
- Student migration: long-term growth is strong; UNESCO says internationally mobile higher-education students tripled since 2000 to about 6.9 million. But OECD-area student inflows recently softened after policy tightening.
- Investment migration: demand exists among high-net-worth individuals, but EU-style golden visas/passports are being restricted.
- Digital nomad migration: fastest by percentage in lifestyle terms. MBO estimates U.S. digital nomads increased 153% since 2019, while EY counted more than 48 jurisdictions with remote-work visa schemes by early 2026. However, digital nomads are not always counted as migrants in official statistics.

Sources: ILO; OECD; UN DESA; UNESCO; MBO Partners; EY. [S6][S5][S1][S7][S13][S14]
