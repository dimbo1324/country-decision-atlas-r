7. LEGISLATIVE AND POLICY TRENDS
Research date: 16 June 2026

7.1 Which countries are simplifying migration rules?

Examples:
- Germany: Skilled Immigration Act reforms, broader EU Blue Card access, and Opportunity Card for job search.
- European Union: EU Blue Card remains a major highly qualified worker route across most EU countries.
- New Zealand: changes to Accredited Employer Work Visa settings in 2024-2025 reduced some employer/work-experience burdens.
- Spain: seasonal-work authorisations were expanded in duration under OECD-reported changes.
- Japan: introduced a digital-nomad style status for eligible remote workers in 2024.
- Thailand: expanded long-stay options including the Destination Thailand Visa for remote workers and related categories.
- Many countries: digital-nomad/remote-work visas spread from 2020 onward, with EY counting more than 48 jurisdictions by early 2026.

Sources: Germany Make it in Germany; European Commission; OECD; Japan MOFA; Reuters; EY. [S20][S21][S5][S15][S16][S14]

7.2 Which countries are tightening migration rules?

Examples:
- Canada: reduced permanent-resident targets for 2025-2027 and introduced targets to reduce the temporary-resident share of population.
- United Kingdom: 2025 policy agenda tightened salary, skills, dependants, care-worker recruitment, and settlement expectations.
- Australia: tightened student-visa rules, English requirements, financial requirements, and onshore application settings.
- EU asylum policy: the EU Pact on Migration and Asylum moves toward stricter border/asylum processing and solidarity mechanisms.
- Some European countries are tightening family, asylum, citizenship, or integration rules because of housing pressure, political pressure, and social tensions.

Sources: Government of Canada; UK Government; UK House of Commons Library; Australia Department of Home Affairs / Study Australia; EU policy reporting. [S17][S18][S19]

7.3 Which countries are closing or limiting golden visa / citizenship-by-investment programs?

Examples:
- Spain: ended its real-estate golden visa route in 2025.
- Malta: the EU Court of Justice ruled Malta's golden-passport scheme illegal in 2025.
- Portugal: redirected its golden visa away from residential real estate and experienced serious processing backlogs.
- Greece: raised investment thresholds in some areas.
- The EU continues to pressure member states over citizenship-by-investment and security concerns.

Interpretation:
Investment migration is not disappearing, but it is becoming more compliance-heavy, more expensive, and more politically sensitive.

Sources: Spain/Malta reports; EU policy context; Henley & Partners. [S22][S23][S30]

7.4 Which countries are becoming more attractive for remote workers?

Examples:
- Thailand: Destination Thailand Visa / longer-stay options.
- Japan: six-month digital-nomad status.
- Italy: digital-nomad visa launched in 2024.
- Spain and Portugal: still popular because of lifestyle, climate, communities, and remote-worker pathways, though housing and tax issues matter.
- Croatia, Estonia, Greece, Malta, and other European states: digital-nomad or remote-work visas.
- UAE/Dubai: tax and business-friendly positioning for remote workers and founders.
- Colombia, Mexico, Costa Rica, Ecuador, and other Latin American destinations: lifestyle and cost appeal, but safety and legal details vary.

Sources: EY; Japan MOFA; Reuters; Google/search trend reporting. [S14][S15][S16][S29]

7.5 Which countries are becoming less friendly to migrants?

Answer: This depends on migrant category. A country can be friendly to skilled workers and hostile to asylum seekers or low-wage migration at the same time.

Examples of less friendly signals:
- Lower caps or targets.
- Higher salary thresholds.
- Fewer dependant rights.
- More difficult family reunification.
- Longer settlement timelines.
- Higher fees.
- Stricter student visa rules.
- More enforcement against irregular migration.
- Anti-immigration political rhetoric.
- Housing policies restricting foreigners or investors.

Countries with notable recent tightening signals include Canada, the UK, Australia, and several European countries under asylum/housing/political pressure. [S17][S18][S19]

7.6 Which legal changes most affect country choice?

Most important legal categories:
1. Visa eligibility and quotas.
2. Work rights.
3. Spouse/dependant rights.
4. Student-to-work transition rules.
5. Permanent residence timeline.
6. Citizenship timeline and dual-citizenship rules.
7. Tax residency and worldwide-income taxation.
8. Non-dom, expat, or special tax regimes.
9. Housing/rent/property rules.
10. Banking and KYC rules.
11. Recognition of qualifications.
12. Healthcare access.
13. School access.
14. Military-service or exit restrictions in origin countries.
15. Political/asylum/family reunification rules.

7.7 Can we determine a country's direction from its laws?

Answer: Yes, approximately, by creating a Country Direction Index.

Recommended indicators:
- Migration openness: number and quality of legal pathways.
- Selectivity: skilled/founder/student/family/asylum treatment.
- Stability: how often rules change and whether changes are retroactive.
- Tax direction: higher/lower burden, special regimes, enforcement.
- Business direction: company setup, banking, permits, remote-work legality.
- Civil liberties: speech, media, LGBTQ/women/minority safety.
- Housing pressure: rent controls, shortages, anti-foreigner property rules.
- Bureaucracy: digitisation, processing time, appointment availability.
- Rule of law: corruption, court reliability, arbitrary enforcement.
- Social acceptance: rhetoric, hate-crime trends, integration requirements.

Output:
Classify countries as:
- Opening to talent.
- Opening to remote workers.
- Opening to capital.
- Closing to low-wage migration.
- Closing to asylum/family migration.
- Politically unstable.
- Tax tightening.
- Housing constrained.
- High opportunity / high bureaucracy.
