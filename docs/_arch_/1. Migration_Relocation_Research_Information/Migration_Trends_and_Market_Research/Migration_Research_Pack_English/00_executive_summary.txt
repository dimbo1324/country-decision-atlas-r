GLOBAL MIGRATION AND RELOCATION PLATFORM RESEARCH PACK
Executive summary
Research date: 16 June 2026
Language: English

Purpose
This pack answers the research questions about global migration dynamics, migration interest, causes of relocation demand, search and social behavior, willingness to pay, legal trends, country competition, and product opportunities for a migration/relocation decision platform.

Top findings

1. The total number of international migrants is still rising in absolute terms.
UN DESA estimates about 304 million international migrants in 2024, nearly double the 154 million estimate for 1990. As a share of world population, the rise is more moderate: about 3.7% in 2024 versus 2.9% in 1990. The market is therefore large and growing, but not exploding as a share of humanity. [S1][S2]

2. Europe, Northern America, and Northern Africa/Western Asia are the largest host regions by stock.
In 2024, Europe hosted around 94 million migrants, Northern America around 61 million, and Northern Africa/Western Asia around 54 million. Europe showed the largest absolute increase since 1990, partly accelerated by refugee flows from Ukraine after 2022. [S1]

3. The United States remains the largest destination country, but its pull has weakened in public desire surveys.
The United States hosted about 52.4 million international migrants in 2024, far ahead of Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, France, Spain, Canada, the UAE, Australia, Russia, Türkiye, and Italy. But Gallup reports that the share of would-be migrants naming the U.S. as their preferred destination fell to 15% in 2025, the lowest level in Gallup's trend since 2007. [S1][S11]

4. India is the largest origin country by diaspora stock.
MPI, using UN-style migrant stock data, identifies India as the largest origin country, with about 18.5 million people born in India living abroad. China, Mexico, Ukraine, and Russia are also among the largest origin countries. [S3]

5. Forced and humanitarian migration has grown especially fast.
UN DESA estimates roughly 38 million refugees, 8 million asylum seekers, and nearly 6 million other people in need of international protection in mid-2024. This group represented around one in six international migrants globally, the highest share since the 1950s. UNHCR reported 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at the end of 2024, including internally displaced people. [S1][S8]

6. Labour migration remains the biggest measurable functional category.
ILO estimated 167.7 million international migrants in the global labour force in 2022. Labour migration is therefore not necessarily the fastest-growing category, but it is the largest directly economic category. [S6]

7. Student migration has grown strongly over the long term, but some major destinations tightened rules recently.
UNESCO reports that internationally mobile higher-education students have tripled since 2000, reaching roughly 6.9 million. OECD countries received more than 2.1 million first study permits in 2023, but recent tightening in Canada, Australia, and the UK suggests student migration is becoming more selective. [S5][S7]

8. Digital-nomad migration is one of the fastest-growing lifestyle categories, though it is still hard to measure as migration stock.
MBO Partners estimated 18.5 million U.S. digital nomads in 2025, up 153% since 2019. EY counted more than 40 jurisdictions with digital-nomad or remote-work visa schemes in 2024 and more than 48 by early 2026. [S13][S14]

9. Desire to migrate is high but not equal to actual migration.
Gallup estimated that 16% of adults worldwide, more than 900 million people, wanted to move permanently to another country in 2023. Gallup also stresses that wanting to move is not the same as planning, qualifying for, or completing a move. [S9]

10. Legal trends are bifurcating.
Many countries are opening routes for skilled workers, digital nomads, founders, and selected investors, while tightening asylum, low-wage labour, family, student, or golden-visa pathways. Germany's Skilled Immigration Act and Opportunity Card are examples of talent opening. Canada, the UK, and Australia show recent tightening in temporary residents, students, salary thresholds, dependants, or lower-skilled recruitment. Spain and Malta show retrenchment in golden-visa/passport models. [S17][S18][S20][S22][S23]

11. The biggest information problem is not lack of content; it is lack of structured, current, personalised, evidence-backed decision support.
Nomad List, Numbeo, Expat.com, Reddit, Telegram, YouTube, government websites, immigration lawyers, tax advisers, and relocation agencies each solve part of the problem. Few products combine personal eligibility, legal trend tracking, risk scoring, cost modelling, official-source evidence, and verified real-user cases. [S32][S33][S34]

12. The strongest product opportunity is a decision engine, not another country blog.
A differentiated platform should produce: a country shortlist, legal route map, rejection-risk estimate, cost and tax model, family feasibility check, safety and healthcare/school profile, policy-direction summary, document checklist, and confidence score with official sources.

Platform thesis
A strong migration platform can monetise personalised analysis rather than generic information. Users already pay for lawyer calls, relocation packages, visa filing, country reports, tax advice, and expert consultations. A product that reduces uncertainty before users spend thousands of dollars on lawyers, tickets, deposits, schools, or failed applications has credible willingness-to-pay potential.

Best first product direction
Build a structured country-selection and pathway-analysis system for three high-intent B2C segments:
1. Skilled professionals and remote workers.
2. Families considering relocation.
3. Entrepreneurs / investors / second-passport seekers.

Best differentiators
- Evidence layer: every legal rule linked to an official source and dated.
- Personal fit engine: passport, income, profession, family, budget, risk tolerance.
- Country Interest Index: search, visa, social, flow, and content signals.
- Country Direction Index: laws, taxes, political signals, rights, bureaucracy, housing pressure.
- Risk scoring: legal, financial, tax, housing, safety, political, and lifestyle risks.
- Verified case library: real timelines, costs, refusals, and mistakes.

Important limitations
1. Google Trends does not provide a single public global historical table for every query unless the analyst exports query-level data. This pack therefore uses public trend snapshots and recommends a repeatable measurement framework.
2. Telegram, Facebook, Reddit, and YouTube do not provide one reliable public global counter for migration communities. This pack uses observable community themes and recommends API/search-based measurement.
3. Commercial market-size figures vary by provider and methodology. They should be treated as directional, not as audited market statistics.
