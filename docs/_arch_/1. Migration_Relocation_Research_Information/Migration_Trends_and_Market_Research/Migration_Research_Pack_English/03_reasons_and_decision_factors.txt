3. REASONS BEHIND MIGRATION INTEREST
Research date: 16 June 2026

3.1 Why do people most often want to move abroad?

Answer: The most common reasons are economic opportunity, safety, political stability, family, education, healthcare, quality of life, legal status/passport access, and climate/lifestyle.

Core categories:
1. Work and income: higher wages, better jobs, lower unemployment, career growth.
2. Safety: war, crime, persecution, domestic violence, state repression.
3. Political conditions: authoritarianism, instability, elections, rights restrictions.
4. Education: university access, children's schooling, language and international credentials.
5. Family: reunification, marriage, dependants, family support networks.
6. Healthcare: better medical systems, insurance access, disability/eldercare support.
7. Quality of life: infrastructure, clean environment, transport, housing, work-life balance.
8. Passport/citizenship: mobility, legal security, second-passport optionality.
9. Taxes/financial freedom: especially for founders, investors, and remote workers.
10. Climate/lifestyle: weather, pollution, heat, disasters, or desire for a specific lifestyle.

Sources: European Parliament; OECD; Gallup; World Bank; UNHCR; IDMC. [S26][S5][S9][S24][S8][S25]

3.2 How strongly does political instability affect migration interest?

Answer: Strongly, especially when instability threatens safety, rights, income, or future predictability.

Evidence:
- Ukraine, Syria, Afghanistan, Sudan, and Venezuela show that political conflict and state crisis can rapidly transform migration flows.
- Gallup found that Lebanon's desire to emigrate rose from 26% in 2018 to 63% in 2021 during severe economic and political crisis.
- After the 2024 U.S. election, public search data showed very large spikes in searches related to moving abroad, especially to Canada, Australia, and other English-speaking destinations.
- Gallup reported a sharp rise in desire to leave among U.S. women aged 15-44, with political attitudes and leadership approval connected to this trend.

Platform implication:
A platform should track political events as early signals, but distinguish temporary panic-search spikes from actual visa-qualified relocation demand.

Sources: UN DESA; Gallup; Sky News; UNHCR. [S1][S10][S11][S28][S8]

3.3 How strongly do economic crises affect migration?

Answer: Strongly, but with a delay and with unequal ability to move.

Mechanism:
- Inflation reduces real income.
- Unemployment pushes job search abroad.
- Currency collapse increases desire to earn in stronger currencies.
- Public-service decline pushes families to consider better schools, healthcare, and safety.
- But the poorest people may want to move while lacking the money or documents to do so.

Examples:
- Lebanon's migration desire rose dramatically during economic crisis.
- Latin America/Caribbean has had high migration desire linked to economic insecurity, crime, and political instability.
- Labour migration corridors from lower-wage to higher-wage regions remain structurally strong.

Sources: Gallup; OECD; ILO; European Parliament. [S10][S9][S5][S6][S26]

3.4 How important are safety and crime levels in choosing a country?

Answer: Very important, especially for families, women, older migrants, high-net-worth individuals, and people leaving violence or repression.

Decision factors:
- Violent crime and personal safety.
- War and terrorism risk.
- Police reliability and corruption.
- Gender safety and minority safety.
- School safety and child protection.
- Political rights and rule of law.

Data challenge:
Safety is hard to summarise with one number. Numbeo provides crowd-sourced safety/crime perceptions, but official crime statistics, homicide rates, terrorism risk, and qualitative minority-safety data should be combined for serious decisions.

Sources: UNHCR; IDMC; Numbeo; European Parliament. [S8][S25][S33][S26]

3.5 How important are taxes and financial freedom?

Answer: Crucial for entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, crypto/finance workers, and high-income professionals; secondary for refugees and many labour migrants.

Common tax questions:
- When do I become a tax resident?
- Will I be taxed on worldwide income?
- Is there a non-dom or special expat regime?
- How are dividends, capital gains, crypto, foreign companies, and property taxed?
- Does the country have double-taxation treaties?
- Can I open bank accounts and receive international income?

Platform implication:
Tax is a major monetisable layer because generic migration sites usually handle it poorly. A country recommendation without tax-residency logic is incomplete for high-income users.

Sources: Henley & Partners; Fragomen; government tax/immigration pages should be used per country. [S30][S37]

3.6 How important is a second passport or citizenship?

Answer: Important for high-net-worth individuals, entrepreneurs, people from weak-passport countries, politically exposed people, and families seeking long-term security.

Main routes:
- Naturalisation after residence.
- Citizenship by descent/ancestry.
- Citizenship by marriage.
- Citizenship by investment, mostly outside the EU or under heavy scrutiny.
- Special talent or contribution pathways.

Trend:
Interest in second passports remains high, but EU-style citizenship-by-investment and real-estate golden visas are under pressure. Spain ended its real-estate golden visa and Malta's golden-passport scheme was ruled illegal by the EU's top court.

Sources: Henley & Partners; Spain/Malta reports. [S30][S22][S23]

3.7 How important are healthcare, education, and quality of life for family migration?

Answer: Very important and often decisive.

Family decision factors:
- School language and curriculum.
- Public/private school cost and availability.
- Childcare costs.
- Healthcare access for dependants.
- Maternity/paternity benefits.
- Safety and bullying/discrimination risk.
- Housing size and neighbourhood quality.
- Work rights for spouses.
- Path to permanent residence and citizenship for children.

Evidence:
Family migration is the largest permanent-migration category in OECD data. Education-driven migration is also large: UNESCO reports around 6.9 million internationally mobile higher-education students.

Sources: OECD; UNESCO. [S5][S7]

3.8 How much do climate and ecology influence country choice?

Answer: Climate already drives displacement, mostly internally, and increasingly affects long-term relocation and lifestyle decisions.

Evidence:
- World Bank Groundswell estimates that up to 216 million people could become internal climate migrants by 2050 across six regions without strong action.
- IDMC reports that disasters triggered tens of millions of internal displacements in 2024, overwhelmingly weather-related.
- Climate interacts with conflict, food security, poverty, housing, and health; it rarely acts as a single isolated cause.
- Among lifestyle migrants and digital nomads, climate/weather also affects destination choice: mild winters, clean air, coastal access, heat risk, and disaster exposure.

Platform implication:
Include a climate-risk layer, but separate “climate displacement risk” from “lifestyle climate preference.”

Sources: World Bank; IDMC; UNHCR. [S24][S25][S8]
