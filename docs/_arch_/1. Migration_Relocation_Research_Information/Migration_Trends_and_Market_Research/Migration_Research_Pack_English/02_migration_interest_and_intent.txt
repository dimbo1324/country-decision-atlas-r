2. INTEREST IN MOVING ABROAD
Research date: 16 June 2026

2.1 Is the number of people who want to move abroad rising?

Answer: Yes compared with the early 2010s, but the latest global desire level is high rather than continuously accelerating.

Gallup findings:
- In 2023, 16% of adults worldwide, more than 900 million people, said they would like to move permanently to another country if they could.
- This was unchanged from 2021-2022 and above the 12-15% range recorded in much of 2011-2018.
- In 2025, Gallup still estimated about 900 million adults wanted to move, but the preferred destination mix changed, especially for the United States.

Important distinction:
Desire is not the same as intent, eligibility, money, visa approval, or actual migration. A relocation platform should separate users into: curiosity, active research, pathway-qualified, financially ready, and application-ready.

Sources: Gallup. [S9][S10][S11]

2.2 In which countries do the most people want to emigrate?

Answer: The highest desire-to-leave rates are concentrated in parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America/Caribbean, crisis-hit states, and countries with weak economic or political outlooks.

Gallup examples:
- Sub-Saharan Africa had the highest regional desire level in 2023, around 37% of adults.
- Latin America/Caribbean was also high, around 28% in 2023, although down from its 2021/2022 peak.
- Countries where about half or more of adults wanted to leave in recent Gallup waves included Liberia, Sierra Leone, Ghana, Republic of the Congo, Nigeria, Gambia, Comoros, Ecuador, Honduras, and Lebanon in crisis years.
- Lebanon rose from 26% wanting to leave in 2018 to 63% in 2021 during economic and political crisis.

Sources: Gallup. [S9][S10]

2.3 Which countries do people name as desired destinations?

Answer: The United States remains number one, but its lead is weaker. Canada, Germany, Australia, Spain, France, Japan, Italy, Saudi Arabia, and the UK also appear among desired destinations.

Gallup destination preferences:
- 2023: 18% of potential migrants named the United States, equal to roughly 170 million adults.
- 2023: 9% named Canada, roughly 85 million adults.
- 2025: the U.S. share fell to 15%, its lowest in Gallup's trend since 2007.
- Canada remained second at about 9%.

Interpretation:
The U.S. is still the dominant dream destination, but Canada, Europe, Australia/New Zealand, and selected lifestyle/talent destinations are becoming more competitive in perception.

Sources: Gallup. [S9][S11]

2.4 Which age groups are most interested in migration?

Answer: Young adults are generally the most mobile and most interested, especially students, early-career professionals, and young families. However, older high-net-worth and retirement/lifestyle migrants are important in premium segments.

Evidence:
- Gallup's Potential Net Migration Index shows that some destination countries could see very large gains in youth populations if migration desires were realised. Canada, New Zealand, Switzerland, and Iceland stand out in youth-gain potential.
- Gallup reported that 40% of U.S. women aged 15-44 would move abroad in 2025 if they could, up from 10% in 2014.
- Student and early-career migration remains a major practical route because visas, study-to-work transitions, and early-career mobility are easier than mid-life relocation for many people.

Sources: Gallup; UNESCO; OECD. [S12][S11][S7][S5]

2.5 Which professions and social groups most often consider moving?

Answer: The highest-intent groups are those with portable skills, shortage occupations, education routes, or strong push factors.

Groups to watch:
- IT/software/data/cybersecurity professionals.
- Engineers and technical specialists.
- Healthcare workers: doctors, nurses, caregivers, allied health.
- Researchers, academics, and students.
- Founders, startup employees, and entrepreneurs.
- Trades and construction workers in shortage markets.
- Hospitality, agriculture, logistics, and domestic/care workers in labour-importing economies.
- Refugees, asylum seekers, and conflict-affected populations.
- High-net-worth individuals seeking tax, safety, education, or second-residence options.

Evidence:
OECD and national policies increasingly target skilled workers, shortage occupations, healthcare, seasonal work, and founders. ILO data confirms a very large global migrant workforce. [S5][S6][S20][S21]

2.6 Is interest rising among highly skilled specialists?

Answer: Yes, and countries are actively competing for them.

Evidence:
- Germany introduced or expanded the Skilled Immigration Act, EU Blue Card access, and the Opportunity Card for job seekers.
- The EU Blue Card exists specifically to attract highly qualified non-EU workers.
- Gallup's Brain Gain Index shows that countries such as Iceland, New Zealand, Denmark, Spain, Finland, Australia, Canada, Portugal, and Norway could gain highly educated adults if migration desires were realised.
- Labour shortages, demographic ageing, healthcare shortages, and technology-sector competition support continued skilled migration demand.

Sources: Germany Make it in Germany; European Commission; Gallup PNMI; OECD. [S20][S21][S12][S5]

2.7 Is interest rising among entrepreneurs and investors?

Answer: Yes, but the market is changing from simple “buy residence/passport” models toward more scrutinised, substance-based models.

Evidence:
- Startup visas and founder relocation programs are widespread, often requiring innovation, funding, incubator participation, job creation, or local business activity.
- Henley & Partners projects record or near-record private wealth migration, with high-net-worth individuals moving for safety, tax, lifestyle, education, and geopolitical diversification.
- At the same time, golden-visa and citizenship-by-investment programs face pressure in Europe. Spain ended its real-estate golden visa, and Malta's golden-passport scheme was ruled illegal by the EU Court of Justice.

Platform implication:
Entrepreneurs and investors will pay for analysis, but they need credible comparison of legal substance, tax exposure, banking, business formation, family residence, and long-term citizenship probability.

Sources: StartupBlink; Henley & Partners; Spain/Malta reports. [S31][S30][S22][S23]
