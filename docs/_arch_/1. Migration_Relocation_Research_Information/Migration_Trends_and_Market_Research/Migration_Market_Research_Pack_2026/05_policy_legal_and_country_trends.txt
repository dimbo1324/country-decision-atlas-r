05_POLICY_LEGAL_AND_COUNTRY_TRENDS

1. Which countries are simplifying migration rules?

A. Germany
Germany reformed skilled immigration and introduced the Opportunity Card. The goal is to make it easier for qualified workers and some jobseekers to enter, search for work, and address labour shortages. [S41][S42]

B. Japan
Japan created a digital-nomad-style specified visa from March 2024 for certain remote workers and families, allowing a temporary stay while working remotely for foreign employers/clients. [S43]

C. South Korea
South Korea launched a Workation Visa in January 2024 for remote workers employed by foreign companies or operating foreign businesses, subject to income and other requirements. [S44]

D. Spain
Spain has an official digital-nomad visa pathway for remote workers meeting the rules. Spain is therefore attractive for remote workers, although tax and bureaucracy can be complex. [S45]

E. Italy, Croatia, Greece, Estonia, Portugal, UAE, Brazil, Colombia, Malaysia and others
These countries are part of the broader remote-worker/digital-nomad/residence-permit competition. Exact rules vary and should be verified against official sources.

F. Portugal Tech Visa and selected startup/talent routes
Portugal and other countries have used tech, startup, and talent programmes to attract high-value workers and founders. [S49][S50]

2. Which countries are tightening migration rules?

A. Canada
Canada’s 2026-2028 Immigration Levels Plan reduces new temporary-resident arrival targets and stabilises permanent admissions. Student and temporary-resident management is a central policy direction. Canada also paused new Start-Up Visa commitment certificates from 1 January 2026. [S36][S37][S38]

B. United Kingdom
The UK tightened legal migration rules in 2024 and 2025, including Skilled Worker salary thresholds, eligible occupations, social-care recruitment, and family migration rules. [S39]

C. Spain
Spain closed its Golden Visa programme in April 2025, largely connected to housing-affordability concerns and limited perceived public benefit. [S46]

D. Portugal
Portugal has moved toward tightening nationality and migration rules. This is important because old online content often still presents Portugal as an easy 5-year citizenship path. Any platform should verify the current official text and not rely on outdated articles. [S47]

E. Australia and New Zealand
Both remain attractive, but student, temporary, and labour pathways are increasingly managed around housing, labour shortages, and migration caps. Australia’s migration planning levels should be monitored directly. [S40]

F. European Union and border-control trend
ICMPD and OECD policy summaries point to a broader trend of more restrictive access, returns, border management, and political pressure in many destination countries, even while skilled-worker routes remain open. [S48]

3. Which countries are closing or limiting golden visa / citizenship-by-investment programmes?

Clear example:
- Spain closed its Golden Visa programme in April 2025. [S46]

Broader trend:
- Property-linked residence-by-investment programmes face pressure where housing affordability is politically sensitive.
- EU-level scrutiny continues around citizenship/residence by investment, security, money laundering, tax transparency, and social legitimacy.
- Portugal had already moved away from some real-estate-linked investment options before the newer nationality tightening debate.

Interpretation: investment migration is not disappearing, but it is moving away from simple real-estate-purchase narratives toward more controlled, expensive, or specialised routes.

4. Which countries are becoming more attractive to remote workers?

Remote-worker attractiveness depends on more than visa availability. It requires a combination of:
- legal remote-work route;
- reasonable income threshold;
- tax clarity;
- housing availability;
- healthcare access;
- internet and infrastructure;
- safety;
- time zone;
- community;
- path to stay longer if desired.

Attractive or increasingly visible destinations:
- Spain;
- Portugal;
- Italy;
- Croatia;
- Greece;
- Estonia;
- Germany for skilled workers, less for pure nomads;
- UAE / Dubai;
- Japan;
- South Korea;
- Brazil;
- Colombia;
- Malaysia;
- Thailand, depending on visa category;
- Uruguay;
- Albania and Montenegro for budget/lifestyle segments.

Caution: “remote-worker friendly” does not always mean tax-friendly, family-friendly, or path-to-citizenship friendly.

5. Which countries are becoming less friendly to migrants?

“Less friendly” can mean law, rhetoric, social tension, housing pressure, or bureaucracy. The most visible patterns:

A. Countries under housing pressure
Canada, Portugal, Spain, Australia, New Zealand, the Netherlands, Ireland, and some popular urban destinations face political pressure because migrants, students, investors, and remote workers are blamed for housing shortages.

B. Countries restricting students and dependants
Canada, the UK, and Australia are tightening international-student or dependent pathways.

C. Countries restricting low-wage labour or care routes
The UK ended overseas recruitment for social-care workers under specific Skilled Worker arrangements in 2025. [S39]

D. Countries restricting investor migration
Spain closed its Golden Visa; others are narrowing or scrutinising investment routes. [S46]

E. Countries with stronger border/enforcement politics
The U.S., EU members, and other destination states are intensifying border, asylum, and return politics.

Important distinction: a country can become less friendly to one group and more friendly to another. For example, it may restrict students and asylum while opening routes for high-skilled workers.

6. Which legal changes most affect country choice?

Highest-impact changes:
1. Visa eligibility criteria.
2. Income/salary thresholds.
3. Work rights for main applicant and spouse.
4. Student work and dependent rules.
5. Route to permanent residence.
6. Naturalisation residence period and language/civic tests.
7. Tax residency and double-tax rules.
8. Housing and rental regulation.
9. Banking/KYC access.
10. Healthcare access for residents.
11. School access for children.
12. Employer sponsorship rules.
13. Remote-work legality.
14. Criminal-record and security-screening rules.
15. Renewal rules and discretionary refusal grounds.
16. Processing times and appointment availability.

7. Can we determine a country’s direction from its laws?

Yes, imperfectly. A platform can build a “country direction index” using legal and policy signals.

Immigration openness signals:
- new visa routes launched;
- income thresholds lowered/raised;
- shortage lists expanded/reduced;
- family rights expanded/restricted;
- students capped/uncapped;
- digital-nomad/talent routes added;
- path to PR/citizenship shortened/lengthened;
- processing capacity expanded/reduced.

Political/institutional direction signals:
- rule-of-law trend;
- judicial independence;
- media freedom;
- corruption indicators;
- election risk;
- emergency powers;
- asylum/refugee policy;
- police/enforcement expansion;
- anti-immigration rhetoric.

Business direction signals:
- corporate tax;
- company-formation speed;
- banking access;
- foreign-owner restrictions;
- startup grants/visas;
- payroll and hiring complexity;
- capital controls;
- crypto/fintech regulation.

Lifestyle direction signals:
- rental affordability;
- healthcare capacity;
- school capacity;
- crime trend;
- climate risk;
- infrastructure;
- social acceptance of foreigners.

8. Policy trend summary

The dominant global pattern is selective openness:
- More open for skilled workers, healthcare workers, founders, researchers, investors, and remote professionals who meet income/compliance rules.
- Less open for low-wage labour, students used as backdoor migration, property investors blamed for housing problems, irregular migration, and politically sensitive family/asylum categories.

For users, this means old country reputations are dangerous. A platform must track live legal direction, not static “best country” lists.
