03_ONLINE_SEARCH_AND_SOCIAL_SIGNALS

1. Is search demand for moving abroad growing?

The strongest open evidence shows episodic spikes rather than a clean universal upward line.

Examples:
- After the 2024 U.S. election, Reuters reported a very large spike in Google searches for “move to Canada,” “move to New Zealand,” and similar queries. [S26]
- NBC also reported post-election spikes in “leaving the country” and “how to move to…” searches. [S27]
- Market sources report strong growth in searches around digital-nomad visas, especially after new programmes launch. [S28]

Interpretation: search demand is highly event-driven. Political shocks, wars, new visa launches, tax changes, housing crises, and viral social-media content can all create short-term spikes.

For a production platform, do not rely on one keyword. Build a keyword basket:
- move abroad;
- immigrate to [country];
- move to [country];
- digital nomad visa [country];
- residence permit [country];
- citizenship by descent [country];
- second passport;
- low tax countries;
- best countries to live;
- cost of living [country/city];
- schools in [city];
- healthcare in [country];
- visa refusal [country].

7. Is interest in digital-nomad visas growing?

Yes, but the category is changing.

Evidence:
- Many countries have created remote-work, workation, freelancer, or digital-nomad visa categories. Japan launched a specified digital-nomad-style stay from March 2024; South Korea launched a Workation Visa in January 2024; Spain has an official digital-nomad visa route. [S43][S44][S45]
- Market sources report very strong search growth for “digital nomad visa” and country-specific launches. [S28]
- MBO Partners’ 2025 digital-nomad research suggests digital nomads are staying longer in fewer places, a “slomad” pattern, rather than constantly moving. [S29]

However, demand is constrained by:
- return-to-office policies;
- employer compliance worries;
- permanent-establishment risk;
- tax residency rules;
- health insurance requirements;
- housing backlash in popular cities;
- visa income thresholds;
- uncertainty over family rights.

The product opportunity is not just “list digital-nomad visas.” It is “can I legally work there, for how long, with what tax risk, and what happens after the visa ends?”

8. Is interest in countries with fast residence, PR, or citizenship growing?

Yes, especially among people who see residence/citizenship as risk insurance.

Growth areas:
- citizenship by descent;
- ancestry citizenship;
- naturalisation timelines;
- second passport;
- residence by investment;
- golden visa alternatives;
- fast permanent residence;
- “easy citizenship” searches.

But policy risk is high:
- Spain closed its Golden Visa programme in April 2025. [S46]
- Portugal has moved toward tightening nationality and migration rules, and users must verify the latest official text before relying on old “5-year citizenship” content. [S47]
- Canada paused new Start-Up Visa commitment certificates from 2026. [S38]

9. Are migration channels and communities growing on Telegram, Reddit, Facebook, and YouTube?

The open evidence strongly suggests high activity, but exact growth requires platform APIs or scraping.

Evidence and signals:
- Mixed Migration Centre studied Spanish-speaking Facebook groups used by people moving through Latin America toward North America. It found that social media is actively used to exchange information on transit, destination countries, routes, and practical problems. [S32]
- Academic work increasingly studies Telegram groups and channels as migration-information spaces. [S33]
- Public Reddit communities such as r/expats, r/IWantOut, and r/digitalnomad are large, active spaces where people ask destination, visa, regret, cost, and adaptation questions. [S34]
- YouTube and TikTok are major discovery engines for country hype, “cost of living in X,” “why I left Y,” and “how to get residency in Z” content.

Product implication: social platforms are useful for early signals, but not reliable as the final truth layer.

13. Which sources do people trust most?

Trust hierarchy:
1. Official government and embassy websites for law, forms, fees, and formal eligibility.
2. Licensed lawyers/regulated migration advisers for legal interpretation.
3. Accountants/tax advisers for tax residency and compliance.
4. People with recent verified experience in the same route.
5. Employer HR/global mobility teams.
6. Specialist forums and communities.
7. YouTube/bloggers/newsletters.
8. General social media and anonymous chats.

The gap: official sources are authoritative but fragmented and hard to understand; communities are practical but often outdated or wrong.

14. Where do people get outdated or contradictory information?

Weak spots:
- old blog posts ranking “easy countries”;
- influencer videos without update dates;
- Telegram and Facebook comments based on one person’s case;
- law-firm SEO pages that are not updated after reform;
- AI answers without source dates;
- unofficial visa checklists;
- translation errors;
- confusion between visa, residence permit, permanent residence, and citizenship;
- confusion between tourist stay and legal remote work;
- confusion between tax residency and immigration residency.

Product implication: every rule in the platform should have source URL, publication date, last checked date, confidence level, and change history.
