# 10. Problems and Unmet Demand

## Key takeaways

- The core unmet demand is not lack of information; it is lack of verified, current, comparable and personalised information.
- Users suffer from fragmented sources: official pages, lawyer blogs, Reddit, YouTube, Telegram, cost tools, news, and personal stories contradict each other.
- Most failures happen because people underestimate housing, taxes, bureaucracy, legal details, language and emotional adaptation.
- A strong product opportunity is a “country decision cockpit”: legal pathways + cost model + risk signals + community evidence + law tracking + scenario simulator.

---

## 101. What relocation information is most often scattered across sources?

Visa rules, official forms, processing times, document requirements, taxes, rent, healthcare, schools, local registration, bank accounts, neighbourhoods, job market, language requirements, PR/citizenship path, community experiences and legal updates. No single source naturally combines official law, real costs, lived experience and personal scenario modelling.

## 102. What data do people most often lack when choosing a country?

They lack:
- Realistic monthly budget by lifestyle.
- Probability of getting a visa/job.
- Real processing times.
- Actual rent availability for foreigners.
- Healthcare access for their status.
- Tax after relocation.
- School/childcare costs.
- Long-term PR/citizenship feasibility.
- Whether rules are changing.
- Whether a country is becoming more anti-immigrant or unstable.
- What people like them experienced after 6–24 months.

## 103. Which relocation questions are hardest to solve independently?

Hardest questions:
- Which legal route fits my exact profile?
- What is the probability of approval?
- Can I work legally in my situation?
- What is my tax residence?
- Will my diploma/profession be recognised?
- Can my spouse work?
- Can my children access public schools?
- What happens if rules change mid-process?
- Which country gives the best trade-off between legal feasibility and quality of life?
These questions require cross-domain reasoning, not just web search.

## 104. Where do people most often encounter outdated information?

Law-firm blogs, YouTube videos, old Reddit answers, old Telegram pinned posts, outdated PDFs, old country guides, SEO pages, affiliate sites and even cached government translations. Visa information from 2021–2023 is especially risky because post-pandemic rules changed quickly. Every rule should have a “last verified” date and official source link.

## 105. Where do people most often encounter contradictory advice?

Reddit vs official sites; lawyer A vs lawyer B; Telegram groups vs consultants; YouTube influencers vs local residents; cost-of-living sites vs real rental portals; expats vs locals; and people with different passports. Contradictions often come from hidden variables: nationality, income, family status, remote/local work, city, year, visa category and tax residence.

## 106. What mistakes do people most often make when choosing a country?

Common mistakes:
- Choosing by vacation experience.
- Ignoring visa path.
- Underestimating rent.
- Using gross salary instead of net.
- Ignoring tax/social contributions.
- Assuming English is enough.
- Not checking diploma recognition.
- Not visiting in bad season.
- Overtrusting influencers.
- Ignoring politics/rule changes.
- Not building emergency savings.
- Thinking cheap country = easy country.

## 107. What mistakes do people most often make after relocation?

Post-arrival mistakes:
- Renting too fast or in the wrong area.
- Missing registration/tax deadlines.
- Not buying proper insurance.
- Working illegally on the wrong visa.
- Not learning the language.
- Staying only in expat bubbles.
- Not documenting income/taxes.
- Assuming home-country habits work.
- Ignoring mental health.
- Burning bridges at home too early.
- Not planning PR/citizenship evidence from day one.

## 108. What decisions do people make based on unverified information?

They choose visas, countries, cities, housing, tax status, school plans, business structure, insurance and even asylum/border routes based on stories from strangers. IOM warns that misinformation and social media can shape migration decisions and increase risks [S38–S39]. This is especially dangerous for irregular migration, asylum, border crossing, work rights and tax.

## 109. What data would people like to see in one place?

A high-value one-place dataset would include: visa routes, eligibility, documents, processing times, PR/citizenship timeline, dual citizenship, cost after tax/rent, safety, healthcare, schools, jobs, salaries, taxes, business setup, property rules, climate, political/legal direction, community reviews, official-source links, expert commentary and rule-change alerts.

## 110. What tools could help people choose a country faster and more confidently?

Useful tools:
- Persona-based country matcher.
- Visa eligibility engine.
- Country comparison matrix.
- Scenario simulator.
- Cost-after-tax calculator.
- PR/citizenship timeline builder.
- Rule-change tracker.
- Source reliability scoring.
- Community evidence map.
- “Red flag” detector.
- Relocation checklist generator.
- Post-arrival task tracker.
- Expert marketplace with transparent credentials.
The product should reduce uncertainty, not merely provide more articles.
