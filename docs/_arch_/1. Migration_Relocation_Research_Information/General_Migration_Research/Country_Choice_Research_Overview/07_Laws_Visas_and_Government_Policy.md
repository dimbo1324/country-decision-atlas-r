# 7. Laws, Visas and Government Policy

## Key takeaways

- The global policy direction is selective: countries tighten broad inflows while opening or preserving routes for talent, shortage sectors, entrepreneurs and strategic workers.
- Student and asylum rules are being tightened in several major destinations [S08, S16, S18–S19].
- Digital nomad visas expanded rapidly, but they differ greatly in income threshold, tax status, duration, family rights and PR path [S15].
- Golden visa programs are under pressure, especially real-estate-based EU programs [S17, S26–S28].
- Official sources must always override community posts and consultant blogs [S22–S23].

---

## 67. Which countries recently simplified residence or permanent-residence rules?

Simplification is usually targeted, not universal. Germany simplified skilled-worker access through reforms including the Opportunity Card [S20]. Canada continues targeted fast processing for skilled temporary workers and priority categories while reducing broad levels [S21, S48]. Some EU countries have improved Blue Card or shortage-occupation rules. Gulf states expanded long-term residence/golden-style permits for investors and professionals. Several Latin American countries remain comparatively accessible for temporary residence, but rules must be checked country-by-country.

## 68. Which countries recently tightened migration rules?

The UK tightened skilled-worker, student/dependant and care-worker routes and considered longer settlement periods [S18, S47]. Canada and Australia tightened student/temporary inflows amid housing and public-service pressure [S16]. The EU’s migration/asylum pact introduced stricter screening, faster asylum procedures and more return emphasis [S19]. The Netherlands and Finland tightened integration obligations in parts of policy [S08]. Sweden, Denmark and other European countries have also pursued more restrictive asylum/integration policy environments.

## 69. Which countries launched digital nomad visas or similar programs?

More than 40 jurisdictions offered digital nomad or remote-worker visas/permits by July 2024, according to EY [S15]. Commonly discussed examples include Portugal, Spain, Estonia, Croatia, Greece, Malta, Italy, Iceland, Norway/Svalbard, UAE/Dubai, Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia, Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Barbados, Curaçao, Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia/Bali-type routes, Japan and others. Conditions differ: income, insurance, no local employment, tax, duration and renewability.

## 70. Which countries closed or restricted golden visas and investment programs?

Spain ended its golden visa mechanism effective 3 April 2025 [S17]. Ireland and the Netherlands had already closed or limited investor routes. Portugal removed real-estate investment from golden visa eligibility earlier and shifted toward funds/culture/research-style options. Greece raised thresholds in high-demand areas. EU institutions have repeatedly criticised real-estate-based golden visas because of housing, security and fairness concerns [S17, S26–S28].

## 71. Which countries simplified relocation for skilled workers?

Germany is one of the clearest examples through the Skilled Immigration Act and Opportunity Card [S20]. Canada continues to use Express Entry, Global Skills Strategy and category targeting for priority sectors [S21, S48]. Australia and New Zealand maintain points/skills pathways but periodically adjust lists and caps. EU Blue Card reforms support high-skilled workers in many EU states. Gulf states compete for professionals with long-term residence and tax/lifestyle incentives.

## 72. Which countries actively compete for entrepreneurs and startups?

Common startup/entrepreneur destinations include Canada, UK, France, Netherlands, Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Germany, Ireland, Singapore, UAE, Chile, New Zealand and Australia. Competition tools include startup visas, founder visas, innovation grants, tax incentives, accelerators, R&D credits, investor residence and simplified company formation. The real test is not the headline visa but bankability, local market, funding access, hiring ability and founder residence stability.

## 73. Which countries restrict foreign real-estate purchases?

Restrictions exist in many forms: outright bans, extra stamp duties, reciprocity rules, border-zone limits, agricultural land restrictions, vacancy taxes, non-resident buyer taxes, or special approvals. Countries with notable restrictions/extra burdens have included Canada, New Zealand, Australia, Singapore, Switzerland, Denmark, Thailand, Mexico border/coastal zones, and some EU/local jurisdictions. Because real-estate rules are highly local and change often, this must be verified through official land/property authorities and local counsel.

## 74. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

Many countries adjust special tax regimes for new residents: Portugal changed the NHR regime; Italy has special inbound/new-resident regimes; Spain has Beckham-law-type rules; Greece has non-dom regimes; UAE introduced corporate tax but remains low-tax for many individuals; UK changed non-dom rules; Canada/Australia/US tax rules are complex for residents and citizens. New residents must compare income tax, social contributions, capital gains, wealth tax, inheritance, tax treaties, exit tax and reporting obligations.

## 75. Which legal changes most affect migrants?

The highest-impact changes are: visa eligibility, salary thresholds, occupation lists, student work rights, dependant rules, proof-of-funds, language requirements, PR/citizenship residence periods, dual-citizenship rules, asylum procedures, family reunification, housing/registration rules, healthcare access, labour-market tests and employer sponsorship rules. A small change in salary threshold or eligible occupation can destroy a relocation plan.

## 76. Which legal changes most affect businesspeople and investors?

High-impact changes: golden visa closure, investment threshold increases, real-estate eligibility removal, tax-residence changes, beneficial ownership registers, AML/KYC banking rules, corporate tax changes, capital controls, foreign-ownership restrictions, sanctions, CRS/FATCA reporting, substance requirements and immigration compliance for founders. Spain’s golden visa closure is a strong example of how a popular investment route can disappear [S17].

## 77. Where can official information about visas, residence, PR and citizenship be found?

Use official government portals first. For the EU, start with the European Commission’s EU Immigration Portal and then national immigration offices [S22]. MPI maintains a list of official government immigration websites for major destination countries [S23]. For Germany, “Make it in Germany” is an official-quality portal [S20]. For Canada, use Canada.ca/IRCC [S21]. For the UK, use GOV.UK; for Australia, immi.homeaffairs.gov.au; for New Zealand, immigration.govt.nz; for the US, USCIS, State Department and CBP. Consultant sites are useful only after official verification.

## 78. How often do migration rules change in popular countries?

Frequently. Major destinations can change rules several times per year through new laws, ministerial instructions, occupation lists, salary thresholds, quotas, caps, processing priorities, court decisions and administrative guidance. Student, asylum, skilled-worker and investor routes are especially volatile. A practical research system should timestamp every rule, store source URLs, detect changes, and warn users when a rule has not been rechecked recently.
