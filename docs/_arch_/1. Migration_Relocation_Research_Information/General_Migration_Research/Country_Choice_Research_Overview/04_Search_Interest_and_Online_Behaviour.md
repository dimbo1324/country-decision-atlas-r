# 4. Search Interest and Online Behaviour

## Key takeaways

- Search interest is highly event-driven: wars, elections, visa announcements, golden visa closures, tax changes, cost-of-living spikes and digital-nomad visa launches produce sudden peaks.
- Google Trends should be treated as a research tool, not a conclusion by itself. It measures relative search interest, not actual migration [S50].
- Fast-growing query clusters are usually: “move to [country]”, “[country] digital nomad visa”, “[country] residence permit”, “cost of living in [city]”, “tax residency”, “citizenship by investment”, “best countries to live”, “safe countries to move to”, and “countries with low taxes”.
- Search behaviour is strongest before the decision. After arrival, users shift to practical queries: housing, registration, bank account, insurance, doctors, schools, tax number, local bureaucracy and community.

---

## 33. Is the number of search queries about moving abroad increasing?

Likely yes over the post-2020 period, but this must be verified with Google Trends by query cluster and country [S50]. General relocation search is not one stable keyword; it is fragmented across “move abroad”, “immigrate to Canada”, “digital nomad visa Spain”, “cost of living Portugal”, “best countries for expats”, “how to get PR”, and similar phrases. Search interest spikes after political shocks, wars, elections, visa-rule changes and viral YouTube/TikTok content.

## 34. Which migration search queries are growing fastest?

Likely high-growth query clusters include: digital nomad visa, remote work visa, second passport, citizenship by investment, golden visa alternatives, low-tax countries, countries to move to from the US/UK/Canada/Russia, cost of living in specific cities, safest countries, easiest countries to immigrate to, and country-vs-country comparisons. Digital nomad visa searches reportedly spiked sharply after major program launches, and EY confirms the expansion of remote-worker visa programs [S15]. Exact query growth requires Google Trends export [S50].

## 35. Which countries are most often searched together with relocation?

Commonly searched countries include Canada, USA, Australia, New Zealand, UK, Germany, Spain, Portugal, Netherlands, France, Italy, UAE, Singapore, Japan, Thailand, Mexico, Costa Rica, Colombia, Uruguay, Argentina, Georgia, Serbia and Turkey. The list differs by origin country: Russians may search Serbia, Armenia, Georgia, Turkey, Kazakhstan, EU, UAE and Latin America; Americans may search Portugal, Spain, Mexico, Canada, Costa Rica, Italy and New Zealand; Indians may search Canada, US, UK, Australia, Germany and Gulf states.

## 36. Which countries are most often compared with each other?

Common comparison pairs:
- Canada vs Australia.
- Portugal vs Spain.
- Germany vs Netherlands.
- Germany vs Canada.
- UAE vs Saudi Arabia.
- Mexico vs Costa Rica.
- Uruguay vs Argentina.
- Portugal vs Greece.
- UK vs Canada.
- Thailand vs Vietnam.
- Spain vs Italy.
- Georgia vs Armenia vs Serbia for post-Soviet relocators.
These comparisons usually reflect trade-offs: visa, cost, language, work, safety, taxes, climate, healthcare and citizenship timeline.

## 37. What topics do people most often search before relocation?

Pre-move search topics include: visa type, required documents, proof of funds, job market, salary, cost of living, rent, neighbourhoods, healthcare, insurance, taxes, schools, safety, local language, bank account, SIM card, pets, driving licence, customs/import rules, recognition of diplomas, remote-work legality, citizenship timeline, permanent residence, and “mistakes to avoid”. The strongest product opportunity is a structured relocation checklist linked to country-specific evidence.

## 38. What questions are most often asked in Google about residence permits, PR and citizenship?

Common questions:
- How do I get a residence permit in [country]?
- How long can I stay without a visa?
- Can I work on a student/digital-nomad/tourist visa?
- How long until permanent residence?
- How long until citizenship?
- Does [country] allow dual citizenship?
- How much money do I need?
- Do I need a job offer?
- Can my spouse work?
- Can I bring children/parents?
- What documents need apostille/translation?
- What happens if I leave the country during the residence period?

## 39. Is interest in digital nomad visas growing?

Yes, especially after remote work normalised in 2020–2022 and countries created formal pathways. EY counted more than 40 jurisdictions offering digital nomad/remote-worker permits by July 2024 [S15]. However, community sentiment is mixed: some people find these visas useful, while others complain about income thresholds, tax ambiguity, lack of PR path, high fees and backlash in popular cities [S43, S46].

## 40. Is interest in second passports and citizenship by investment growing?

Yes among HNWIs, entrepreneurs and politically concerned middle/upper-class groups. Henley reported record millionaire migration and strong investment-migration demand in 2025 [S26–S27]. IMF research also treats residence/citizenship by investment as a significant policy and economic phenomenon [S28]. Interest is driven by travel freedom, geopolitical risk, tax planning, children’s education, wealth protection and “backup plan” thinking.

## 41. Is interest in low-tax countries growing?

Yes, especially among remote workers, founders, investors, crypto/finance professionals and high earners. Popular low-tax discussions involve UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Monaco, Andorra, Paraguay, Panama, Georgia, Estonia, Bulgaria, Cyprus and some Caribbean jurisdictions. However, low tax alone is not enough: users increasingly ask about banking, substance requirements, CRS/FATCA, exit tax, double-tax treaties, healthcare, schools and actual residence obligations.

## 42. Is interest in countries with low cost of living growing?

Yes, mainly due to housing inflation and stagnant real wages in high-income countries. Digital nomads and retirees often search Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Georgia, Albania, Portugal interior regions, Eastern Europe and Latin America. But communities warn that cost-of-living websites can be misleading because expat rent, neighbourhood choice, healthcare, visas and lifestyle differ from local averages [S30–S32, S44].

## 43. What fears appear most often in relocation searches?

Common fears: visa rejection, deportation, lack of job, running out of money, housing scams, crime, medical emergency, not fitting in, loneliness, language failure, school problems for children, tax mistakes, losing home-country ties, political instability in destination, bureaucracy, and being unable to return. For forced migrants, fears include border denial, asylum rejection, detention, document loss and persecution.

## 44. Which YouTube relocation topics get the most views?

High-performing YouTube topics usually include:
- “Best countries to move to in 2026”.
- “Countries begging for foreigners/retirees/digital nomads”.
- “Where NOT to move”.
- “Cost of living in [country/city]”.
- “Digital nomad visa step-by-step”.
- “Portugal/Spain/Canada/Germany relocation guide”.
- “Mistakes I made after moving abroad”.
- “Reality of living in [country]”.
- “Cheap countries with good quality of life”.
- “Golden visa / second passport explained”.
Video is strong because relocation is emotional and visual: viewers want lived experience, apartment tours, budget breakdowns, bureaucracy stories and honest failures.
