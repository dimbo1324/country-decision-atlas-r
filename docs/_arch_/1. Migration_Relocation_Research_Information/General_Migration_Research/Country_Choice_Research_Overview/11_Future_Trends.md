# 11. Future Trends

## Key takeaways

- Future migration will be shaped by demography, climate, AI, remote work, housing shortages, political polarisation and selective talent competition.
- Countries will compete for skilled workers and HNWIs while restricting lower-trust, high-volume or politically controversial routes.
- Digital nomadism will not disappear, but it will become more regulated, tax-aware and less “work from anywhere without consequences”.
- Climate migration will mostly be internal at first, but will indirectly affect international migration through conflict, livelihoods, food prices and urban pressure [S24].
- AI may both reduce and increase migration: it can automate some white-collar tasks, but also increase demand for high-skill AI-complementary workers and global talent [S49].

## 111. Which migration destinations may become more popular in the next 5–10 years?

Likely growth destinations:
- Germany and selected EU states needing skilled labour.
- Canada and Australia selectively, despite tighter caps.
- UAE, Saudi Arabia and Gulf hubs for professionals, investors and entrepreneurs.
- Portugal, Spain, Italy and Greece for lifestyle and HNWIs, though housing/policy risk remains.
- Central/Eastern Europe for cost + EU access.
- Latin American stable countries such as Uruguay, Costa Rica, Panama and potentially Paraguay for lifestyle, safety or residence.
- Southeast Asia for digital nomads and retirees, if visa/legal clarity improves.
- Japan and South Korea may open more due to demography, but language and integration barriers remain.

## 112. Which countries may lose attractiveness in the next 5–10 years?

Countries may lose attractiveness if they combine high housing costs, restrictive migration policy, weak salaries, political polarisation, poor healthcare access or anti-immigrant sentiment. Risk candidates by segment include: UK for some workers/students due to tightening and cost; Canada/Australia for students if caps remain; Portugal/Spain urban hotspots if housing backlash worsens; some Gulf states for long-term citizenship seekers; climate-stressed or politically unstable destinations; and countries with unpredictable taxation or legal systems.

## 113. How does remote work affect migration?

Remote work expands mobility for people with portable income. It creates digital nomad visas, slow travel, “trial relocation”, geoarbitrage and hybrid expat lifestyles. But it also creates tax, labour-law, employer-compliance and housing-pressure problems. The next phase is more formalised: employers will restrict locations, governments will define remote-worker permits, and users will need tax-residence tracking [S15, S32, S35–S36].

## 114. How can AI and automation affect migration of specialists?

AI can reduce demand for some routine digital work but increase demand for AI-complementary specialists: AI engineers, data engineers, cybersecurity, automation, robotics, healthcare tech, industrial AI, systems integration and domain experts. It may also make immigration selection more skills-based and evidence-based. For migrants, AI raises the skill bar: junior white-collar migrants may face more competition, while experienced specialists who can use AI productively may become more attractive [S49].

## 115. How can climate change affect migration flows?

Climate change will first intensify internal migration: rural-to-urban movement, coastal retreat, drought/flood displacement and livelihood loss. World Bank Groundswell projects up to 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 in six regions under pessimistic pathways [S24]. International migration effects will be indirect: food insecurity, conflict, economic collapse, insurance/property loss, health risks and pressure on cities. Climate resilience will become a country-choice criterion.

## 116. How does rising cost of living affect country choice?

It pushes people away from expensive cities/countries and toward lower-cost destinations, but also makes popular “cheap” destinations more expensive. Cost pressure drives remote workers, retirees and families to compare rent, taxes, healthcare and schools more carefully. It also creates political backlash: locals may blame migrants/nomads for rent inflation. Cost-of-living tools must be paired with real rental supply and local wages [S30–S31, S44, S46].

## 117. How does political polarisation affect desire to move?

Political polarisation increases “exit planning”, especially among educated, young, wealthy, minority or politically vulnerable groups. It does not always produce immediate migration, but it increases searches for second citizenship, residence options, safer countries and “where to move if...” content. Gallup’s US data about younger women’s desire to move abroad is one example of political/social dissatisfaction translating into migration desire [S11].

## 118. How does demographic crisis in developed countries affect migrant attraction?

Ageing societies need workers, taxpayers, caregivers, doctors, nurses, engineers and service workers. OECD notes population ageing and shrinking working-age populations as major labour-market forces [S25]. This creates pressure to attract migrants, but politics pushes in the opposite direction. The result is selective openness: more pathways for shortage sectors and skilled workers, fewer broad or low-trust pathways.

## 119. Which countries will compete more actively for talent?

Likely competitors: Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, USA, UK despite restrictions, Ireland, Netherlands, Denmark, Finland, Singapore, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Japan, South Korea and selected EU states. Competition will focus on healthcare, STEM, AI, energy transition, defence, semiconductor supply chains, construction, elderly care and strategic infrastructure. Countries with fast processing and clear PR paths will have an advantage.

## 120. Which countries may become new centres for digital nomads, entrepreneurs and remote specialists?

Potential centres: Spain, Portugal, Greece, Italy, Estonia, Croatia, Malta, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Albania, Montenegro, Mexico, Colombia, Costa Rica, Panama, Uruguay, Argentina, Brazil, Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Japan and South Korea. Winners will combine legal remote-work status, good internet, safety, reasonable rent, tax clarity, community, banking and long-term options. Losers will be destinations where housing pressure, backlash, visa ambiguity or tax uncertainty become too high [S15, S32, S43, S46].
