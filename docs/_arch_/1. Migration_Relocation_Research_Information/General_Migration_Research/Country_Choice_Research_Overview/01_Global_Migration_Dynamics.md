# 1. Global Migration Dynamics

## Key takeaways

- The global stock of international migrants is rising in absolute terms. UN DESA estimates about 304 million international migrants in 2024, up from 275 million in 2020 and nearly double the 1990 level [S01–S03].
- Migration as a share of world population has risen much more slowly: around 3.7% in 2024 versus 2.9% in 1990 [S01–S03].
- OECD permanent migration cooled in 2024 after a post-pandemic surge: 6.2 million new permanent immigrants, down 4% from 2023 but still 15% above 2019 [S08].
- Forced displacement remains one of the strongest growth areas: 123.2 million people were forcibly displaced at end-2024 [S06].
- COVID-19 created a mobility shock, then a rebound. The next phase is more selective: governments are tightening low-skilled, student and asylum routes while competing for skilled workers, healthcare workers, entrepreneurs and strategic sectors [S08, S16, S18–S21].

---

## 1. Is the total number of international migrants in the world increasing or decreasing?

Increasing in absolute terms. The number of international migrants reached about 304 million in 2024, up from about 275 million in 2020 and about 154 million in 1990 [S01–S03]. However, the share of the world population living outside the country of birth is still relatively small, around 3.7% in 2024 [S01–S03]. This means the world is not “mostly migrating”; rather, migration is growing faster than population but still concerns a minority of people.

## 2. How has international migration changed over the last 10–20 years?

The main changes are: larger absolute migrant stock; stronger South-to-North and South-to-South flows; more forced displacement; more student mobility until recent policy tightening; more skilled-worker competition; and more digital/remote-work mobility after COVID-19. In OECD countries, foreign-born shares rose significantly over the last decade, and OECD reports more than 160 million foreign-born people living in OECD countries by 2024 [S08]. Since 2020, the pattern is especially volatile: border closures, rebound, labour shortages, then tightening.

## 3. Which regions currently receive the most migrants?

Europe and Northern America together host the largest share of international migrants, about half of the global total according to MPI’s UN-based synthesis [S03]. Northern Africa and Western Asia, which includes Gulf labour-importing states, is another major destination region [S03]. Within destination countries, the United States remains the largest host; Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom and France are also major destinations [S03].

## 4. From which regions and countries do people most often leave?

Major origin countries include India, China, Mexico, Ukraine, Russia, Syria, Bangladesh, Pakistan, the Philippines and Afghanistan, depending on the exact metric and year [S01–S03]. Broad sending regions include South Asia, Latin America, parts of Eastern Europe and Central Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and conflict-affected Middle East/North Africa. Forced-displacement origin countries are heavily shaped by conflict: Syria, Ukraine, Afghanistan, Sudan, Venezuela, Myanmar and others [S06–S07].

## 5. Which countries are the main migration destinations?

By migrant stock, the main destinations include the United States, Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom and France [S03]. Canada, Australia, Spain, Italy, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Turkey and other Gulf/European states are also important depending on whether one measures migrant stock, inflow, labour migration, refugees, or student migration [S03, S08]. For desired migration, Gallup still finds the US, Canada, Germany, Spain, Switzerland, Japan and Saudi Arabia important in different regions [S10–S12].

## 6. Which countries became more popular for relocation over the last five years?

Several clusters became more popular:
- Canada and Australia during the post-pandemic migration rebound, although both later tightened student and temporary routes [S08, S16].
- Germany due to skilled-worker reforms and the Opportunity Card [S20].
- Portugal, Spain, Greece, Italy and Malta among lifestyle, digital-nomad and investment-migration audiences, though golden visa rules changed in some places [S15, S17, S26].
- UAE, Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states for high-income professionals, entrepreneurs and HNWIs [S26–S27].
- Mexico, Costa Rica, Colombia, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam, Georgia and Albania among digital nomads and budget-sensitive relocators, though local housing and backlash issues are rising in some hotspots [S32, S43, S46].

## 7. Which countries are losing attractiveness for migrants?

This depends on segment. The UK has tightened several routes and saw net migration fall sharply from its 2023 peak [S18, S47]. Canada remains attractive but is less “open-ended” because of housing pressure and caps on international students and temporary residents [S16, S48]. Australia also tightened student migration [S16]. The US remains a top desired destination but Gallup reports declining desire to move there from several regions in 2025 [S11]. For wealthy migrants, Henley projected large net HNWI outflows from the UK and China in 2025 [S26]. Some digital-nomad hotspots lose attractiveness because of housing inflation, anti-tourism protests, visa complexity and cost [S43, S46].

## 8. Which migration types are growing fastest: labour, study, family, investment, forced migration or digital nomads?

The strongest structural growth is forced displacement, skilled/targeted labour migration, and remote-worker/digital-nomad-style mobility. OECD data show humanitarian migration increased in 2024, while labour migration declined from a high base and temporary labour migration remained historically high [S08]. Student migration grew strongly post-pandemic but fell sharply in 2024 in OECD top destinations because of policy tightening [S08, S16]. Investment migration is smaller by volume but high-value and growing among HNWIs, especially Americans and wealthy families seeking “backup” residence/citizenship options [S26–S28]. Digital-nomad visas expanded rapidly after 2020; EY counted more than 40 jurisdictions offering remote-worker permits by July 2024 [S15].

## 9. How did migration flows change after COVID-19?

COVID-19 first suppressed cross-border movement through travel bans, border closures and administrative delays. Then 2021–2023 produced a rebound: labour shortages, student deferrals, family reunification backlogs and asylum backlogs created unusually high inflows in many OECD states [S08]. By 2024–2026, the rebound became politically contested. Many countries moved from “reopen and recruit” toward “select and control”: higher salary thresholds, caps on students, stricter asylum procedures, more returns, and targeted pathways for shortage occupations [S08, S16, S18–S21].

## 10. Which global events most affected migration in recent years?

The most important events include: the COVID-19 pandemic; Russia’s invasion of Ukraine; conflicts in Syria, Sudan, Afghanistan, Gaza and Myanmar; Venezuela’s displacement crisis; climate-related disasters; cost-of-living and housing crises; global labour shortages in ageing countries; and the rise of remote work. In policy terms, the strongest effects came from housing pressure and public-service capacity concerns in Canada, Australia, the UK and parts of Europe; EU asylum reform; and strategic talent competition in Germany, Canada, Gulf states and Asian economies [S06–S08, S16–S21, S24–S25].
