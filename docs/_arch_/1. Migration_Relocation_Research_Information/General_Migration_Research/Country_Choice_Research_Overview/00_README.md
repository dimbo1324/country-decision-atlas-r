# Migration, Relocation and Country Choice — Research Archive

Prepared: 2026-06-16

## Scope

This archive answers 120 general research questions about migration, relocation and country choice. The goal is not to promote a specific project, but to create a broad research base that independent researchers can use without knowing the product idea behind it.

## Methodology

The material combines:

1. Official and semi-official statistical sources: UN DESA, IOM, UNHCR, OECD, ILO, World Bank, EU, national immigration portals.
2. Commercial and expert sources: Henley & Partners, EY, InterNations, Remitly, relocation/global mobility vendors, cost-of-living platforms.
3. Public online-community signals: Reddit communities, public YouTube topic patterns, public Telegram/channel examples where indexed, and expat/digital-nomad community discussions.

## Important limitations

- Closed Telegram chats, closed Facebook groups, private Discord communities and YouTube comment sections cannot be reliably audited from public search alone.
- Community sources are useful for discovering pains, fears, contradictions and vocabulary, but they are not reliable sources for legal rules.
- Visa, residence, permanent-residence and citizenship rules change frequently. Any country-specific rule must be rechecked on an official government website before use.
- Cost-of-living platforms are useful for first approximations but often diverge from real expat costs by neighbourhood, lifestyle, currency, tax, healthcare and housing-access constraints.

## Very short executive summary

Global migration is still structurally rising: the international migrant stock reached about 304 million in 2024, around 3.7% of the world population [S01–S03]. But the post-pandemic surge is cooling in several OECD categories: permanent migration to OECD countries fell 4% in 2024 after three years of sharp increases, while international tertiary-student inflows fell 13% [S08]. Forced displacement remains historically high: 123.2 million people were forcibly displaced at end-2024 [S06], with a slight decrease reported for 2025 but still at severe levels [S07].

Demand to move is not uniform. Gallup found global desire to migrate held at record-high levels around 16% in 2021–2024, then dipped to 15% in 2025, while still representing hundreds of millions of people [S10–S11]. Interest is shifting: the US remains a major desired destination but lost attractiveness in several regions in 2025 [S11]. Canada, Australia/New Zealand, parts of Europe, Gulf states, and selected low-tax or lifestyle jurisdictions remain important magnets [S08, S12, S26].

The most important user need is not merely “more country information”. It is verified, current, comparable, scenario-based information: immigration pathways, cost after tax and rent, safety, healthcare, schools, labour market, bureaucracy, political/legal direction, and real post-arrival experiences.
