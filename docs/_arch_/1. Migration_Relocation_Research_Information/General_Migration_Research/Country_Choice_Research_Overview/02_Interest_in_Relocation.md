# 2. Interest in Relocation

## Key takeaways

- Desire to migrate is much larger than actual migration. Gallup repeatedly warns that wanting to move is not the same as having concrete plans or legal ability [S10–S11].
- Gallup found global desire to migrate at about 16% in 2021–2024, then 15% in 2025, still hundreds of millions of adults [S11].
- Interest is especially strong among young adults, skilled workers, students, politically dissatisfied groups, entrepreneurs, investors and internationally employable professionals.
- The user segment “people comparing countries before relocation” is broad but can be split into personas: budget relocator, skilled worker, student, family, entrepreneur, investor/HNWI, digital nomad, safety/political refugee, retiree.

---

## 11. Is the number of people who want to move to another country increasing?

Over the last decade, yes, but the latest data suggest a partial cooling. Gallup reported record-high global desire to migrate around 16% in 2021–2024, then a dip to 15% in 2025 [S10–S11]. That still represents an enormous pool — Gallup estimated roughly 900 million adults who would like to move if they had the opportunity [S11]. The key nuance: desire is high, but actual migration is constrained by money, visas, language, family obligations, risk tolerance and employability.

## 12. In which countries is the share of people wishing to emigrate highest?

Gallup consistently finds high migration desire in sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Latin America and the Caribbean, and some fragile or economically constrained states [S10–S12]. In 2025, Gallup reported sub-Saharan Africa still had the highest regional desire at about 33%, while Latin America and the Caribbean fell to about 25% after earlier peaks [S11]. Country-level spikes often occur in countries with economic crisis, insecurity, weak institutions, political repression, conflict, or limited youth opportunity.

## 13. Which countries do people most often name as desired places to live?

The US remains a top desired destination globally, but Gallup shows its attractiveness declined in several regions in 2025 [S11]. Canada, Germany, Spain, Switzerland, Japan and Saudi Arabia appear as important alternatives depending on region [S10–S12]. In lifestyle and public-search datasets, Canada, Australia, the UK, the US, Spain, Portugal, New Zealand, Germany, Switzerland and Japan appear frequently [S10–S14]. For wealthy migrants, UAE, USA, Italy, Switzerland, Portugal, Greece and Saudi Arabia are prominent [S26–S27].

## 14. Which age groups are most interested in relocation?

Young adults and early/mid-career people are the most mobile. They have fewer fixed assets, fewer family constraints, stronger career-upside motivation, and more time to benefit from long-term residence/citizenship. Gallup also found a striking US-specific signal: 40% of US women aged 15–44 said in 2025 they would move permanently abroad if they had the opportunity, up from 10% in 2014 [S11]. Students and workers aged roughly 18–40 dominate online relocation discussions.

## 15. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

Common groups include IT/software workers, engineers, healthcare workers, researchers, academics, skilled trades, finance professionals, startup founders, remote workers, creative freelancers, teachers, hospitality workers, construction workers and care workers. OECD countries increasingly design routes for labour-market shortages, especially healthcare and technical roles [S08, S20–S21, S48]. Online communities also show many marketing, design, admin, language-teaching and freelance workers looking for lower-cost or higher-quality locations [S41–S43].

## 16. Is interest in migration growing among qualified specialists?

Yes, structurally. Ageing economies face labour shortages and compete for skilled workers [S25]. Germany expanded skilled migration routes, including the Opportunity Card [S20]. Canada continues targeting critical sectors even while reducing broad immigration levels [S21, S48]. OECD notes labour policies are increasingly tailored to talent attraction and specific labour-market needs [S08]. The direction is not “open migration for everyone”; it is selective migration for shortages, strategic sectors, healthcare, research, engineering and digital skills.

## 17. Is interest growing among entrepreneurs, investors and business owners?

Yes, especially among HNWIs, founders, politically concerned wealthy families, and globally mobile entrepreneurs. Henley reported record millionaire migration projected in 2025 and major interest in residence/citizenship planning [S26–S27]. Drivers include tax planning, political-risk hedging, children’s education, banking diversification, healthcare access, and business-market access. However, golden visas based on real estate have become politically controversial because of housing pressure, and some programs have closed or tightened [S17, S26–S28].

## 18. Is interest growing among students?

Long-term, yes; short-term, it has become more restricted and uncertain. International education remains a major route to migration, but OECD reported a 13% decline in new international tertiary students to OECD countries in 2024, driven by declines in the US, UK, Canada and Australia [S08]. Canada, Australia, the UK and the Netherlands tightened or reviewed student rules because of housing, labour-market and post-study concerns [S16]. Students are still interested, but the “study as migration pathway” strategy requires more careful country selection.

## 19. Is interest growing among families with children?

Yes, especially among families seeking safety, education, healthcare, political stability and long-term citizenship. Families are less flexible than single relocators, so they ask more detailed questions: schools, child benefits, healthcare, neighbourhood safety, language acquisition, childcare costs, family reunification, spouse work rights, and long-term residence. Community discussions show family obligations and guilt about leaving relatives as major barriers [S41, S45]. Family migration is also the leading reason for permanent migration to OECD countries [S08].

## 20. Which groups most often search for second citizenship, residence permits or permanent residence?

The main groups are HNWIs/investors, entrepreneurs, internationally mobile professionals, families from unstable countries, citizens of weak-passport countries, people facing political insecurity, retirees, tax-sensitive individuals, and digital nomads seeking legal stability. HNWI interest is increasingly framed as “sovereign portfolio” planning: multiple residence/citizenship options to reduce dependence on one jurisdiction [S26–S28]. Mass-market users search for residence and PR when they need a legal route to stay, work, study, reunite with family or eventually naturalise.
