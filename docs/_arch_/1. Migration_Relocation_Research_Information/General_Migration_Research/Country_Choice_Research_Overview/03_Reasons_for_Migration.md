# 3. Reasons for Migration

## Key takeaways

- Migration motives are multi-factor: economic, political, safety, family, education, healthcare, climate, lifestyle and legal-status pathways overlap.
- Remitly’s survey-style material places better quality of life, lifestyle and career opportunities among the leading stated reasons [S13].
- Actual migration decisions are constrained by visas, money, employability, housing, language, family, uncertainty, fear and bureaucracy.
- Online communities reveal a strong gap between “pre-move fantasy” and “post-move reality”: housing, loneliness, language, taxes, healthcare access and bureaucracy dominate after arrival [S41–S46].

---

## 21. Why do people most often want to move to another country?

The most common reasons are better quality of life, higher income, better work opportunities, safety, political freedom, better education for children, healthcare, personal freedom, family reunification, climate/lifestyle, and long-term legal security. Survey material from Remitly puts “better quality of life”, “lifestyle” and “career opportunities” at the top [S13]. In real communities, people often combine motives: “I want safer streets, better schools, less political pressure, and a job market where my skills are valued.”

## 22. How important are economic reasons: salaries, jobs, taxes and cost of living?

Very important. For most migrants, economics is the first filter: can I legally work, can I earn enough, can I afford rent, and is the salary-to-cost ratio better than at home? For high-income migrants, taxes and investment rules matter more; for low/mid-income migrants, rent, healthcare, public transport and job access matter more. OECD data show labour migration remains historically high even when permanent labour migration declined in 2024 [S08]. Online discussions repeatedly show money as a decisive barrier and source of post-arrival stress [S41, S44].

## 23. How important are political reasons: instability, restrictions, authoritarianism and wars?

Very important for specific groups, and increasingly visible. Political motives include war, mobilisation risk, repression, corruption, censorship, lack of judicial independence, discrimination, authoritarianism, polarisation and fear of future legal changes. Forced displacement statistics capture the extreme end of this: 123.2 million forcibly displaced at end-2024 [S06]. For non-refugee migrants, political reasons often appear as a “risk hedge”: people may leave before conditions become unbearable.

## 24. How important are safety and crime levels when choosing a country?

Safety is a core criterion, especially for families, women, retirees and people leaving violence. Users compare crime rates, police reliability, road safety, domestic stability, terrorism risk, scams and corruption. However, safety is often subjective: a city can be statistically safe but feel unsafe because of neighbourhood, language barriers or discrimination. Safety also interacts with housing: cheaper neighbourhoods can have weaker safety or infrastructure.

## 25. How important are healthcare and healthcare-system quality?

Healthcare is highly important for families, older migrants, people with chronic conditions and wealthy retirees. It includes not only quality, but access, waiting times, insurance cost, language, eligibility, private/public split and medicine availability. HNWI migration reports increasingly mention healthcare resilience and cost as part of residence planning [S26–S27]. For ordinary migrants, healthcare confusion after arrival is a major pain point: registration, insurance, doctors, prescriptions and emergency access.

## 26. How important are education and opportunities for children?

Extremely important for families and middle-class migrants. Parents compare public school quality, international schools, language burden, university pathway, safety, bullying, integration, childcare costs and long-term citizenship. Education is also important for student migrants, but for families the question is broader: “Will my child have a better life trajectory here?” This can outweigh taxes or weather.

## 27. How important are climate, ecology and urban environment?

Increasingly important, especially for remote workers, retirees, families and lifestyle migrants. Climate affects health, energy costs, transport, outdoor life, mood and long-term risk. Environmental quality includes air pollution, water quality, heat, floods, wildfires, green space and walkability. Climate risk is also becoming a structural migration driver; the World Bank projects up to 216 million internal climate migrants by 2050 under pessimistic pathways [S24].

## 28. How important is fast access to residence, permanent residence or citizenship?

Very important for people who want legal stability, not just a temporary stay. Fast residence matters for digital nomads, freelancers, families and entrepreneurs. Fast citizenship matters for weak-passport holders, politically exposed people, investors and families planning children’s education. But “fast” can be misleading: some countries offer easy temporary residence but slow PR/citizenship; others offer citizenship on paper but difficult integration or unstable rules.

## 29. How important are taxes and financial freedom?

Taxes are crucial for entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, high-income professionals and retirees. For average workers, the bigger issue is often net salary after tax, rent and healthcare. Low-tax countries can be attractive but may have weak public services, high private costs, expensive schools, limited healthcare, or no clear path to citizenship. Serious comparisons must calculate: income tax, social contributions, corporate tax, VAT, capital gains, wealth/exit taxes, pension rights and tax-residence rules.

## 30. How important is the ability to open a business or invest?

Very important for entrepreneurs, investors and freelancers. They evaluate company formation, banking access, accounting burden, visa linked to business, tax regime, capital controls, corruption, market size, hiring rules, IP protection and political/legal stability. Some countries actively compete for founders through startup visas and golden visas; others restrict foreign ownership or make banking difficult. Business migration needs a separate risk model from employment migration.

## 31. How important are language, culture and ease of adaptation?

Critical. Language affects employment, bureaucracy, healthcare, schools, friendship, dating, legal safety and psychological comfort. Culture affects daily friction: communication style, punctuality, hierarchy, bureaucracy, gender norms, customer service, trust and social integration. Online communities repeatedly warn that moving without language and support can create isolation, even if the country looks good on paper [S45]. Ease of adaptation is often underestimated before relocation.

## 32. What reasons most often make people abandon relocation?

Common blockers: lack of money, no visa pathway, weak employability, family obligations, fear, uncertainty, pets, health issues, lack of language, partner disagreement, children’s schooling, elderly parents, property/business ties, tax complexity, difficulty transferring credentials, and inability to find housing. Community discussions also highlight mental cost, loneliness, guilt, and loss of identity/status as under-discussed barriers [S41, S45].
