# 4. Search Interest and Online Behaviour

## 33. Is the number of search queries about moving abroad increasing?

There is strong evidence that interest is high, but global search-volume measurement requires Google Trends/Semrush/Ahrefs sampling by keyword and country. Public reports show spikes around “digital nomad visa”, “move to [country]”, “golden visa”, “second passport”, “Americans moving abroad” and country-specific visa changes. The safest conclusion is: interest is not uniformly rising everywhere, but spikes strongly after political events, visa launches, wars, elections, cost-of-living crises and remote-work shifts.

**Sources:** S17, S19, S54, S55.

## 34. Which migration-related search queries are growing fastest?

Likely fast-growing query clusters include: digital nomad visa, remote work visa, second passport, citizenship by investment, golden visa alternatives, move to Portugal/Spain/Mexico/Canada, cheapest countries to live, safest countries, tax-free countries, best countries for families, countries with easy PR, and “how to leave [country]”. Exact ranking needs keyword-tool validation.

**Sources:** S27, S28, S48, S54, S55.

## 35. Which countries are most often searched together with relocation?

Commonly searched destinations include Canada, Portugal, Spain, Germany, Australia, New Zealand, the UK, the United States, Mexico, the UAE, Thailand, Georgia, Greece, Italy, the Netherlands, Ireland and Japan. Search patterns are origin-specific: Americans often search Portugal, Mexico, Canada, Spain and Ireland; Russians often search Serbia, Georgia, Armenia, Kazakhstan, Turkey, UAE, Argentina and Latin America; Indians often search Canada, Germany, Australia, UK, UAE and US.

**Sources:** S17, S18, S27, S28, S31, S32.

## 36. Which countries do people most often compare?

Frequent comparisons include Portugal vs Spain, Canada vs Australia, Germany vs Netherlands, Germany vs Canada, Spain vs Italy, UAE vs Qatar, Mexico vs Colombia, Thailand vs Vietnam, Argentina vs Uruguay, Poland vs Czechia, Ireland vs UK, and Portugal vs Greece for residence-by-investment. People compare countries that satisfy the same “job-to-be-done”: low cost, EU access, citizenship, remote-work life, family safety, tax optimisation or student pathway.

**Sources:** S20, S22, S23, S24, S25.

## 37. Which topics do people search before moving?

Core topics are visas, residence permits, permanent residence, citizenship timeline, cost of living, rent, salaries, taxes, jobs, schools, healthcare, safety, banking, SIM cards, transport, language, neighbourhoods, document apostilles, pet relocation, driving licence exchange, weather, social life and local scams. Advanced users search tax residency, double taxation, business formation and exit rules.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S22, S23.

## 38. What questions are most often asked in Google about residence permits, permanent residence and citizenship?

Common questions include: “How do I get residence in [country]?”, “How long until permanent residence?”, “Can I work on this visa?”, “Can I bring my family?”, “Does time as student count?”, “What documents do I need?”, “Can I apply from inside the country?”, “How much money do I need?”, “Can I lose residence?”, “When can I apply for citizenship?”, and “Does [country] allow dual citizenship?”.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S40, S41, S42, S43.

## 39. Is interest in digital nomad visas growing?

Yes. The number of digital nomads grew strongly after 2019 and many countries introduced remote-work visas. MBO Partners estimated 18.5 million American digital nomads in 2025, up 153% from 2019. Global Citizen Solutions tracks 64 digital-nomad visa schemes in its 2025 report. Interest remains high, but growth is maturing because companies are tightening compliance and some workers are returning to more stable arrangements.

**Sources:** S27, S28, S29, S30, S55.

## 40. Is interest in second passports and citizenship by investment growing?

Yes among wealthy and politically anxious groups. Investment-migration firms report strong demand, especially where people seek geopolitical insurance, travel freedom, tax planning or family security. However, governments increasingly scrutinise such schemes for housing inflation, money laundering, tax avoidance and inequality.

**Sources:** S43, S45, S48, S49.

## 41. Is interest in low-tax countries growing?

Yes, especially among founders, investors, crypto holders, remote workers and high earners. Popular themes include UAE tax residency, territorial-tax systems, non-dom regimes, low capital-gains jurisdictions, and countries with no wealth tax. However, low tax alone is rarely enough: banking, legal stability, healthcare, school options and residence security matter too.

**Sources:** S48, S49, S52.

## 42. Is interest in low-cost countries growing?

Yes, particularly among remote workers, retirees, freelancers and people priced out of high-cost cities. But community discussions show a common disappointment: “low cost” can be misleading if foreigners pay higher rents, use private healthcare, require international schools, or live in expat districts. Cost-of-living tools are useful but must be corrected with local/community evidence.

**Sources:** S25, S33, S35, S36.

## 43. What fears appear most often in relocation searches?

Frequent fears include visa refusal, losing money, not finding a job, being lonely, not speaking the language, unsafe neighbourhoods, healthcare access, school problems, scams, discrimination, tax mistakes, inability to return, family conflict, and choosing the wrong country. A strong product should convert these fears into checklists and risk scores.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S22, S23, S26.

## 44. Which YouTube relocation topics get the most views?

The most visible themes are: “why I left [country]”, “cost of living in [city]”, “best countries to move to”, “digital nomad visas”, “mistakes after moving abroad”, “Portugal/Spain/Mexico/Thailand/Canada relocation”, “how much I spend”, “truth about living in [country]”, and “second passport/golden visa”. YouTube is valuable for discovering emotions and lived experience, but it is risky as legal evidence because creators often simplify or date-sensitive information becomes outdated.

**Sources:** S24, S36, S54, S56.
