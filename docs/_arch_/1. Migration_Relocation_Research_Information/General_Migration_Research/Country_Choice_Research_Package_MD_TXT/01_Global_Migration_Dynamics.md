# 1. General Dynamics of Migration

## 1. Is the total number of international migrants rising or falling?

It is rising in absolute terms. The most current IOM/UN-DESA baseline states that there were about **304 million international migrants** in mid-2024, representing **3.7% of the world population**. This is up from about 275 million in 2020 and about 154 million in 1990. However, the share of the world population that is international migrant remains small and has grown gradually rather than explosively.

**Sources:** S1, S2, S3, S4, S5.

## 2. How has international migration changed over the last 10–20 years?

The last 10–20 years brought growth in migrant stock, more complex flows and stronger political contestation. Major changes include the Syrian refugee crisis, Venezuelan displacement, Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, post-pandemic labour shortages, student mobility recovery, digital-nomad visas, stricter asylum control in Europe and North America, and more competition for skilled workers. Migration is no longer seen only as labour movement; it is now tied to security, demographics, housing, healthcare systems, climate pressure, education and geopolitics.

**Sources:** S1, S6, S8, S38.

## 3. Which regions currently receive the most migrants?

Europe and Asia host very large migrant populations. IOM’s earlier and newer reporting shows Europe and Asia as the two largest regional hosts, while North America remains a major destination in absolute numbers and as a high-income pull region. The Gulf states have very high migrant shares relative to population because their labour markets depend heavily on foreign workers.

**Sources:** S1, S4, S5.

## 4. From which regions and countries do people most often leave?

The largest origin countries include India, China, Mexico, Ukraine, Russia, Syria and others depending on the exact year and definition. Regionally, large outflows come from South Asia, Latin America, Eastern Europe, parts of the Middle East, North Africa and Sub-Saharan Africa. Reasons differ: labour migration from South Asia, conflict-driven migration from Syria/Sudan/Ukraine/Afghanistan, economic migration from Latin America, and mixed economic-political migration from several authoritarian or unstable states.

**Sources:** S4, S5, S8.

## 5. Which countries are the main migration destinations?

The United States remains the largest destination by migrant stock. Other leading destination countries include Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, France, Spain, Canada, the United Arab Emirates, Australia, Russia, Türkiye and Italy. In relative population terms, Gulf states such as the UAE, Qatar and Kuwait are especially migrant-dependent.

**Sources:** S4, S5.

## 6. Which countries became more popular for relocation in the last five years?

Popularity depends on segment. For skilled workers and students, Germany, Canada, Australia, the UK, the Netherlands, France and parts of the Gulf remain important. For digital nomads, Portugal, Spain, Estonia, Croatia, Georgia, Thailand, Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, Dubai/UAE and parts of Southeast Asia gained attention. For investors, Greece, Portugal, Malta, the UAE and New Zealand have been prominent, although rules have changed. For lower-cost remote work, Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Georgia, Türkiye, Thailand, Vietnam and Indonesia appear frequently in community discussions.

**Sources:** S27, S28, S31, S32, S43, S48.

## 7. Which countries are losing attractiveness for migrants?

Some countries remain attractive but are becoming harder or less comfortable for certain groups. Canada has tightened temporary resident and student settings after housing and capacity pressure. The UK tightened skilled-worker, family and care-worker pathways. Australia tightened student controls and migration management. Spain ended its golden visa. Portugal removed real-estate routes for golden visas. These countries are not “unattractive” overall; rather, specific channels have become harder, more expensive or less predictable.

**Sources:** S31, S38, S39, S40, S42, S45.

## 8. Which types of migration are growing fastest: labour, study, family, investment, forced or digital nomad migration?

There is no single global winner. Labour migration is structurally supported by ageing societies and shortages. Student mobility has recovered and continues to grow globally, although the Big Four destinations are tightening. Family migration remains the largest permanent category in OECD countries. Forced displacement remains historically high. Digital-nomad migration grew quickly from a small base after COVID-19. Investment migration is volatile: demand is high among wealthy groups, but many countries are restricting real-estate routes.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S8, S12, S27, S48.

## 9. How did migration flows change after COVID-19?

COVID-19 initially suppressed mobility through border closures, travel restrictions and visa-processing delays. After restrictions eased, many OECD countries saw a strong rebound and catch-up in permanent migration, temporary work, students and asylum applications. Remote work also created a new mobility category: people who were not classic immigrants, but who could live abroad while earning income elsewhere. By 2024–2026, the post-COVID surge had partly normalised, while political pressure over housing, integration and public services increased.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S27, S31.

## 10. Which world events most affected migration in recent years?

The strongest events were COVID-19, Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine, the Syrian conflict, Taliban return to power in Afghanistan, conflict in Sudan, Venezuelan political/economic collapse, Gaza/Israel regional instability, climate-related disasters, inflation and housing crises, demographic ageing in rich countries, and the rise of remote work. Migration is now shaped by overlapping shocks rather than one single cause.

**Sources:** S1, S8, S9, S47.
