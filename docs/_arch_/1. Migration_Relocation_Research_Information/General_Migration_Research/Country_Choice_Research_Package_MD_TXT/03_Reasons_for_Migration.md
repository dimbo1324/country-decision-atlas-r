# 3. Reasons for Migration

## 21. Why do people most often want to move to another country?

The most common reasons are better income, better jobs, personal safety, political stability, education, healthcare, family reunification, freedom, long-term security, children’s future, lifestyle and climate. The exact ranking depends heavily on income level and origin country. A high-skilled software engineer, a refugee, a student and a retiree are solving very different problems.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S17, S20.

## 22. How important are economic reasons: salaries, jobs, taxes and cost of living?

They are central. Wage differentials remain one of the strongest migration drivers. For many migrants, moving can multiply earnings, while remittances support families and origin economies. For middle-class relocants, the calculation is more complex: salary may rise, but rent, tax, childcare, healthcare and integration costs can erase the benefit. For remote workers, the key equation is often foreign income plus lower local costs.

**Sources:** S6, S10, S11, S33, S35.

## 23. How important are political reasons: instability, restrictions, authoritarianisation and war?

Very important, especially in countries affected by war, repression, sanctions, polarisation, corruption or uncertainty about future freedoms. Political reasons are often mixed with economic reasons: political instability reduces investment, jobs and trust. For high-skilled workers and entrepreneurs, rule of law and predictable institutions can be as important as salary.

**Sources:** S8, S17, S19, S47.

## 24. How important are safety and crime levels when choosing a country?

Safety is one of the highest-priority factors for families, women, LGBTQ people, retirees and people leaving violent environments. Users rarely look only at homicide statistics; they also care about street harassment, police behaviour, traffic safety, political violence, school safety and everyday predictability. Community discussions often reveal safety perceptions that official indexes do not capture.

**Sources:** S14, S15, S20, S22, S23.

## 25. How important are medicine and healthcare quality?

Healthcare is a major selection criterion for families, older migrants, people with chronic conditions and Americans considering Europe or Latin America. The key variables are not just quality, but access: insurance rules, waiting times, cost, language, private vs public systems, prescription availability and emergency care. Many countries look good statistically but are difficult for newcomers before legal residence or insurance is arranged.

**Sources:** S14, S15, S20.

## 26. How important are education and opportunities for children?

Extremely important for families and students. Parents compare school safety, language of instruction, international schools, university access, citizenship/residency for children, special-needs support, and whether qualifications are recognised internationally. For students, education is also a migration pathway because study can lead to work permits and permanent residence.

**Sources:** S12, S13, S31, S32.

## 27. How important are climate, ecology and urban environment?

They are increasingly important, especially for remote workers, families and retirees. Climate comfort, air quality, heat stress, walkability, public transport, green space, pollution and disaster risk shape long-term satisfaction. Climate also works as a push factor: droughts, floods and heat can damage livelihoods and increase internal or international migration pressure.

**Sources:** S1, S14, S15.

## 28. How important is fast access to residence, permanent residence or citizenship?

Very important because legal certainty reduces anxiety and allows planning. People often prefer a country with lower salary but a clear pathway over a wealthier country where legal status remains temporary or employer-dependent. Fast citizenship is particularly important for weak-passport holders, families and people escaping political risk.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S40, S43.

## 29. How important are taxes and financial freedom?

Taxes are very important for entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, freelancers and high earners. They compare income tax, wealth tax, exit tax, social contributions, capital gains, territorial taxation, tax residency rules, double-tax treaties, banking access, crypto rules and reporting requirements. Many relocation mistakes happen because people confuse visa residence with tax residence.

**Sources:** S48, S49, S52.

## 30. How important is the ability to open a business or invest?

It is central for founders, investors and self-employed professionals. They care about company formation, bank accounts, tax regime, labour law, access to talent, corruption, currency controls, capital movement, startup visas, investor visas and local market size. A country can be pleasant to live in but poor for business if bureaucracy, banking or taxes are hostile.

**Sources:** S38, S43, S48, S49, S50.

## 31. How important are language, culture and ease of adaptation?

They are decisive for long-term success. Many people underestimate how much language affects banking, healthcare, contracts, social life, job access and mental health. Community evidence repeatedly shows that a country that looks attractive on paper can become exhausting if the person cannot integrate socially or handle bureaucracy in the local language.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S22, S26.

## 32. Which reasons most often make people abandon relocation?

The most common blockers are lack of visa eligibility, insufficient savings, job uncertainty, family obligations, fear of loneliness, housing costs, language barrier, credential recognition, healthcare concerns, taxes, risk of losing status, and contradictory information. Many people stop not because they dislike the destination, but because the path feels too uncertain.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S22, S23, S25, S26.
