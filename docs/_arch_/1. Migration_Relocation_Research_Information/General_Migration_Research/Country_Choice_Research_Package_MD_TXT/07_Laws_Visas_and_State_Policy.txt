# 7. Laws, Visas and State Policy

## 67. Which countries recently simplified residence or permanent residence rules?

Simplification is often targeted, not general. New Zealand relaxed its Active Investor Plus visa settings in April 2025, lowering/clarifying investor categories and simplifying requirements. Many countries introduced digital nomad or remote-work visas. Some OECD countries continue to simplify skilled-worker routes, digital processing or talent attraction while tightening other categories.

**Sources:** S38, S43, S44, S30.

## 68. Which countries recently tightened migration rules?

Canada is reducing the share of temporary residents and stabilising permanent admissions. The UK tightened skilled-worker and care-worker routes and raised thresholds. Australia tightened student controls and migration management. The EU migration pact strengthens border/asylum processing. Spain abolished its golden visa. These changes reflect housing pressure, political pressure, public-service capacity and security concerns.

**Sources:** S39, S40, S42, S45, S47.

## 69. Which countries launched digital nomad visas or similar programs?

Many countries now offer remote-work or digital-nomad-style routes, including Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Croatia, Greece, Italy, Malta, UAE/Dubai, Brazil, Colombia, Mexico variants, Thailand, Indonesia, Costa Rica, Barbados, Bahamas and others. Global Citizen Solutions tracked 64 schemes in 2025. New programs and rule updates continue, so any list must include a last-checked date.

**Sources:** S28, S29, S30.

## 70. Which countries closed or restricted golden visa and investment programs?

Spain abolished its golden visa effective April 2025. Portugal removed real-estate and capital-transfer routes in 2023 while keeping other investment options. Greece increased real-estate thresholds in many areas. The broader European trend is to restrict property-based residency where it worsens housing pressure or creates reputational risk.

**Sources:** S45, S46, S48, S49.

## 71. Which countries simplified relocation for qualified specialists?

Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, the UK, the EU Blue Card countries, Japan, Singapore, UAE and some Gulf states compete for skilled workers, but each uses different filters: salary, shortage occupation, employer sponsorship, points, language, education or experience. The trend is not “open borders”; it is selective competition for labour-market needs.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S38, S40, S42.

## 72. Which countries actively compete for entrepreneurs and startups?

Common examples include Canada, UK, France, Netherlands, Estonia, Portugal, Ireland, Singapore, UAE, Chile, New Zealand and Australia. Competition is based on startup visas, founder permits, investment incentives, digital government, tax policy, accelerator ecosystems and access to capital.

**Sources:** S38, S43, S48, S50.

## 73. Which countries restrict foreign property purchases?

Restrictions vary widely. Canada has used restrictions on foreign buyers. New Zealand has had restrictions but allows certain investor-visa-related high-value purchases under specific settings. Australia, Singapore, Thailand, Switzerland and parts of the EU apply restrictions, taxes or approval rules for foreign buyers. This topic must be checked country-by-country because rules change quickly and differ by resident status.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S43, S44.

## 74. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

Several countries have changed or debated special tax regimes for newcomers, high earners, pensioners or investors. Portugal changed its non-habitual resident regime. The UK changed non-dom rules. The UAE continues to attract tax residents while building more formal compliance frameworks. Some countries introduce exit taxes or reporting rules. Tax-residency planning is now a major relocation risk area.

**Sources:** S48, S49, S52.

## 75. Which legal changes most affect migrants?

The most impactful changes are: visa eligibility thresholds, salary thresholds, language requirements, family/dependant rules, student work rights, post-study work rights, asylum processing, deportation rules, regularisation programs, citizenship timelines, dual-citizenship policy, rent/property rules and tax-residency criteria.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S40, S41, S42, S47.

## 76. Which legal changes most affect businesspeople and investors?

The key changes are: golden visa closures, real-estate route restrictions, minimum investment thresholds, source-of-funds checks, tax-residency rules, banking compliance, beneficial ownership registers, crypto reporting, company-substance requirements and sanctions screening. Investors need monitoring because programs can close with transitional rules and deadlines.

**Sources:** S43, S44, S45, S48, S49.

## 77. Where can official visa, residence, permanent residence and citizenship information be found?

Use government immigration portals first: Canada IRCC, UK GOV.UK, Australia Home Affairs, New Zealand Immigration, EU member-state immigration portals, U.S. USCIS/State Department, official consulate pages and official gazettes/laws. For comparison, use OECD, IOM, UNHCR, Migration Data Portal and Eurostat. Lawyers and blogs are useful only after official verification.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S40, S41, S42, S43, S44, S47.

## 78. How often do migration rules change in popular countries?

Popular destinations change rules frequently, often several times per year through regulations, salary thresholds, occupation lists, processing priorities, quotas, fees and document requirements. Major law changes may be less frequent, but administrative changes can affect applicants immediately. A serious relocation product must track “last updated” and “effective from” dates for every rule.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S40, S41, S42, S43.
