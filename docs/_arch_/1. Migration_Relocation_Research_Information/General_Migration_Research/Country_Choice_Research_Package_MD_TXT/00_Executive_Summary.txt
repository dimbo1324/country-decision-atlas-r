# Executive Summary

## 1. Global migration is increasing in absolute numbers, but still involves a minority of humanity

The newest IOM/UN-DESA baseline is that around **304 million people** were living outside their country of birth by mid-2024, equal to about **3.7% of the world population**. That is up from roughly 275 million in 2020 and far above 154 million in 1990. The important interpretation is: migration is growing in absolute numbers, but most people still do not migrate internationally. Migration is a huge global phenomenon, but not a majority behaviour.

## 2. Post-pandemic migration rebounded strongly, then started normalising in some rich destinations

OECD permanent migration reached a record in 2023, then declined slightly in 2024 while remaining above pre-pandemic levels. This suggests not a collapse, but a shift from “post-COVID catch-up” to a more managed and politically contested phase. Family migration remains the largest permanent category in OECD countries; labour migration, humanitarian migration, student mobility and free-movement migration each behave differently.

## 3. Forced displacement remains historically high

UNHCR estimated 123.2 million forcibly displaced people at the end of 2024. Later reporting indicates a slight decline in 2025, but the level remains extremely high by historical standards. Conflicts in Ukraine, Sudan, Syria, Afghanistan, Venezuela, Myanmar and other crises continue to shape migration policy and public attitudes.

## 4. Desire to migrate is high and structurally driven

Gallup found that 16% of adults worldwide in 2023 would like to move permanently to another country if they could, projecting to over 900 million people. This is a desire measure, not actual migration. The conversion rate from desire to real migration is low because of visas, money, language, family obligations, credentials, risk and uncertainty.

## 5. The fastest-growing demand is not one single migration type

The most visible growth areas are: skilled migration, healthcare migration, student-to-work pathways, family migration after earlier labour migration, digital nomad/remote-work mobility, investment migration among wealthy groups, and forced displacement from conflict and climate-related stress. Each has different user needs and different willingness to pay.

## 6. Country choice is becoming more complex

People no longer choose only by salary or climate. They compare visa path, taxes, safety, healthcare, schooling, housing, language, political stability, banking access, digital bureaucracy, community support, passport/citizenship timeline, and whether the country is becoming more open or more restrictive. This creates demand for structured comparison tools.

## 7. Policy is moving in two directions at once

Many countries are competing for skilled workers, founders, investors and remote professionals. At the same time, many are tightening student, asylum, family, low-wage labour and real-estate investor routes because of housing pressure, public backlash and security concerns. This dual movement is one of the core trends for 2026–2030.

## 8. Current information is fragmented and often unreliable

Users combine official government pages, lawyers, YouTube, Telegram, Reddit, Facebook groups, blogs, Numbeo, Nomad List, InterNations, country forums and personal networks. The repeated complaint is not “no information”; it is “too much scattered, outdated and contradictory information”. This is the main unmet demand.

## 9. Existing platforms solve pieces of the problem, not the whole journey

Numbeo and Expatistan help with cost of living. Nomad List helps digital nomads. OECD/UNDP/World Bank provide official indicators. Henley-style firms serve high-net-worth clients. Localyze/Benivo/Deel/Remote-type platforms serve employers. Reddit/Telegram/YouTube provide lived experience. Few tools combine verified official data, legal change tracking, community evidence, scenario scoring and country comparison in one user-centred product.

## 10. Best product opportunity

A strong platform would combine: verified country cards, policy-change monitoring, visa/citizenship route comparison, cost-of-life reality checks, community evidence with reliability scoring, expert/legal review, personalised scenario simulation, and alerts when rules change. The strongest paid use cases are not generic articles; they are decision support, verified pathways, checklists, document planning, tax/legal risk summaries, and relocation readiness scoring.
