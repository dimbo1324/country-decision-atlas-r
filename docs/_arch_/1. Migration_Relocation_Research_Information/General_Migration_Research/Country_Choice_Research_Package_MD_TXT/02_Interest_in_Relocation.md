# 2. People’s Interest in Moving Abroad

## 11. Is the number of people who want to move to another country increasing?

Yes, desire to migrate is high and has risen compared with the early 2010s. Gallup reported that in 2023, **16% of adults worldwide** said they would like to leave their own country permanently if they could, projecting to more than 900 million people. This is desire, not actual migration. The gap between desire and action is very large because of visas, money, family, language, job access and risk.

**Sources:** S17, S18.

## 12. In which countries is the share of people wanting to emigrate highest?

The highest desire to emigrate is usually found in countries with weak economic prospects, insecurity, conflict, political repression, corruption, or low trust in institutions. Sub-Saharan African countries, parts of Latin America and the Caribbean, some Middle Eastern and North African countries, and countries affected by war or authoritarian pressure often show high potential emigration. Exact ranking changes by survey year, so country-by-country Gallup data should be checked before publishing a final ranking.

**Sources:** S17, S18.

## 13. Which countries do people most often name as desired places to live?

The United States has historically been the top desired destination, but Gallup indicates that preference for moving to the U.S. has weakened compared with earlier years. Canada, Germany, the UK, Australia, France, Spain, Italy, Switzerland, the UAE and Nordic countries are common aspiration destinations depending on region and class. For remote workers, Portugal, Spain, Thailand, Mexico, Colombia, Georgia and the UAE appear frequently in online discussions.

**Sources:** S17, S18, S27, S28.

## 14. Which age groups are most interested in relocation?

Young adults and early/mid-career professionals usually show the strongest relocation interest because they have more time to benefit from better education, jobs, income growth and citizenship pathways. Students and people aged roughly 18–35 are especially mobile. Families with children are also interested, but they face more constraints: schooling, healthcare, housing, partner employment and stability.

**Sources:** S17, S19, S20.

## 15. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

The most migration-prone professional groups include IT and software specialists, healthcare workers, engineers, researchers, academics, finance professionals, startup founders, international students, skilled tradespeople, hospitality workers, seafarers, care workers and construction workers. At the lower-income end, migration is often wage-driven. At the high-skill end, it is driven by salary, career growth, safety, quality of life, taxation and citizenship options.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S38.

## 16. Is interest in migration growing among qualified specialists?

Yes. Ageing economies and labour shortages are pushing countries to compete for healthcare, engineering, technology, AI, semiconductor, cybersecurity and green-transition skills. At the same time, skilled workers compare not only salary but also visa certainty, family pathways, housing affordability, tax burden, political stability and long-term residence options.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S38.

## 17. Is interest growing among entrepreneurs, investors and business owners?

Yes, especially among high-net-worth individuals and founders who want optionality, political risk hedging, banking diversification, tax planning, access to larger markets or a better legal environment. However, the policy environment is mixed: some countries court investors, while others restrict real-estate golden visas because of housing pressure and anti-elite political backlash.

**Sources:** S43, S44, S45, S48, S49.

## 18. Is interest growing among students?

Globally, yes. UNESCO reports that international student mobility has tripled over two decades, reaching around 7.3 million students studying abroad. However, traditional top destinations such as Canada, Australia and the UK have introduced tighter rules, so demand is being redistributed toward continental Europe, Asia and alternative destinations.

**Sources:** S12, S13, S31, S32.

## 19. Is interest growing among families with children?

Yes, but family relocation is more selective. Families are driven by safety, education, healthcare, clean environment, political stability and children’s future citizenship or university access. They are also more cautious because mistakes are costly: unsuitable schools, housing instability, medical access issues, spouse isolation and language barriers can make relocation fail.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S26.

## 20. Which groups most often search for second citizenship, residence permits or permanent residence?

The main groups are high-net-worth individuals, entrepreneurs, politically exposed or politically anxious citizens, citizens of countries with weak passports, families planning education for children, remote workers seeking tax/legal stability, and people from unstable or authoritarian environments. A second passport or residence permit is often framed as an “insurance policy” against political, economic, currency or travel restrictions.

**Sources:** S17, S19, S48, S49.
