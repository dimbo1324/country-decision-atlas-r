# 11. Future Trends

## 111. Which migration destinations may become more popular in the next 5–10 years?

Likely winners include countries with labour shortages, clear skilled pathways, safe cities, decent healthcare, and manageable cost of living: Germany, Canada if policy stabilises, Australia, New Zealand, parts of the EU, Ireland, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Singapore, Japan, South Korea, Portugal/Spain alternatives, Poland/Czechia, and selected Latin American countries. For digital nomads, Southeast Asia, Latin America, Eastern Europe and Gulf hubs may continue to compete.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S27, S28, S38.

## 112. Which countries may lose attractiveness in the next 5–10 years?

Countries may lose attractiveness if housing becomes unaffordable, immigration routes tighten, taxes rise, healthcare access worsens, politics polarise, safety declines, or job markets weaken. Some high-brand destinations — Canada, UK, Australia, Netherlands, Portugal, Spain and Germany — may remain attractive but face stronger criticism because expectations are high and practical barriers are growing.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S31, S39, S40, S42.

## 113. How does remote work affect migration?

Remote work separates income source from residence location. It enables people to live in lower-cost or higher-quality places without local employment. It also creates legal and tax risks for employers and workers, which is why many companies now restrict work-from-anywhere. The future is likely structured remote mobility, not unlimited remote freedom.

**Sources:** S27, S28, S29, S30, S52.

## 114. How might AI and automation affect skilled migration?

AI may reduce demand for some routine white-collar tasks while increasing demand for AI engineers, automation specialists, cybersecurity experts, data specialists, robotics, semiconductor talent and industrial digitalisation skills. Countries will compete for high-productivity specialists, but mid-skill migration may face more automation pressure. AI will also be used in immigration processing, risk scoring and border control, raising bias and transparency concerns.

**Sources:** S1, S6, S38.

## 115. How might climate change affect migration flows?

Climate change will increase internal displacement and some international migration pressure through heat, drought, flooding, storms, crop failure, water stress and conflict interaction. Most climate-related movement is expected to be internal or regional, but long-term pressure may affect city growth, border politics, labour markets and humanitarian systems.

**Sources:** S1, S8, S9.

## 116. How does rising cost of living influence country choice?

High living costs push people away from expensive cities and toward cheaper countries, smaller cities or remote-work hubs. However, when many foreigners move to low-cost destinations, rents can rise and local resentment can grow. Cost-of-living arbitrage is powerful but unstable.

**Sources:** S25, S33, S35, S36.

## 117. How does political polarisation affect the desire to move?

Political polarisation increases migration desire among groups who feel unsafe, unrepresented or pessimistic about national direction. Gallup’s U.S. data on younger women shows how political and social factors can sharply increase emigration desire even in a wealthy country. This trend may become more common as elections, culture wars and institutional trust shape life-planning decisions.

**Sources:** S17, S19.

## 118. How does demographic crisis in developed countries affect migrant attraction?

Ageing societies need workers, taxpayers, caregivers, nurses, engineers and skilled tradespeople. This creates structural demand for immigration even when politics becomes restrictive. The likely result is selective immigration: countries will make it easier for wanted skills and harder for categories considered politically costly.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S38.

## 119. Which countries will compete more actively for talent?

Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, the UK, France, Netherlands, Ireland, Singapore, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Japan, South Korea and selected EU countries are likely to compete for healthcare, tech, AI, engineering, green-transition and research talent. The competition will be based on speed, clarity, family rights, PR/citizenship path and quality of life.

**Sources:** S6, S7, S38.

## 120. Which countries may become new centres for digital nomads, entrepreneurs and remote specialists?

Potential centres include Portugal and Spain despite tighter housing politics, UAE/Dubai, Saudi Arabia’s business hubs, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam, Georgia, Armenia, Albania, Croatia, Greece, Colombia, Mexico, Brazil, Argentina and Uruguay. The best hubs will combine legal stay, stable internet, safety, low/moderate costs, international community, tax clarity, banking access and founder infrastructure.

**Sources:** S27, S28, S29, S30, S36.
