# 10. Problems and Unmet Demand

## 101. What relocation information is most scattered across sources?

The most scattered information is visa eligibility, document requirements, tax residency, real cost of living, housing availability, healthcare access, school options, local bureaucracy, banking, city-level safety, community experience and rule-change history. Users must combine official pages, blogs, lawyers, YouTube, Reddit, Telegram and personal contacts.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S22, S23, S25.

## 102. What data do people most often lack when choosing a country?

They lack personalised feasibility data: “Can I move legally?”, “How much will it really cost for my family?”, “What are my realistic job chances?”, “What tax will I pay?”, “How long to PR/citizenship?”, “What can go wrong?”, and “What changed recently?”. They also lack confidence scoring for sources.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S25, S38.

## 103. Which relocation questions are hardest to solve independently?

The hardest are tax residency, dual taxation, immigration eligibility, family/dependant rights, business setup, credential recognition, school access, healthcare eligibility, property purchase restrictions and long-term citizenship strategy. These require both legal rules and practical interpretation.

**Sources:** S38, S39, S40, S42, S43, S48.

## 104. Where do people most often encounter outdated information?

They encounter outdated information in blog posts, YouTube videos, old forum threads, Telegram pinned posts, old lawyer articles, cached snippets, AI-generated summaries and visa-list websites without last-updated dates. Even official pages can be confusing when transitional rules apply.

**Sources:** S22, S23, S24, S38, S41.

## 105. Where do people most often encounter contradictory advice?

Contradictions appear in community groups, lawyer marketing pages, visa agencies, comments under YouTube videos, country-specific chats and cost-of-living discussions. Often both answers are “true” for different nationalities, dates, visa types, consulates or family situations.

**Sources:** S22, S23, S24, S25.

## 106. What mistakes do people most often make when choosing a country?

Common mistakes include choosing by tourism impression, relying on outdated visa info, ignoring tax residency, underestimating rent, assuming remote work is always legal, ignoring language, romanticising lifestyle content, choosing a country without a realistic job path, and not checking healthcare or schooling before arrival.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S25, S26.

## 107. What mistakes do people make after relocation?

They delay paperwork, miss deadlines, fail to register address, misunderstand tax obligations, rent too quickly, overpay for expat housing, stay in expat bubbles, neglect language learning, underestimate emotional stress, fail to build emergency savings, and ignore legal renewal timelines.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S26.

## 108. What decisions do people make based on unverified information?

People choose visa routes, countries, schools, apartments, tax plans, work arrangements, banking setups and even citizenship strategies based on forum comments or influencer videos. The highest-risk area is legal/tax advice from non-professionals.

**Sources:** S22, S23, S24, S25.

## 109. What data would people like to see in one place?

They want visa options, PR/citizenship timelines, real costs, taxes, safety, healthcare, schools, jobs, salaries, business rules, housing reality, community reviews, law-change alerts, document lists, official links, and confidence/reliability indicators. The unmet need is an integrated decision dashboard.

**Sources:** S20, S21, S25, S33, S36, S38.

## 110. What tools could help a person choose a country faster and more confidently?

Useful tools include personalised route matching, country-comparison cards, visa eligibility calculator, relocation budget simulator, document checklist, tax-residency risk flag, law-change alerts, community evidence map, expert-reviewed summaries, family/student/founder/digital-nomad personas, and scenario scoring. The strongest product would combine official rules with lived-experience signals.

**Sources:** S14, S20, S33, S36, S38, S50.
