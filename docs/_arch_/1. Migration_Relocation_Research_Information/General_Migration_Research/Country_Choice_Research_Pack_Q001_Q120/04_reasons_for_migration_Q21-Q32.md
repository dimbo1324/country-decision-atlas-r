# 3. Reasons for Migration — Questions 21–32

## Q21. Why do people most often want to move abroad?

The main reasons are economic opportunity, safety, political stability, education, family reunification, lifestyle, healthcare, personal freedom and long-term security. Most real decisions mix several motives. A professional may say “salary,” but also mean rule of law, better future, safer streets, stronger passport and less arbitrary bureaucracy. A family may say “children,” but that includes schools, healthcare, public safety, citizenship and social environment.

**Community signal:** forum posts rarely ask only “which country is beautiful?” They ask whether a country is realistic given income, visa, language, job market, housing and family constraints.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q22. How important are economic reasons: salary, work, taxes and cost of living?

Very important. Economic reasons are often the first filter. People compare net salary, job sponsorship probability, unemployment, rent, taxes, healthcare costs, childcare, transport and savings potential. A country with high wages can be unattractive if rent and taxes absorb the gain. A cheap country can be unattractive if legal status, job access or healthcare are weak.

The most practical formula is not “salary abroad,” but **expected disposable income after tax, rent, healthcare, visa costs, schooling and adaptation costs**.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q23. How important are political reasons: instability, restrictions, authoritarianisation and wars?

Very important for crisis migration and increasingly important for middle-class relocation. War, mobilisation, censorship, sanctions, repression, polarisation and loss of institutional trust can convert ordinary relocation interest into urgent migration. Ukraine, Russia, Belarus, Afghanistan, Venezuela, Syria, Sudan, Lebanon and parts of the Middle East show how political events generate both forced and voluntary emigration.

Political motives are also visible in wealthy countries. Some Americans and Europeans discuss moving because of polarisation, gun violence, reproductive rights, democratic decline or social conflict.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q24. How important are safety and crime levels when choosing a country?

Safety is one of the strongest non-economic criteria. People evaluate violent crime, petty crime, political violence, terrorism, policing quality, corruption, gender safety, child safety and road safety. Safety also has a psychological dimension: people want predictability and trust. Global Peace Index, government travel advisories, homicide statistics and community reports are useful but must be combined because “safe for tourists” is not the same as “safe for residents.”

**Confidence:** high.

## Q25. How important are healthcare and the quality of the healthcare system?

Healthcare is critical for families, retirees, people with chronic conditions and anyone planning long-term settlement. Migrants compare:
- access rights during temporary residence;
- public vs private insurance;
- waiting times;
- language of care;
- medicine availability;
- maternity care;
- mental healthcare;
- emergency care;
- recognition of foreign medical records.

Healthcare can reverse an otherwise attractive move. The US may offer high salaries but high healthcare costs; some European countries offer public coverage but require residence, contributions and waiting periods.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q26. How important are education and opportunities for children?

For families, education is often decisive. Parents evaluate public schools, international schools, language transition, university pathways, safety, bullying, special-needs support, school costs and citizenship outcomes. Countries with strong public education, low crime and clear long-term status are highly attractive even if taxes are higher.

For students, education is both a credential and a migration pathway. Post-study work rights, tuition fees and employer sponsorship matter as much as university ranking.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q27. How important are climate, ecology and urban quality?

They are increasingly important, especially for remote workers, families and retirees. People compare air quality, heat, winter darkness, walkability, public transport, green space, pollution, disaster risk and water stress. Climate used to be seen as lifestyle; it is now also a risk factor. Heatwaves, floods, drought and wildfire risk affect housing, insurance, health and future habitability.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q28. How important is the ability to quickly obtain residence, permanent residence or citizenship?

Extremely important for people seeking stability rather than travel. A beautiful country is less attractive if it offers no realistic path beyond temporary visas. Many migrants compare:
- first residence permit probability;
- renewal rules;
- work rights;
- family reunification;
- permanent residence timeline;
- citizenship timeline;
- dual citizenship rules;
- language and integration tests;
- physical presence requirements;
- processing backlog.

Fast citizenship is especially important for people with weak passports, families, politically exposed people and investors.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q29. How important are taxes and financial freedom?

Taxes are highly important for entrepreneurs, investors, remote workers, high earners, retirees and US citizens abroad. Tax questions include income tax, social security, wealth tax, capital gains, inheritance tax, tax treaties, foreign company treatment, crypto taxation, controlled foreign company rules and exit tax. Many people underestimate tax residency. A digital nomad visa does not automatically mean low tax.

**Community signal:** r/digitalnomad repeatedly debates whether remote workers owe tax in host countries and whether “LLC + foreign living” strategies are legal or myths.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q30. How important is the opportunity to open a business or invest?

It is very important for entrepreneurs, investors and some skilled migrants. They compare company formation, banking access, tax rates, labour law, bureaucracy, corruption, contract enforcement, market size, access to talent, startup grants and investor visas. Some countries advertise startup visas, but real attractiveness depends on ecosystem quality and the ability to operate in English or a familiar language.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q31. How important are language, culture and ease of adaptation?

Very important, but often underestimated. People may tolerate lower salaries if integration is easier. Language affects jobs, bureaucracy, healthcare, friendship, dating, school integration and mental health. Cultural distance affects work style, punctuality, hierarchy, gender norms, family roles, conflict style and humour. A country can be legally easy but socially hard.

**Community signal:** expat forums are full of posts about loneliness, culture shock, local bureaucracy and the difference between vacation impressions and resident life.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q32. What reasons most often make people abandon relocation?

The most common blockers are:
- no realistic visa path;
- insufficient savings;
- inability to find a sponsored job;
- housing costs;
- family obligations;
- language barrier;
- fear of losing professional status;
- spouse/children adaptation concerns;
- healthcare risk;
- tax complexity;
- uncertainty about citizenship/permanent residence;
- fear of loneliness or regret;
- contradictory information.

The most rational users abandon a move when they discover the legal pathway, job market or cost structure does not fit their profile.

**Confidence:** high.
