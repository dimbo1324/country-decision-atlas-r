# 4. Search Interest and Online Behaviour — Questions 33–44

## Q33. Is the number of search queries about moving abroad growing?

There is strong evidence of high and sometimes rising search interest, but it must be measured by query and country. Google Trends is the correct primary tool, but it does not give one universal “migration interest” metric. Secondary studies show major interest in queries like “move to Canada,” “move to Australia,” “digital nomad visa,” “second passport,” “Portugal D7,” “Spain digital nomad visa” and “cost of living in X.”

The clearest post-2020 growth is in remote-work, digital-nomad and tax/residency queries. Traditional migration queries also spike around wars, elections, visa rule changes and economic crises.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q39. Is interest in digital nomad visas growing?

Yes. Search and market evidence show a strong post-pandemic surge. Dozens of countries now offer digital nomad, remote worker or long-stay freelancer permits. KPMG and EY track these programmes because corporate mobility and tax teams need to manage remote-work compliance. However, there is a counter-trend: employers are more cautious about “work from anywhere” because of tax, labour law, data security and payroll risks. Digital nomadism is maturing from informal tourist-visa work into regulated remote-work migration.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q40. Is interest in second passports and citizenship by investment growing?

Yes among high-net-worth individuals and citizens of politically/economically unstable countries. Drivers include geopolitical risk, sanctions, weak passports, tax planning, children’s education, banking access and mobility. However, the EU has become more sceptical of citizenship and residence by investment. Spain closed its real-estate golden visa; Portugal removed real-estate routes; Greece increased thresholds. The market is moving toward stricter due diligence, higher prices and non-real-estate routes.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q41. Is interest in low-tax countries growing?

Yes, especially among entrepreneurs, crypto investors, remote workers, retirees and high earners. Popular low-tax or tax-advantaged destinations discussed online include UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Monaco, Andorra, Cyprus, Malta, Panama, Paraguay, Georgia and some Caribbean jurisdictions. But many online discussions oversimplify tax. Real tax outcome depends on residence days, centre of vital interests, company structure, source of income, treaties and anti-avoidance rules.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q42. Is interest in low-cost countries growing?

Yes. Inflation and housing costs in North America and Europe push users toward lower-cost destinations. Digital nomads and retirees frequently search Southeast Asia, Latin America, Eastern Europe, the Balkans and parts of the Caucasus. But “cheap” can be misleading: long-term legal status, healthcare, schools, imported goods, flight costs, safety and currency risk can erase apparent savings.

**Community signal:** Reddit discussions often ask where one can live legally on USD 1,500–2,000/month without constant visa runs.

**Confidence:** high.
