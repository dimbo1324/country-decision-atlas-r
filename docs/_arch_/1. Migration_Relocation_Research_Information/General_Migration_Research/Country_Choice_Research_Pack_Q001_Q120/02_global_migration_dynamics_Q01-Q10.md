# 1. Global Migration Dynamics — Questions 1–10

## Q1. Is the total number of international migrants in the world growing or falling?

It is growing in stock terms. UN DESA estimates about **304 million international migrants in 2024**, equal to roughly **3.7% of the world population**. This is almost double the 1990 estimate of around 154 million. The share of the world population living outside their country of birth has risen more slowly than the absolute number, because the global population also grew. The important nuance is that “international migrant stock” measures people living outside their country of birth or citizenship at a point in time. It is not the same as annual migration flows.

**Interpretation:** migration is not a marginal phenomenon, but it is still a minority experience globally. Most people do not migrate internationally. The world is becoming more connected, but legal, economic and social barriers still keep international migration selective.

**Confidence:** high.  
**Main sources:** UN DESA International Migrant Stock 2024; Migration Data Portal; Migration Policy Institute.

## Q2. How has international migration changed over the last 10–20 years?

Over 10–20 years, international migration has become larger, more diverse and more politically contested. Between the early 2000s and 2024, the migrant stock rose substantially, while remittances became a major development finance channel. IOM reported that remittances grew from USD 128 billion in 2000 to more than USD 800 billion by 2022. Migration also became more crisis-driven: forced displacement from Syria, Afghanistan, Venezuela, Ukraine, Sudan and other crises changed many corridors.

After 2020, migration patterns were shaped by a COVID pause, then a strong rebound. OECD permanent migration reached historically high levels after the pandemic, then declined slightly in 2024 but remained above 2019 levels. Student migration, labour migration and humanitarian migration moved differently: labour and student flows were affected by policy caps and market conditions, while humanitarian migration was driven by conflict.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q3. Which world regions currently receive the most migrants?

Europe and Northern America together host the largest number of international migrants. Migration Policy Institute’s summary of UN 2024 data says Europe and Northern America hosted around 155 million migrants, about 51% of all international migrants. Northern Africa and Western Asia, including Gulf labour destinations, is another major receiving region. Important receiving countries include the United States, Germany, Saudi Arabia, Russia, the United Kingdom, the United Arab Emirates, Canada, France, Australia and Spain.

**Interpretation:** high-income labour markets, ageing societies, established diasporas, universities, humanitarian systems and Gulf temporary-worker programmes explain most large destination clusters.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q4. From which regions and countries do people most often leave?

Major origin regions include Europe/Northern America, Central and Southern Asia, Latin America and the Caribbean, and Northern Africa/Western Asia. At the country level, the largest origin countries in 2024 include **India, China, Mexico, Ukraine and Russia**. India has the largest diaspora, while China and Mexico remain major sources of students, workers and family migrants. Ukraine’s ranking rose sharply because of the war. Russia remains large because of historical post-Soviet mobility, labour migration, political emigration and recent war-related departures.

**Important nuance:** “origin” does not always mean poverty or failure. Some high-income countries also generate migrants because their citizens are globally mobile.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q5. Which countries are the main destinations for migration?

The United States remains the largest single destination country. Other major destinations include Germany, Saudi Arabia, Russia, the United Kingdom, the United Arab Emirates, Canada, France, Australia and Spain. These countries attract different migrant types:
- the US, Canada, Australia and Germany attract skilled, family, student and humanitarian migrants;
- Gulf countries attract large temporary labour populations;
- Spain, Italy and Portugal attract Latin American, European, retiree, digital-nomad and regularisation-related migration;
- Russia and Kazakhstan historically host large post-Soviet migrant populations.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q6. Which countries became more popular for relocation over the last five years?

Popularity depends on the migrant group. For skilled workers and students, Canada, Germany, Australia, the UK, the US and increasingly Spain, Portugal, the Netherlands and some Nordic countries remained prominent, although policy tightening affected several of them. For digital nomads, Portugal, Spain, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Estonia, Thailand, Malaysia, Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, Costa Rica and the UAE became more visible. For wealthy individuals, Portugal, Greece, Malta, UAE, Italy and Caribbean citizenship programmes received attention, though some EU routes tightened.

A separate post-2022 pattern is relocation from Russia, Ukraine and Belarus to countries such as Armenia, Georgia, Kazakhstan, Serbia, Turkey, Montenegro, UAE, Portugal, Spain, Germany and Latin America.

**Confidence:** medium-high. Popularity is hard to measure because search interest, applications and actual settlement differ.

## Q7. Which countries are losing attractiveness for migrants?

Some countries are not becoming unattractive overall, but their attractiveness is weakening for specific groups. Examples:
- **Canada**: still desired, but housing costs, student caps and lower immigration targets damaged its “easy move + high quality of life” image.
- **UK**: high costs, stricter work/student/family rules and long settlement changes reduce attractiveness for some migrants.
- **Australia**: housing costs and tighter student settings create friction.
- **Portugal**: still attractive, but housing pressure, tax changes, backlog and citizenship-rule uncertainty reduce the old “easy EU pathway” narrative.
- **United States**: still a top dream destination, but Gallup shows lower desire to move permanently to the US, while visa uncertainty, healthcare costs, politics and gun violence reduce appeal for some groups.
- **Gulf states**: attractive for income, but less attractive for people seeking citizenship, long-term integration or family rights.

**Confidence:** medium. “Losing attractiveness” varies by profile.

## Q8. Which types of migration are growing fastest: labour, study, family, investment, forced migration or digital nomads?

The fastest visible growth depends on measurement:
- **Forced displacement** grew dramatically over the last decade, though UNHCR reported a decline in 2025 from 2024’s peak.
- **Digital nomad/remote-work mobility** grew quickly from a small base because more than 40–50 jurisdictions now offer remote-work or digital nomad permits.
- **Student migration** grew strongly over two decades, but OECD data show a 2024 drop in international student inflows to top destinations after policy tightening.
- **Skilled labour migration** remains strategically important, but governments are becoming more selective.
- **Investment migration** grew in visibility but is politically constrained, especially in the EU.
- **Family migration** remains the largest category for permanent OECD migration.

**Confidence:** high for direction, medium for exact “fastest” ranking.

## Q9. How did migration flows change after COVID-19?

COVID-19 created a temporary mobility shock, then a rebound. Border closures, embassy delays and travel restrictions reduced flows in 2020–2021. After reopening, many delayed moves happened at once. OECD countries then saw historically high permanent migration. The pandemic also normalised remote work and helped create demand for digital nomad visas, work-from-anywhere policies and long-stay mobility.

However, post-COVID migration also triggered backlash. Housing markets in Canada, Australia, Portugal, the Netherlands and other destinations became part of migration debates. Governments increasingly link migration policy with housing, infrastructure, labour shortages and political pressure.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q10. Which global events most affected migration in recent years?

The strongest events were:
1. COVID-19 travel restrictions and labour-market disruption.
2. Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022.
3. Long-running crises in Syria, Afghanistan and Venezuela.
4. Sudan’s civil war and regional displacement.
5. Economic crises, inflation and cost-of-living shocks.
6. Political repression and authoritarian shifts in several countries.
7. Climate-related disasters and drought/flood risk.
8. Labour shortages in ageing high-income societies.
9. Housing affordability crises in destination countries.
10. EU/UK/Canada/Australia policy tightening after post-pandemic migration peaks.

**Confidence:** high.
