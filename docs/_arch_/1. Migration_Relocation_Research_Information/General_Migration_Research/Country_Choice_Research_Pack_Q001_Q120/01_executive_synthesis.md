# Executive Synthesis

## Main answer

Global migration is structurally rising in stock terms, but not every migration category is rising at the same speed. The world had about **304 million international migrants in 2024**, around **3.7% of the global population**, almost double the 1990 figure. The largest receiving macro-regions remain **Europe and Northern America**, while major origin regions include **Europe/Northern America, Central and Southern Asia, and Latin America and the Caribbean**. The largest origin countries include India, China, Mexico, Ukraine and Russia. Forced displacement remains historically high, although UNHCR data for 2025 indicate the first decline in total forced displacement in roughly a decade after increased returns and naturalisations.

## What changed after COVID-19

COVID-19 did not stop migration permanently. It temporarily disrupted mobility, then helped accelerate three patterns:
1. **Post-pandemic rebound migration** — OECD permanent migration surged after travel restrictions ended and remained historically high even after a 2024 decline.
2. **Remote-work mobility** — digital nomad visas and remote work permits became normal policy tools in many countries.
3. **Policy backlash** — housing pressure, student visa dependence and political pressure pushed Canada, the UK, Australia, Portugal and parts of the EU toward stricter rules.

## What people usually want

Most people do not choose a country by one metric. They combine:
- visa/residence feasibility;
- income and job probability;
- cost of living and housing;
- safety and political stability;
- taxes and banking;
- healthcare;
- education for children;
- language and cultural adaptation;
- pathway to permanent residence or citizenship;
- community support and “real-life” reviews.

The most common mistake is comparing countries as if they were tourist destinations rather than legal, tax, labour-market and adaptation systems.

## Strongest demand signals

The strongest demand signals come from:
- Gallup potential migration surveys;
- Google/search trend patterns around “move to X”, “digital nomad visa”, “second passport”, “tax residency”, “cost of living”;
- Reddit/Facebook/Telegram communities where the same questions repeat: visas, money, job sponsorship, healthcare, schools, housing, tax residency, loneliness, language, bureaucracy;
- increasing use of platforms such as Numbeo, Expatistan, Nomad List/Nomads.com, Expat.com, InterNations, Localyze, Deel, Remote and investment migration consultancies.

## Big platform opportunity

Existing information is fragmented. Cost-of-living sites rarely solve visa/legal/tax questions. Visa sites rarely include lived experience. Expat communities are useful but noisy, contradictory and often outdated. Corporate mobility tools are strong for HR but not designed for independent migrants. A strong product opportunity exists for a platform that combines:

1. objective country data;
2. live legal/policy monitoring;
3. AI summaries with source traceability;
4. community experience separated from verified evidence;
5. persona-based country comparison;
6. decision support for different user groups: single professionals, families, students, investors, entrepreneurs and digital nomads.

## High-confidence future trends

The next 5–10 years will probably be shaped by:
- ageing societies competing for workers;
- tighter asylum and irregular migration rules;
- more selective skilled migration;
- pressure on student migration in Canada, UK, Australia and similar markets;
- ongoing demand for second residence/citizenship among wealthy and politically anxious groups;
- climate-related internal migration;
- remote work becoming more regulated rather than disappearing;
- countries competing for AI, cybersecurity, healthcare, engineering and green-transition talent;
- rising importance of housing affordability as a migration-policy constraint.

## Strategic conclusion

The core unmet need is **trusted, comparative, updated, source-backed decision support**. People are not only asking “where should I move?” They are asking: “Which country fits my profile, budget, risk tolerance, profession, family situation, legal pathway and long-term citizenship/tax strategy — and which information can I trust?”
