# 7. Laws, Visas and State Policy — Questions 67–78

## Q67. Which countries have simplified residence or permanent residence rules recently?

Simplification has mostly targeted skilled workers, shortage occupations, remote workers, founders and investors rather than all migrants. Examples include:
- EU Blue Card reforms and national skilled-worker routes;
- Germany’s skilled immigration reforms;
- Spain’s startup/digital nomad framework;
- UAE golden/green visa reforms;
- Portugal’s D7/D8 visibility, though recent rules are tightening;
- Japan, South Korea and Taiwan trying to attract specialists or remote workers;
- Canada and Australia maintaining skills-based systems, though with caps and recalibration.

**Confidence:** medium-high. Exact status changes often depend on implementation dates.

## Q68. Which countries tightened migration rules recently?

Notable tightening:
- UK: higher salary thresholds, dependant restrictions, settlement proposals and care-worker limits.
- Canada: lower targets/caps on temporary residents and international students.
- Australia: stricter student and temporary migration settings.
- Portugal: proposed/approved tougher citizenship and family rules; backlog reform.
- EU: stricter asylum and border-management framework under the Migration Pact.
- Spain: closed real-estate golden visa in 2025.
- Greece: higher golden visa thresholds.
- Netherlands/Sweden/Denmark/France/Italy: stricter asylum/family/citizenship debates in various forms.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q69. Which countries launched digital nomad visas or similar programmes?

Many countries now offer remote-work/digital-nomad options. Examples include Spain, Portugal, Italy, Greece, Croatia, Estonia, Latvia, Romania, Malta, Cyprus, Iceland, Norway, Georgia, Germany-like freelancer/self-employment routes, UAE, South Korea, Japan, Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia/Bali-related routes, Brazil, Colombia, Costa Rica, Panama, Mexico-related temporary residence routes, Barbados, Bermuda, Bahamas and Mauritius.

Not every programme is called “digital nomad visa”; some are temporary stay, freelancer, remote worker or long-stay permits.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q70. Which countries closed or limited golden visa and investment programmes?

Recent restrictions:
- Spain closed the real-estate golden visa route effective April 2025.
- Portugal removed real-estate-based golden visa routes in 2023.
- Ireland closed its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023.
- Greece increased property-investment thresholds in high-demand areas.
- EU institutions continue to criticise citizenship-by-investment and risky residence-by-investment models.
- Some Caribbean CBI programmes face stronger due diligence and international pressure.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q71. Which countries simplified relocation for skilled specialists?

Key examples:
- Germany through skilled-worker and EU Blue Card reforms;
- EU countries implementing updated Blue Card rules;
- Canada through Express Entry/category-based selection, though targets fluctuate;
- Australia through skilled-migration reforms;
- New Zealand through skilled migrant and accredited-employer systems;
- UK through skilled-worker route but with higher thresholds;
- UAE, Saudi Arabia and Singapore through talent/residency schemes;
- France, Netherlands and Sweden through talent/highly skilled or startup pathways.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q72. Which countries actively compete for entrepreneurs and startups?

Active competitors include Canada, UK, France, Netherlands, Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Germany, Sweden, Denmark, Singapore, UAE, New Zealand, Australia and Ireland. The strongest systems combine founder visas with startup ecosystem, funding, tax clarity, English support, digital government and family residence. OECD talent-attractiveness work highlights that migration policy is only one dimension; ecosystem quality matters heavily.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q73. Which countries restrict foreign property purchases?

Restrictions vary widely. Examples include Canada’s restrictions on some foreign residential purchases, New Zealand’s foreign buyer restrictions, Australia’s foreign investment approval/surcharge system, Singapore stamp duties for foreigners, Thailand land-ownership limits, Denmark/Switzerland restrictions in some contexts and Spain/Portugal policy debates around housing pressure. Rules change quickly and can apply differently to residents, citizens, EU nationals and non-residents.

**Confidence:** medium.

## Q74. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

Examples:
- Portugal ended or replaced parts of its old NHR attractiveness and introduced new regimes with narrower eligibility.
- Italy has modified tax incentives for inbound workers and maintains special regimes for high-net-worth individuals.
- Spain has Beckham Law and digital-nomad-related tax discussions.
- UK replaced non-dom rules with a residence-based foreign-income/gains regime.
- UAE introduced corporate tax but remains no personal income tax for many residents.
- Greece, Malta, Cyprus and Ireland have special residence/tax regimes but rules shift.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q75. Which legal changes most affect migrants?

Most impactful:
- income/salary thresholds;
- shortage occupation lists;
- dependant rights;
- student work/post-study rules;
- asylum processing rules;
- permanent residence/citizenship timelines;
- language/integration tests;
- digital appointment systems;
- document legalisation rules;
- health-insurance obligations;
- tax-residency rules;
- restrictions on changing employers;
- housing/rental registration requirements.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q76. Which legal changes most affect businesspeople and investors?

Most impactful:
- closure or reform of golden visas;
- source-of-funds due diligence;
- anti-money-laundering checks;
- tax residency and CFC rules;
- corporate tax changes;
- banking compliance;
- property purchase restrictions;
- beneficial ownership registers;
- startup visa criteria;
- sanctions and politically exposed person rules;
- minimum investment thresholds;
- substance requirements for companies.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q77. Where can official information about visas, residence, PR and citizenship be found?

Use official government portals first:
- national immigration ministry / home affairs website;
- national consulate/embassy website in your country;
- EU Immigration Portal for EU-wide categories;
- official Blue Card portals and national immigration offices;
- government gazettes/legal databases for law changes;
- official tax authority websites;
- official citizenship/naturalisation authority;
- migration data portals for statistics, not individual legal advice.

Non-official sites are useful for explanation, but final rules must be verified against official law and official application instructions.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q78. How often do migration rules change in popular countries?

Often. Popular destinations can change several times per year through:
- annual salary threshold updates;
- quotas/caps;
- shortage occupation list changes;
- student/dependant rules;
- appointment systems;
- forms and fees;
- processing times;
- tax rules;
- citizenship laws;
- property/investment thresholds.

For high-demand countries, information older than 6–12 months should be treated as suspicious. For fast-moving categories like digital nomad visas, student visas and investment migration, even 3–6 months can be too old.

**Confidence:** high.
