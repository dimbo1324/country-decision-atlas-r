# 2. People's Interest in Moving Abroad — Questions 11–20

## Q11. Is the number of people who want to move to another country growing?

At the global level, the desire to move remains high, but it does not rise uniformly everywhere. Gallup has repeatedly found that hundreds of millions of adults would like to move permanently to another country if they had the chance. In 2021, Gallup estimated that 16% of adults worldwide — nearly 900 million people — wanted to leave their country permanently. Later Gallup analysis shows desire remains historically high, but some regions, such as Latin America and the Caribbean, recently declined from their 2021–2022 peak.

**Interpretation:** demand for migration is structurally high, but it responds to economic cycles, political crises, visa barriers and destination reputation.

**Confidence:** high for broad trend, medium for exact current total.

## Q12. In which countries is the share of people wishing to emigrate highest?

The highest emigration desire is usually found in countries with some combination of low economic opportunity, insecurity, weak governance, war, repression or limited youth prospects. Gallup’s regional data historically show especially high desire in parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America and the Caribbean, and some fragile states. Country-level leaders change by year, but common high-pressure origin contexts include Haiti, Lebanon, Afghanistan, Venezuela, parts of Central America, parts of West Africa, Sudan, Syria and countries with severe political/economic crises.

**Important nuance:** desire to emigrate does not equal ability to migrate. Visa access, money, documents and networks filter who actually leaves.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q13. Which countries are most often named as desired life destinations?

Desired destinations differ by data source. Gallup historically shows the United States, Canada, Germany, the UK, France, Australia and Spain as major desired destinations. A 2024 Gallup result highlighted Canada as a top desired destination among potential migrants. Search-based studies such as Remitly and relocation companies often show Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Spain, the UK, Portugal, Germany, Japan, Switzerland and the US. Dubai/UAE also appears strongly in city-level search studies.

**Interpretation:** people often name countries associated with safety, income, English language, healthcare, education, natural beauty, prestige, or an easier legal route.

**Confidence:** medium-high. Survey and search methods produce different rankings.

## Q14. Which age groups are most interested in moving?

Younger adults are usually the most mobile and migration-interested group, especially people aged roughly 18–35. Students, early-career workers and young professionals have fewer family constraints, more time to rebuild credentials and more tolerance for uncertainty. Interest remains meaningful in the 35–45 group, especially among skilled workers, families seeking better children’s futures and entrepreneurs. Older groups are more likely to consider retirement migration, investment migration or lifestyle relocation, but they face stronger healthcare, pension and tax constraints.

**Community signal:** Reddit threads often show younger users asking “how do I get out?”, while older users ask about pensions, tax treaties, healthcare and family obligations.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q15. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

High-interest groups include:
- technology professionals and engineers;
- healthcare workers;
- skilled trades and construction workers;
- academics and researchers;
- students and recent graduates;
- finance/compliance/legal professionals with transferable skills;
- entrepreneurs and startup founders;
- remote workers, freelancers and digital nomads;
- workers in shortage occupations such as nursing, aged care, teaching and some engineering fields.

**Why:** these groups either have internationally transferable skills, global labour shortages, remote income, or strong incentives to access better institutions and markets.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q16. Is interest in migration among qualified specialists growing?

Yes, interest among qualified specialists remains strong, and governments actively compete for them. OECD policy analysis shows labour migration policies increasingly tailored to talent attraction and specific labour-market needs. Countries adjust salary thresholds, quotas, points systems, shortage lists and Blue Card rules to attract high-skilled workers. Demand is especially strong in AI, software engineering, cybersecurity, healthcare, semiconductor/chip design, green energy, engineering and advanced manufacturing.

**Counter-trend:** pathways are becoming more selective. “Qualified” increasingly means not only degree-holder but someone matching shortage lists, salary thresholds, language requirements and employer sponsorship.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q17. Is interest growing among entrepreneurs, investors and business owners?

Yes, but the market is splitting. Startup visas, entrepreneur visas and talent visas remain politically acceptable because they promise jobs and innovation. Investment migration and golden visas face more scrutiny, especially where property investment is linked to housing inflation. Spain closed its real-estate golden visa in 2025; Portugal removed real-estate routes in 2023; Greece raised thresholds. Wealthy individuals still seek “sovereign portfolios” — multiple residences, tax options and citizenship pathways — but governments are demanding stronger substance, compliance and source-of-funds checks.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q18. Is interest growing among students?

Over the long term, yes. UNESCO reports that global higher education enrolment more than doubled over 20 years and international mobility tripled, reaching roughly 7.3 million students abroad. However, short-term flows are sensitive to policy. OECD data show international tertiary student inflows to OECD countries declined in 2024, especially in Canada, Australia, the UK and the US, because of policy changes, cost and post-pandemic normalisation.

**Interpretation:** student migration remains a major pathway, but top destinations are becoming more expensive and more restrictive.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q19. Is interest growing among families with children?

Yes, but family migration is more cautious and information-hungry. Families care less about lifestyle glamour and more about:
- safety;
- school quality;
- healthcare;
- housing stability;
- childcare costs;
- parental visas and spouse work rights;
- long-term residence/citizenship;
- language adaptation;
- risks of separation if the main applicant loses status.

Family migration is also highly affected by government restrictions on dependants. The UK and Canada introduced or discussed measures affecting students and dependants, which immediately changed family calculations.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q20. Which groups most often search for second citizenship, residence permits or permanent residence?

The highest-intent groups are:
- wealthy individuals seeking asset protection, mobility and geopolitical risk hedging;
- entrepreneurs needing market access and banking options;
- families seeking security and children’s education;
- politically exposed or politically anxious citizens;
- remote workers seeking legal long-stay options;
- citizens from countries with weak passports;
- high-skilled workers seeking permanent residence rather than temporary visas;
- retirees seeking healthcare, safety and lower living costs.

Search terms vary: “second passport,” “citizenship by investment,” “golden visa,” “permanent residence,” “D7,” “digital nomad visa,” “tax residency,” “non-dom,” “EU passport,” and “fast citizenship.”

**Confidence:** high.
