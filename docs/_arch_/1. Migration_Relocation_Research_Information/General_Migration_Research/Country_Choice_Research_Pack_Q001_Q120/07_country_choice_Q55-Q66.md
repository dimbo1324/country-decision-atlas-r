# 6. Choosing a Country — Questions 55–66

## Q55. By what criteria do people usually choose a country for relocation?

People usually combine hard and soft criteria:
- visa feasibility;
- work and income;
- cost of living;
- housing;
- taxes;
- safety;
- healthcare;
- education;
- citizenship path;
- language;
- culture;
- climate;
- quality of life;
- community/diaspora;
- political stability;
- business environment;
- distance from family;
- passport strength after naturalisation.

Good country choice requires weighting criteria by persona. A digital nomad, a family and an investor should not use the same ranking.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q56. Which criteria matter most for single people?

For single people:
- job opportunities;
- visa simplicity;
- rent and shared housing;
- social life;
- dating/friendship environment;
- language;
- public transport;
- safety at night;
- career growth;
- ability to change jobs;
- city energy;
- mental health and loneliness risk.

Single migrants can move faster and take more risk, but they may suffer more from isolation if they lack community.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q57. Which criteria matter most for families?

For families:
- legal status for all family members;
- spouse work rights;
- school quality and language transition;
- childcare;
- healthcare;
- safe neighbourhoods;
- stable housing;
- long-term citizenship/PR;
- cost predictability;
- family-friendly culture;
- support for special needs;
- maternity/parental benefits;
- proximity to community.

Families need lower legal uncertainty than single professionals.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q58. Which criteria matter most for students?

For students:
- tuition fees;
- scholarship options;
- university reputation;
- language of instruction;
- visa acceptance;
- work rights during study;
- post-study work visa;
- path to permanent residence;
- employability after graduation;
- living costs;
- recognition of degree;
- safety;
- student housing.

A country can have excellent universities but weak migration outcomes if post-study work rights are limited.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q59. Which criteria matter most for entrepreneurs?

For entrepreneurs:
- company formation ease;
- banking access;
- tax regime;
- startup visa/entrepreneur visa;
- local market size;
- access to talent;
- investor ecosystem;
- contract enforcement;
- corruption level;
- IP protection;
- English/legal support;
- labour law flexibility;
- digital government;
- cost of hiring;
- residence rights for founders and family.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q60. Which criteria matter most for investors?

For investors:
- rule of law;
- capital controls;
- tax treaties;
- wealth/capital gains/inheritance tax;
- property rights;
- political stability;
- currency risk;
- banking access;
- investment migration route;
- due diligence requirements;
- exit options;
- double citizenship rules;
- family coverage;
- reputation of programme;
- long-term policy stability.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q61. Which criteria matter most for digital nomads?

For digital nomads:
- legal remote-work visa;
- income threshold;
- tax residency rules;
- internet quality;
- coworking/community;
- short/medium-term rent;
- safety;
- healthcare insurance;
- time zone;
- airport connectivity;
- climate;
- cost of living;
- banking/fintech access;
- lifestyle;
- ability to renew;
- family inclusion.

Digital nomads often over-focus on lifestyle and under-focus on tax and legal work rights.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q62. Which countries are often chosen by people with limited budgets?

Common budget destinations include Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Philippines, Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Albania, Bosnia, North Macedonia, Turkey, Mexico, Colombia, Peru, Argentina, Paraguay and parts of Eastern Europe. For EU access, Portugal and Spain used to be seen as moderate-cost but are no longer clearly cheap in major cities. Budget suitability depends on legal status and income source: a cheap country without legal work rights may not be practical.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q63. Which countries are often chosen by people prioritising safety?

Countries often associated with safety include Iceland, Ireland, New Zealand, Austria, Singapore, Portugal, Denmark, Slovenia, Finland, Switzerland, Japan, Norway, Netherlands and Canada. Safety rankings vary by index and year, and local city/neighbourhood matters. Some safe countries are expensive or legally difficult, so safety must be balanced with cost and residence feasibility.

**Confidence:** high.

## Q64. Which countries are often chosen for a fast path to citizenship?

Examples often discussed include Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Portugal, Spain for certain Latin American nationals, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, some Caribbean citizenship-by-investment states, Turkey by investment, Malta under strict exceptional naturalisation rules, and some Latin American countries with shorter residence requirements. But “fast” is often misleading: processing backlogs, physical presence, language tests, income, criminal-record checks and dual citizenship rules matter.

**Confidence:** medium. This must be verified country-by-country before action.

## Q65. Which countries are chosen for taxes and business environment?

Common choices include UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Ireland, Netherlands, Estonia, Georgia, Cyprus, Malta, Andorra, Panama, Paraguay, Hong Kong, Luxembourg and some US states for company formation. For startups, Canada, UK, France, Germany, Netherlands, Sweden, Estonia, Singapore, UAE and New Zealand often appear in discussions. Tax attractiveness is highly profile-specific and can change quickly.

**Confidence:** medium-high.

## Q66. Which countries look attractive at first but create problems after relocation?

Typical “looks better from outside” cases:
- **Portugal:** lifestyle and visa appeal, but housing, bureaucracy, tax changes and backlogs.
- **Canada:** high reputation, but housing costs, job-market credential barriers and climate.
- **UK:** prestige and English, but high cost, visa tightening and healthcare waiting concerns.
- **Australia/New Zealand:** lifestyle and safety, but distance, housing and strict visa systems.
- **UAE/Dubai:** tax and income appeal, but heat, cost, family expenses and limited citizenship path.
- **Thailand/Bali/Mexico/Colombia:** lifestyle and cost appeal, but visa, healthcare, safety, infrastructure and long-term integration issues.
- **Germany/Netherlands:** jobs and quality of life, but housing shortage, bureaucracy and language.

**Confidence:** high.
