# 3. Reasons for Migration

## 21. Why do people most often want to move abroad?

The main reasons are economic opportunity, safety, political stability, quality of life, education, healthcare, family reunification, freedom, climate/lifestyle and long-term security. For forced migrants, survival and protection dominate. For skilled workers, job and income dominate. For families, children’s future and stability dominate. For investors, risk diversification and tax/legal planning dominate.

## 22. How important are economic reasons: salaries, jobs, taxes and cost of living?

Very important. Economic factors are usually the first filter because they determine feasibility. People ask: Can I legally work? How much will I earn? What is net income after taxes? Can I afford rent? Can my spouse work? Will my profession be recognised?

But cost of living is often misunderstood. A cheap country is not always good if salaries are low, bureaucracy is difficult, healthcare is weak, or legal status is unstable. The real metric is disposable income after tax, housing, healthcare, schooling and visa costs.

## 23. How important are political reasons: instability, restrictions, authoritarianism and wars?

Extremely important for people from unstable or authoritarian environments. Political migration does not always appear as formal asylum: many people leave through work, study, business or residence routes because they want predictability, rule of law, freedom of speech, protection from mobilisation, lower corruption or less state intrusion.

Political reasons are especially visible in migration from Russia, Belarus, Ukraine, Venezuela, Iran, Afghanistan, Myanmar, Syria, Sudan and other crisis contexts.

## 24. How important are safety and crime levels?

Safety is one of the strongest deciding factors for families, women, LGBTQ+ people, retirees and people moving from high-crime environments. Users often compare homicide rates, theft, scams, harassment, police reliability, political violence, disaster risk and neighbourhood safety.

Informal communities frequently reveal that “safe country” and “safe daily life” are different. A country can have good national rankings but still have neighbourhood-specific crime, scams targeting foreigners or weak emergency response.

## 25. How important are healthcare quality and access?

Healthcare is a high-priority criterion for families, people with chronic illness, retirees, pregnant women and high-risk workers. Users need more than “healthcare ranking”; they need practical answers:

- Can migrants access public healthcare?
- Is private insurance mandatory?
- Are pre-existing conditions covered?
- How long are waiting lists?
- Are English-speaking doctors available?
- Are medicines available and affordable?
- What happens before permanent residence?

## 26. How important are education and children’s opportunities?

For families, education is often decisive. The key questions are school quality, language of instruction, international schools, public school access, university pathways, bullying/discrimination risk, special-needs support and cost.

Common pain: a country looks affordable until international school fees are included. For example, families considering Thailand, Portugal, Spain, UAE or Mexico often discover that schooling choices can completely change the budget.

## 27. How important are climate, ecology and urban environment?

They matter more than many official migration models show. Lifestyle migrants and remote workers often prioritise weather, walkability, beaches, mountains, clean air, green space, public transport and urban comfort.

Climate can attract and repel. Southern Europe attracts for lifestyle, but heatwaves, water stress and housing pressure are growing concerns. Southeast Asia attracts for cost and climate, but air pollution, humidity and healthcare access can become problems.

## 28. How important is the ability to obtain residence, permanent residence or citizenship quickly?

Very important for people seeking a “Plan B” or long-term security. Temporary visa attractiveness falls sharply if it does not lead to permanent residence, family reunification or citizenship. Digital nomad visas are attractive for lifestyle, but many do not provide a serious settlement path.

Users often confuse:
- tourist stay;
- temporary residence;
- work permit;
- tax residence;
- permanent residence;
- citizenship eligibility;
- actual naturalisation probability.

A strong platform should explain these differences clearly.

## 29. How important are taxes and financial freedom?

For ordinary workers, taxes matter but after salary, rent and legal status. For entrepreneurs, investors, freelancers and remote workers, taxes can be central. They ask about worldwide income, territorial taxation, double tax treaties, social contributions, exit tax, crypto taxation, company formation and banking access.

Tax misinformation is one of the most dangerous online areas because it is highly country-specific and changes frequently.

## 30. How important is the ability to open a business or invest?

Highly important for entrepreneurs, self-employed professionals, startup founders and investors. They need data on incorporation, banking, tax, accounting, hiring, access to payment processors, visa options, foreign ownership restrictions, real-estate rules and capital controls.

Countries competing for entrepreneurs usually combine startup visas, e-residency, tax incentives, founder networks, accelerators and simple company formation.

## 31. How important are language, culture and adaptation?

Very important for long-term satisfaction. Many people underestimate language and culture because they focus on visa approval. Community discussions repeatedly mention loneliness, language barriers, bureaucracy, lack of friends, identity loss and not feeling at home.

Language affects work, healthcare, school, dating, social life, bureaucracy and citizenship. A country can be excellent on paper but hard to integrate into without the local language.

## 32. What reasons most often make people abandon relocation plans?

The most common reasons are:

- visa ineligibility;
- insufficient savings;
- inability to find work;
- spouse/family resistance;
- housing costs;
- school costs;
- healthcare uncertainty;
- fear of loneliness or culture shock;
- pets/documents/logistics;
- bureaucracy and unclear rules;
- tax complexity;
- giving up career status at home;
- lack of confidence in information.

## Block-level conclusion

Migration decisions combine hard eligibility and soft life fit. Most platforms cover only one side. A strong decision tool should combine visa eligibility, cost model, tax/residence consequences, family constraints, adaptation difficulty and long-term legal path.
