# 7. Laws, Visas and Government Policy

## 67. Which countries recently simplified residence or permanent residence rules?

Examples:

- Germany simplified skilled migration through EU Blue Card changes, expanded shortage occupations and the Opportunity Card.
- Several countries introduced digital nomad / remote work permits.
- Some countries created startup/founder routes or improved talent routes.
- Spain, Portugal, Greece, UAE and others promoted specific residence categories for remote workers, investors or entrepreneurs.
- Japan and other countries introduced or expanded remote-worker options, though often with high income thresholds.

Simplification usually targets selected groups, not all migrants.

## 68. Which countries recently tightened migration rules?

Examples:

- United Kingdom: skilled-worker eligibility tightened, social-care overseas recruitment ended, English requirements increased, graduate route changes are planned.
- Canada: student caps, stricter temporary resident controls and policy tightening after housing/public service pressure.
- Australia: stricter student and temporary migration policy after post-COVID surge.
- Netherlands, Portugal and Spain: more scrutiny around investor routes and housing-linked migration.
- Several EU states: stricter asylum, family reunification or irregular migration measures.

## 69. Which countries launched digital nomad visas or similar programmes?

Many countries now offer remote-work or digital nomad visas. Commonly listed examples include Portugal, Spain, Italy, Croatia, Greece, Malta, Cyprus, Estonia, Latvia, Romania, Hungary, Georgia, Iceland, Norway, UAE, Brazil, Costa Rica, Mexico-linked options, Colombia, Argentina, Barbados and several Caribbean states.

The number varies by definition because some are true digital nomad visas, some are self-employment visas, some are temporary stay visas, and some are remote-work permits.

## 70. Which countries closed or restricted golden visa and investment programmes?

Important examples:

- Spain formally closed its Golden Visa programme in April 2025.
- Portugal removed real-estate routes from the Golden Visa and changed the attractiveness of the path.
- Ireland ended its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023.
- The Netherlands abolished its foreign investor scheme in April 2024.
- Greece raised real-estate thresholds in high-demand areas.
- EU pressure has pushed programmes toward stricter due diligence and away from passive real-estate investment.

## 71. Which countries simplified migration for skilled specialists?

Common examples:

- Germany: Skilled Immigration Act, Opportunity Card, lower Blue Card thresholds for shortage occupations.
- Canada: Express Entry and targeted occupation draws, though more constrained recently.
- Australia: points-based skilled migration, although policy settings shift.
- New Zealand: skilled pathways and talent attraction.
- UK: Global Talent and selected high-skilled routes, despite broader tightening.
- EU countries using Blue Card reforms.

## 72. Which countries actively compete for entrepreneurs and startups?

Common examples:

- Canada Startup Visa;
- UK Innovator Founder / Global Talent;
- Netherlands startup residence;
- Estonia startup ecosystem and e-residency;
- Portugal Startup Visa;
- France Tech Visa;
- Germany startup/entrepreneurial routes;
- Singapore EntrePass;
- UAE business/free-zone ecosystem;
- Chile Start-Up Chile legacy;
- Spain entrepreneur/startup ecosystem.

## 73. Which countries restrict foreign property purchase?

Restrictions vary widely. Common patterns include:

- higher taxes for foreign buyers;
- bans or restrictions on residential property by non-residents;
- special approval requirements;
- region-specific bans;
- restrictions on agricultural/strategic land;
- higher golden-visa thresholds or bans on short-term rental use.

Countries with significant public debate include Canada, New Zealand, Australia, Spain, Portugal, Greece, Netherlands and parts of Southeast Asia.

## 74. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

Examples and themes:

- Portugal changed its non-habitual resident regime and tax incentives.
- Italy, Spain and Greece have special tax regimes but adjust them periodically.
- UAE introduced corporate tax while maintaining strong personal tax appeal for many.
- UK changed non-dom taxation.
- Several countries are tightening reporting for foreign income, crypto and offshore structures.
- Digital nomad visas increasingly clarify or complicate tax residence.

## 75. Which legal changes most affect migrants?

The most impactful legal changes are:

- visa eligibility thresholds;
- salary thresholds;
- student dependant rules;
- work rights for spouses;
- language requirements;
- PR/citizenship residency periods;
- asylum rules;
- housing-linked visa restrictions;
- tax residence rules;
- health insurance requirements;
- document legalisation rules;
- processing delays and appointment systems.

## 76. Which legal changes most affect businesspeople and investors?

The biggest changes concern:

- investor visa closure/restriction;
- real-estate investment thresholds;
- source-of-funds and due diligence;
- tax-resident regimes;
- corporate tax and substance requirements;
- banking compliance;
- beneficial ownership registers;
- capital controls/sanctions;
- crypto regulation;
- property purchase restrictions;
- startup visa eligibility.

## 77. Where can official visa/residence/citizenship information be found?

Best official source types:

- national immigration ministry websites;
- foreign ministry/consular websites;
- official e-visa portals;
- government gazettes and legal databases;
- parliamentary/legal briefing services;
- embassy websites;
- tax authority websites;
- social security and health authority websites;
- EU portals for Schengen/EU Blue Card matters;
- OECD and IOM for comparative context.

A good platform should link each claim to the exact official source and show “last checked” date.

## 78. How often do migration rules change in popular countries?

Popular countries can change rules multiple times per year, especially around student visas, skilled-worker thresholds, asylum, health surcharges, investor visas and tax regimes. Even when the law does not change, implementation can change through appointment availability, local office practice, document requirements and processing times.

## Block-level conclusion

Visa/policy tracking must be treated as a living data product, not a static guide. “Last updated” and “source hierarchy” are essential features.
