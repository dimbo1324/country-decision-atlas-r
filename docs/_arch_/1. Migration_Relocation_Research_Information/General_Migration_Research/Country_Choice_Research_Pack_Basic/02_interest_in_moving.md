# 2. Interest in Moving Abroad

## 11. Is the number of people who want to move to another country increasing?

The long-term answer is yes compared with earlier decades, but the latest Gallup signal is more nuanced. Gallup found that global desire to migrate held at a record-high 16% between 2021 and 2024, then declined to 15% in 2025, the lowest level since 2018. This still represents a very large potential market, but it warns against assuming endless linear growth.

The key product insight: aspiration is much larger than action. Many people dream about migration; fewer create a plan; fewer still apply for visas, buy tickets, move, and stay.

## 12. Which countries have the highest share of people wanting to emigrate?

Gallup’s data show the highest regional migration desire in Sub-Saharan Africa, followed by Latin America and the Caribbean, although both declined in 2025. Country-level high-emigration desire is often found in places with low wages, weak institutions, conflict, poor security, limited youth opportunities or political pressure.

Common high-interest origin contexts include: parts of West Africa, Central America, the Caribbean, Venezuela, Lebanon, parts of the Balkans, Afghanistan, Syria, Sudan, parts of South Asia, Russia/Belarus/Ukraine in specific political contexts, and countries with young populations but weak labour absorption.

## 13. Which countries are most often named as desired destinations?

Classic “dream destinations” include Canada, the United States, Australia, Germany, the United Kingdom, New Zealand and Switzerland. Canada performs especially strongly in Gallup’s potential migration data, with high potential net population, youth and brain gain. In the EU, Luxembourg, Ireland, Denmark, Portugal, Finland, Sweden, Austria and Spain perform well in Gallup’s potential net migration measures.

For digital nomads and lifestyle migrants, the list differs: Portugal, Spain, Mexico, Thailand, Vietnam, Bali/Indonesia, Colombia, Georgia, UAE, Costa Rica and Panama appear frequently in online content.

## 14. Which age groups are most interested in moving?

Young adults are the most migration-oriented group. The 15–29 and 20–35 age ranges show the strongest practical interest because they have fewer fixed assets, fewer family constraints, greater education/work mobility and stronger willingness to accept uncertainty.

Families with children are also active, but they search differently: they focus more on schools, healthcare, safety, visa stability, family reunification, childcare, neighbourhoods and long-term citizenship.

Older groups are relevant in the retirement, tax-residency and healthcare-access segments, especially for Spain, Portugal, Panama, Costa Rica, Mexico and parts of Southeast Asia.

## 15. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

The most mobile professional groups are:

- IT and software engineering;
- healthcare and nursing;
- engineering and industrial technical roles;
- academic/research roles;
- finance, consulting and professional services;
- digital marketing, design, content and online business;
- entrepreneurs and startup founders;
- students and early-career professionals;
- construction, logistics, hospitality and care workers in labour-shortage markets.

High mobility appears where skills are internationally transferable and where visa systems explicitly recognise shortages.

## 16. Is interest rising among skilled professionals?

Yes, especially because many high-income countries have demographic ageing and skill shortages. Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, the UK and several EU states have created or modified skilled routes. OECD talent-attractiveness work shows that governments increasingly compete for highly educated workers, entrepreneurs, students and startup founders.

However, skilled professionals are becoming more selective. They do not only ask “can I get a visa?” They ask: net salary after tax, housing, school access, career growth, language burden, spouse employment, and citizenship path.

## 17. Is interest rising among entrepreneurs, investors and business owners?

Yes. The reasons include tax planning, geopolitical risk hedging, banking access, second citizenship, asset protection, family security and market access. UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Portugal, Greece, Malta, Cyprus, Panama, Paraguay, Uruguay and Caribbean CBI jurisdictions appear frequently in this segment.

At the same time, governments are restricting passive investment routes, especially real-estate-linked golden visas. This pushes the market from “buy property, get residence” toward active investment, business creation, innovation, startup visas and stricter due diligence.

## 18. Is interest rising among students?

Over the last decade, yes: OECD international student inflows were much higher in 2023 than in 2013. However, 2024 brought a decline in OECD tertiary student inflows, especially in Canada, Australia, the United States and the UK. The decline is partly policy-driven: student caps, higher fees, stricter dependant rules and concern that student visas are used as migration backdoors.

Student migration remains very large because education is both a human-capital investment and a potential immigration pathway.

## 19. Is interest rising among families with children?

Yes, especially among middle-class families facing political insecurity, war risk, weak education, healthcare concerns, environmental concerns or fear about children’s future. Families are less impulsive than single migrants: they search for schools, residence stability, healthcare, legal path, childcare, safe neighbourhoods and adaptation.

Family migration demand is often undercounted because the principal applicant may be a skilled worker, student, investor or asylum seeker while the real decision is family-level.

## 20. Which groups most often search for second citizenship, residence permits or permanent residence?

The strongest groups are:

- high-net-worth individuals seeking mobility, tax planning and political risk diversification;
- entrepreneurs and investors wanting business access and banking flexibility;
- citizens of politically unstable or sanctioned countries;
- parents wanting children to study and live in safer countries;
- remote workers seeking legal long-stay options;
- retirees seeking climate, healthcare and lower cost of living;
- professionals wanting a long-term settlement route rather than temporary work.

## Block-level conclusion

Migration interest is broad but segmented. A useful platform should not treat “migrants” as one audience. It should separate skilled workers, students, families, digital nomads, investors, entrepreneurs, retirees, political migrants and low-budget movers because they use different criteria and pay for different information.
