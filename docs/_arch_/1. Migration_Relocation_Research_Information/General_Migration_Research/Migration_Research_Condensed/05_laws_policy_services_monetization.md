## Section 7: Laws, Visas & Government Policy

### Q67. Which countries have recently simplified residency or permanent residency rules?

**Countries with notable simplification (2022–2025):**

- **Germany**: Introduced the **Chancenkarte** (Opportunity Card) in 2024 — a points-based job-search visa allowing skilled workers to enter Germany and look for work for up to one year. Also simplified the EU Blue Card; Germany issues ~78% of all EU Blue Cards.
- **UAE**: Streamlined Golden Visa (10-year renewable) for HNWIs, investors, and skilled professionals; introduced remote work visa; expanded eligibility categories.
- **Georgia**: Already simple; residency automatic after 365-day stay for many nationalities; popular for its frictionless system.
- **Portugal**: While the Golden Visa real estate route was removed (2023), new routes remained; D8 digital nomad visa launched (2022); D2 startup visa active.
- **Spain**: Launched digital nomad visa (2023–2024); simplified startup visa under Startup Law.
- **Japan**: Launched Highly Skilled Professional Visa (J-Skip) fast track in 2023; new Digital Nomad Visa (March 2024).
- **South Korea**: Workation Visa (F-1-D) launched January 2024.
- **UK**: Despite overall tightening, expanded the High Potential Individual (HPI) visa and Global Talent visa.
- **Albania**: Streamlined entry for digital nomads; very low cost, easy access.
- **Croatia**: Extended digital nomad visa from 1 year to 18 months (August 2025).

### Q68. Which countries have recently tightened immigration rules?

**Major immigration tightenings (2023–2025):**

- **Canada**: First-ever cap on temporary residents; reduced international student caps; tighter post-graduation work permit (PGWP) language requirements; lower PR targets announced.
- **UK**: "White Paper" (May 2025) aimed to halve net migration; raised income thresholds for family visas and skilled worker visas; limited dependents for some visa categories; raised English language requirements.
- **Australia**: Cap on international student enrollment; two-year ban on foreign purchases of existing residential properties (2025–2027); tighter temporary work visa rules.
- **New Zealand**: Tightened general work visa English language requirements (though partially reversed for investor/luxury property in late 2025).
- **Netherlands**: Coalition Agreement 2024 explicitly targeted reducing migration numbers; stricter asylum rules.
- **Belgium**: 2025 Federal Coalition Agreement aimed to reduce family reunification and asylum migration.
- **US (under Trump 2025)**: Mass enforcement actions; refugee resettlement suspended; H-1B under pressure; foreign student scrutiny increased.
- **EU broadly**: New EU Pact on Migration and Asylum (2024) introduced faster asylum processing, more deportations, offshore processing discussions.
- **Hungary**: Ended single-permit system (2024); introduced more restrictive "guest worker" permits; restricted ~300 job roles for non-EU workers.
- **Finland, France, Chile**: Tightened asylum processing rules in 2024.

### Q70. Which countries have closed or restricted their golden visa and investment programs?

**Closures and major restrictions:**

- **Malta**: EU citizenship by investment program effectively ended under EU pressure (2022); residency program remains.
- **Portugal**: Real estate route removed from Golden Visa (October 2023); capital transfer option also removed; fund route and cultural contributions remain.
- **UK**: Tier 1 Investor visa (£2M minimum) closed in February 2022 due to corruption concerns.
- **Ireland**: Investor Program suspended (February 2023) amid transparency concerns.
- **EU generally**: EU Commission pressured all member states to end or restructure CBI programs; Austria and Cyprus had programs effectively shut down.
- **Cyprus**: CBI program (citizenship) shut down in 2020 after corruption scandal; residency program continues.

**Programs that changed significantly but remain:**
- **Greece**: Minimum investment raised from €250,000 to €500,000 in popular areas (2023).
- **Spain**: Announced plans to abolish the investor/real estate Golden Visa (2024) — ongoing political debate.
- **Portugal's NHR tax regime**: Discontinued January 2024; replaced by IFICI (targeting science, tech, innovation workers).

### Q73. Which countries restrict foreign nationals from buying property?

**Countries with significant restrictions on foreign property purchases:**

- **New Zealand**: Strong restrictions since 2018; foreign buyers generally cannot purchase existing residential property (luxury investor exemption introduced 2025 for Active Investor Plus visa holders).
- **Australia**: Foreign buyers cannot purchase existing residential properties (2025–2027 ban); new construction allowed with FIRB approval.
- **Canada**: Prohibition on Non-Canadians purchasing residential property (2023–2027), with some exceptions.
- **Denmark**: Must have permanent residence or 5+ years of continuous residence.
- **Switzerland**: Strict limits (Lex Koller) on foreign purchase of residential property; limited quotas.
- **Thailand**: Foreigners cannot own land (only condos, up to 49% of units in a building); complex workarounds via long-term leases.
- **Indonesia**: Foreigners cannot own freehold land or standard residential property; can own apartments designated for foreigners.
- **Vietnam**: Foreigners can own apartments (50-year lease) since 2015; land ownership not permitted.
- **Philippines**: Foreigners cannot own land; can own condominiums (up to 40% of units).
- **Balearic Islands (Spain)**: Explored ban on non-resident purchases (2024).
- **Portugal**: No formal ban but political pressure; NHR discontinuation reduced incentive for some investors.

### Q74. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

**Major tax regime changes (2022–2025):**

- **Portugal**: NHR (Non-Habitual Resident) regime — 10% flat tax for retirees, 0% on foreign income for some — **discontinued January 2024**. Replaced by IFICI (targeting scientists, tech, innovation workers).
- **Italy**: "Impatriates" regime (90% income exemption for 5 years) continues; Flat Tax of €100,000/year for HNWIs; attracting retirees, remote workers, and celebrities.
- **Greece**: Flat €100,000/year tax for non-domiciled HNWIs; 7% flat tax for foreign-pension retirees.
- **UAE**: Corporate tax of 9% introduced (June 2023) — breaking the 0% corporate rate — though personal income tax remains 0%.
- **Spain**: Beckham Law — flat 24% tax rate for 6 years for qualifying new residents (expanded to remote workers 2023).
- **Bulgaria, Romania**: Flat 10% income tax maintained, attracting EU residents seeking simplicity.
- **Georgia**: Territorial tax system (only locally-sourced income taxed) continues; small business flat tax remains attractive.
- **UK**: Non-domicile (non-dom) status abolished from April 2025 — significant change affecting HNWIs who resided in UK using non-dom regime.
- **Ireland**: Non-dom regime also modified alongside UK changes.
- **Canada**: Capital gains inclusion rate increased (2024 budget) — drove some high-income earners to consider departure.

### Q75. Which legislative changes most affect migrants?

**Most impactful legislative changes (2022–2025):**

1. **Canada's temporary resident cap** — first-ever target on overall temporary resident population share.
2. **UK's income threshold increases** — minimum income for family visas raised to £38,700 (from ~£18,600), dramatically reducing family reunification.
3. **US enforcement expansion (2025)** — expanded deportation, refugee program suspension, H-4 EAD changes.
4. **EU Pact on Migration** — first comprehensive EU framework; accelerates deportations, creates border procedures.
5. **Portugal's citizenship timeline extension** — residency requirement for citizenship raised from 5 to 10 years for most applicants (April 2026).
6. **UK non-dom abolition (April 2025)** — affects HNWIs' tax planning.
7. **Australia's foreign property ban (2025)** — two-year ban on existing residential property purchases.
8. **Germany's Chancenkarte (2024)** — positive: opens Germany to job-seeking professionals without a pre-arranged job.
9. **Portugal's NHR discontinuation (2024)** — pushed many planned Portugal movers to look elsewhere.
10. **Multiple EU countries' asylum processing tightening** — faster rejections, more deportation pressure.

### Q78. How often do immigration rules change in popular countries?

**Rule change frequency by country:**

- **Portugal**: Multiple significant changes 2022–2026 (Golden Visa routes, NHR discontinuation, citizenship timeline extension, AIMA backlogs). Major change approximately every 6–12 months.
- **Canada**: Near-annual significant policy changes since 2022 (caps, PGWP rules, Express Entry overhaul, PR targets).
- **UK**: Frequent updates — income thresholds, dependent rules, salary floors — changed significantly 2023, 2024, 2025.
- **Australia**: Yearly reforms; skills lists updated quarterly; visa processing times fluctuate.
- **Germany**: Significant reform in 2020 and 2024 (Chancenkarte); ongoing EU Blue Card updates.
- **UAE**: Relatively stable framework since 2021 reforms, with additions (Golden Visa expansions) but no sudden reversals.
- **Singapore**: Relatively stable and predictable; periodic EP salary threshold updates.
- **US**: Highly dependent on administration; major policy swings between administrations (Biden → Trump 2025 was a drastic shift).
- **Spain**: NHR-equivalent (Beckham Law) stable; digital nomad visa launched 2023–2024; planned Golden Visa abolition creates uncertainty.

**General rule**: In fast-changing countries, **any information older than 6–12 months should be re-verified**. Immigration rule changes are among the most volatile pieces of information in this space.

### Q90. What are the weakest or most inconvenient features of these services?

**Most commonly cited weaknesses:**

1. **Outdated information** — most platforms struggle to keep immigration rules current; policies change faster than content is updated (Numbeo, Expat.com, many visa guides).
2. **Crowdsourced data bias** — Numbeo data reflects what price-range reporters submit; skews toward expat neighborhoods and higher-end options; 12–28% higher than official data in tested cases.
3. **Missing nuance for specific situations** — generic info doesn't account for nationality, profession, family situation, or income source.
4. **No integrated journey** — users must visit 5–10 different sites to research one decision; no single platform covers visas + cost + community + housing + taxes.
5. **Visa info not linked to nationality** — most guides list requirements generically; filterable nationality-specific data is rare.
6. **Forum content decays quickly** — Reddit and Expat.com posts from 2020 or 2022 are often wrong; no reliable freshness indicators.
7. **Poor mobile experience** — many data-heavy tools are desktop-only.
8. **Limited coverage of smaller countries** — focused on 20–30 major destinations; niche destinations (Georgia, Albania, Paraguay, Uruguay) underserved.
9. **No personalization** — same data shown to everyone regardless of situation, goals, or starting point.
10. **Limited real-time data** — housing markets, processing times, and local prices change faster than any static database can keep up with.

### Q100. Which paid features do users consider truly valuable?

Based on user feedback, reviews, and satisfaction data across migration platforms:

**Most valued paid features:**
1. **Access to a real expert** (lawyer, tax advisor, relocation specialist) — consistently rated highest; people pay for human judgment in complex personal situations.
2. **Current, verified information** — willingness to pay for accuracy and recency; frustration with free outdated info is high.
3. **Full visa application management** — reducing the cognitive burden of paperwork is highly valued.
4. **Personalized country matching** — based on specific circumstances (nationality, profession, family size, income source, priorities).
5. **Banking and financial setup assistance** — one of the most practically frustrating aspects of relocation; concierge solutions are valued.
6. **Housing search support** — finding vetted rentals before arrival; access to listings unavailable on public platforms.
7. **Local contact networks** — vetted professionals (lawyers, doctors, accountants) in destination country.
8. **Tax planning** — especially for dual/complex tax situations.
9. **Community with verified members** — higher-quality discourse than free forums.
10. **Emergency support** — someone to call when something goes wrong after the move.
