# Part 2: Reasons for Migration
## Questions 21–32

---

### Q21. Why do people most often want to move to another country?

People migrate for a complex mix of reasons, rarely just one factor. Surveys consistently show the following primary motivators:

1. **Economic opportunity**: Better wages, career prospects, lower taxes, or better cost-of-living-to-salary ratio.
2. **Safety and political stability**: Escaping conflict, authoritarianism, corruption, or crime.
3. **Quality of life**: Healthcare, education, environment, urban infrastructure, work-life balance.
4. **Family reunification**: Joining family members who already migrated.
5. **Education**: Access to better universities and schools for self or children.
6. **Freedom and rights**: Civil liberties, minority rights, LGBTQ+ protections, freedom of expression.
7. **Climate and environment**: Preference for specific climates or escaping environmental degradation.
8. **Remote work freedom**: The ability to live anywhere and continue working for a foreign employer.

A Gallup-International survey (57 countries) found that **economic circumstances dominate** the decision to emigrate, but cultural identity, political climate, and personal networks also play major roles.

---

### Q22. How important are economic reasons: wages, work, taxes, cost of living?

**Very important** — economic factors are the single most cited category in migration research:
- Higher wages abroad are the primary driver for labor migrants from developing countries (South Asia, Africa, Latin America).
- For skilled professionals, **salary differentials** can be 2–5× vs. their home country.
- Cost-of-living arbitrage is a major driver for digital nomads: a US or Western European salary stretches dramatically further in Southeast Asia (Vietnam, Thailand, Indonesia), Latin America (Mexico, Colombia), or Eastern Europe (Georgia, Bulgaria, Albania).
- Tax optimization is increasingly important: jurisdictions like UAE (0% income tax), Bulgaria (10% flat rate), Georgia (1% for small business), Paraguay, and Panama attract HNW individuals and entrepreneurs.
- For expats moving *within* high-income countries, financial factors remain important but are often secondary to lifestyle factors.

**InterNations 2024 Expat Insider**: Countries where expats feel financially best off — Vietnam (3rd year at #1 in personal finance), Mexico, Indonesia, Colombia — are all relatively low-cost countries.

---

### Q23. How important are political reasons: instability, restrictions, authoritarianism, wars?

**Critical in some contexts, secondary in others**:
- For migrants from Russia (post-2022), Belarus, Iran, Venezuela, Myanmar, Afghanistan, and Ukraine — political factors are **primary or exclusive** drivers.
- The number of forcibly displaced people (driven by political violence and conflict) hit a **record 123.2 million** in 2024.
- In democratic, stable countries — political factors are less dominant but increasingly cited. Gallup shows rising emigration desire in the US correlating with political polarization.
- For HNWIs globally, political risk to assets and personal safety is a major driver of "plan B" residency and passport acquisition.
- **Authoritarian trend**: Countries where political freedoms have narrowed in recent years see measurable increases in emigration of educated classes — Russia, Turkey, Hungary are prominent examples.

---

### Q24. How important is safety and crime level when choosing a country?

**Highly important**, especially for:
- Families with children: Safety is consistently ranked among the top 2–3 decision factors.
- Women migrating alone: Personal safety is a primary concern.
- Retirees: Safety and stability of environment ranks very high.
- People leaving Latin American or Sub-Saharan African countries: Often cite crime as a primary push factor.

Countries that rank highest in safety (Global Peace Index 2024–2025): Iceland, Ireland, Austria, New Zealand, Singapore, Switzerland, Portugal, Slovenia, Czech Republic, Japan.

Countries often avoided due to crime/safety concerns: Venezuela, Honduras, El Salvador (historically), South Africa, parts of Mexico (though expat enclaves have lower risk).

Note: There is often a **perception-vs-reality gap** in crime assessment — expats sometimes have outdated or distorted perceptions of crime in destination countries (both overestimating and underestimating it).

---

### Q25. How important is healthcare quality?

**Very important**, and gaining in importance:
- For retirees: Often the #1 factor after finances. Access to affordable, quality healthcare can determine the entire migration decision.
- For families: Pediatric care access, vaccination availability, and hospital quality are critical.
- For people with chronic conditions: Medication availability and specialist access can be decisive.
- For young professionals: Often underweighted in planning, then becomes a major pain point post-move.

**Countries with best-rated healthcare** for expats: Germany (#3 globally by some indices), France, Switzerland, Singapore, Japan, South Korea, Canada (public universal), Portugal (public universal, wait times an issue).

**Important distinction**: Countries with *universal* public healthcare vs. those requiring private insurance (or both). In UAE, US, Singapore — private insurance is typically required for expats.

---

### Q26. How important is education for children?

**Extremely important for families** — often the deciding factor:
- The International School Consultancy estimates 6+ million children attend international schools globally, with enrollment growing steadily.
- **Top countries for international schooling**: Singapore, UAE, Netherlands, Germany, UK, Switzerland, Malaysia.
- For university education, families often choose countries where children can attend quality state universities with tuition advantages (EU/EEA countries for EU residents; Canada, Australia for QCR/PR holders).
- Language of instruction is a major filter: English-language education is the most sought-after globally.
- School quality (PISA scores) is consulted: Finland, Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Estonia consistently top rankings.

---

### Q27. How important are climate, ecology, and urban environment quality?

**Increasingly important** — especially in post-pandemic decision-making:
- Climate preference is highly subjective but strongly influences destination choice: warm-climate countries (Spain, Portugal, Mexico, Thailand, Colombia, Malta) attract those fleeing cold northern winters.
- Air quality matters particularly for families with children or health conditions. Concerns about pollution affect perception of China, India, and some Eastern European cities.
- Urban quality of life: walkability, cycling infrastructure, public transit, green spaces. Cities like Vienna, Amsterdam, Zurich, Copenhagen consistently rank highest in quality-of-urban-life indices.
- Environmental degradation and flooding risk are emerging factors, especially for long-term decisions. Climate-exposed countries (Bangladesh, Pacific islands, flood-prone areas) see increased emigration pressure.
- **Outdoor lifestyle** access — nature, mountains, beaches — is a significant lifestyle motivator, especially for digital nomads and retirees.

---

### Q28. How important is the speed of obtaining residency/citizenship?

**Very important**, and often the deciding factor between otherwise equivalent destinations:
- Fast residency access is a major competitive advantage for destination countries.
- **Fast residency programs**: UAE (investor/skilled visa often issued in weeks), Portugal D7/D8 (process: weeks to months), Georgia (automatic residency after 1 year), Paraguay (residency in ~3–6 months), Panama (pensionado program, fast).
- **Slow programs causing frustration**: Portugal's Golden Visa faced severe backlash over multi-year processing delays (AIMA backlogs); Canadian PR increasingly competitive and unpredictable.
- **Citizenship timeline** is often just as important as residency: Portugal (5 years), Bulgaria (5 years), Georgia (5+ years), Panama (5 years). Malta citizenship by investment (1–3 years, under EU scrutiny).
- **Processing bottlenecks** are a major pain point: outdated immigration systems, understaffed agencies, and paperwork delays are frequently cited in expat communities as among the most frustrating aspects of relocating.

---

### Q29. How important are taxes and financial freedom?

**Critical for HNW individuals, entrepreneurs, and remote workers with location flexibility**:
- Zero income tax: UAE, Cayman Islands, Bahamas, Monaco, Qatar.
- Territorial tax (only local income taxed): Panama, Costa Rica, Georgia, Malaysia, Singapore, Paraguay, Belize.
- Low flat tax: Bulgaria (10%), Georgia (20%), Romania (10%), Hungary (15%).
- Special expat tax regimes: Italy's "impatriates" regime (90% exemption for 5 years), Greece's flat €100,000/year tax for non-domiciled HNWIs, Malta's Global Residence Programme.
- Portugal's NHR regime (10% flat tax for retirees; reduced/zero for certain professions) was **discontinued in 2024**, but a new "IFICI" regime was introduced targeting scientists, tech workers, and investors.
- Tax treaties between countries matter: avoiding double taxation is a primary concern for globally mobile professionals.

---

### Q30. How important is the ability to open a business or invest?

**Very important for entrepreneurs, startup founders, and investors**:
- Ease of business registration: Estonia (fully online, e-residency system is globally pioneering), Singapore (hours to register), New Zealand, Denmark — regularly top indices.
- Business-friendly tax: UAE (9% corporate tax since 2023, previously 0%), Ireland (12.5% corporate), Bulgaria (10%), Singapore (17%).
- Access to capital and ecosystem: US (Silicon Valley, NYC, Boston), UK (London), Singapore, Estonia, Israel for tech startups.
- Startup visa programs: Portugal D2, Canada Startup Visa, UK Global Talent, Netherlands Self-Employment, Germany Self-Employment/Freelancer, Singapore EntrePass — all target entrepreneurs.
- **Countries competing for startups**: Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Germany, UAE, Singapore, Canada.

---

### Q31. How important are language, culture, and ease of adaptation?

**More important than many anticipate** — and a major source of post-move regret:
- **Language barrier** is consistently cited as a top challenge for expats in non-English-speaking countries.
- Countries where English is widely spoken (even if not official): Netherlands, Scandinavia, Singapore, UAE, Malta — very popular with English-speaking expats.
- **Cultural compatibility** is harder to quantify but matters greatly for long-term satisfaction: similarity of working culture, social norms, attitudes toward foreigners.
- **Social isolation** is one of the most common complaints post-move, particularly in countries with close-knit local cultures (Japan, South Korea, Germany — known for being hard to "break into" socially).
- **Expat community size**: Countries with large existing expat communities (Portugal, Spain, UAE, Singapore, Thailand) ease adaptation significantly.
- **Language learning investment**: Countries that offer free language integration courses (Germany's Integrationskurs, Netherlands' inburgering) reduce this barrier.

### Q32. What most often makes people give up on moving?

The most common barriers to acting on migration intent:
1. **Financial cost of moving**: Relocation, legal fees, lost income during transition, cost of living setup.
2. **Complexity of visa/residency paperwork**: Long, opaque processes deter many potential migrants.
3. **Fear of losing career momentum**: Concerns about professional network, credentials recognition, or employer recognition.
4. **Family ties**: Reluctance to leave aging parents, close friends, established support networks.
5. **Language barriers**: Lack of confidence in navigating daily life in a foreign language.
6. **Uncertainty about income stability**: Not having a job offer in hand before moving.
7. **Housing and schooling concerns**: Finding suitable accommodation and schools from abroad is daunting.
8. **Fear of isolation**: Concern about difficulty making friends and building social life.
9. **Partner/spouse reluctance**: One partner being unwilling to move is a major blocker for families.
10. **Political/bureaucratic unpredictability**: Rule changes, visa refusals, processing delays — these create anxiety and cause delays even when intent is strong.
11. **Information overload and contradictory advice**: The sheer volume of conflicting information online paralyzes decision-making.
