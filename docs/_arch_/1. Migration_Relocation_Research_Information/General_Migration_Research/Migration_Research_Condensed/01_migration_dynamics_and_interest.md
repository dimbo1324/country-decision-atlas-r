# Part 1: Global Migration Dynamics & Interest in Moving Abroad
## Questions 1–20

---

## Section 1: General Migration Dynamics

### Q1. Is the total number of international migrants growing or declining?

It is clearly **growing**. According to the UN Department of Economic and Social Affairs (UNDESA), the global number of international migrants reached **304 million in 2024**, nearly doubling since 1990 (when the figure was approximately 154 million). However, as a share of the global population, the increase is more modest: migrants now make up about **3.7%** of the global population, up only modestly from 2.9% in 1990.

---

### Q2. How has international migration changed over the last 10–20 years?

Migration has increased substantially in volume, diversity, and complexity:

- **Volume**: From roughly 220 million in 2010 to 304 million in 2024.
- **Composition**: Women now account for 48% of all international migrants (146 million), with the number of female migrants growing 35.5% since 2010.
- **Forced displacement**: The number of forcibly displaced people worldwide hit a **record high of 123.2 million** at the end of 2024 (UNHCR), including 36.8 million refugees, 8.4 million asylum seekers, and 73.5 million internally displaced persons (IDPs).
- **Remittances**: International remittances surged by 650%, from USD 128 billion to USD 831 billion, between 2000 and 2022.
- **Post-pandemic rebound**: After COVID-19 disrupted mobility in 2020–2021, migration to OECD countries rebounded sharply in 2022–2023, then normalized slightly in 2024 (OECD International Migration Outlook 2025).

Key types that have grown fastest include **humanitarian/forced migration**, **family-based migration**, and **highly skilled labor migration**.

---

### Q3. Which regions of the world are currently receiving the most migrants?

- **Europe** is the largest host region globally, with **94 million** international migrants in 2024.
- **Northern America** (US, Canada, and territories) hosted approximately **61 million**.
- **Asia** (particularly Gulf states) hosts large migrant populations relative to national populations — Qatar (76.7% foreign-born), UAE (~74% foreign-born), Kuwait, and Bahrain consistently top the list by share.
- **Sub-Saharan Africa** and **Latin America** also receive significant intra-regional migration flows.

---

### Q4. From which regions and countries do people most often leave?

Top origin regions and countries:
- **South and Southeast Asia**: India, Bangladesh, Philippines, Pakistan — largest sources of labor migrants globally.
- **Latin America**: Mexico, El Salvador, Honduras, Venezuela (with Venezuela seeing dramatic outflows post-2017).
- **Sub-Saharan Africa**: Nigeria, Ethiopia, Democratic Republic of Congo, Ghana.
- **Eastern Europe**: Ukraine (especially after the 2022 Russian invasion), Moldova, Romania.
- **North Africa and Middle East**: Syria, Afghanistan, Morocco, Egypt.

The **highest emigration intent** (share of population wanting to leave) is found in Sub-Saharan Africa (56%) and Latin America (54%), per Gallup International data.

---

### Q5. Which countries are the main migration destinations?

The **top 5 destination countries** by total migrant stock as of 2024 (UN data):
1. **United States** — 50+ million foreign-born residents (~15% of population)
2. **Germany** — ~15.7 million foreign-born (EU's largest recipient by volume)
3. **Saudi Arabia** — ~13.5 million migrants
4. **Russia** — ~12 million migrants
5. **United Kingdom** — ~9–10 million migrants

Other consistently top-ranked destinations: **France, Canada, Australia, Spain, UAE**.

For permanent migration flows to OECD countries in 2024, the US alone received **1.4 million new permanent immigrants**, representing about 25% of all OECD permanent migration.

---

### Q6. Which countries have become more popular for relocation in the last 5 years?

Countries that gained significantly in migrant flows or desirability since 2019–2020:
- **Spain**: Grew 3.5 percentage points in migrant share; became particularly popular with digital nomads and Latin Americans.
- **Portugal**: Highly popular among remote workers and expats until visa route changes; still highly desirable.
- **Germany**: Major spike in skilled migration after the Skilled Immigration Act 2020 reforms; introduced new "Opportunity Card" (Chancenkarte) in 2024.
- **UAE / Dubai**: UAE attracted 173,000 highly skilled workers in 2024 alone — a 21% increase; became the 4th most popular global destination for skilled professionals (BCG).
- **Mexico**: Major nearshoring hub; received a large influx of American and other expats, especially remote workers and digital nomads.
- **Colombia, Uruguay, Albania, Georgia**: Gained traction as low-cost digital nomad or relocation destinations.
- **South Korea**: Launched its Workation Visa in 2024 attracting tech nomads.

---

### Q7. Which countries are losing attractiveness for migrants?

- **UK**: Post-Brexit immigration policies created barriers; net migration initially spiked but faced political backlash and new restrictions in 2023–2024.
- **Portugal (partially)**: NHR (Non-Habitual Resident) tax regime discontinued in 2024; real estate removed from Golden Visa in 2023; housing costs rose dramatically.
- **Russia**: Mass emigration of educated Russians post-2022 invasion of Ukraine; country now a net emigration source among high-skilled population.
- **Canada (perception shift)**: Rising housing costs, lower salaries vs. cost of living, and backlash against high immigration volumes made some reconsider.
- **Some Gulf states** (Saudi Arabia, UAE): Slight decline in migrant share of population (by 2024 data), though absolute numbers grew.

---

### Q8. Which types of migration are growing fastest?

By growth rate (2020–2025):
1. **Humanitarian / forced migration**: Fastest growth in absolute numbers — record 123.2M displaced globally in 2024, driven by Ukraine, Sudan, Myanmar, and Afghanistan.
2. **Digital nomad / remote work migration**: Institutionalized post-COVID; 45+ countries now offer dedicated digital nomad visas (up from <5 in 2019).
3. **Family-based migration**: Still the largest single category in OECD countries by volume.
4. **Highly skilled labor migration**: Growing, especially in tech, healthcare, engineering; programs multiplying (EU Blue Card, Canada Express Entry, Germany's Chancenkarte).
5. **Education migration**: Grew until 2023 but declined ~13% in 2024 per OECD data.
6. **Investment / citizenship-by-investment (CBI)**: Growing demand but supply constrained as many EU countries closed or tightened programs.

---

### Q9. How did migration patterns change after COVID-19?

COVID-19 caused the **largest single-year drop in international migration in decades** in 2020, then triggered an unprecedented rebound:
- Global migration slowed in 2020–2021 due to border closures, lockdowns, and economic contraction.
- **Post-pandemic rebound (2022–2023)**: Permanent migration to OECD countries hit record levels — 6.2M in 2024, still 15% above 2019 levels.
- **Remote work normalization** fundamentally changed *why* and *where* people relocated — geography decoupled from employment for millions.
- **"Zoom towns"** and digital nomad migration surged — people moved to lower-cost countries or smaller cities without leaving their jobs.
- International student flows rebounded strongly in 2022–2023 before declining again in 2024.
- Temporary work migration stabilized at historically high levels (~2.3M work permits/authorizations in OECD, 26% above 2019).

---

### Q10. Which global events most affected migration in recent years?

1. **Russia's invasion of Ukraine (2022)**: Triggered the **fastest mass displacement in Europe since WWII** — over 6.5 million refugees registered outside Ukraine; also caused a large-scale emigration of educated Russians ("brain drain").
2. **COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022)**: Shut borders, reshaped remote work, and restructured migration priorities.
3. **Afghan crisis (2021)**: Taliban takeover triggered mass displacement; over 6 million Afghans displaced.
4. **Sudan civil war (2023–)**: Triggered one of the largest African displacement crises.
5. **Venezuelan crisis (ongoing)**: 7.7+ million Venezuelans displaced — one of the world's largest ongoing displacement situations.
6. **Global cost-of-living crisis (2022–2024)**: Rising inflation and housing costs drove outmigration from high-cost countries and shifted destination preferences.
7. **Rise of right-wing populism and immigration restrictions**: UK (post-Brexit), US (border crisis politics), and EU-wide anti-immigration sentiment increased friction for certain migrant categories.

---

## Section 2: Interest in Moving Abroad

### Q11. Is the number of people who want to move to another country growing?

Yes, significantly. Gallup data shows that **desire to migrate remains at a record high**:
- In 2021, **16% of adults worldwide** (~900 million people) said they would like to leave their country permanently.
- By 2023, desire to migrate had increased in most regions compared to 2011 baselines.
- In the US specifically, **21% of Americans** said they wanted to move abroad in 2024 — a record high (up from 10% in 2011).
- Among US women aged 18–44, desire to migrate reached **41–45%** by 2024–2025, the highest on record.

Globally, Gallup-International found that **1 in 3 adults** in a 57-country study expressed willingness to emigrate.

---

### Q12. Which countries have the highest share of people wanting to emigrate?

Countries with the highest emigration desire (Gallup and Gallup-International data):
- **Sierra Leone**: ~84%
- **Ghana**: ~81%
- **Nigeria**: ~71%
- **Guyana**: >50%
- **Congo (DRC)**: >50%

Countries with the lowest emigration desire:
- **India**: ~4%
- **Vietnam**: ~8%
- **Japan**: ~14%
- **Saudi Arabia**: ~1%
- **Australia, Thailand, South Africa**: low single digits

The pattern is clear: emigration desire is highest in **Sub-Saharan Africa** (56% average) and **Latin America** (54%), and lowest in **South and East Asia** (10% average).

---

### Q13. Which countries do people most often name as desired migration destinations?

Consistently, year after year (Gallup World Poll since 2007):
1. **United States** — top desired destination globally every year since 2007
2. **Canada** — especially popular in Asia, Africa, Latin America
3. **Germany** — top choice in many European surveys
4. **Australia** — highly desired in Asia, South Asia
5. **United Kingdom** — popular globally despite Brexit
6. **Switzerland** — ranked #1 in Remitly's 2026 Immigration Index
7. **France, Spain, Italy** — popular destinations in surveys

For **expats already living abroad** (InterNations 2024 Expat Insider):
- **Panama** ranked #1 for overall expat satisfaction in 2024
- **Vietnam** ranked #1 for personal finance/affordability in 2025
- **Mexico** consistently appears for Americans

---

### Q14. Which age groups are most interested in relocation?

Globally, younger adults are most mobile:
- **18–34 year-olds** express the highest desire to migrate in virtually every country surveyed.
- **35–44 year-olds** form the largest group of actual economic migrants.
- The gap between desire and action narrows in the 25–40 range, where careers and income make migration financially feasible.
- Among Americans, **younger women (18–44)** saw the steepest rise in emigration desire in 2024–2025.
- **Students and early-career professionals** drive education migration and early-stage work migration.

The "most mobile" demographic globally is **unmarried adults under 35 without children**, who face fewer structural barriers. However, family migration (couples with children) represents a large share of **actual** migration flows.

---

### Q15. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

Most likely to consider migration:
1. **IT and tech professionals**: Highest international demand; companies actively recruit globally; remote work enables location independence.
2. **Healthcare workers** (doctors, nurses): Chronic shortages in OECD countries; dedicated recruitment programs in UK, Canada, Germany, Australia.
3. **Engineers** (civil, mechanical, electrical): Strong demand in Germany, UAE, Australia.
4. **Academics and researchers**: EU research permit programs, high international mobility.
5. **Entrepreneurs and startup founders**: Drawn by startup visa programs (Portugal D2, UAE, Singapore, Germany, Canada).
6. **Digital nomads / freelancers**: Location-independent by profession; 45+ countries now offer dedicated visas.
7. **Finance and business professionals**: High demand in Singapore, Hong Kong, UAE, Luxembourg.

---

### Q16. Is interest in migration growing among skilled professionals?

Yes. BCG's Top Talent Tracker reports approximately **2.6 million highly skilled professionals** crossed borders in 2024. While overall mobility slightly declined (–0.4%), **STEM professionals** and especially **AI specialists** move at nearly twice the rate of other workers. Countries are aggressively competing for this group:
- Germany introduced the **Chancenkarte** (Opportunity Card) in 2024 — a points-based pre-arrival job search visa.
- UAE: 21% increase in highly skilled worker arrivals in 2024.
- Singapore, Canada, UK, Australia all expanded or simplified skilled migration pathways.

---

### Q17. Is interest in migration growing among entrepreneurs and investors?

Yes, strongly. Key trends:
- **Citizenship-by-investment (CBI)** and **golden visa** programs remain in high demand globally despite some EU closures.
- **Startup visa programs** are expanding — Portugal D2, Germany, Netherlands, Singapore, Canada all offer entrepreneur pathways.
- **Low-tax jurisdictions** (UAE, Malta, Bulgaria, Georgia, Paraguay) attract HNW individuals.
- **Portugal's Golden Visa** hit a **record 4,987 applications in 2024** — a 72% year-on-year increase — even after real estate was removed as a qualifying route.
- Americans ranked as the top nationality investing in Portugal's Golden Visa in 2025.

---

### Q18. Is interest in migration growing among students?

- International student numbers grew sharply post-COVID through 2023, then **declined ~13% in 2024** (OECD).
- Longer-term, the global student mobility market is projected to grow: the US, UK, Australia, Canada, and Germany remain the top 5 host countries.
- Rising costs and visa restrictions in the **UK and Canada** caused declines; **Germany** (free public university tuition) gained significantly.
- APAC countries (Japan, South Korea, Singapore) are investing heavily in attracting international students.

---

### Q19. Is interest in migration growing among families with children?

Yes, and distinctly:
- **Education quality** has become the #1 factor for family migration decisions (above economic factors in many surveys).
- **Safety** and **healthcare** quality are second and third drivers for families.
- Countries with strong international school networks (UAE, Singapore, Germany, Netherlands) attract expat families.
- "Family-based migration" remains the **largest single category** of permanent migration to OECD countries.
- Spain ranked #1 as the most family-friendly country for migrants (Remitly 2026 Immigration Index).
- Sri Lanka and China also scored highly for family-friendly factors (education costs, childcare).

---

### Q20. Which groups most often look for information about second passports, residency permits, or permanent residency?

The most active searchers for second passports / dual residency / citizenship-by-investment:
1. **High-net-worth individuals (HNWIs)**: Seeking tax optimization, travel freedom, asset protection.
2. **Citizens of countries with weak passports**: Russia, China, Iran, Nigeria, Pakistan — citizens actively seek EU/Caribbean/other passports for visa-free travel.
3. **Tech entrepreneurs and startup founders**: Seeking operational flexibility.
4. **Professionals from politically unstable countries**: Searching for a "plan B."
5. **Remote workers and digital nomads**: Seeking long-term legal status in preferred locations.
6. **Americans**: Rising interest in EU residency post-2016 and especially post-2024 elections.
7. **Retirees**: Looking for affordable, warm countries with healthcare (Portugal, Spain, Mexico, Thailand, Panama).
