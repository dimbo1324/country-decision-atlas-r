# 3. Reasons for Migration

## 21. Why do people most often want to move to another country?

The most common reasons are better income, safer life, career opportunities, political stability, family reunification, education, healthcare, personal freedom and long-term prospects for children. In poorer countries, economic opportunity dominates. In middle-income and politically unstable countries, people often combine income, safety and rule-of-law concerns. In high-income countries, relocation can be driven by lifestyle, taxes, healthcare costs, political polarisation or retirement plans. Migration is rarely caused by one factor; it is usually a bundle of “push” dissatisfaction and “pull” opportunity.

## 22. How important are economic reasons: salaries, jobs, taxes and cost of living?

Economic reasons are among the strongest and most measurable drivers. Salary gaps, unemployment, inflation, currency depreciation and career ceilings push people out; higher wages, stable currency, professional recognition and predictable taxes pull them in. However, cost of living can reverse the logic: a country with high salaries may be unattractive if housing, childcare, taxes and healthcare absorb most income. In communities, the real question is not “where is the salary highest?” but “where is the disposable income and life stability best for my profile?”

## 23. How important are political reasons: instability, restrictions, authoritarianism and war?

Political reasons are highly important when they affect personal safety, rights, conscription, censorship, property security, business freedom or children’s future. War and persecution create forced migration. Authoritarian drift and political polarisation create “optionality migration”: people acquire residence or citizenship elsewhere before they are forced to use it. Political motives are visible in post-election relocation searches, investment-migration inquiries and communities such as AmerExit. The strongest signal is not always immediate departure; it is preparation — documents, passports, foreign bank accounts and backup residence.

## 24. How important are safety and crime levels when choosing a country?

Safety is central, especially for families, women, LGBTQ+ migrants, retirees and people moving from high-crime environments. People evaluate homicide rates, street crime, corruption, police trust, domestic violence protection, terrorism risk, war risk and social stability. Community discussions show that perceived safety can differ from official rankings: a statistically safe country may feel unsafe because of scams, harassment or racism; a statistically less safe country may feel acceptable in specific neighbourhoods. Safety is therefore both data-driven and experiential.

## 25. How important are healthcare and healthcare-system quality?

Healthcare is extremely important for families, retirees, people with chronic conditions, pregnant women, and high-net-worth individuals planning long-term residence. The key criteria are public access, private insurance cost, waiting times, medication availability, language access, emergency care and recognition of foreign prescriptions. Wealth-migration reporting increasingly notes healthcare cost and resilience as a factor. For migrants, healthcare is not just “quality”; it is legal eligibility. A world-class system is less useful if newcomers are excluded, uninsured or face long waiting periods.

## 26. How important are education and opportunities for children?

For families, education is often a top-three factor. Parents evaluate school quality, language of instruction, bullying, integration support, university access, tuition fees, special needs provision and whether children can obtain citizenship or long-term residence. Countries with good public schools, safe cities and accessible universities become attractive even if salaries are not the highest. Education also drives student migration directly: students choose countries based on university reputation, post-study work rights, tuition, language, job market and future residence pathway.

## 27. How important are climate, ecology and urban quality?

Climate and urban environment are increasingly important but usually secondary to legality, money and safety. People care about air quality, heat, humidity, winter severity, walkability, public transport, noise, green space, water security and natural disaster exposure. Digital nomads often over-weight climate and lifestyle; families and long-term migrants later discover that bureaucracy, taxes and schools matter more. Climate risk is also becoming strategic: flood, wildfire, drought and heat risk may affect insurance, property values, health and future migration flows.

## 28. How important is the ability to quickly obtain residence, permanent residence or citizenship?

It is extremely important for people seeking legal certainty. Fast residence matters for immediate relocation; permanent residence matters for job stability and family life; citizenship matters for passport strength, voting rights and protection from policy changes. Many people choose countries not because they are the richest, but because legal status is achievable. Examples include Paraguay/Uruguay/Argentina-style residence discussions in Latin America, Portugal’s former five-year citizenship appeal, Canada’s PR pathway, and Germany’s skilled routes. Recent tightening in Portugal and Canada shows why speed and predictability matter.

## 29. How important are taxes and financial freedom?

Taxes are decisive for entrepreneurs, investors, freelancers, remote workers and high earners. They are less decisive for low-income migrants unless tax affects net salary and social benefits. Key issues are tax residency, worldwide income taxation, double-tax treaties, social contributions, capital gains, CFC rules, inheritance tax, exit tax and crypto/asset rules. Many people wrongly compare headline income tax rates while ignoring social security, VAT, municipal taxes and healthcare contributions. Tax attractiveness can change quickly when governments face housing or revenue pressures.

## 30. How important is the ability to open a business or invest?

It is highly important for entrepreneurs, investors and self-employed migrants. Criteria include company formation speed, banking access, payment processing, corruption, labour law, taxes, permits, property rights, investor visas, startup visas and access to talent. Countries actively competing for entrepreneurs often offer startup visas, innovation grants, English-speaking administration, e-residency or low corporate tax. But “easy to open a company” is not enough: the real test is whether the country has customers, capital, legal predictability and a realistic route to residence.

## 31. How important are language, culture and ease of adaptation?

Language and culture often determine whether a technically successful relocation becomes emotionally sustainable. English-speaking countries attract migrants partly because adaptation feels easier, but they are often expensive and legally selective. Countries with lower English proficiency may be cheaper and legally easier but harder socially. Community complaints after relocation often involve loneliness, bureaucracy, local friendship barriers, language fatigue, different work culture and feeling permanently foreign. Adaptation is especially important for spouses and children, not only the main applicant.

## 32. What reasons most often make people abandon relocation plans?

The most common blockers are visa ineligibility, lack of money, fear of job loss, family obligations, language barriers, housing costs, tax complexity, uncertainty about children’s adaptation, fear of loneliness, bureaucracy, medical risks and inability to sell property or transfer money. Many people also abandon plans after discovering that online advice was oversimplified. A country may be attractive in YouTube videos but impossible for a specific passport, profession, budget or family structure.
