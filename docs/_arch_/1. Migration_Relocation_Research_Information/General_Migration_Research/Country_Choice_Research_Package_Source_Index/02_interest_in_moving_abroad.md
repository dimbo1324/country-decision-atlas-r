# 2. People’s Interest in Moving Abroad

## 11. Is the number of people who want to move to another country increasing?

The desire to migrate is historically high but not always rising every year. Gallup found that in 2023 about **16% of adults worldwide**, over 900 million people, said they would like to leave their country permanently if they could. This was higher than most levels recorded between 2011 and 2018, but Gallup’s later update suggests global desire has recently softened in some places. The deeper signal is not a simple linear rise; it is a sustained high baseline with spikes after political, economic or security shocks.

## 12. In which countries is the share of people wanting to emigrate highest?

High emigration desire tends to cluster in countries with weak job markets, insecurity, political instability, youth unemployment, currency weakness or weak trust in institutions. Gallup’s potential youth-loss and brain-loss measures highlight parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, North Africa, Latin America, the Caribbean and some crisis-affected countries. Examples often include Sierra Leone, Liberia, Ghana, Honduras, Togo, Lebanon, Peru, Bolivia and Bangladesh depending on the index and period. The important nuance: high desire does not equal high actual migration, because visas, money and networks constrain movement.

## 13. Which countries do people most often name as desired places to live?

The United States has long been the most desired single destination in Gallup polling, but Canada, Germany, the UK, Australia, France, Spain, New Zealand, Switzerland and the UAE are also recurring aspiration destinations. Search-based and relocation-industry signals often add Portugal, Italy, Ireland, Mexico, Costa Rica, Japan and Scandinavian countries. Desired destinations differ by origin group: students look at the U.S., UK, Canada, Australia and Germany; digital nomads look at Portugal, Spain, Mexico, Thailand, Indonesia and Colombia; investors look at the UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Italy, Portugal, Greece and Caribbean CBI jurisdictions.

## 14. Which age groups are most interested in moving?

Young adults are usually the most migration-oriented group. The 15–29 and 20–35 age ranges show the highest interest because they have fewer family constraints, longer career horizons and stronger tolerance for uncertainty. Students, early-career professionals and young skilled workers are especially mobile. Families often become interested later, but their threshold is higher because they must evaluate schools, healthcare, safety, housing and spouse employment. Older people move less often for work but may consider retirement, healthcare, tax and lifestyle relocation.

## 15. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

The most migration-oriented professional groups include technology workers, engineers, healthcare workers, researchers, academics, finance professionals, entrepreneurs, startup founders, skilled trades in shortage occupations, seasonal/agricultural workers, hospitality workers and care workers. Healthcare is structurally important because OECD health systems rely heavily on migrant doctors and nurses. Tech and engineering professionals are attracted by higher salaries and global labour shortages, although the white-collar slowdown after 2023–2025 reduced some international job-search intensity.

## 16. Is interest in migration growing among skilled professionals?

Yes, but unevenly. Governments are competing for skilled workers through points systems, EU Blue Card reforms, Germany’s Opportunity Card, Australia’s Skills in Demand visa, Canada’s Express Entry category-based selection and sector-specific pathways. Skilled professionals are interested because international migration can multiply income, improve career prospects and provide legal stability. However, demand is now more selective: countries want specific occupations, language ability, recognised qualifications and high salaries. Skilled migration is no longer simply “educated people can move”; it is “the right skill, in the right shortage, with the right documentation.”

## 17. Is interest growing among entrepreneurs, investors and business owners?

Yes. Wealth migration and investment migration are increasingly driven by geopolitical hedging, tax planning, optionality, banking access, children’s education, safety and estate planning. Henley’s wealth migration reporting points to record or near-record millionaire migration, with strong interest in the UAE, U.S., Italy, Switzerland, Portugal, Greece, Singapore, New Zealand and selected Caribbean programmes. But Europe is simultaneously restricting some real-estate golden visas, especially where housing affordability became politically sensitive. Investors are moving from “buy property for a visa” toward broader “sovereign portfolio” strategies.

## 18. Is interest growing among students?

Long-term student mobility is strongly up. UNESCO reports that international student mobility has tripled over two decades, reaching about 7.3 million students abroad. OECD data show international students in OECD countries rose from roughly 3.0 million in 2014 to more than 4.6 million by 2022. However, destination policy is changing: Canada, Australia, the UK and the Netherlands have introduced measures to reduce or stabilise student inflows. Students still want international education, but the destination map may diversify toward Germany, France, Spain, Japan, South Korea, the UAE and emerging regional hubs.

## 19. Is interest growing among families with children?

Family relocation interest is growing where parents worry about security, education, healthcare, political instability, conscription risk, pollution, children’s rights or long-term citizenship options. Families are more conservative than single migrants: they need schools, paediatric care, safe neighbourhoods, predictable legal status and spouse work rights. Online family migration communities often focus on childcare costs, bullying, language adaptation, special needs, school quality and whether a country is genuinely child-friendly. Families are less likely to move only for lifestyle; they usually need a strong safety/legal/education argument.

## 20. Which groups most often search for second citizenship, residence permits or permanent residence?

The strongest groups are high-net-worth individuals, politically anxious middle-class families, globally mobile professionals, entrepreneurs, people from countries with weak passports, citizens of unstable or sanctioned countries, retirees seeking healthcare/cost advantages, and parents planning education for children. U.S. citizens have become more visible in second-citizenship and residency discussions because of political polarisation and global tax complications. Russians, Chinese, Indians, Middle Eastern citizens, South Africans and Latin Americans also appear frequently in investment-migration and residence-by-investment markets.
