# 7. Laws, Visas and State Policy

## 67. Which countries have simplified residence or permanent residence rules in recent years?

Simplification is selective. Germany expanded skilled-worker routes and launched the Opportunity Card. Australia introduced the Skills in Demand visa with clearer skilled pathways. New Zealand announced or adjusted skilled and seasonal pathways. Japan is reforming its trainee system into a more formal labour-migration channel. Spain adjusted seasonal-work authorisations. Some countries launched digital nomad or startup routes. However, simplification usually targets specific groups: skilled workers, shortage occupations, entrepreneurs, remote workers, investors or seasonal labour — not general migration.

## 68. Which countries have tightened migration rules recently?

Canada reduced temporary resident and international student targets. The UK tightened student dependants, skilled-worker salary thresholds, care-worker recruitment and settlement rules. Australia tightened student and temporary migration settings while creating targeted skilled routes. Portugal moved toward stricter immigration and nationality rules. Several EU states tightened asylum or family-reunification rules. Sweden, the Netherlands, France, Italy, Greece and others have seen stronger restriction-oriented debates. The pattern is “tighten broad inflows, protect selected talent channels.”

## 69. Which countries launched digital nomad visas or similar programmes?

Many countries launched or formalised remote-work permits after 2020. Examples include Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Croatia, Greece, Malta, Cyprus, Italy, Latvia, Romania, Iceland, Norway, Georgia, UAE/Dubai, Brazil, Argentina, Colombia, Costa Rica, Panama, Barbados, Bermuda, Curaçao, Mauritius, Seychelles, Cape Verde, Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia/Bali-related remote-work discussions, Japan and South Korea-style remote/stay programmes. Requirements vary: foreign income, health insurance, clean criminal record, minimum income and no local employment.

## 70. Which countries closed or restricted golden visa and investment programmes?

Spain ended its real-estate golden visa route in April 2025. Portugal removed real estate from its golden visa path earlier and shifted toward funds/donations/other routes while facing backlogs. Ireland ended its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023. The UK closed the Tier 1 Investor route in 2022. The Netherlands ended its investor scheme. Greece raised thresholds in popular areas. EU institutions continue to pressure risky programmes. Vanuatu lost EU visa-free access because of concerns around its passport-for-investment scheme.

## 71. Which countries simplified relocation for skilled professionals?

Germany, Australia, Canada, New Zealand, Japan, Ireland, Spain and some Gulf countries are notable. Germany’s Skilled Immigration Act and Opportunity Card are designed to make job search and recognition easier. Australia’s Skills in Demand visa targets employer-sponsored skilled labour. Canada still uses Express Entry and category-based selection, though overall levels are being recalibrated. Japan, Korea and Singapore compete for specialised talent. The key trend is shortage-based selection, not general openness.

## 72. Which countries actively compete for entrepreneurs and startups?

Countries competing for founders include Canada, the UK, France, Germany, the Netherlands, Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Ireland, Denmark, Singapore, UAE, Chile, New Zealand and Australia. They use startup visas, innovation grants, accelerator-linked visas, tax incentives, e-residency, English-friendly incorporation and public-private startup ecosystems. The most attractive countries combine legal residence, banking, investor access, talent pools and predictable regulation. A startup visa without customers or capital is weak.

## 73. Which countries restrict foreign property purchases?

Restrictions exist or have expanded in Canada, New Zealand, Australia, Singapore, Thailand, Denmark, Switzerland, Spain discussions, parts of Mexico’s coastal/border zones through trust structures, and many local jurisdictions with anti-speculation taxes. Restrictions can include outright bans, higher stamp duties, vacancy taxes, non-resident buyer taxes, approval requirements or limits on land ownership. Housing affordability pressure is making property-based migration politically sensitive.

## 74. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

The UK abolished or overhauled the non-dom regime. Portugal closed the original NHR regime and replaced it with narrower incentives. Italy raised or adjusted its flat-tax regime for wealthy foreigners. Greece, Cyprus, Malta, Spain, the UAE, Saudi Arabia and several Latin American jurisdictions continue to modify tax-residence incentives. Tax rules often change faster than immigration marketing. New residents should verify income source, days, double-tax treaties and exit rules before moving.

## 75. Which legal changes most affect migrants?

The most impactful changes are visa eligibility, salary thresholds, dependent rules, study-permit caps, work rights, PR/citizenship timelines, family reunification, asylum procedures, healthcare access, rental rules, foreign-document recognition, border-entry practice and processing backlogs. Even small legal changes can reshape migration decisions if they affect spouses, children or time counted toward permanent residence. Policy uncertainty itself becomes a migration factor.

## 76. Which legal changes most affect businesspeople and investors?

The most important changes are golden visa closures, investment thresholds, real-estate restrictions, tax-residence reforms, beneficial-ownership transparency, banking due diligence, CFC rules, exit taxes, sanctions, capital controls, anti-money-laundering rules and citizenship-by-investment due diligence. Investors now need more than a visa: they need a compliance strategy across tax, banking, company structure, residency and family planning.

## 77. Where can official information on visas, residence, PR and citizenship be found?

The safest sources are government immigration portals, foreign ministry/consulate websites, official gazettes, migration-agency pages, parliamentary legal databases and official e-visa portals. Secondary sources can help interpret rules, but final verification should always use official pages. For cross-country monitoring, useful sources include OECD policy reports, IOM, Migration Data Portal, UNHCR for protection, EU Commission/Council pages, national immigration departments and reputable immigration law firms.

## 78. How often do migration rules change in popular countries?

Popular countries can change rules several times per year, especially for student visas, shortage occupations, salary thresholds, processing quotas and family rules. Major reforms may happen annually or after elections. Canada, the UK, Australia and Portugal show how quickly a previously attractive pathway can tighten. Digital nomad and investment programmes also change frequently because they are politically visible. Any platform or guide must timestamp every rule and show last verification date.
