# 11. Future Trends

## 111. Which migration directions may become more popular in the next 5–10 years?

Likely growth directions include skilled migration to ageing economies, healthcare-worker migration to OECD countries, student flows to more affordable education hubs, remote workers to mid-cost countries, investor migration to tax-stable safe jurisdictions, and regional migration within Africa, Latin America and Asia. Countries such as Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Portugal/Spain alternatives, Poland, Czechia, Ireland, Uruguay, Panama, Costa Rica, Malaysia and Thailand may remain important depending on policy.

## 112. Which countries may lose attractiveness in the next 5–10 years?

Countries may lose attractiveness if housing becomes unaffordable, immigration rules tighten, taxes rise, political instability grows, healthcare worsens, anti-migrant sentiment increases or climate risk intensifies. Canada, UK, Portugal, Netherlands, parts of Australia and major U.S. cities face affordability and policy-pressure risks. The UK may remain elite-attractive but less broadly attractive. Some digital nomad hotspots may lose appeal because of rent inflation and local backlash.

## 113. How does remote work affect migration?

Remote work decouples income from location and enables trial relocation, slow travel and residence diversification. It created digital nomad visas and made mid-cost countries more attractive to high-income workers. But it also creates tax, labour-law, social-security, data-security and employer-compliance risks. The future is likely not unlimited “work from anywhere”; it is controlled remote mobility with approved countries, time limits, compliance platforms and specialised visas.

## 114. How might AI and automation affect specialist migration?

AI may reduce demand for some routine white-collar migration while increasing demand for AI engineers, data specialists, cybersecurity experts, robotics engineers, automation engineers, healthcare technologists and industrial digitalisation specialists. Countries will compete for high-skill talent but may be less willing to import generic office labour. Automation can also reduce migration demand in manufacturing and services, but ageing societies will still need healthcare, care work, construction and infrastructure labour. Credential recognition and continuous reskilling will become more important.

## 115. How could climate change affect migration flows?

Climate change will intensify internal displacement and gradually reshape international migration pressure. Floods, droughts, heat, wildfires, water stress, food insecurity and sea-level rise will push people away from high-risk regions. Most climate mobility will initially be internal or regional, not long-distance international migration, because the poorest people often lack resources to move far. Over time, climate risk will also influence destination choice: migrants and investors will avoid cities with extreme heat, insurance risk, water scarcity or recurrent disasters.

## 116. How does rising cost of living affect country choice?

Rising cost of living pushes people away from expensive global hubs and toward secondary cities, mid-cost countries and remote-work destinations. It also reduces actual migration because relocation requires savings. Canada, Portugal, Spain, the UK, Ireland, Australia and major U.S. cities illustrate the affordability paradox: attractive countries can become too expensive for the migrants they attract. Future country-choice tools must compare disposable income after rent, tax, healthcare, childcare and transport, not just average prices.

## 117. How does political polarisation affect the desire to move?

Political polarisation increases backup planning, dual citizenship interest and relocation searches. It is visible not only in authoritarian countries but also in democracies where people fear rights changes, instability, violence or institutional decline. U.S. relocation searches and investment-migration inquiries after elections are a strong example. Political migration is not always immediate; it often starts as “Plan B” documentation, foreign residence, bank diversification or children’s education planning.

## 118. How does demographic crisis in developed countries affect migrant attraction?

Ageing and shrinking workforces push developed countries to attract workers, students and caregivers. The contradiction is that voters may oppose high migration while economies need it. This leads to selective openness: skilled-worker visas, health-worker recruitment, student-to-work pathways, seasonal labour schemes and family limits. Countries that manage integration, housing and credential recognition well will compete better for talent.

## 119. Which countries will compete more actively for talent?

Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, Singapore, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Ireland, Netherlands, France, Spain, Portugal alternatives, Denmark, Finland, Estonia, Poland and Czechia are likely to compete actively. The competition will focus on AI, healthcare, engineering, green energy, semiconductor, automation, cybersecurity, defence, infrastructure, education and care sectors. Talent attraction will require not only visas but housing, schools, spouse rights, language support and fast credential recognition.

## 120. Which countries may become new centres for digital nomads, entrepreneurs and remote specialists?

Potential centres include Spain beyond Barcelona/Madrid, Portugal beyond Lisbon, Italy secondary cities, Greece, Croatia, Estonia, Georgia, Armenia, Albania, Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania, Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Uruguay, Panama, Costa Rica, Brazil, Thailand, Malaysia, Vietnam, Indonesia, Japan, South Korea, UAE and Saudi Arabia. The winning destinations will combine legal remote-work pathways, stable internet, reasonable costs, safety, international communities, tax clarity, housing capacity and political tolerance for foreign remote workers.
