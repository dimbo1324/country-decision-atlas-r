# 1. General Migration Dynamics

## 1. Is the total number of international migrants in the world increasing or decreasing?

It is increasing in absolute terms. UN DESA estimates that the number of international migrants reached about **304 million in 2024**, almost double the 1990 level of about 154 million. However, the share of humanity living outside its country of birth remains relatively small: roughly **3.7% of the world population** in 2024. The key interpretation is that migration feels politically huge because it is concentrated in certain corridors and cities, but globally it is still a minority condition. The long-term direction is upward, but not explosive relative to world population.

**Research note:** separate migrant stock from annual flows. Stock changes slowly; flows react quickly to wars, visa rules, job markets and crises.

## 2. How has international migration changed over the last 10–20 years?

The last two decades show four major shifts: more forced displacement, more intra-regional movement, more student and skilled-worker mobility, and more policy segmentation. Traditional corridors such as Latin America to North America, South Asia to the Gulf, North Africa/Middle East to Europe, and Asia to North America remain central. Since 2010, forced displacement has become a much larger share of total international migration, especially due to Syria, Venezuela, Afghanistan, Ukraine and Sudan. At the same time, developed countries increasingly distinguish between “wanted” migration — skilled workers, students, investors, health workers — and “unwanted” migration — irregular arrivals, asylum backlogs and low-wage dependence.

## 3. Which world regions currently receive the most migrants?

Europe hosts the largest migrant stock by region, followed by Northern America and Northern Africa/Western Asia. UN DESA’s 2024 figures show Europe with about **94 million** international migrants, Northern America with about **61 million**, and Northern Africa/Western Asia with about **54 million**. In proportional terms, Oceania, Northern America and Europe have much higher migrant shares of population than most Asian regions. The Gulf countries also remain structurally dependent on foreign labour, although they are classified within the Northern Africa/Western Asia region.

## 4. From which regions and countries do people most often leave?

Large origin regions include Central and Southern Asia, Latin America and the Caribbean, Northern Africa and Western Asia, and Eastern/South-Eastern Asia. In absolute diaspora terms, India, Mexico, Russia, China, Bangladesh, Syria, Pakistan, Ukraine, the Philippines, Afghanistan and Venezuela are repeatedly important origin countries depending on the dataset and year. Reasons differ: India and the Philippines are heavily labour- and skills-linked; Syria, Ukraine, Afghanistan, Sudan and Venezuela are linked to conflict, crisis or political/economic collapse; Mexico and Central America combine labour demand, networks, violence and economic inequality.

## 5. Which countries are the main destinations for migration?

The United States remains the largest single destination by migrant stock, followed by Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, France, Spain, Canada, the UAE, Australia, Russia, Türkiye and Italy. These are not all attractive for the same reason. The U.S., Canada and Australia combine jobs, universities and family networks. Germany and the UK attract students, skilled workers and intra-European migrants. Saudi Arabia and the UAE are labour-importing economies. Spain, France and Italy combine EU mobility, lifestyle, family, labour and refugee/humanitarian flows.

## 6. Which countries have become more popular for relocation in the last five years?

The strongest popularity gains are visible in countries that match one or more of these conditions: remote-work lifestyle, simpler residence path, lower cost than core Western hubs, safety, tax advantages or talent programmes. Examples include Portugal, Spain, Greece, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Uruguay, Colombia, Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Albania, Thailand, Malaysia and parts of Eastern Europe. Canada remained attractive, but its recent policy tightening reduced the sense of easy access. Germany gained attention through its skilled migration reforms and Opportunity Card. New Zealand periodically becomes attractive when it loosens skilled and investor pathways.

## 7. Which countries are losing attractiveness for migrants?

Attractiveness can decline for different groups. The UK has become less attractive for many students, families and lower-paid skilled workers because of salary thresholds, dependent restrictions and settlement changes, while it remains attractive for elite education and high-skill careers. Canada’s attractiveness has been affected by housing costs and reduced temporary resident/student targets. Portugal’s attractiveness has been weakened by immigration backlogs, housing pressure and proposed citizenship tightening. Some traditional Western European destinations face affordability, bureaucracy and political backlash. Russia and Belarus lost attractiveness for many international workers because of war, sanctions and geopolitical risk.

## 8. Which types of migration are growing fastest?

The fastest-growing or most visible categories are forced displacement, international students, skilled labour migration, health-worker migration and remote-work/digital-nomad mobility. Student mobility has expanded strongly over the past two decades, although Canada, Australia, the Netherlands and the UK are now trying to stabilise or reduce inflows. Digital nomad visas grew rapidly after 2020, but the lifestyle is now becoming more regulated because of tax, employer compliance and local housing pressure. Investment migration remains important among wealthy people, but many golden visa routes are being restricted.

## 9. How did migration flows change after COVID-19?

COVID-19 initially reduced mobility through border closures, flight disruption and visa processing delays. After restrictions lifted, migration rebounded strongly in many OECD countries, especially through students, labour shortages, family reunification and humanitarian protection. Remote work created a new “trial relocation” pattern: people first test countries for several months before committing to residence. At the same time, post-pandemic housing inflation made many destinations less affordable. Governments now face a contradiction: they need migrant workers and students, but voters are increasingly sensitive to housing, public services and border control.

## 10. Which world events have most affected migration in recent years?

The biggest events include the COVID-19 pandemic, Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, the Syrian conflict, the Taliban takeover in Afghanistan, Venezuela’s economic-political crisis, Sudan’s civil war, conflicts in Gaza and the wider Middle East, inflation and housing crises, return-to-office policies, and climate-related disasters. These events affected different migration types: war drove forced displacement; inflation changed destination preferences; remote work changed temporary relocation; political polarisation increased interest in second passports; and climate disasters intensified internal displacement and long-term relocation planning.
