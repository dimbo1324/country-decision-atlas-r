# 4. Search Interest and Online Behaviour

## 33. Is the number of search queries about moving abroad increasing?

The answer is nuanced. Search interest rises sharply around political events, wars, visa launches and economic crises. Public reporting shows spikes in U.S. searches after elections and policy shocks. Search interest in digital nomad visas grew dramatically after 2020, especially after new programmes launched. But search volume does not necessarily mean actual migration; many people research “escape options” without moving. For analysis, search interest should be separated into curiosity, active planning, document preparation and completed relocation.

## 34. Which migration search queries are growing fastest?

Fast-growing query families include “digital nomad visa”, “how to move to [country]”, “second passport”, “citizenship by investment”, “golden visa alternatives”, “remote work visa”, “tax residency”, “best countries to move to”, “cheapest countries to live”, “safe countries for families”, “move abroad with children”, and “where can Americans/Russians/Indians move”. Spikes often follow policy changes: Spain ending golden visas, Italy launching a digital nomad visa, Canada reducing study permits, Germany launching the Opportunity Card, and Portugal citizenship-rule debates.

## 35. Which countries are most often searched together with relocation?

Frequently searched relocation destinations include Canada, Australia, New Zealand, the UK, Ireland, Germany, Spain, Portugal, Italy, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, Uruguay, Thailand, Japan, the UAE and the Netherlands. The set depends heavily on origin country. Americans often search Canada, UK, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, Mexico, Costa Rica and New Zealand. Russians and post-Soviet migrants often search Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Turkey, Montenegro, Germany, Spain, Portugal, UAE, Kazakhstan and Latin America.

## 36. Which countries are most often compared with each other?

Common comparison pairs include Canada vs Australia, Portugal vs Spain, Germany vs Netherlands, UK vs Ireland, UAE vs Saudi Arabia, Mexico vs Costa Rica, Uruguay vs Argentina, Georgia vs Armenia, Serbia vs Montenegro, Thailand vs Malaysia, Portugal vs Italy, and Canada vs New Zealand. Comparisons usually reflect a practical dilemma: similar lifestyle image, similar visa routes, similar language/culture region, or similar tax/cost trade-off. Country comparison content performs well because migrants rarely choose from the whole world; they choose between two or three realistic options.

## 37. What topics do people most often search before moving?

The most frequent topics are visas, residence permits, citizenship timelines, work permits, job market, cost of living, rent, taxes, healthcare, schools, safety, banking, money transfer, language, pet relocation, driving licences, recognition of diplomas, spouse work rights, childcare, neighbourhoods, expat communities, moving checklists and mistakes to avoid. After the first wave of research, people usually move to more specific queries: “documents required”, “processing time”, “tax after 183 days”, “how to rent without local credit history”, and “best districts for families.”

## 38. What questions are most often asked in Google about residence, PR and citizenship?

Common questions are: “How long does it take to get citizenship in [country]?”, “Can I get PR after studying?”, “Can I bring my family?”, “Do I need a job offer?”, “How much money do I need?”, “Can I work remotely?”, “Does time on a student visa count for citizenship?”, “Can I have dual citizenship?”, “What documents need apostille?”, “How long is processing?”, and “Can my application be rejected?” The strongest pain point is uncertainty: people want not just rules, but probabilities and real processing experience.

## 39. Is interest in digital nomad visas growing?

Yes, strongly since 2020. EY reported that more than 40 jurisdictions offered digital nomad or remote-worker permits by mid-2024, and Global Citizen Solutions reported that the majority of tracked nomad/remote-worker visas were launched after 2020. This reflects post-pandemic remote work and governments trying to attract spending without giving full labour-market access. However, the trend is maturing: employers worry about tax and compliance, cities worry about rent inflation, and workers increasingly look for stable residence rather than endless movement.

## 40. Is interest in second passports and citizenship by investment growing?

Yes, especially among wealthy individuals and people from politically unstable countries or weak-passport countries. Interest also rose among affluent Americans after political shocks. At the same time, citizenship-by-investment programmes face more scrutiny because of security, money laundering and EU visa-waiver concerns. The market is shifting from simple “buy a passport” marketing toward due diligence, residence quality, tax planning and long-term legal reliability. Caribbean CBI remains active; EU real-estate golden visas are more constrained.

## 41. Is interest in low-tax countries growing?

Yes, especially among entrepreneurs, investors, crypto holders, high earners and remote workers. Popular search themes include UAE tax residency, Portugal NHR alternatives, Italy flat tax, Greece non-dom regime, Cyprus, Malta, Andorra, Paraguay, Panama and territorial-tax jurisdictions. But tax migration is risky if users misunderstand tax residency and reporting obligations. The main unresolved demand is for clear, personalised tax-residency comparison that includes income source, nationality, company structure and days of presence.

## 42. Is interest in low-cost countries growing?

Yes. Inflation, housing crises and remote work pushed people to search for countries where income stretches further. Popular low-cost or mid-cost destinations include Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Albania, Bulgaria, Romania, Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia and parts of Turkey. But online cost-of-living data can be misleading: expat lifestyles, imported goods, private healthcare, international schools and safe neighbourhood rents may be much more expensive than local averages.

## 43. What fears appear most often in relocation search queries?

Common fears include visa rejection, running out of money, inability to find work, language failure, loneliness, scams, unsafe neighbourhoods, poor healthcare, children not adapting, spouse unemployment, tax mistakes, losing original citizenship, military mobilisation, political persecution, racism, bureaucracy, housing fraud, and being forced to return. Search behaviour often starts optimistic (“best countries to move to”) and becomes fear-based as the person gets closer to action (“what can go wrong moving to…”).

## 44. Which YouTube relocation topics get the most views?

High-performing YouTube topics include “best countries to move to”, “countries that want you”, “countries to avoid”, “how I moved abroad”, “cost of living in [city]”, “mistakes I made”, “digital nomad visa”, “retire abroad”, “moving with kids”, “Portugal/Spain/Mexico/Thailand reality”, “why I left [country]”, “hidden costs”, “tax residency explained”, and “citizenship/passport ranking”. The most clickable videos often combine aspiration and fear: low cost, better life, simple visa, or brutal truth.
