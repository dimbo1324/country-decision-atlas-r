# 9. Monetisation and Willingness to Pay

## 91. What relocation information are people willing to pay for?

People pay for information that reduces risk: visa eligibility, document checklists, processing strategy, tax residency, school selection, healthcare access, realistic budget, neighbourhood choice, job-market assessment, business setup, citizenship timeline and personalised country comparison. Generic “best countries” content is usually free; personalised, up-to-date, legally referenced guidance is monetisable. Users pay when the cost of a mistake is high.

## 92. What migration-related services do people already pay for?

They pay for immigration lawyers, visa consultants, document translation, apostille/legalisation, tax advice, relocation agents, school consultants, real-estate agents, insurance, temporary accommodation, bank-account help, company formation, investment migration, language courses, career coaching and shipping/pet relocation. Companies pay for employee relocation packages, work permits, tax equalisation, payroll compliance, destination services and mobility software.

## 93. How much do consultations on migration, visas, residence, PR and citizenship cost?

Prices vary dramatically. Simple consultations can range from free discovery calls to about $100–$300 for a short session, while specialist immigration lawyers may charge roughly $150–$500 per hour in many developed markets. Flat-fee visa cases can range from hundreds to several thousand dollars; complex U.S. immigration, investor, asylum or litigation cases can cost many thousands. Investment migration advisory may be far more expensive because it includes legal, due diligence and investment work.

## 94. How much do paid country reports, guides and checklists cost?

Consumer guides often range from $10–$100 for simple PDFs to $100–$500 for detailed relocation packages, depending on market and creator authority. Premium reports for investors, tax planning or corporate mobility can be much more expensive or bundled into consultations. The main value drivers are specificity, freshness, official-source references, templates, checklists, calculators and access to expert updates.

## 95. Which services use a subscription model?

Nomads.com/Nomad List uses membership-style access for advanced filters and community features. InterNations uses membership tiers for community and events. Legislative-monitoring platforms such as FiscalNote and Quorum use B2B subscription contracts. Global mobility software platforms such as Localyze, Jobbatical, Deel, Topia and others often use SaaS or service-based subscription/enterprise pricing. Subscription works best when information changes frequently or users need continuous support.

## 96. Which services earn from legal consultations?

Immigration law firms, global mobility firms, investment migration consultancies, tax advisory firms and relocation agencies earn directly from consultations. Some content sites monetise by lead generation: free country guides attract users, then sell them a consultation with lawyers, tax advisers, real-estate agents or investment migration specialists. The conflict-of-interest risk is that content may over-promote countries or programmes tied to revenue.

## 97. Which services earn from affiliate programmes?

Common affiliates include insurance, international money transfers, banks/fintech accounts, eSIMs, relocation shipping, temporary housing, real-estate brokers, language schools, visa services, tax consultations, company formation and travel products. Digital nomad blogs and YouTube channels frequently use these models. Affiliate monetisation is scalable, but it can reduce trust if recommendations are not transparent or if legal/tax content is mixed with sales.

## 98. What B2B services exist in global mobility and relocation?

B2B services include immigration case management, employee relocation, tax compliance, payroll, Employer of Record, posted-worker compliance, business travel compliance, remote-work risk assessment, destination services, household goods shipping, temporary housing, school search, vendor management and analytics. Players include Deel, Remote, Localyze, Jobbatical, Topia, Equus, Aires, Cartus, Sirva/BGRS, Graebel, Santa Fe, Crown and Big Four mobility practices.

## 99. What do companies pay for in employee relocation?

Companies pay for work permits, legal compliance, relocation allowances, flights, shipping, temporary housing, home search, school search, tax equalisation, payroll setup, social security, spouse support, cultural training, language training, destination support, policy management and risk reporting. They pay because failed relocation is expensive: loss of employee, visa penalties, tax exposure, business delays and reputational risk.

## 100. Which paid functions do users consider truly valuable?

Valuable paid functions are personalised eligibility checks, official-source legal updates, document checklist generator, deadline reminders, probability/risk assessment, tax-residency simulator, cost calculator based on actual lifestyle, family/school matching, neighbourhood risk map, verified expert Q&A, real processing-time tracker, comparison by persona and “what changed since last month” alerts. Users are less willing to pay for generic country rankings unless they are beautifully packaged and continuously updated.
