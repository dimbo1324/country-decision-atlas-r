# 10. Problems and Unmet Demand — Questions 101–110

## Q101. What relocation information is most scattered across sources?

The most scattered information includes visa rules, tax rules, cost of living, rental reality, healthcare access, school enrolment, bank account opening, document requirements, user experience, city/neighbourhood quality, official-law changes and practical local bureaucracy.

People must currently combine government sites, lawyers, blogs, YouTube, Reddit, Telegram and paid consultations.

## Q102. What data do people lack most when choosing a country?

Missing data:

- realistic total monthly budget;
- real visa success probability;
- processing-time reality;
- hidden bureaucracy;
- neighbourhood-level safety;
- school/healthcare access for foreigners;
- tax obligations after arrival;
- local job-market fit;
- real rent availability;
- integration difficulty;
- future policy direction;
- user experiences filtered by profile.

## Q103. Which relocation questions are hardest to solve alone?

Hardest questions:

- Which legal route fits my exact nationality/income/family?
- Will I become tax resident?
- Can I work remotely legally?
- Can I bring family and pets?
- How much money is enough for a failed job search?
- Is this country safe for my specific family/profile?
- Will my profession be recognised?
- Can I realistically get PR/citizenship?
- Which city/neighbourhood fits my budget and lifestyle?

## Q104. Where do people most often encounter outdated information?

Outdated information appears in:

- old blog posts;
- YouTube videos without update notes;
- Telegram pinned messages;
- Reddit comments;
- agency websites optimised for SEO;
- PDFs shared in chats;
- unofficial visa databases;
- AI-generated articles;
- country guides copied from older sources.

## Q105. Where do people most often encounter contradictory advice?

Contradictions occur in:

- tax residence discussions;
- remote work legality;
- visa renewal rules;
- citizenship timelines;
- dual citizenship;
- whether to apply inside or outside a country;
- required documents;
- bank account opening;
- local job prospects;
- safety and cost-of-living experiences.

Contradiction often happens because people have different nationalities, income types, dates of application and local offices.

## Q106. What mistakes do people most often make when choosing a country?

Common mistakes:

- choosing by YouTube lifestyle rather than legal route;
- underestimating rent;
- ignoring tax residence;
- assuming tourist experience equals resident life;
- not checking healthcare;
- overestimating English use;
- ignoring school needs;
- trusting one agent;
- not budgeting for delays;
- ignoring political/legal direction;
- choosing a country without visiting or testing daily life.

## Q107. What mistakes do people make after moving?

Post-move mistakes:

- delaying residence registration;
- missing tax deadlines;
- signing bad rental contracts;
- using unverified lawyers/agents;
- staying in expat bubble only;
- not learning the language;
- overspending first months;
- failing to document income;
- ignoring insurance;
- not building local support network;
- assuming bureaucracy will be quick.

## Q108. What decisions do people make based on unverified information?

Risky decisions:

- entering on tourist status intending to stay permanently;
- working remotely where it is not allowed;
- becoming tax resident accidentally;
- buying property for residence without legal certainty;
- choosing a school from social media claims;
- moving pets without complete documents;
- relying on “everyone does it” advice;
- paying agents upfront;
- overstaying while waiting for informal advice.

## Q109. What data would people like to see in one place?

Ideal combined dataset:

- visa/residence/citizenship rules;
- official sources and update dates;
- cost of living by lifestyle;
- rent and housing availability;
- taxes;
- healthcare;
- schools;
- safety;
- job market;
- business environment;
- community reviews;
- document checklist;
- processing times;
- expert contacts;
- country trend / political direction;
- risk alerts.

## Q110. What tools could help a person choose a country faster and more confidently?

High-value tools:

- persona-based country matcher;
- country comparison matrix;
- visa eligibility wizard;
- relocation budget calculator;
- tax-residence warning engine;
- document checklist generator;
- official-law update tracker;
- city/neighbourhood shortlist generator;
- community evidence summariser;
- expert marketplace;
- scenario simulator: “What happens if I move with $X, remote income Y, family Z?”

This is where a new platform can be differentiated: not by listing countries, but by reducing uncertainty through structured decision support.
