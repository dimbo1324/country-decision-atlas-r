# 6. Country Choice — Questions 55–66

## Q55. What criteria do people usually use to choose a country?

Core criteria:

- legal pathway;
- safety;
- cost of living;
- job market / income compatibility;
- taxes;
- healthcare;
- schools;
- language;
- climate;
- housing;
- bureaucracy;
- culture and integration;
- citizenship timeline;
- passport strength;
- business environment;
- distance from home;
- existing community;
- family/pet feasibility.

A serious comparison tool should allow weighting criteria by persona.

## Q56. Which criteria matter most for single people?

Single people often prioritise:

- job opportunities;
- cost of living;
- social life;
- dating and friendship opportunities;
- safety;
- language and integration;
- nightlife/culture;
- flexibility of visa;
- ability to travel;
- career growth.

They may tolerate smaller housing or weaker family infrastructure if the country offers opportunity and social energy.

## Q57. Which criteria matter most for families?

Families prioritise:

- safety;
- schools and childcare;
- healthcare;
- stable legal status;
- housing size and price;
- family reunification rules;
- child language adaptation;
- parks and urban quality;
- predictable income;
- citizenship/permanent residence path.

Families are more risk-averse than singles because failure affects children and spouse/partner careers.

## Q58. Which criteria matter most for students?

Students prioritise:

- university quality;
- tuition fees;
- scholarships;
- cost of living;
- student visa success rate;
- legal work rights;
- internships;
- post-study work visa;
- route to skilled work and PR;
- language of instruction;
- employer reputation of the degree.

## Q59. Which criteria matter most for entrepreneurs?

Entrepreneurs prioritise:

- company formation speed;
- banking access;
- tax structure;
- legal predictability;
- residence route for founders;
- market access;
- ability to hire;
- startup ecosystem;
- investor access;
- IP protection;
- accounting burden;
- internet and infrastructure.

## Q60. Which criteria matter most for investors?

Investors prioritise:

- property rights;
- tax regime;
- political stability;
- banking and capital controls;
- investment residence/citizenship routes;
- asset protection;
- currency risk;
- due diligence burden;
- exit options;
- inheritance and estate rules;
- family office / legal services ecosystem.

## Q61. Which criteria matter most for digital nomads?

Digital nomads prioritise:

- legal remote-work permission;
- tax residence clarity;
- cost of living;
- internet speed;
- coworking spaces;
- safety;
- housing availability;
- community;
- climate;
- time zones;
- visa length and renewability;
- healthcare insurance;
- banking/cards.

Nomad List/Nomads.com-style ranking criteria often include cost, internet, weather, fun, safety and community.

## Q62. Which countries do people with limited budgets often choose?

Common budget-friendly choices:

- Latin America: Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Paraguay, Peru, Ecuador.
- Europe / nearby: Georgia, Armenia, Serbia, Albania, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Bulgaria, Romania.
- Southeast Asia: Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Indonesia, Philippines.
- North Africa / Middle East: Morocco, Tunisia, Türkiye, Egypt, depending on safety/legal needs.

Budget choices must be filtered by legal pathway and local income. A country may be cheap for a remote earner but difficult for a local job seeker.

## Q63. Which countries do people choose when safety is most important?

Often considered safe or stable: Switzerland, Japan, Singapore, New Zealand, Canada, Australia, Netherlands, Denmark, Norway, Finland, Iceland, Austria, Portugal, Spain, Ireland and Uruguay.

But safety is local. City/neighbourhood data matters more than national reputation. Japan can be extremely safe but difficult for language/integration; Nordic countries can be safe but socially hard; Uruguay is safer than many regional alternatives but still has city-level differences.

## Q64. Which countries do people choose for a fast path to citizenship?

Commonly discussed fast or relatively accessible citizenship routes include:

- Argentina and Uruguay in Latin America, depending on residence and interpretation.
- Paraguay, depending on current rules and actual naturalisation practice.
- Portugal historically attractive, but backlogs and legal changes matter.
- Spain for some Latin American citizens after shorter residence periods.
- Canada and Australia through skilled/permanent residence routes, but not “fast” and increasingly selective.
- Caribbean citizenship-by-investment countries for high-net-worth applicants.
- Türkiye through investment route.

Important warning: legal citizenship timelines on paper often differ from real processing time.

## Q65. Which countries do people choose for taxes and business environment?

Popular tax/business choices include UAE, Singapore, Switzerland, Ireland, Estonia, Georgia, Cyprus, Malta, Portugal under specific regimes, Spain under Beckham Law for qualifying people, Italy under special regimes, Andorra, Panama, Paraguay and some Caribbean jurisdictions.

Each has trade-offs: high costs, substance requirements, residence days, banking due diligence, reputational issues or limited route to citizenship.

## Q66. Which countries look attractive at first but cause problems after relocation?

Examples frequently criticised after arrival:

- **Portugal**: attractive lifestyle but rent inflation, bureaucracy, visa backlogs.
- **Spain**: lifestyle and tax regimes attractive, but bureaucracy, housing and taxes can surprise people.
- **Canada**: strong brand, but housing costs, healthcare waits and job-market credential issues.
- **Germany**: excellent economy, but language, bureaucracy, housing and social integration are hard.
- **Netherlands**: high English use and jobs, but housing crisis and high costs.
- **UAE**: tax/business advantages, but high costs, heat, residence dependence and social/legal restrictions.
- **Thailand/Bali/Vietnam**: lifestyle attractive, but long-term legal status, tax, healthcare and local-work restrictions can be difficult.

The pattern: tourist attractiveness is not the same as resident viability.
