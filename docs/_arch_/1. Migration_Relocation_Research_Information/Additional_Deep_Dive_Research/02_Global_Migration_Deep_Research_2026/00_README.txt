# Migration and Relocation Deep Research Pack 2026

Research date: 2026-06-17  
Language: English  
Scope: global migration market, relocation interest, search demand, and real discussions in online migrant/expat/digital-nomad communities.

## How this package is organised

- `01_Global_Migration_Market.md` — answers the questions about migrant stock, destination/origin regions, growing and declining migration directions, migration types, shocks after 2020, and 2030–2040 outlook.
- `02_Relocation_Interest.md` — answers the questions about people’s desire to move abroad, age/professional groups, skilled workers, entrepreneurs, investors, digital nomads, and barriers.
- `03_Search_Demand.md` — answers the questions about Google/search behaviour, fast-growing relocation queries, country-linked queries, country comparisons, digital nomad visa searches, second passport/citizenship searches, and pre-move fears.
- `04_Social_Media_and_Real_Discussions.md` — answers the questions about Reddit, Telegram, Facebook Groups, YouTube, common pre-move/post-move questions, trusted sources, outdated sources, and controversial topics.
- `05_Source_Index.md` — list of sources used.
- `ALL_ANSWERS_COMBINED.md` — all files merged for quick reading.

TXT copies are also included for easier import into simple editors.

## Methodological notes

1. International migrant stock is not the same as annual migration flow. Stock measures people living outside their country of birth/citizenship at a point in time; flow measures entries/exits over a period.
2. “People who want to move” is not the same as people who actually migrate. Gallup migration desire is a strong signal of aspiration, not a forecast of completed moves.
3. Search demand is difficult to measure exactly without direct Google Trends exports, keyword-planner exports, or paid SEO tools. This pack therefore separates hard evidence from directional interpretation.
4. Telegram, Facebook, WhatsApp, Discord and many country-specific migrant communities are partly private. Their signals should be treated as qualitative evidence, not statistically representative data.
5. Policy data changes quickly. Visa, residence, work-permit, student and investment-migration rules should always be re-checked on official government pages before use.

## Evidence-quality labels

- A = official data or intergovernmental source.
- B = large survey, established research institute, or reputable academic source.
- C = commercial platform/report, useful but definition-dependent.
- D = community/social-media observation, useful for user pain points but not statistically representative.

## Executive summary

Global migration is structurally rising, but not in a simple “everyone is moving” way. The UN estimates 304 million international migrants in 2024, up from 275 million in 2020 and roughly 154 million in 1990. The migrant share of humanity is still modest, around 3.7% in 2024, but the absolute number is at a record level. The largest destination remains the United States, followed by Germany, Saudi Arabia, the United Kingdom, France, Spain, Canada, the UAE and Australia. The most important origin countries include India, China, Mexico, Ukraine and Russia, with major humanitarian-origin flows from Syria, Afghanistan, Venezuela, Sudan and Ukraine.

Demand is fragmenting. People still want to move for income, safety, family, education and quality of life, but post-2020 migration is shaped by shocks: COVID-19, remote work, the Russia-Ukraine war, Afghanistan, Sudan, Venezuela, inflation, housing crises, student-visa tightening, digital-nomad regulation, and a political backlash against high migration in several rich countries. OECD permanent migration reached very high levels after the pandemic, then declined in 2024 while staying above 2019 levels. Forced displacement hit historic highs in 2024, then slightly declined in 2025 but remained extremely high.

The strongest market opportunity is not a generic “best country” ranking. The unmet need is a trusted decision layer: updated laws, realistic visa/residence pathways, cost-of-living models, tax/residency warnings, job-market realism, family/school/healthcare checklists, community evidence, and country risk signals.
