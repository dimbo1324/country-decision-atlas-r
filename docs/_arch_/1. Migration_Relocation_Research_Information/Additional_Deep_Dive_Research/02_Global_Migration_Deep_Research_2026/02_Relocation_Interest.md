# 02. Interest in Relocation

Research date: 2026-06-17

## 1. Is the number of people who want to move to another country growing?

Broadly, yes over the long term, but with recent regional cooling. Gallup’s 2024 analysis said global desire to migrate remained at a record high and higher than a decade earlier. However, the 2026 Gallup update showed that in 2025 migration desire declined in some regions, especially Latin America and the Caribbean and sub-Saharan Africa, while remaining high by historical standards. Sources: [S15], [S16].

Interpretation:
- The long-term market for relocation information is growing.
- But “desire to move” is cyclical. It rises with economic stress, political dissatisfaction, conflict, insecurity and media narratives; it falls when borders tighten, migration becomes more expensive, destination countries look less welcoming, or home-country conditions improve.
- The strongest product opportunity is not only for people who already decided to move, but for people in the “considering / comparing / uncertain” stage.

## 2. In which countries is the share of people wanting to emigrate highest?

The strongest regional desire is generally found in sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America and the Caribbean, some small island states, and countries under economic, political or security stress. Gallup’s 2025 data put sub-Saharan Africa at about 33% of adults wanting to move permanently, still the highest among major regions, while Latin America and the Caribbean was about 25%, down from its 2021–2022 peak. Sources: [S16], [S17].

Country-level leaders vary by survey wave. Gallup has repeatedly highlighted high migration desire in parts of West Africa and Central Africa, including countries such as Liberia, Sierra Leone, Ghana, Republic of Congo, Nigeria, Gambia and Comoros in previous analyses. In Latin America, Honduras and El Salvador have shown very high desire in previous years but declined sharply in 2025. Sources: [S15], [S16].

Practical implication:
- High desire does not automatically create high legal migration, because capability matters: money, passports, visa eligibility, education, language, family networks and employer sponsorship.
- The platform/product opportunity is strongest where desire is high but legal-pathway clarity is low.

## 3. Which countries do people most often name as desired destinations?

The United States remains the top dream destination globally, but its dominance is weakening. In 2025, Gallup reported that 15% of adults who wanted to move permanently named the U.S. as their preferred destination — the lowest share in nearly two decades. Canada ranked second with about 9% of potential migrants. Other consistently desired destinations include the United Kingdom, Germany, Australia, Spain, France, Japan, Italy and Saudi Arabia. Sources: [S16].

Interpretation by segment:
- U.S.: salaries, universities, tech, entrepreneurship, family networks, entertainment/cultural influence.
- Canada: perceived openness, safety, education, social services, pathway to citizenship, but housing and caps are now concerns.
- Germany: skilled work, EU access, engineering/industrial base, education.
- Australia/New Zealand: lifestyle, English language, safety, wages.
- Spain/Portugal/Italy: lifestyle, climate, EU access, retirement/remote work.
- UAE/Saudi: tax, salary, business, Gulf labour and investor routes.

## 4. Which age groups are most interested in moving?

Younger adults are consistently more likely to want to migrate. Gallup/IOM research finds that migration desire is strongly related to age and education: young people are more likely to express migration aspirations, and people with at least some secondary education are generally more likely to want to move than those with less education. Sources: [S18], [S19].

Strongest age groups:
- 15–24: highest aspiration, especially for education, early career and escape from weak local prospects.
- 25–34: high practical migration capacity; this group often converts interest into work, study, spouse/family, digital nomad or entrepreneurship routes.
- 35–44: still strong interest, but more constrained by children, mortgages, careers, spouses and elderly parents.
- 45+: more likely to consider retirement, investment, healthcare, safety, tax and quality of life than career migration.

Product implication:
- Young users need education, first job, scholarship, language and legal-pathway information.
- 25–44 users need realistic route planning: work permits, tax, partner/children, career transfer, property, healthcare and long-term residence.
- Older users need healthcare, pensions, taxes, inheritance, retirement residence and risk control.

## 5. Which professional groups most often consider migration?

The most migration-active professional groups are those with internationally transferable skills or labour-shortage leverage.

High-interest groups:
- IT, software, cybersecurity, AI/data, cloud and product roles.
- Engineers: electrical, mechanical, automation, civil, energy, mining, industrial systems.
- Healthcare: doctors, nurses, care workers, pharmacists, allied health professionals.
- Academia and students: researchers, PhD candidates, international students.
- Finance, consulting, legal, compliance and corporate specialists.
- Skilled trades: welders, electricians, plumbers, mechanics, construction specialists.
- Entrepreneurs, startup founders and investors.
- Remote workers, freelancers, creators and digital nomads.
- Professionals from unstable countries whose skills are exportable but whose domestic market is shrinking.

The OECD explicitly tracks talent attractiveness for highly educated workers, entrepreneurs, university students and startup founders, which confirms that these groups are central to government competition for migrants. Sources: [S25].

## 6. What reasons most often make people think about moving?

Common reasons:

1. Better income and career opportunity.  
The strongest global driver remains economic: higher wages, more stable jobs, better career ladders and stronger institutions.

2. Safety and political stability.  
War, crime, authoritarianism, repression, corruption and fear of mobilisation/prosecution push people to seek alternatives.

3. Education.  
Students and parents move for universities, schools, language environment and children’s future.

4. Family reunification and relationships.  
Diaspora networks lower risk and create chain migration.

5. Quality of life.  
Climate, work-life balance, healthcare, urban environment, transport, safety, rights and social trust matter.

6. Cost of living.  
Since 2021–2022, inflation and rent have pushed people to consider lower-cost countries, especially remote workers and retirees. Sources: [S42].

7. Tax and business environment.  
Entrepreneurs and investors compare corporate tax, personal tax, banking, legal certainty, visas and access to markets.

8. Long-term backup plan.  
Interest in second citizenship/residence has grown among people who want optionality, especially wealthy households and politically anxious citizens. Sources: [S33], [S34].

The World Bank’s useful frame is “match and motive”: migrants move because of both opportunity match and motive, ranging from voluntary economic movement to forced protection needs. Sources: [S23].

## 7. What reasons most often stop people from moving?

Main barriers:

1. Visa/legal eligibility.  
Many people want to move but do not qualify for work, study, family, ancestry, investment or humanitarian routes.

2. Money.  
Proof of funds, relocation costs, deposits, flights, emergency reserves, translations, legal fees and housing deposits stop many people.

3. Employment uncertainty.  
People fear not finding a job, losing professional status, or being stuck in low-paid work.

4. Language.  
Even when a visa is possible, language affects work, integration, bureaucracy, schools and social life.

5. Family responsibilities.  
Spouse, children, elderly parents and custody issues slow or block relocation.

6. Housing.  
Destination housing crises are now a major barrier in Canada, UK, Australia, Netherlands, Ireland, Portugal, Spain and many large cities.

7. Tax complexity.  
Digital nomads, entrepreneurs and investors fear double taxation, tax residency traps and non-compliance.

8. Fear of regret.  
Many people ask whether others regretted moving, whether they became lonely, whether integration failed, or whether the destination was oversold.

9. Documents and bureaucracy.  
Apostilles, translations, police certificates, diplomas, medical exams, bank statements and appointment bottlenecks often delay migration.

## 8. How is interest changing among skilled specialists?

Interest among skilled specialists remains high, but it is becoming more selective and more policy-driven. Countries want healthcare workers, engineers, IT specialists, researchers and other shortage-skill groups. At the same time, governments are raising salary thresholds, tightening employer compliance and prioritising “high value” skills. Sources: [S7], [S8], [S9], [S25], [S26].

Key trend:
- Before 2020: skilled migration was mostly employer/job-offer driven.
- After 2020: skilled migration is also about remote work, lifestyle, safety, tax, family, residence stability and long-term citizenship optionality.
- 2025–2026: countries are filtering harder. Skilled migrants are welcome when they fit labour-market shortages; lower-paid or ambiguous roles face more barriers.

Strong growth fields:
- Healthcare and elder care.
- IT, AI, cybersecurity, data, cloud.
- Engineering and industrial automation.
- Energy transition, construction and infrastructure.
- Education/research.
- Advanced manufacturing.

## 9. How is interest changing among entrepreneurs and investors?

Entrepreneur and investor interest is rising, but investment migration is moving away from simple “buy property, get residency” models. Wealthy people increasingly seek residence/citizenship optionality for geopolitical risk, taxation, banking access, children’s education, healthcare and mobility. Henley projected record millionaire migration in 2025. Sources: [S33].

At the same time, governments and regulators are more sceptical of residence/citizenship-by-investment schemes. The IMF analyses risks around tax avoidance/evasion, and the EU has long criticised investor citizenship schemes. Spain ended its property-based golden visa in 2025. Sources: [S34], [S35], [S36].

What is growing:
- Founder/startup visas.
- Innovation visas.
- High-net-worth residence without immediate citizenship.
- Investment funds rather than real estate.
- Tax-residence planning.
- “Plan B” citizenship/residence portfolios.

What is weakening:
- Pure real-estate golden visas in politically sensitive housing markets.
- Citizenship-by-investment schemes with weak due diligence.
- Promises of fast EU citizenship without real residence.

## 10. How is interest changing among digital nomads and remote workers?

Digital nomadism is one of the fastest-growing relocation segments, but it is maturing. MBO Partners estimated 18.5 million American digital nomads in 2025, 153% above 2019. Many countries now offer digital-nomad or remote-work visa schemes, and the IBA’s 2026 report highlights growing legal, tax and immigration complexity. Sources: [S20], [S21].

Growth drivers:
- Remote work normalisation.
- High rents in home countries.
- Desire for better climate and lifestyle.
- Arbitrage: earning in a strong currency while living in a cheaper country.
- Social media and creator culture.
- More countries marketing remote-worker visas.

Maturity problems:
- Tax residency confusion.
- Employer compliance risk.
- Local housing backlash.
- Border-run fatigue.
- Loneliness and unstable community.
- Healthcare and insurance gaps.
- Internet/workspace reliability.
- Families with children need school/legal clarity, not only beaches and cafes.

Product implication:
Digital nomads do not only need destination rankings. They need visa duration, tax-residency warnings, banking, health insurance, housing, coworking reliability, safety, time zones, community quality, school options, pet rules and exit-plan guidance.
