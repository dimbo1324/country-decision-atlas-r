# 03. Search Demand

Research date: 2026-06-17

## 1. Which relocation-abroad queries are most popular in Google?

Without a direct Google Trends export or paid keyword database, exact global rankings should be treated cautiously. The strongest recurring query families are:

- “move to [country]”
- “how to move to [country]”
- “immigrate to [country]”
- “[country] visa requirements”
- “[country] residence permit”
- “[country] permanent residency”
- “[country] citizenship”
- “work visa [country]”
- “student visa [country]”
- “digital nomad visa [country]”
- “cost of living in [country/city]”
- “best countries to live in”
- “best countries for expats”
- “cheapest countries to live in”
- “taxes for expats in [country]”
- “healthcare in [country] for expats”
- “schools in [country] for expats”

Google Trends is the correct tool for direct measurement, but it reports relative interest rather than exact search volumes. Source: [S37].

## 2. Which migration queries are growing fastest?

Fast-growing query themes after 2020:

1. Digital nomad visa queries.  
ExpatNetwork reported that “digital nomad visa” searches reached a global peak in 2024 and rose 1,135%, driven partly by new visa launches such as Italy’s. Source: [S38].

2. Country-specific remote-work visa queries.  
Examples: Spain digital nomad visa, Portugal D8/D7, Italy digital nomad visa, Greece digital nomad visa, Thailand long-term visa, Indonesia/Bali remote work, Croatia/Estonia/Georgia digital nomad routes.

3. “Move abroad from the US / UK / Canada” style queries.  
Political polarisation, housing costs, healthcare costs and work-life-balance concerns make outbound relocation searches from rich countries more visible.

4. Second passport / dual citizenship / citizenship by descent.  
Interest grows when people seek backup options, EU access, ancestry-based citizenship or protection from political uncertainty.

5. Golden visa alternatives.  
Spain’s closure of the property-based golden visa and Portugal’s programme changes increased searches for alternatives: Greece, Italy, Malta residence, UAE Golden Visa, Caribbean citizenship and Latin American residence paths.

6. Cost-of-living arbitrage queries.  
Queries around “cheap countries to live”, “live abroad on $1000/month”, “retire abroad”, “remote work countries” and “best countries for expats” remain strong because inflation and rent pressure persist. Sources: [S40], [S42].

## 5. How has interest in “digital nomad visa” changed over recent years?

The trend rose sharply after 2020 because remote work became normalised and countries started creating specific visa products for remote workers. Search interest accelerated in 2021–2024 and reached a high point around 2024 in several published analyses. ExpatNetwork reported a 1,135% increase in global searches for digital nomad visas, with Italy’s 2024 visa launch generating especially strong interest. Source: [S38].

By 2025–2026, the market is no longer only about curiosity. It is becoming regulated:
- More countries have programmes.
- Income thresholds are more visible.
- Applicants ask about taxes, social security, legal work status and local registration.
- Host countries are concerned about housing pressure and tax compliance.
- Employers worry about permanent-establishment and labour-law exposure. Source: [S21].

Interpretation:
Digital nomad search demand is moving from “Where can I go?” to “Can I legally live there, pay the correct taxes, keep my job, rent housing, get healthcare and avoid overstaying?”

## 6. How has interest changed for “second passport”, “dual citizenship”, “citizenship by investment”?

Interest is rising among high-income, politically anxious and globally mobile households. Demand is driven by:
- Backup residence/citizenship.
- Visa-free travel.
- Access to banking and financial systems.
- Children’s education.
- Tax and estate planning.
- Political/geopolitical risk.
- Ability to live/work in the EU or other attractive regions.

Henley projected record millionaire migration in 2025, while the IMF analysed residence/citizenship-by-investment schemes and their risks. At the same time, Europe is becoming stricter: the EU continues to criticise investor citizenship, and Spain closed its property-based golden visa. Sources: [S33], [S34], [S35], [S36].

Search behaviour is likely splitting into three groups:
1. Ancestry citizenship: Italy, Ireland, Poland, Germany, Portugal/Sephardic routes where relevant, Canada citizenship-by-descent changes.
2. Residence by investment: Portugal, Greece, Italy, UAE, Malta residence, Singapore, Panama, Paraguay/Uruguay-style residence options.
3. Citizenship by investment: Caribbean programmes, Vanuatu-type programmes, Malta controversy, due diligence and EU restrictions.

## 7. How has interest changed for “best countries to live”, “best countries for expats”, “cheapest countries to live”?

These searches remain high because they serve the earliest stage of the relocation funnel. They are usually broad, emotional and exploratory rather than legally precise.

What users want from these searches:
- A shortlist.
- Quick comparison.
- Safety and quality of life.
- Cost of living.
- Climate and lifestyle.
- Taxes.
- Ease of getting residence.
- Healthcare and schools.
- Expat community.

But this query family has a quality problem: many results are generic, SEO-driven, outdated or misleading. A country can rank as “cheap” but be weak on visas, employment, safety, healthcare, banking, language, housing, or long-term citizenship.

Useful benchmark sources include InterNations Expat Insider, Numbeo-type cost indices, official visa pages, OECD/World Bank data and recent community evidence. InterNations 2025 surveyed more than 10,000 expats across 46 destinations and ranks destinations by factors such as Working Abroad, Personal Finance, Quality of Life, Expat Essentials and Ease of Settling In. Sources: [S40], [S41].

## 8. Which topics do people search before moving most often: visas, housing, taxes, work, medicine, schools, safety?

Most common pre-move topic hierarchy:

1. Visa/residence/legal status.  
People first ask whether they can legally stay, work, study, bring family and later renew or obtain permanent residence/citizenship.

2. Work and income.  
Can they get a job? Is remote work legal? Are salaries enough? Are foreign qualifications recognised?

3. Housing.  
Rent levels, deposits, scams, documents, neighbourhoods, guarantors, pets, short-term vs long-term rentals.

4. Cost of living.  
Monthly budget, groceries, utilities, transport, childcare, schooling, healthcare, taxes.

5. Taxes and banking.  
Tax residency, double taxation, social security, opening bank accounts, sanctions/compliance, crypto/capital controls.

6. Healthcare and insurance.  
Public/private healthcare, waiting times, medication availability, pre-existing conditions.

7. Schools and children.  
Public/private/international schools, language adaptation, homeschooling/worldschooling legality.

8. Safety and politics.  
Crime, corruption, war risk, rule of law, discrimination, women’s/LGBTQ+ safety, police reliability.

9. Documents.  
Apostilles, translations, birth/marriage certificates, police records, diplomas, medical records.

10. Community and integration.  
Language, friends, local culture, loneliness, expat groups, local attitudes to foreigners.

## 9. Which fears are most common in relocation search queries?

Common fear-based search patterns:

- “Can I move without a job?”
- “How much money do I need to move to [country]?”
- “Can I get deported if…?”
- “What happens if visa is denied?”
- “Is [country] safe for foreigners?”
- “Can I work remotely on a tourist visa?”
- “Do digital nomads pay tax in [country]?”
- “Can I bring my spouse/children?”
- “Will my diploma be recognised?”
- “Can I get healthcare as an expat?”
- “Is [city] expensive?”
- “Is [country] good for families?”
- “Do people regret moving to [country]?”
- “How hard is it to make friends?”
- “What if I fail and need to return home?”

These fears map to four core anxieties:
1. Legal failure.
2. Financial failure.
3. Social/integration failure.
4. Safety/health/family failure.

A strong relocation platform should turn each fear into a checklist, probability estimate, documents list, cost model and fallback plan.

## 10. Which countries have high search interest but little quality information?

High-interest / low-quality-information gaps often appear in:

1. Countries with fast-changing rules.  
Examples: Canada, UK, Australia, Portugal, Spain, Germany, Italy, UAE. Information quickly becomes outdated.

2. Smaller attractive countries.  
Examples: Uruguay, Paraguay, Panama, Costa Rica, Montenegro, Serbia, Georgia, Armenia, Albania, Malta, Cyprus, Slovenia, Croatia.

3. Emerging remote-work destinations.  
Examples: Indonesia/Bali, Thailand, Vietnam, Malaysia, Colombia, Argentina, Mexico, Brazil, Georgia.

4. Countries with language barriers.  
Official information may exist but not in accessible English/Russian/Spanish, or legal language is too technical.

5. Countries with fragmented bureaucracy.  
Where national, provincial, municipal, tax and migration rules differ, users find contradictory advice.

6. Countries affected by sanctions or banking restrictions.  
Russian, Belarusian, Iranian and other sanctioned-country citizens often face extra banking and compliance questions not covered in generic guides.

7. Family relocation destinations.  
There is often less high-quality integrated information on schools, healthcare, child adaptation, documents and spouse work rights than on visas alone.

Measurement recommendation:
Use a combined dataset: Google Trends + keyword-planner/SEO tool + Reddit/Telegram/Facebook scraping where legally allowed + YouTube comments + official-policy change tracker + manual expert validation.
