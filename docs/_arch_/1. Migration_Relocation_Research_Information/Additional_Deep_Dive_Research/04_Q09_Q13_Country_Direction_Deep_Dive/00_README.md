## Important limitations

1. Immigration, tax, citizenship, property and visa rules change frequently. Every country-specific rule should be rechecked on an official government page before being used in a product, legal guide or paid recommendation.
2. Community sources are useful for discovering real user pains, contradictions and vocabulary. They are not reliable sources for law, taxes, visa eligibility or official processing rules.
3. This pack uses public web research and publicly accessible community signals. It does not replace primary user interviews, surveys or professional legal/tax advice.
4. Country-risk conclusions should be treated as dynamic signals, not fixed labels. The strongest product approach is to show time-series changes and source-backed evidence, not one permanent score.

## Short executive summary

The strongest opportunity is not another generic “best country to move to” ranking. The unmet need is a **verified, timestamped, source-linked, scenario-based country decision system**. Existing competitors often combine official datasets, crowdsourced cost-of-living data, editorial advice and user reviews, but they rarely expose source freshness, legal uncertainty, local practice variance or personalised trade-offs clearly enough.

The legal environment is moving in two directions at the same time: broad immigration routes are tightening in many high-demand destinations, while targeted routes for skilled workers, entrepreneurs, strategic investors and remote workers continue to expand. Digital nomad visas have become a mature policy category, with more than 48 jurisdictions offering remote-work or digital-nomad visas/permits as of February 2026 [S16].

Country direction can be measured, but not by one index. A credible country-direction model should combine rule of law, political rights, corruption, economic freedom, business environment, peace/safety, migration policy, tax policy and legislative trend data [S46–S56].

For users, the key pain is comparison under uncertainty: people need to understand not only “where is cheap/safe/free”, but **what can go wrong after arrival**: housing access, registration, banking, taxes, healthcare, schools, processing delays, social isolation, and mismatch between online advice and local reality [S14–S15, S61–S66].
