# 11. Country Risk and Country Direction

## Key takeaways

- Country direction can be measured, but only as a multidimensional trajectory.
- No single index is enough. Political freedom, rule of law, corruption, business climate, safety, migration policy, tax, housing and economic indicators may point in different directions.
- The safest product method is to show evidence-backed trends over 5–10 years rather than a single “good/bad country” label.
- “Country risk” must be persona-sensitive. A country may be excellent for a remote worker but poor for a family, strong for investors but weak for students, or stable politically but risky for entrepreneurs.

---

## 11.1. Which countries are becoming more politically stable?

Political stability should be measured through a combination of:

- Global Peace Index;
- World Bank Political Stability and Absence of Violence/Terrorism;
- Freedom House scores;
- V-Dem regime trajectory;
- WJP constraints on government powers;
- election integrity and transfer-of-power history;
- protest/conflict intensity;
- sovereign risk and policy continuity.

Candidate categories:

1. **Long-term stable democracies:** Uruguay, Costa Rica, Portugal, Ireland, New Zealand, Denmark, Switzerland, Finland, Norway, Japan and Singapore often perform well across stability, safety or governance measures, though each has different weaknesses.
2. **Recovery/democratization cases:** V-Dem’s 2025-data release identified Brazil and Poland as continuing democratization processes and added Botswana, Guatemala and Mauritius as new democratizing countries [S50]. These should be treated as positive trajectory signals, not final conclusions.
3. **Post-crisis improvement candidates:** Some countries improve after institutional correction, peaceful transitions, anti-corruption reform or conflict reduction. The product should require at least 3–5 years of data before calling a country structurally more stable.

The product should avoid saying “X country is stable” without showing why. Stability should be decomposed into security, regime continuity, institutional checks, macro stability and social conflict.

## 11.2. Which countries are becoming less politically stable?

Less stable countries usually show several combined signals:

- conflict or war;
- coups or constitutional breakdown;
- weakening judiciary;
- political violence;
- emergency rule;
- election manipulation;
- state-capacity decline;
- worsening corruption;
- capital controls or sanctions;
- public-service collapse;
- rapid migration outflows.

Examples of high-risk or deteriorating categories include:

- active war/conflict zones: Ukraine, Russia, Sudan, Myanmar, parts of the Sahel, Syria, Yemen;
- state-fragility and security crises: Haiti, Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, parts of DRC;
- entrenched authoritarian or deteriorating governance: Venezuela, Nicaragua, Belarus, Russia;
- polarisation / rule-of-law pressure cases: varies by year and source, requiring careful index-by-index review.

The WJP reported that the global rule-of-law recession accelerated in 2025, with 68% of countries declining in rule of law [S46]. Freedom House reported global freedom declined for the 20th consecutive year in 2025 [S47]. These are broad warning signals: even countries considered “safe” can deteriorate in specific dimensions.

## 11.3. Which countries are becoming more free and liberal?

A country may become more free through improvements in:

- free and fair elections;
- media freedom;
- civil society space;
- judicial independence;
- anti-corruption enforcement;
- minority rights;
- women’s rights;
- personal autonomy;
- internet freedom;
- academic freedom;
- decentralisation and local democracy.

V-Dem’s 2026 press release noted democratizing countries in the 2025 data, including Brazil and Poland continuing democratization and Botswana, Guatemala and Mauritius as new democratizers [S50]. These are useful signals for “direction”, especially when confirmed by Freedom House, WJP and local legal changes.

For product design, do not reduce “liberal” to one ideological direction. A user may mean:

- personal freedoms;
- low government intrusion;
- business freedom;
- LGBT rights;
- gender equality;
- free speech;
- religious tolerance;
- low censorship;
- drug/criminal justice policy;
- immigration openness.

Each needs a separate indicator.

## 11.4. Which countries are becoming more authoritarian?

Authoritarian direction is usually visible through:

- attacks on courts;
- media capture;
- opposition repression;
- civil-society restrictions;
- electoral manipulation;
- emergency powers;
- internet censorship;
- corruption impunity;
- politicised prosecution;
- anti-NGO laws;
- military influence;
- concentration of executive power.

Freedom House and V-Dem are the core sources for this question [S47–S50]. Freedom House reported a 20-year global freedom decline by 2026 [S47]. V-Dem’s democracy reports focus specifically on autocratization and democratic backsliding [S49].

Potential high-risk categories include entrenched authoritarian states, recent coup states, countries with captured courts and media, and hybrid regimes moving away from checks and balances.

A serious product should avoid political sloganeering and show concrete evidence:

- law passed;
- court independence score changed;
- media freedom score changed;
- election integrity changed;
- NGO restrictions introduced;
- protest rights limited;
- opposition candidates barred;
- constitutional changes adopted.

## 11.5. Which countries are becoming more pro-business?

“Pro-business” has several meanings:

- low corporate taxes;
- easy company formation;
- strong property rights;
- predictable courts;
- low corruption;
- flexible labour regulation;
- good infrastructure;
- simple tax administration;
- strong contract enforcement;
- talent access;
- foreign-investment openness;
- stable currency and banking.

Useful sources:

- World Bank B-READY [S53];
- World Bank WGI regulatory quality and government effectiveness [S52];
- WJP rule of law and absence of corruption [S46];
- Fraser Economic Freedom [S54];
- Heritage Index of Economic Freedom;
- OECD FDI restrictiveness and tax data;
- national reform packages.

Countries often seen as structurally pro-business include Singapore, UAE, Ireland, Estonia, Switzerland, New Zealand, Denmark, Netherlands, Luxembourg, Australia and selected Gulf/Asian hubs. But the product should rank by persona: a small SaaS founder, manufacturing investor, crypto founder, restaurant owner and freelancer need different indicators.

## 11.6. Which countries are becoming less favourable for business?

Business climate deterioration can come from:

- weak rule of law;
- rising corruption;
- capital controls;
- sanctions;
- currency instability;
- tax unpredictability;
- arbitrary licensing;
- expropriation risk;
- political interference in courts;
- public-security deterioration;
- energy unreliability;
- bureaucratic delays;
- sudden foreign-ownership restrictions.

The strongest warning signal is not one bad tax rate. It is the combination of legal unpredictability and weak courts.

A product should monitor:

- WJP rule-of-law trend;
- WGI regulatory quality and rule of law;
- CPI corruption trend;
- economic freedom trend;
- sanctions lists;
- capital-control news;
- tax law changes;
- company-registration friction;
- bankruptcy/contract enforcement;
- investor-state disputes;
- business exit stories.

## 11.7. Which countries are improving institutions, courts, property rights and anti-corruption?

Institutional improvement is slow and should be judged over 5–10 years.

Best indicators:

- WJP Rule of Law Index: constraints on government powers, absence of corruption, open government, fundamental rights, regulatory enforcement, civil/criminal justice [S46].
- World Bank WGI: rule of law, control of corruption, government effectiveness, regulatory quality [S52].
- Transparency International CPI: perceived public-sector corruption [S51].
- V-Dem: judicial constraints, electoral democracy, liberal democracy, media freedom [S49–S50].
- B-READY: regulatory framework, public services and operational efficiency [S53].

Improvement should be supported by evidence such as judicial reform, procurement reform, anti-corruption convictions, digitised public services, land-register reform, business registry reform, tax administration improvement and reduced court delays.

## 11.8. Which countries are worsening in free speech, rule of law, corruption and political rights?

Global direction is worrying. Freedom House reported decline in global freedom for the 20th consecutive year in 2025, with 54 countries deteriorating and 35 improving [S47]. The WJP reported that 68% of countries declined in rule of law in 2025 [S46]. Transparency International’s CPI 2025 stated that many countries fail to control corruption, with a global average score of 42/100 and 122 of 182 countries scoring below 50 [S51].

For a product, “worsening” should be assigned only after comparing multiple indicators and legal evidence. A good country-direction card should show:

- score 10 years ago;
- score 5 years ago;
- latest score;
- direction arrow;
- confidence level;
- source disagreement;
- important laws/events explaining the movement.

## 11.9. Which indexes best show country direction?

Recommended index stack:

| Dimension | Best sources | Why useful |
|---|---|---|
| Democracy / autocratization | V-Dem, Freedom House, EIU | Captures political rights, elections, media, civil liberties |
| Rule of law | WJP, WGI | Captures courts, corruption, open government, enforcement |
| Corruption | Transparency CPI, WGI Control of Corruption | Tracks institutional integrity and bribery risk |
| Business environment | B-READY, Fraser, Heritage, WGI Regulatory Quality | Shows investment/operation friction |
| Peace/safety | Global Peace Index, UNODC homicide, national crime | Captures conflict, security and violent crime |
| Quality of life | OECD Better Life, Eurostat, UN/World Bank, InterNations | Captures material and subjective well-being |
| Labour market | OECD, World Bank, national statistics, job boards | Shows employability and wage pressure |
| Housing | Eurostat, national statistics, listing data | Captures affordability and access |
| Migration openness | official visa routes, OECD policy monitoring | Shows legal access by persona |
| Tax attractiveness | tax authorities, OECD, PwC | Shows after-tax reality |

The indexes should be normalised and weighted by persona, not globally fixed.

## 11.10. Can country dynamics be determined over 5–10 years using laws, indexes and economic indicators?

Yes, but only probabilistically. A 5–10 year country-direction model can be built from:

1. Index time series.
2. Legislative changes.
3. Court decisions.
4. Migration policy changes.
5. Tax changes.
6. Business regulation changes.
7. Property ownership changes.
8. Inflation and currency stability.
9. Employment and wage trends.
10. Housing affordability.
11. Crime and conflict trends.
12. Demographic trends.
13. Public-service capacity.
14. User experience signals.

Recommended method:

- Create dimension scores, not a single black-box score.
- Track each country yearly and major legal updates continuously.
- Store evidence events: law, index movement, court decision, tax budget, visa reform.
- Separate short-term shock from structural direction.
- Flag index disagreement.
- Show confidence level and data gaps.

Example output:

> Country X: pro-business improving, migration tightening, housing affordability worsening, rule-of-law stable, political freedom declining slightly. Suitable for high-income remote workers; risky for low-budget families and local jobseekers.

This is more useful than “Country X score: 78/100”.
