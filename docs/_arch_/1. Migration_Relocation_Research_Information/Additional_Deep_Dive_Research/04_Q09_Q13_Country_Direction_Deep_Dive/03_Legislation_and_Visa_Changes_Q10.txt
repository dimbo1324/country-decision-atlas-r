# 10. Legislation and Visa Changes

## Key takeaways

- The global direction is selective: broad inflows are under pressure, while skilled-worker, investor, founder and remote-worker routes remain active.
- Countries with housing pressure are restricting foreign property purchase, student inflows, dependants, real-estate golden visas and low-wage sponsorship.
- Countries with talent shortages are simplifying entry for skilled workers, shortage occupations, founders, researchers and high-income remote workers.
- The platform should track not only adopted laws, but also bills, court decisions, official guidance, salary thresholds, eligible occupation lists and transitional rules.

---

## 10.1. Which countries recently simplified residence, PR or citizenship rules?

Recent simplification is usually targeted, not general.

### Germany

Germany is one of the clearest recent simplification examples for skilled workers. The Skilled Immigration Act reforms and the Opportunity Card make it easier for qualified non-EU jobseekers to enter Germany and look for employment under defined conditions [S19–S20]. The reform is not “open immigration”; it is a targeted labour-market tool.

### Japan

Japan introduced a digital nomad specified visa route in 2024 for remote work up to six months, with no extension [S32]. This is a new legal stay option, but not a route to settlement.

### Italy

Italy launched a digital nomad / remote worker visa route for non-EU citizens who work remotely and meet eligibility requirements [S31]. It creates a legal basis for remote work from Italy, but the route remains selective and administratively demanding.

### New Zealand

New Zealand relaxed parts of its investor migration/property connection in 2025 for certain wealthy investor-visa holders, while keeping broader foreign-buyer restrictions. This is a simplification for a narrow investor group, not a general opening.

### Gulf states and selected Asian hubs

UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Singapore and other hubs continue to use long-term residence, golden-style permits, talent visas, business incentives and low-tax positioning to attract specialists, founders, executives and investors. Rules are often route-specific and should be checked on official portals.

## 10.2. Which countries recently tightened migration rules?

### Canada

Canada moved from a high-growth immigration posture toward “stabilisation” and reduced targets. The 2025–2027 plan reduced permanent-resident admission targets to 395,000 in 2025, 380,000 in 2026 and 365,000 in 2027 [S22]. The 2026–2028 plan emphasised reducing temporary-resident arrivals and restoring balance [S21].

### United Kingdom

The UK raised Skilled Worker salary requirements and changed occupation/salary-list rules. GOV.UK now states the standard salary requirement is at least GBP 41,700 per year or the going rate, whichever is higher, unless exceptions apply [S23–S24]. This makes sponsorship harder for lower-paid roles and some regions.

### Portugal

Portugal removed real-estate golden visa options in 2023 [S26]. In 2025 it moved toward stricter immigration and nationality rules, including proposals around longer naturalisation residence periods and tighter family-reunification conditions; some provisions faced constitutional review and were amended [S27–S29].

### Australia and New Zealand

Both countries have been more restrictive around housing, students or foreign property access, while still competing for selected skilled workers and investors. Australia increased scrutiny and costs for foreign residential investment [S35]. New Zealand continues to restrict residential purchases by overseas persons subject to exceptions [S34].

### EU asylum and migration management

Across Europe, asylum, irregular migration, returns, integration and family reunification have become more politically contested. The direction differs by country, but the broader pattern is more enforcement, screening, capacity management and conditionality.

## 10.3. Which countries launched digital nomad visas or similar programmes?

Digital nomad visas are now a mature category. EY reported that more than 48 jurisdictions worldwide offered digital nomad or remote-work visas/permits as of February 2026, with Europe representing about 40% of programmes and the Americas about 29% [S16].

Recent notable examples:

- **Italy** — digital nomad / remote worker visa for non-EU remote workers [S31].
- **Japan** — six-month digital nomad specified visa, no extension [S32].
- **South Korea** — workation/digital nomad-style route launched around 2024; official details should be checked through Korean immigration.
- **Spain, Portugal, Croatia, Greece, Malta, Estonia** — established European remote-worker / digital nomad-style routes.
- **UAE/Dubai, Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, Costa Rica, Panama, Colombia, Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Barbados** — widely discussed remote-worker or long-stay options; requirements differ sharply.

Key differences to track:

- income threshold;
- family/dependant rights;
- tax treatment;
- health insurance requirement;
- remote employer location;
- local labour-market access;
- renewal rights;
- PR/citizenship pathway;
- social-security obligations;
- criminal-record requirements.

## 10.4. Which countries closed or restricted golden visa programmes?

### Spain

Spain ended its golden visa route effective 3 April 2025, subject to transitional rules [S25]. This is one of the most important recent examples because Spain had been a major EU residence-by-investment destination.

### Portugal

Portugal did not fully close its golden visa programme, but it removed real-estate-based and pure capital-transfer routes in 2023, shifting the programme toward funds, culture, research and business/job-creation options [S26].

### Greece

Greece has kept its programme but raised or tiered real-estate thresholds in high-demand areas. Official programme information should be tracked through the Greek migration ministry and implementation rules [S30].

### Ireland / Netherlands / UK / EU pressure

Ireland closed its Immigrant Investor Programme in 2023. The Netherlands has also moved away from investor residence. EU institutions have repeatedly criticised real-estate-based golden visas because of housing, corruption, money-laundering, security and fairness concerns.

The trend is not the death of investment migration. It is a shift from **passive real estate** to **productive investment, innovation, funds, cultural/research contribution and higher scrutiny**.

## 10.5. Which countries are changing tax rules for new residents?

### United Kingdom

The UK replaced the remittance-basis/non-dom framework with a four-year foreign income and gains regime from 6 April 2025 [S37]. This significantly changes the UK proposition for globally mobile wealthy residents.

### Portugal

Portugal closed the old NHR route to most new entrants and introduced the IFICI / “NHR 2.0” type incentive focused on scientific research, innovation and qualified activities [S38]. This narrows the target audience compared with the earlier broad expat-friendly NHR reputation.

### Italy

Italy doubled the flat tax on foreign income for future wealthy new residents from EUR 100,000 to EUR 200,000 per year in 2024 [S39]. It remains attractive for some HNWIs but shows that special regimes can change.

### General pattern

Tax regimes for newcomers increasingly respond to housing pressure, inequality concerns, EU scrutiny, budget needs and competition for talent. For users, the important issue is not only the headline tax rate, but:

- tax residence trigger;
- worldwide income exposure;
- capital gains;
- social security;
- CFC rules;
- foreign company ownership;
- crypto and investment income;
- tax treaties;
- exit tax;
- inheritance/wealth tax;
- reporting obligations.

## 10.6. Which countries restrict foreign real-estate purchases?

Foreign property restrictions are common and politically sensitive.

Examples:

- **Canada** extended the ban on foreign ownership of Canadian housing to 1 January 2027 [S33].
- **New Zealand** restricts residential property purchases by overseas persons, subject to exceptions [S34].
- **Australia** applies approval rules and significant fees to foreign residential investors [S35].
- **Singapore** uses high Additional Buyer’s Stamp Duty for foreign buyers [S36].
- **Switzerland, Denmark, Thailand, Mexico, Indonesia and many others** have special approval systems, land restrictions, leasehold rules, border/coastal-zone rules or local ownership limitations.

For a relocation platform, property rights should not be simplified into “foreigners can buy / cannot buy”. A better model is:

- can buy residential property;
- can buy land;
- can buy condo/apartment;
- can buy only new builds;
- approval required;
- extra stamp duty or taxes;
- minimum investment threshold;
- restrictions by region;
- restrictions by visa/residence status;
- inheritance and resale restrictions;
- mortgage access.

## 10.7. Which countries actively attract specialists, entrepreneurs and investors?

Active talent-attraction countries usually combine immigration, tax, business, education and branding.

Strong categories:

- **Skilled worker competition:** Germany, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, UK, Netherlands, Ireland, Denmark, Sweden, Japan, Singapore.
- **Founder/startup competition:** Canada, UK, France, Netherlands, Estonia, Portugal, Spain, Germany, Ireland, Singapore, UAE, Chile.
- **Investor/HNWI competition:** UAE, Singapore, Italy, Greece, Portugal, Malta, Monaco, Switzerland, New Zealand, selected Caribbean jurisdictions.
- **Remote-worker competition:** Portugal, Spain, Italy, Croatia, Greece, Estonia, UAE, Costa Rica, Colombia, Brazil, Japan and others [S16–S17, S31–S32].

However, “actively attracts” does not always mean “easy to settle”. Some countries offer attractive entry but difficult housing, high costs, slow bureaucracy, weak PR path or complex taxes.

## 10.8. Which countries are becoming less migrant-friendly?

Countries become less migrant-friendly in different ways:

- higher salary thresholds;
- fewer eligible occupations;
- reduced student permits;
- restricted dependants;
- longer citizenship residence requirements;
- tougher family reunification;
- foreign property limits;
- stricter proof-of-funds;
- anti-migration political rhetoric;
- more removals/enforcement;
- harder banking/KYC;
- fewer routes for low-skilled workers.

Recent examples include Canada’s reduction of targets and temporary-resident pressure [S21–S22], the UK’s salary-threshold changes [S23–S24], Portugal’s tightening direction [S27–S29], and Spain’s golden visa closure [S25].

The product should not label countries simply as “friendly” or “unfriendly”. It should be persona-specific:

- friendly to skilled nurses but not students;
- friendly to HNWIs but not low-budget migrants;
- friendly to remote workers but not local jobseekers;
- friendly to investors but not family reunification;
- friendly legally but hostile in housing.

## 10.9. Which legal changes most affect country choice?

Highest-impact legal changes:

1. Visa eligibility and route closure.
2. Salary thresholds and occupation lists.
3. Proof-of-funds requirements.
4. Student work rights and dependant rules.
5. Family reunification conditions.
6. PR and citizenship residence periods.
7. Dual citizenship rules.
8. Tax residence and special tax regimes.
9. Social security obligations.
10. Foreign property rules.
11. Business ownership and company formation restrictions.
12. Local registration rules.
13. Healthcare entitlement.
14. School access for children.
15. Sanctions, banking and KYC barriers.
16. Processing times and appointment availability.
17. Language and integration requirements.
18. Criminal record and security checks.
19. Transitional provisions for existing applicants.
20. Administrative practice after courts or ministry guidance.

A small legal change can completely invalidate a relocation plan. Example: a salary threshold increase can make an otherwise valid job offer non-sponsorable.

### Global / multi-country

- Migration Policy Institute official portal directory [S06].
- IOM Migration Data Portal [S08].
- OECD International Migration Outlook and databases [S02, S07].
- ILO NATLEX for labour/social-security law [S43].
- PwC Worldwide Tax Summaries for tax context [S40].

### EU / Europe

- EU Immigration Portal [S05].
- EUR-Lex and Official Journal [S41].
- N-Lex for national law databases [S42].
- European Commission Migration and Home Affairs.
- National immigration, interior and foreign-affairs ministries.

### Country-specific examples

- UK: GOV.UK immigration rules, Home Office statements, HMRC.
- Canada: Canada.ca / IRCC, provincial nominee programme pages, CRA.
- Germany: Make it in Germany, Federal Interior Ministry, Federal Foreign Office, official law databases.
- Australia: Home Affairs, ATO, official skill occupation lists.
- New Zealand: Immigration New Zealand, LINZ, Inland Revenue.
- USA: USCIS, State Department, CBP, IRS.
- Spain: BOE, immigration ministry, tax agency, consulates.
- Portugal: Diário da República, AIMA, tax authority, parliament.
- Italy: official gazette, consulates, Ministry of Foreign Affairs, tax agency.

A serious product should build a monitoring system around these sources, not manually rewrite blog summaries.
