# 13. User Research

## Key takeaways

- People usually begin with broad emotional search: YouTube, blogs, Reddit, Telegram/Facebook groups, Google queries and “best countries” rankings.
- The hardest part is not finding information; it is deciding what is current, official, personally applicable and realistic after arrival.
- Public community signals repeatedly mention bureaucracy, housing, cost mismatch, loneliness, taxes, healthcare, language barriers and contradictory advice.
- A product should validate demand through interviews with separate personas: digital nomads, families, students, specialists, low-budget migrants, founders and investors.

---

## 13.1. What did people do when they first started thinking about moving?

Most people start with a non-systematic exploration phase:

1. Search “best countries to move to”.
2. Watch YouTube videos about expat life.
3. Read blogs and personal stories.
4. Join Reddit, Facebook, Telegram or WhatsApp communities.
5. Compare cost of living on Numbeo, Expatistan or Nomad List.
6. Search visa names: digital nomad visa, student visa, work visa, golden visa, permanent residence.
7. Ask people who already moved.
8. Check job boards or university websites.
9. Look at apartments and rent prices.
10. Search safety, healthcare, taxes and schools.
11. Contact a lawyer/consultant only after confusion increases.

The first stage is emotional and exploratory: “Could I live there?” The second stage is practical: “Can I legally and financially make it work?” The third stage is risk management: “What can go wrong?”

## 13.2. Which sites, channels and services did they use?

Common sources:

- Google Search.
- YouTube country/expat channels.
- Reddit: r/expats, r/digitalnomad, country-specific subreddits.
- Facebook expat groups.
- Telegram channels and chats.
- WhatsApp communities.
- Nomads.com / Nomad List.
- Numbeo and Expatistan.
- InterNations.
- Government immigration pages.
- Visa consultant blogs.
- Law-firm and Big Four updates.
- Job boards: LinkedIn, Indeed, local boards.
- University pages.
- Real-estate listing portals.
- Tax calculators and salary calculators.
- Embassy/consulate pages.

Users often combine official sources and community advice because official sources answer legal questions but do not explain lived friction.

## 13.3. What was hardest about searching for information?

Hardest problems:

1. **Contradictory advice.** One person says a route works; another says it no longer works.
2. **Outdated information.** Visa rules, tax regimes and fees change, but old pages remain indexed.
3. **No personal applicability.** Most guides ignore passport, income type, family status, profession and timeline.
4. **Language barriers.** Official sources may not be available in English or may use legal language.
5. **Local variation.** National rules do not explain city-level housing and bureaucracy.
6. **Hidden costs.** Deposits, insurance, translations, apostilles, tax filing, private healthcare and school fees are omitted.
7. **Processing uncertainty.** Official timelines differ from actual timelines.
8. **Tax complexity.** Remote workers and company owners struggle to understand tax residence and social security.
9. **Community bias.** Users generalise from one person’s lifestyle or bad experience.
10. **No source hierarchy.** Blog, forum, law and official page are mixed together.

Public Reddit discussions show recurring dissatisfaction with cost-of-living tools and contradictory lived experience, especially around Numbeo/Nomad List style estimates [S61, S66].

## 13.4. What information turned out to be wrong or outdated?

Common outdated/wrong areas:

- digital nomad income thresholds;
- visa appointment availability;
- work rights for students/spouses;
- dependant eligibility;
- proof-of-funds amounts;
- PR/citizenship timelines;
- golden visa availability;
- property-purchase eligibility;
- tax-residence rules;
- rental prices;
- healthcare access;
- school waiting lists;
- opening a bank account;
- recognition of qualifications;
- local registration deadlines;
- remote-work legality.

The highest-risk mistake is using a blog article or YouTube video after a rule has changed. Spain’s golden visa ending in April 2025 and Portugal’s real-estate golden visa removal in 2023 are examples where old content can remain misleading [S25–S26].

## 13.5. What wrong decisions did people make?

Frequent wrong decisions:

1. Choosing a country because it is popular online.
2. Moving before confirming legal work rights.
3. Underestimating monthly cost after rent.
4. Assuming tourist experience equals resident experience.
5. Ignoring tax residence.
6. Renting in the wrong area without understanding transport/safety.
7. Believing that English is enough everywhere.
8. Assuming a visa leads to PR/citizenship.
9. Moving with too little cash buffer.
10. Not checking healthcare access.
11. Not validating school options before arrival.
12. Trusting one consultant without cross-checking official sources.
13. Buying property before understanding tax, ownership and resale rules.
14. Moving for romance/friends without independent legal/financial plan.
15. Overvaluing low headline taxes and undervaluing bureaucracy.

## 13.6. What did they regret after relocation?

Common regret themes:

- loneliness and loss of support network;
- housing difficulty;
- bureaucracy fatigue;
- language isolation;
- lower actual disposable income;
- weaker career growth than expected;
- healthcare access problems;
- school/integration problems for children;
- feeling like an outsider;
- missing family and familiar culture;
- being stuck between two countries;
- wrong neighbourhood/city choice;
- underestimated taxes and insurance;
- difficulty moving money or opening bank accounts.

Community discussions repeatedly mention bureaucracy, loneliness, language integration, identity shift and mismatch between expectations and reality [S62–S65].

## 13.7. What help would they be willing to pay for?

People are more likely to pay for help that reduces irreversible risk:

1. Personal visa-route assessment.
2. Tax-residence and remote-work risk check.
3. Country shortlist based on real scenario.
4. Verified relocation checklist.
5. Document preparation roadmap.
6. Cost-of-living budget adjusted by lifestyle and city.
7. Housing access guide.
8. School/childcare guide.
9. Legal change alerts.
10. PR/citizenship timeline simulation.
11. Consultation with verified experts.
12. “Before you move” risk report.
13. Post-arrival bureaucracy plan.
14. Banking/tax/social-security setup guide.
15. Country comparison report for two or three shortlisted countries.

Willingness to pay is highest when the user faces a deadline, family risk, high investment, legal uncertainty or expensive mistake.

## 13.8. Which help format is most convenient?

Different formats solve different problems:

| Format | Best for | Weakness |
|---|---|---|
| Table | Comparison and filtering | Can oversimplify legal nuance |
| Map | Geographic exploration | Weak for legal/tax detail |
| AI chat | Personal questions and scenario exploration | Must be source-grounded to avoid hallucination |
| Report | Serious decision, paid output | Less interactive |
| Consultation | Complex law/tax/family cases | Expensive, low scalability |
| Checklist | Execution and document preparation | Not enough for country choice |
| Forum | Real experience and warnings | Noisy, biased, contradictory |
| Alerts | Legal/tax changes | Requires strong monitoring |
| Simulator | Budget, tax, PR, suitability | Needs accurate data model |

The best product should combine several formats: interactive comparison, AI-assisted explanation, source-linked report, checklist and community evidence.

## 13.9. What data did they want before choosing a country?

Most wanted data:

- available legal routes;
- probability of eligibility;
- total timeline to residence/PR/citizenship;
- monthly cost by city and lifestyle;
- rent availability and deposit norms;
- tax after relocation;
- healthcare access and insurance cost;
- school/childcare access;
- safety by city/neighbourhood;
- local job market by profession;
- remote-work legality and tax risk;
- banking access;
- language/integration difficulty;
- political and legal direction;
- property ownership restrictions;
- processing time and document list;
- real migrant experience by similar profile;
- hidden first-year costs;
- risk of rule changes.

The key product opportunity is to structure all of this into a “decision dossier” rather than scattered articles.

## 13.10. Which functions did they consider useless?

Likely low-value or risky features:

1. Generic “best country” score without personal weighting.
2. Pretty rankings without source links.
3. AI answers without citations or last-checked dates.
4. User reviews that cannot be filtered by passport, income, family status or year.
5. Cost-of-living numbers without rent type, lifestyle and sample size.
6. Country descriptions copied from Wikipedia-like summaries.
7. Forum noise without moderation.
8. Visa pages without official source links.
9. Static PDFs that go stale quickly.
10. One-size-fits-all checklists.
11. Lifestyle quizzes that ignore legal eligibility.
12. Maps that show attractiveness but not feasibility.
13. Scores that hide trade-offs.
14. Paid “reports” based only on public generic information.

Users do not need another beautiful but vague ranking. They need a trustworthy decision system that tells them what is true, what is uncertain, what changed, what applies to them and what they should verify before committing.

## 13.11. Recommended primary user-research plan

To move from desk research to validated product knowledge, run 50–80 structured interviews across these personas:

- 10 digital nomads;
- 10 families with children;
- 10 students;
- 10 qualified specialists;
- 10 low-budget migrants;
- 10 entrepreneurs/founders;
- 10 investors/HNWIs;
- 10 people who returned home or regretted the move.

Core interview questions:

1. When did you first start thinking about moving?
2. Which sources did you use first?
3. Which source did you trust most?
4. What turned out to be wrong?
5. What was more expensive than expected?
6. What was harder than expected?
7. Which decision would you change?
8. Which information would you have paid for?
9. Which format would have helped most?
10. Which advice online was useless or misleading?
11. What did official sources fail to explain?
12. What did communities explain better than official sources?
13. What would make you trust a relocation platform?
14. What would make you pay?
15. What would make you stop using it?

Output should be coded into pain categories, trigger moments, paid-intent signals, source-trust hierarchy and feature requirements.
