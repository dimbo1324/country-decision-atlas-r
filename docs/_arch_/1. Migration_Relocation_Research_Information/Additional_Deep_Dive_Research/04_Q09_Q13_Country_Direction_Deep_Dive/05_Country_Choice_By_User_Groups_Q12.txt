# 12. Country Choice by Different User Groups

## Key takeaways

- Different groups use different decision logic. A single “best countries” ranking is misleading.
- Universal criteria exist, but each persona weights them differently.
- A strong platform should ask for scenario data first, then build a personalised country shortlist.

---

## 12.1. Criteria for digital nomads

Digital nomads usually prioritise:

1. Legal ability to stay and work remotely.
2. Visa duration and renewal.
3. Tax-residence risk.
4. Monthly cost for their lifestyle.
5. Rent availability and short-term housing.
6. Internet reliability and coworking options.
7. Time zone compatibility with clients/employer.
8. Safety and healthcare access.
9. Community and social life.
10. Climate and lifestyle.
11. Banking and payment access.
12. Travel connectivity.
13. Whether family/partner can join.
14. Whether the route can lead to PR/citizenship.

Digital nomads are vulnerable to tax mistakes. Many focus on visa permission but ignore tax residence, permanent establishment, social security and local registration.

## 12.2. Criteria for families with children

Families usually prioritise:

1. Safety by neighbourhood.
2. School access, language and curriculum.
3. Healthcare quality and waiting times.
4. Housing size and rent/purchase access.
5. Residence stability and family reunification.
6. Childcare cost and availability.
7. Cost after taxes, rent and healthcare.
8. Integration environment and discrimination risk.
9. Public transport and family infrastructure.
10. Long-term PR/citizenship path.
11. Dual citizenship and children’s citizenship rights.
12. Special-needs support where relevant.
13. Legal rights for spouse/partner to work.

Families need more certainty than solo migrants. “Try it for six months” is often not realistic because schools, leases, healthcare and children’s adaptation create switching costs.

## 12.3. Criteria for students

Students prioritise:

1. Tuition and scholarship availability.
2. Visa approval probability.
3. Proof-of-funds requirement.
4. Work rights during study.
5. Post-study work route.
6. PR pathway after graduation.
7. Recognition of degree internationally.
8. Housing availability near campus.
9. Language requirements.
10. Healthcare insurance.
11. Part-time job market.
12. Cost of living.
13. Safety and discrimination risk.
14. University reputation and employer links.

Students are especially affected by sudden policy changes in study permits, dependant rights, post-study work and proof-of-funds requirements.

## 12.4. Criteria for entrepreneurs

Entrepreneurs prioritise:

1. Founder/startup visa eligibility.
2. Company formation cost and speed.
3. Banking and payment access.
4. Corporate tax and dividend tax.
5. Hiring rules and talent access.
6. Investor access and startup ecosystem.
7. IP protection and contract enforcement.
8. Regulation for their industry.
9. Remote/hybrid team legality.
10. Accounting complexity.
11. Visa stability for founder and family.
12. Exit options and capital gains tax.
13. Local market size.
14. Ability to sell internationally.
15. Sanctions/KYC risk.

A country can be great for lifestyle but poor for entrepreneurship if banking, taxes and bureaucracy are difficult.

## 12.5. Criteria for investors

Investors prioritise:

1. Legal residence/investment route.
2. Minimum investment amount.
3. Eligible asset types.
4. Lock-up period.
5. Due diligence and AML requirements.
6. Family inclusion.
7. PR/citizenship timeline.
8. Tax treatment of global income and assets.
9. Property purchase restrictions.
10. Currency and capital controls.
11. Political stability and rule of law.
12. Property rights and courts.
13. Exit liquidity.
14. Banking access.
15. Reputation risk.

Investment migration is moving away from passive real estate in several jurisdictions. Product tracking should distinguish “residence by investment”, “citizenship by investment”, “startup investment”, “productive investment”, “fund route” and “property route”.

## 12.6. Criteria for qualified specialists

Qualified specialists prioritise:

1. Eligible occupation lists.
2. Salary thresholds.
3. Employer sponsorship rules.
4. Recognition of qualifications.
5. Language requirements.
6. Labour-market demand.
7. Net salary after taxes and rent.
8. PR path.
9. Family/dependant rights.
10. Job security and employee rights.
11. Professional licensing.
12. Local experience requirement.
13. Industry cluster strength.
14. Career growth.
15. Remote/hybrid work culture.

They need a “job-market reality” layer, not just visa eligibility. A route may exist, but employers may not sponsor foreigners.

## 12.7. Criteria for low-budget migrants

Low-budget migrants prioritise:

1. Low entry cost.
2. Low proof-of-funds requirement.
3. Cheap temporary housing.
4. Ability to work legally soon.
5. Informal support networks.
6. Public transport.
7. Low healthcare entry cost.
8. Low language barrier.
9. Low bureaucracy.
10. Low risk of visa denial.
11. Emergency fallback options.
12. Food and essentials cost.
13. Safety in affordable areas.
14. Ability to regularise status.

This group is most vulnerable to misinformation. A “cheap country” may become expensive if legal work is impossible, registration is slow, landlords demand deposits, or healthcare requires private insurance.

## 12.8. Criteria for people needing a fast passport

Fast-passport seekers prioritise:

1. Legal path to citizenship.
2. Residence period before application.
3. Physical presence requirement.
4. Processing time after application.
5. Dual citizenship permission.
6. Language/integration tests.
7. Clean criminal record and source-of-funds checks.
8. Stability of the law.
9. Citizenship revocation risk.
10. Passport strength and visa-free access.
11. Family inclusion.
12. Tax residence consequences.
13. Military obligations or civic duties.
14. Cost of maintaining residence.

They often underestimate the gap between “eligible after X years” and “passport in hand after X years”. A platform should show realistic total timeline, not only legal minimum.

## 12.9. Criteria universal to almost all groups

Universal criteria:

- legal entry and residence route;
- cost after rent and taxes;
- safety;
- healthcare access;
- housing availability;
- job/income feasibility;
- language and integration barrier;
- banking and documents;
- political/legal stability;
- quality of life;
- family/partner rights;
- long-term status path;
- reliability of information;
- probability of policy change;
- exit/fallback options.

The universal question is: “Can I legally, financially and psychologically sustain life there?”

## 12.10. Criteria important only for specific groups

| Criterion | Mainly important for |
|---|---|
| School curriculum and admissions | Families with children |
| Post-study work rights | Students |
| Employer sponsorship likelihood | Qualified specialists |
| Permanent establishment risk | Remote workers / business owners |
| Investor asset eligibility | Investors |
| Dual citizenship | Passport seekers, families, HNWIs |
| English-language public services | Low-language migrants, families |
| Startup ecosystem | Entrepreneurs |
| Social-security totalisation | remote employees, specialists |
| Property ownership restrictions | investors, families, retirees |
| Healthcare cost over ageing horizon | retirees, HNWIs, families |
| Visa-free travel value | fast-passport seekers |
| Short-term rental seasonality | digital nomads |
| Local licensing | doctors, engineers, lawyers, teachers |
| Sanctions/KYC exposure | migrants from sanctioned/high-risk countries |

Product implication: the platform should not ask users to compare all countries by generic score. It should ask who they are, then calculate which dimensions matter.
